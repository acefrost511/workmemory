## Abstract
As higher education increasingly adopts blended learning, understanding how instructors provide online feedback is critical for informing pedagogically grounded feedback design and supporting instructors’ professional development in blended teaching. This study examined feedback practices in a university-wide blended course with 1,370 undergraduates and 40 instructors. By integrating content analysis, sequence mining and clustering, and process mapping, the study presents a multidimensional view of how feedback is provided over time. A total of 520 instructor feedback messages and 49,955 actions within 1,343 behavioral sessions were analyzed to investigate both semantic and behavioral patterns. The results showed that Didactic Conceptual feedback, characterized by content-rich explanatory responses, was the most frequently used, particularly in response to student confusion. Four behavioral trajectories were identified: \_Task Focused, Video Oriented, Reply and Like Driven,\_ and \_Progress Centered\_. On the basis of these patterns, instructors were grouped into three roles: \_Highly Interactive, Contemplative,\_ and \_Lurking\_. \_Highly Interactive\_ instructors frequently provided concise didactic responses. \_Contemplative\_ instructors, although contributing fewer messages, offered longer reflective feedback based on sustained engagement with course materials. \_Lurking\_ instructors contributed minimal feedback, typically in the form of brief guidance. By integrating content analysis with trajectory-based behavioral analysis, this study offers a process-oriented perspective on how instructor feedback unfolds in a large-scale blended course.
## Introduction
Online and blended learning formats have become increasingly common in the post-pandemic era as institutions seek to expand access without compromising instructional quality (Shea et al., 2022). The shift toward digital delivery reflects a broader transformation in higher education, aimed at addressing the long-standing challenge of balancing scalability with academic rigor. However, this shift has also introduced new challenges. Large lecture sizes and reduced opportunities for in-person interaction mean that much of the teaching and learning support must occur in online spaces. Thus, understanding how instructors provide online feedback in blended courses has become a critical issue.
Feedback has long been identified as one of the most influential factors that shape student learning and engagement (Dennen et al., 2007; Hattie, 2008; Lim et al., 2021). In large-scale blended courses, where direct contact between instructors and students is necessarily constrained, online feedback plays a particularly important role (Sedrakyan et al., 2025). While digital tools such as video lectures, social annotation platforms, and bullet-point comment systems can support self-directed and interactive learning (Jung & Wise, 2025; Larison et al., 2024; List & Lin, 2023; Mamun et al., 2022; Sun & Gao, 2017; Xiang et al., 2025), they cannot replace the pedagogical expertise and guidance that instructor feedback provides (Lahza et al., 2025). Peer feedback initiatives are increasingly adopted to supplement instructor input (Er et al., 2021; C. Huang et al., 2023; Tan & Chen, 2022); nevertheless, empirical studies consistently demonstrate that students place higher value on instructor feedback (Hew, 2015), particularly in cultural contexts where authoritative and expert input is prioritized (Correia, 2020; Zhang et al., 2016). Compared with peer comments, instructor feedback is typically more effective in supporting learning satisfaction and outcomes, and the development of self-regulated learning skills (Oyarzun et al., 2018; Pereira et al., 2016; Zheng et al., 2023).
Despite the growing use of multimodal channels and synchronous livestream formats to deliver high-quality feedback, their implementation in large-scale blended courses poses significant challenges. Instructors often face high student-to-instructor ratios, heavy workloads, and limited time, which make personalized and timely feedback difficult to sustain (Henderson et al., 2019; Lee, 2023; Paris, 2022). Asynchronous text-based feedback offers a scalable solution for institution-wide implementation. Despite its limitations in fostering sustained dialogue (Comer & Lenaghan, 2013; Yin & Shi, 2021), it remains a practical solution in higher education. However, much of the existing literature focuses on Massive Open Online Courses (MOOCs), whereas feedback practices in blended courses occur under different conditions, in which instructor presence, learner support, and required assessment remain central. Understanding the patterns of instructors’ online feedback behavior in structured and credit-bearing courses is therefore a meaningful contribution, because it provides insight into how feedback is actually delivered at scale and informs the design of pedagogically grounded and sustainable feedback practices. Such understanding may also support professional development for instructors teaching in blended programs and guide institutional decision-making related to feedback design and support.

## Discussion
This study investigated instructor feedback practices in higher education by integrating content analysis, sequence mining and clustering, and process mapping.
