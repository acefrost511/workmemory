Abstract
--------

A simulation-based learning (SBL) approach that enables pre-service teachers (PSTs) to develop practice-based teaching skills within simulated classroom environments can be effective as a complementary element in increasing their teaching skills in teaching practicum. This research aims to test the effectiveness of an SBL approach in teaching practicum on PSTs’ Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge (TPACK), classroom management, and lesson planning skills. A quasi-experimental design with a pre-post test control group was used. A total of 129 PSTs were divided into two groups. An SBL approach was applied to the experimental group, while the control group received training within the current teaching practicum course. The results show that, compared to the control group, the experimental group’s use of an SBL approach in teaching practicum significantly improved their TPACK, classroom management, and lesson planning skills. Overall, the findings of this study reveal the effectiveness of SBL-based teaching practicum and contribute to bridging theory and practice in teacher education by supporting the development of future teachers’ teaching skills.

1 Introduction
--------------

It is crucial for pre-service teachers (PSTs) to experience teaching in a classroom environment and get instant feedback regarding their performance (Urhahne & Wijnia, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR80)) before beginning their professional career. However, this is not always possible during teaching practicum in PST training. A limited time and number of teaching experiences in the real classroom environment (Medula, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR54); Presnilla-Espada, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR64)) and limited feedback from peers and mentor teachers on their teaching performance may lead to a failure of PSTs to acquire the necessary teaching skills.

A simulation-based learning (SBL) approach can provide an opportunity to improve teacher education by facilitating the transition from theory to practice (Chernikova et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR16); Hillary, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR36); Hopwood et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR37); Levin & Flavian, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR46)). Various digital tools in SBL environments are recommended to support face-to-face classroom experiences during PST training (Lampert, [2001](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR45); Linder & Weissblueth, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR49); Randolph et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR65); Rayner & Fluck, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR66)) and virtual classroom simulations are considered to be useful tools (Özhan et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR59); Özhan & Altun, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR60); Syed et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR77)). By using SBL, PSTs can rehearse teaching practices in a controlled virtual classroom environment, supporting skill development prior to real-world teaching experiences (Dittrich et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR26); Fischetti et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR30); Hixon & So, [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR35); Meritt et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR55); Rayner & Fluck, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR66); Regalla et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR67)). Participants have been observed suffering embarrassment, stress, and anxiety in SBL environments, which, while conceptualized as SBL hindrances, signify departure from their comfort zones and have been identified as a mediating mechanism shaping learning outcomes (Levin et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR47)). The results of current studies point out that SBL may be utilized to give PSTs more practical training in real-world and virtual settings (Bondie et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR11); Hixon & So, [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR35); Kelleci et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR5); Meritt et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR55); Rayner & Fluck, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR66); Regalla et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR67); Singh-Pillay, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR76); Syed et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR77)). Hillyar et al. ([2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR36)) found that simulations are typically small-scale, topic-specific, and not fully integrated into teacher training programs, and that they make limited use of large, diverse samples. In light of the previous studies, the primary motivation of this study is to evaluate the effectiveness of an SBL approach in developing PSTs’ teaching skills in teaching practicum.

2 Designing teacher education through simulation-based learning
---------------------------------------------------------------

The SBL approach uses real-life imitated environments to support learning through practice, feedback, and reflection (Flavian & Levin, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR31)). This approach specifically focuses on developing the essential skills of learners through practice and feedback (Salas et al., [2005](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR68)). In such an approach, situations where real-life application possibilities are limited can be applied in simulated learning environments, eliminating the risks of negatively affecting real students (Foley & McAllister, [2005](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR32)). Thus, PSTs can improve complex skills on a controlled and simplified platform through practice (Chernikova et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR15)). In the SBL approach, PSTs can experiment, make mistakes, change their teaching strategies, and make repeated attempts. In addition, PSTs can do all these activities independently and at their own pace. It is safe to say that the SBL approach serves as a bridge between PSTs’ theoretical knowledge and practical teaching, thereby contributing to their professional skills (Theelen et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR79)).

Virtual classrooms following an SBL approach are incorporated into the context of teaching practice as a complementary component of teacher education rather than replacing the instructor. Targeted skills can be taught more effectively by integrating virtual classroom simulations into teacher training programs (Dittrich et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR26); Lugrin et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR50); Shernoff et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR70)). Virtual classroom simulations such as TeachLive, simSchool, and SimInClass are used in teacher education. TeachLive, an avatar-based simulation with a mixed reality system (Dieker et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR25)), includes a virtual classroom in which simulated students can be taught (Wallace & Whitten, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR81)). The behaviors of real students are simultaneously reflected in the simulation with avatars. SimSchool is an artificial intelligence-based virtual classroom simulation that offers the opportunity to analyze student differences, tailor instructional processes based on student needs, collect data on teaching effects, and observe teaching results in a two-dimensional virtual classroom (simSchool, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR73)).

SimInClass is an artificial intelligence-based virtual classroom simulator that enables teachers to practice using mobile devices or computers. In this simulation, PSTs can experience classroom management with features such as lesson planning, recognizing students’ characteristics, and coping with misbehavior in class in different classroom seating arrangements. SimInClass helps PSTs choose appropriate pedagogical methods and digital tools for teaching the subject. In this way, PSTs can master their classroom management abilities while reducing their stress levels (Kelleci et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR5)). In addition, SimInClass was designed based on the Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge (TPACK) Theoretical Framework (Dehghan, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR23)), which conceptualizes technology integration with the knowledge and skills of teachers on using technology effectively in the classroom (Mishra & Koehler, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR56)). In SimInClass, PSTs can experience technology integration practices using digital tools such as e-books, tablets, and smart boards during the lesson with different teaching methods and learning outcomes. Therefore, it aims to increase the level of PSTs’ technology integration, classroom management and lesson planning skills.

The SBL approach is effective in teaching complex skills such as classroom management, lesson planning, and conducting the planned lesson in higher education (Chernikova et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR15); Lindberg & Jönsson, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR48); Samuelsson et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR69); Şeker & Yeşiltaş, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR78)). Virtual internships and simulation-based training support PSTs’ technology integration competencies (Aksoy & Kelleci, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR4); Bayer & Öner, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR10); Kim et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR41); Polikarpus et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR63)). While the effectiveness of SBL approaches has been emphasized in various studies, there is a need for practical implementations in virtual classroom environments where the impact of different contextual factors on the teaching process can be observed, particularly in enhancing PSTs’ TPACK development (Öner, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR58)). In this regard, simulation-based teacher training enables TPACK to develop as a transformative and integrated form of knowledge. Long-term and structured simulation experiences allow the components of TPACK to interact holistically within context-rich simulation scenarios (Kulaksız & Kelleci, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR6)). Furthermore, Doğan et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR27)) report positive opinions of PSTs, and Kelleci and Aksoy ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR3)) found that the course participants’ user experience in terms of teacher educators and PSTs was beneficial in the context of SimInClass.

Current experimental studies on simulation-based teacher education, such as those employing simSchool (Badiee & Kaufman, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR8); Christensen et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR17)), TeachLive (Bautista & Boone, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR9); Dawson, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR22); Randolph et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR65)), and Second Life (Mahon et al., [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR52)), have primarily focused on the development of specific teaching skills, particularly self-efficacy, classroom management, and lesson planning. However, these studies typically investigate isolated teaching competencies within short-term intervention designs, which restricts their ability to capture long-term and integrative developmental processes. Chernikova et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR15)), in their meta-analysis on the use of SBL in higher education, report that most implementations last less than a day or a few weeks, with long-term studies being relatively rare. This gap highlights the need for research investigating the sustained impact of SBL on the development of complex, interrelated teaching competencies.

In this context, TPACK, classroom management, and lesson planning were selected as the core dependent variables because they represent the foundational and interconnected domains of teaching competence in today’s technology-enhanced learning environments. TPACK reflects teachers’ ability to integrate technology, pedagogy, and content knowledge; lesson planning operationalizes this integration into structured instructional design; and classroom management demonstrates the practical application of these skills in simulated teaching settings. Examining these variables together enables a holistic understanding of how simulation-based learning supports pre-service teachers in developing both conceptual and practical dimensions of teaching. Moreover, addressing these competencies over a longer intervention period helps to overcome the short-term novelty effects of technology (Al-Balushi & Al-Balushi, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR2); Koch et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR42)) and provides more robust insights into the enduring effects of simulation-based teacher education.

3 Research aim
--------------

The aim of this study is to test the effectiveness of an SBL in teaching practicum in terms of PSTs’ development in TPACK, classroom management, and lesson planning skills. For this purpose, PSTs, mentor teachers, and teacher educators, who are stakeholders in teaching practicum and school-based teacher education, were brought together in a virtual environment. This is to ensure that the PSTs can be observed and given feedback by peer PSTs, mentor teachers, and teacher educators during their teaching experience in the virtual classroom. The process is also supported by artificial intelligence-based feedback in the virtual classroom simulation. To achieve the aim of the study and examine these aspects with reference to the relevant literature, the study tests the following hypotheses.

* H1. PSTs attending SBL in teaching practicum demonstrate higher TPACK than those in the control group.
* H2. PSTs attending SBL in teaching practicum demonstrate higher classroom management skills than those in the control group.
* H3. PSTs attending SBL in teaching practicum demonstrate higher lesson planning and implementation skills than those in the control group.

4 Methodology
-------------

### 4.1 Research design

This study is conducted through a quasi-experimental research design with a pre-test and post-test control group to determine whether the SBL approach in teaching practicum has an effect on the TPACK, classroom management, and lesson planning skills of PSTs. According to Fraenkel & Wallen ([2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR33)), quasi-experimental research involves the implementation of an intervention in situations where it is not feasible to satisfy all the control conditions required for true experimental research. This design was chosen because the study was conducted within an existing teacher education context, where participants were selected through purposeful sampling and participation was voluntary, limiting the feasibility of full experimental control. Nevertheless, to strengthen internal validity, intact groups were randomly assigned to experimental and control conditions, and pre-test measures were employed to examine group equivalence, as recommended by Fraenkel & Wallen ([2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR33)). In addition, the random assignment of intact groups helped reduce the potential influence of confounding variables such as prior technological experience, teaching knowledge and skills, and familiarity with virtual learning environments. In this way, threats to internal validity arising from participant characteristics were further controlled.

Prior to the implementation, a briefing session was held to inform PSTs regarding the purpose and procedures of the study. PSTs who provided informed consent and agreed to take part voluntarily were included as participants in the study. Ethics committee approval was obtained from the ethics committee of the Hasan Kalyoncu University.

### 4.2 Participants and research context

The participants consisted of 129 (104 female and 25 male, *N*experimental = 70, *N*control = 59) senior year PSTs from four departments: Computer Education and Instructional Technologies (*n* = 10), English Language Teaching (*n* = 18), Mathematics Education (*n* = 87), and Primary School Education (*n* = 14). The study was conducted in two universities in Türkiye. The participants were selected using purposeful sampling. This is a non-random sampling strategy in which the researcher deliberately selects participants, cases, or situations that are considered information-rich in relation to the purpose of the study (Creswell & Poth, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR19); Patton, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR62)). The inclusion criteria for the sampling was senior-year PSTs who had completed the compulsory courses of the first three years of the teacher education program, ensuring they possessed the prerequisite knowledge to manage the virtual classroom and teach in the classroom simulation.

To ensure sampling transparency and procedural consistency, inclusion criteria and group composition were defined prior to the intervention and were maintained consistently throughout the study. All participants were enrolled in the same teaching practicum course and followed an identical course structure and duration across both universities. Methodological transparency was enhanced and potential threats to internal validity associated with sampling instability were reduced by maintaining stable participant coverage and minimizing sampling-related variation (Carroll et al., [2007](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR14)).

### 4.3 Virtual classroom simulation environment

In the simulation-based teaching practicum, the participants in the experimental group utilized the “SimInClass Standard” and “SimInClass MP” versions of the SimInClass virtual classroom simulation. Since the study was conducted in Türkiye, the reason for integrating this simulation into teaching practicum is that it provides Turkish language support and the contextual elements such as classroom environment, student characteristics, and course scenarios are similar to the real classroom context in which PSTs practice within the scope of teaching practicum.

SimInClass is a three-dimensional virtual classroom simulation environment developed to train teachers (SimInClass, [2022b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR72)). In the SimInClass simulation, PSTs can analyze students’ personality traits, prepare technology-supported lesson plans, practice in a virtual classroom, and receive feedback. The general features and versions of the SimInClass simulation are described below.

#### 4.3.1 Versions of SimInClass

SimInClass simulation has three versions: “SimInClass Standard”, “SimInClass MP”, and “SimInClass MR”. The “SimInClass Standard” version allows users to use the simulation individually in a single-player format and receive feedback from the simulation.

The “SimInClass MP” version is the multiplayer version of the “SimInClass Standard” mode. In this version, users can practice together with peer PSTs in the virtual classroom. During this time, they can be observed by mentor teachers and teacher educators and receive feedback from both the simulation and their mentors. This version allows for peer teaching where PSTs can observe others’ virtual classroom experiences and give them feedback. The “Mixed Reality (MR)” mode allows pre-service teachers to optionally use the “SimInClass Standard” and “SimInClass MP” versions in a computer-assisted mixed reality environment (SimInClass, [2022a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR71)).

#### 4.3.2 Features of SimInClass

### Virtual students

In the virtual classroom, the virtual students’ behaviors are based on a rule-based artificial intelligence algorithm. Virtual students are designed as intelligent agents so that the user can interact with them and affect the environment and the agents’ behaviors. The architecture of virtual students is based on the Belief Desire Intention (BDI) model (Köknar, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR43)), which provides a framework for simulating human-like behaviors and decision-making processes. Students’ beliefs are represented by observable behaviors, which are consistent across all students. The desires of the virtual students, representing their goals, are influenced by personality traits modelled using the OCEAN Personality Theory (SimInClass, [2022b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR72)). This model, also known as the Five Factor model, defines personality across five orthogonal dimensions (Wiggins, [1996](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR82)): openness (creativity and imagination), conscientiousness (organization and carefulness), extroversion (sociability and outgoing nature), agreeableness (friendliness and generosity), and neuroticism (emotional instability). This dynamic interaction between beliefs, desires, and actions reflects how PSTs can manage virtual classroom behaviors in a controlled and simulated environment. These behaviors resemble real student behaviors, allowing PSTs to experience the authentic teaching challenges they may encounter in real classrooms. By considering these personality traits, PSTs can prepare their lesson plans and determine the methods for dealing with misbehaviors during their practice in the virtual classroom (Simsoft, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR74)).

### Lesson planning

In the simulation, PSTs can practice lesson planning in accordance with Gagne’s instructional design model of teaching activities, choose appropriate methods and techniques, and use technology effectively (Aksoy & Kelleci, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR4); Doğan et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR27)). Gagne et al.’s ([1992](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR34)) instructional design model, widely used in education, presents a series of ordered teaching steps, such as gaining attention and presenting content, that meet the necessary requirements for effective learning. The primary goal of Gagne’s instructional model is to optimize learning by systematically aligning instructional events with the essential conditions of learning, thereby ensuring that lesson objectives are effectively achieved. SimInClass provides 16 levels containing 16 different lesson scenarios at the primary school level. Users cannot proceed to the next level without completing the previous level (Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#Fig1)a). PSTs can also perform the pre-planned lesson scenarios on various subjects, which are presented in a mixed order at each level of the simulation (Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#Fig1)b).

**Fig. 1**

Interface images of SimInClass's lesson planning and scenarios **a**) Lesson scenarios interface **b**) Lesson planning interface

Prior to practicing in the virtual classroom, PSTs analyzed the student profiles, sorted the lesson scenarios using the drag-and-drop method provided in the simulation in accordance with Gagne’s model of teaching activities (Gagne et al., [1992](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR34)), and after preparing the lesson plan, they practice the lesson according to their lesson plan in the virtual classroom environment (Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#Fig1)b). This method refers to an interactive graphical interface feature that allows users to actively manipulate and rearrange digital lesson components.

Practice in virtual classroom: While applying the lesson plan in the virtual classroom, users are provided with instructions (Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#Fig2)a). For example, users can open and use various technologies (e.g., smart boards, tablets and e-books) they plan to use through menus on the interface and by clicking on the relevant device in the classroom. While they practice, they can observe the effects of their lesson plan and the instructional decisions they make during the virtual practice on students’ levels of knowledge, concentration, and entertainment, as well as the frequency and type of misbehaviors that occur (Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#Fig2)a and b).

Additionally, as the levels progress, the number of virtual students in the simulation and the frequency of misbehaviors in the virtual classroom increase. Users can address the virtual students’ misbehavior by choosing the appropriate coping method and making eye contact, helping to improve their classroom management skills during their virtual practice. Users receive immediate feedback from the simulation about the effectiveness of the coping method. They can also make eye contact and have dialog with the virtual students by clicking on them (Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#Fig2)b).

### Feedback and reports

At the end of the virtual practice session, PSTs can receive detailed feedback from the simulation, encompassing both lesson planning and instructional performance (Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#Fig3)a). Additionally, PSTs can receive feedback from their mentor teachers and teacher educators based on their observations of the PSTs’ virtual classroom performances (Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#Fig3)b). The performance report containing this feedback can be generated at the end of the users’ virtual classroom experience (Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#Fig3)a). This report, based on a rule-based artificial intelligence system, includes an evaluation of the user’s teaching performance together with the feedback given to the users by the system as well as by mentor teachers and teacher educators. This dynamic feedback assists users in adjusting their teaching approaches in real-time. The evaluation includes planning and implementing the lesson in accordance with Gagne’s model of teaching activities (Gagne et al., [1992](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR34)), using strategies to deal with misbehaviors, making effective use of technology in the lesson, and interacting with students by analyzing their profiles. The report also includes a graph showing the students’ knowledge, concentration, and entertainment levels during the user’s virtual classroom experience. The feedback system in the practice report is structured to reflect the assessment criteria used in the real classroom (Kelleci et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR5)). In this context, the user’s teaching performance is evaluated holistically, including key components in the evaluation of PSTs in real classrooms, such as lesson planning, classroom management, technology integration, and student interaction (Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#Fig3)b).

### 4.4 Procedure

The implementation process with participants in both the experimental and control groups was carried out in two universities in Türkiye during a 14-week teaching practicum (Fig. [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#Fig4)a). The teaching practicum course included a 2-hour theoretical session at the university and 6-hour practice sessions at the internship school. The first week was a preparation week in which the teaching practicum process was introduced to all participants. Additionally, in the first week, participants in the experimental group received pre-training on how to use the virtual classroom simulation. In the theoretical session of the teaching practicum, a practical guide with information on lesson planning and practice, TPACK practice, analysis of classroom management, student characteristics and classroom layout topics was provided, with each component complementing the others to ensure that PSTs gain a comprehensive understanding of the requirements of teaching practice and feel ready to embark on their teaching practicum experience. Next, in the practice session of the teaching practicum, all participants observed students and mentor teachers in real classrooms on a weekly basis and reflected on their observations with teacher educators. In addition, they prepared four different lesson plans on different topics related to their subject and implemented them in a real classroom for four weeks, receiving feedback on their teaching performance and lesson plans from both teacher educators and mentor teachers.

**Fig. 4**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z/figures/4)

Teaching practicum and simulation-based teaching practicum process

The teaching practicum process was conducted using an SBL approach for the experimental group participants (Fig. [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#Fig4)b). Each week, before engaging in real teaching practice, participants in the experimental group applied the pedagogical prerequisite knowledge learned from the practical guide in the virtual classroom simulation in the theoretical session of the teaching practicum and subsequently implemented their lesson plans on different topics related to their subject in real classrooms within the same week. They received feedback on their virtual performance from the simulation itself and from their peers. For this process, they used their own personal computers. The tasks in the virtual classroom simulation were aligned with those in the practical guide, allowing participants to integrate their virtual classroom simulation experience with the tasks outlined in the guide and to share their experiences with teacher educators. At this step the lesson scenarios embedded in the simulation were used as pedagogical contexts to support the preparation and implementation of lesson plans in accordance with Gagne’s instructional design model, the development of effective classroom management strategies, and instructional decision-making within the TPACK framework. Next, they transferred the pedagogical knowledge and skills gained from the virtual classroom simulation to real classroom settings, integrating these insights into their own subject areas. Finally, they received feedback from both mentor teachers and teacher educators on their performance in both the real and virtual classroom environments. In order to ensure the standardized implementation of the teaching methods during the intervention, mentor teachers, teacher educators, and PSTs followed a structured syllabus. To minimize differences among the PST groups during the implementation process, all activities were standardized through the use of a practical guide. Regular briefing sessions were held to prevent participant attrition, motivational elements were incorporated to encourage active participation, and technical support was provided for the use of the simulation. As a result, the impact of participant attrition on the research findings was minimized (Fraenkel & Wallen, [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR33)).

Within the implementation fidelity framework proposed by Carroll et al. ([2007](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR14)), these procedures primarily functioned as facilitation strategies, defined as mechanisms that support and standardize the delivery of an intervention. The use of a structured syllabus and a practical guide supported protocol adherence by ensuring that the intended content, sequence, and duration of the intervention were delivered consistently across groups. To monitor adherence, session plans and instructional materials were applied uniformly, and session records were maintained throughout the intervention period. Regular briefing sessions and motivational elements supported participant responsiveness by sustaining engagement and active participation. In addition, continuous technical support minimized implementation-related disruptions and helped maintain stable delivery conditions. Together, these measures strengthened overall intervention fidelity and procedural consistency across sites.

### 4.5 Instruments

The following section explains the self-reported scales used in this study, which were filled out by the PSTs.

### TPACK-practical scale

This scale is a 5-point Likert-type scale with 22 items. It was developed by Yeh et al. ([2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR83)) and adapted to Turkish by Ay et al. ([2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR7)). The scale is composed of five pedagogical areas as sub-dimensions: (1) Learners – how to use technology to understand students; (2) Subject Content – how to use technology to clarify the content; (3) Curriculum Design – planning technology-based lessons and using digital materials and teaching strategies; (4) Practical Teaching – the implementation of technology-based lessons in different teaching contexts; and (5) Assessments – how to use technology to assess students. There are no reverse-coded items in the scale. Total scores are calculated by totalling the scores of all items; therefore, the possible total scores range from 22 to 110. Cronbach’s Alpha reliability coefficient was 0.89. In this study, the pre-test alpha value was 0.97 and the post-test alpha value was 0.96, indicating excellent internal consistency.

### Classroom management efficacy scale

The 30-item, 5-point Likert-type scale was developed by Elçiçek et al. ([2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR28)). The scale has five factors: (1) Relationship Management, which means ensuring communication in the class; (2) Instructional Management, which includes conducting different learning activities to sustain the instruction; (3) Behavior Management, which covers eliminating misbehaviors; (4) Physical Layout Management, which defines designing class layout and instructional materials regarding student needs and activities, and (5) Time Management, which focuses on the efficient use of class time for instructional purposes. The scale includes five reverse-coded items. Total scores can be obtained both for each subscale and for the overall scale. The scores vary between 30 and 150. Cronbach’s Alpharceliability coefficient was calculated at 0.87. In this study, it was 0.87 for the pre-test and 0.86 for the post-test, indicating good internal consistency.

### Lesson planning and implementation (LPI) situations scale

The 5-point Likert-type scale was developed by Davran ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR21)) and consists of 28 items. The scale includes two factors: (1) Planning, which defines lesson plan preparation skills; and (2) Practice, which describes PSTs’ teaching process implementation skills. There are no reverse-coded items in the scale. Total scores are calculated by totalling the scores of all items; therefore, the possible total scores range from 28 to 140. Cronbach’s Alpha reliability coefficient was 0.94. The pre-test alpha value was 0.96 and 0.97 for the post-test, indicating excellent internal consistency.

### 4.6 Data collection and analysis

An SBL approach was applied to the experimental group, and the control group received the current teaching practicum course. Both experimental and control groups were administered three scales as pre- and post-test. The pre-test was administered to both groups prior to the intervention to check group equivalence. In this way, threats to internal validity due to participant characteristics were controlled. Valid and reliable measurement instruments were used, and the scales also showed good internal consistency in both pre- and post-measurements (α > 0.80) in this study (Field, [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR29)). The scales were administered in a face-to-face session using an online survey tool (Google Forms), with standardized instructions provided to all participants. This approach helped to prevent errors arising from the measurement instruments (Fraenkel & Wallen, [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR33)).

To analyze the data, descriptive statistics and independent samples t-test on change scores were conducted to examine the impact of SBL approach on PSTs’ TPACK, classroom management, and lesson planning skills. An independent samples t-test was preferred to analyze any significant differences between two unrelated sample means (Büyüköztürk, [2007](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR12)). An independent t-test was conducted to identify the statistically significant difference between the experimental and control groups’ pre-test scores, and to compare the changes between the pre-and post-test results obtained from both groups. The change in pre-and post-test scores was calculated by subtracting one set of the pair from the other to get the difference scores for each pair (Jennings & Cribbie, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR39)). The change score technique allows for controlling unobserved confounders in the case of no significant difference in pre-test scores between groups (Lüdtke & Robitzsch, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR51)). In order to compare the change score series, the independent group t-test was used (Can, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR13)). Finally, Cohen’s d value was used to determine the effect size of the differences, which was evaluated as 0.2 = small effect, 0.5 = medium effect, and 0.8 = large effect (Cohen, [1988](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR18)). The p-value significance level was accepted as 0.01 to mitigate the potential for Type 1 errors arising from multiple testing (Pallant, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR61)).

5 Findings
----------

### 5.1 TPACK development

At the beginning of the implementation process, to identify the difference between the TPACK pre-test scores of the experimental and control groups, an independent group t-test was used. There was no significant difference between groups in terms of TPACK total scores (*t*(127) = −1.015, *p* >.01). There were also no significant differences found in the subdimensions of learners (*t*(127) = − 0.400, *p* >.01), subject content (*t*(127) = −1.325, *p* >.01), curriculum design (*t*(127) = −1.213, *p* >.01), practical teaching (*t*(127) = − 0.812, *p* >.01), or assessment (*t*(127) = − 0.901, *p* >.01).

* H1. PSTs attending SBL in teaching practicum demonstrate higher TPACK than those in the control group.

To test hypothesis 1, the change scores between the pre-test and post-test were calculated for both groups. Then, the average change scores were compared by conducting the independent t-test and the results are presented in Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#Tab1). The results show that the experimental group’s total scores of TPACK and all sub-dimensions of the TPACK scale are significantly higher than the mean scores of the control group (*p* <.01). Significant differences in the curriculum design (*t*(127) = 4.492, *p* <.01, *d* = 0.80) and practical teaching (*t*(127) = 3.983, *p* <.01, *d* = 0.71) sub-dimensions and TPACK total scores (*t*(127) = 4.290, *p* <.01, *d* = 0.76) have a large effect size. Also, the learners (*t**(*127) = 3.026, *p* <.01, *d* = 0.53), subject content (*t*(127) = 3.379, *p* <.01, *d* = 0.60), and assessment (*t*(127) = 3.261, *p* <.01, *d* = 0.58) sub-dimensions of the scale had a moderate effect. These results suggest the SBL approach in teaching practicum had a significant impact on the development of PSTs’ TPACK compared to the regular teaching practicum.

6 Discussion
------------

This study claims that using SBL in teaching practicum yields significant improvement in TPACK, lesson planning, and classroom management skills. A closer examination of each variable is presented below.

### 6.1 The effects of SBL on TPACK

The present study emphasizes the importance of SBL in teaching practicum for developing TPACK levels of PSTs in terms of curriculum design and practical teaching together with learner, subject content, and assessment dimensions. Virtual classroom simulations act as a bridge between theory and practice in teaching for PSTs, contributing to their professional development (Kasperski et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR40); Theelen et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR79)) and promoting their teaching skills (McPherson et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR53)). Therefore, an SBL in teaching practicum can be argued to contribute to the development of PSTs’ TPACK levels. This is in line with several studies (Abbitt, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR1); Jang & Tsai, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR38)) that show the positive effects of using technology in teaching activities to increase the TPACK levels of PSTs. Kim et al. ([2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR41)) found that experiential simulation training significantly improved technology integration competence compared to traditional coursework. Similarly, Kulaksız & Karaca ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR44)) found that experience of using technology in the teaching process positively affects teachers’ TPACK levels. Kelleci et al., ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR5)) also highlight that a simulation-based training platform with integrated multi-source feedback significantly facilitates the application of pedagogical knowledge into practical contexts, offering a comprehensive enhancement of TPACK. In line with these findings, the results of this study suggest that PSTs developed stronger TPACK through simulation-based lesson planning and teaching practice.

The SBL approach in the teaching practicum was found to be effective for the development of each TPACK component. The findings show that the effect size was higher for curriculum design and practical teaching compared to other dimensions. This finding can be attributed to the fact that, unlike the PSTs in the control group, those in the experimental group prepared lesson plans integrating technology according to Gagne’s instructional design model (Gagne et al., [1992](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR34)). Moreover, one of the indicators of development in practical teaching is related to the use of ICT tools in instructional management (Yeh et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR83)). The PSTs in the experimental group may have improved their practical teaching by completing tasks during virtual practice sessions that involve TPACK components and receiving feedback from teacher educators and mentor teachers who observed their performance through the administrative module. Another indicator of practical teaching development is the use of appropriate technologies for teaching contexts (Yeh et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR83)). In this study, it was expected that PSTs in the experimental group would share lesson content on interactive smart boards for students’ tablets. Teacher educators and mentor teachers observed and provided feedback on these tasks. Consequently, PSTs were able to both plan an ICT integrated curriculum and to engage in practical teaching by selecting suitable methods and techniques, effectively utilizing technology in virtual teaching. Thus, PSTs were able to transfer experiences between virtual teaching and real classroom teaching, further reinforcing their TPACK (Kelleci et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR5)).

### 6.2 The effects of SBL on classroom management

Another contribution of the SBL in teaching practicum is the development of PSTs’ classroom management skills in general and in particular in relationship and instructional management. As revealed by several studies, the use of virtual classroom simulation supports PSTs in developing their classroom management skills (Dalgarno et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR20); Dittrich et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR26); Lugrin et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR50); Shernoff et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR70)). Recent studies also emphasize that classroom simulations increase novice teachers’ confidence while reducing their anxiety in the classroom (Delamarre et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR24); Singh-Pillay, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR76)). Research conducted with different simulation platforms consistently demonstrates similar outcomes. For instance, Mahon et al. ([2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR52)) conclude that the Second Life simulation helps PSTs gain self-confidence in classroom management. Likewise, studies using SimInClass simulation indicate that PSTs awareness and skills related to classroom and time management improved through simulation-based practice (Doğan et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR27)). Another study conducted using the SimInClass simulation states that PSTs felt that the simulation was effective in improving their classroom management skills (Şeker & Yeşiltaş, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR78)). Supporting these findings, Samuelsson et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR69)) found that PSTs who practiced with avatars in a simulation reported higher levels of efficacy beliefs in classroom management compared to those who practiced with their peers.

Related studies also support the finding that the SBL in teaching practicum is particularly effective in enhancing the relationship and instructional management dimensions of classroom management. Relationship management entails fostering effective communication within the classroom, in which PSTs were able to engage. Instructional management involves the implementation of various learning activities to maintain effective instruction (Elçiçek et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR28)). In this context, PSTs were able to develop meaningful communication with their virtual students through making eye contact and actively moving around the classroom and were subsequently able to transfer the experiences gained from SBL activities from the virtual classroom context to real classroom contexts (Kelleci et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR5)). In this respect, the more pronounced differentiation of the SBL approach in these two dimensions compared to the others may be associated with the aforementioned factors.

However, no significant difference was found regarding physical classroom layout and time management. In the simulation, users can change the seating layout of virtual students within the scope of physical classroom management. Since PSTs do not have the responsibility of altering the physical layout of real classrooms, they may not have been able to transfer what they have learned to real classroom settings. Therefore, no significant difference may have emerged in the dimension of physical classroom management. Similarly, the lack of significant improvement in time management may be explained by the limited temporal realism of the simulation environment. While participants followed structured lesson scenarios, they were not exposed to authentic classroom interruptions, transitions, or institutional constraints that typically shape time management in real settings. Without experiencing these dynamic pressures, opportunities to develop adaptive pacing strategies may have remained restricted. Recent studies highlight that temporal regulation skills often require prolonged real-world exposure rather than controlled simulation practice (Flavian & Levin, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR31)).

Behavior management is a particularly complex dimension of classroom management. While no significant difference was found regarding this dimension, the literature reports varying findings. Randolph et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR65)) found that PSTs who practiced with TeachLive showed higher levels of success in applying behavior management strategies, suggesting the effectiveness of immersive simulations in teacher training. Similarly, a systematic review study concluded that virtual simulations are crucial in enhancing general teaching skills, including handling behavioral issues and implementing classroom management strategies (Lindberg & Jönsson, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR48)). However, the same study emphasizes that the development of behavior management through scripted or short-term simulations is among the most challenging and context-sensitive competencies. Therefore, the absence of a significant effect should not be interpreted as ineffectiveness of the SBL approach but rather as an indication of the inherently complex and situational nature of behavior management skills. The difference in findings between the present study and the existing literature regarding the behavior management subdimension may be explained by the context-dependent nature of misbehaviors. Student misbehaviors vary across real classroom settings depending on student characteristics, teacher attributes, and cultural factors (O’Brennan et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR57)). Simulated misbehavior on the other hand is contingent on the specific student characteristics and scenarios available within the virtual environment and predetermined patterns of student behavior and interaction. In contrast, the complexity and unpredictability of real classroom student interactions often exceed the capacity of simulated scenarios, potentially hindering the transfer of learning. Therefore, the misbehaviors represented in the simulation may not fully correspond to those encountered in authentic classroom contexts. This contextual variation may have constrained the transfer of behavior management practices from the virtual environment to real classroom settings.

### 6.3 The effects of SBL on lesson planning and implementation

Furthermore, SBL improved PSTs’ lesson planning skills and teaching practice skills in general. PSTs can find enriched learning opportunities in a simulated virtual classroom environment before assuming full responsibility in a real classroom (Hixon & So, [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR35); Meritt et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR55); Rayner & Fluck, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR66); Regalla et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR67)). Therefore, users can try alternative methods, which is more challenging in real classroom environments, encouraging users to experiment, make mistakes and learn from them. In this regard, it was stated by PSTs that virtual classroom simulations improved their teaching and enabled them to practice without the fear of making mistakes (Rayner & Fluck, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR66)). Furthermore, simulations give users a chance to organize the course content with various teaching methods and techniques, use measurement and evaluation methods, and have control over the timing of classroom activities (Kim et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR41)). This finding is also supported by a systematic review study (Kasperski et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR40)) on various types of simulations and their effectiveness, emphasizing that simulations which incorporate various student characteristics and dynamic class environments help PSTs better adapt to real-life teaching scenarios. Additionally, Chernikova et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR16)) highlight that authentic and well-designed simulations contribute more effectively to the transfer of skills, emphasizing the importance of integrating SBL into teacher education.

In the experimental group, PSTs prepared lesson plans tailored to different learning outcomes and scenarios that followed Gagne’s model of teaching activities. Through simulation, they received feedback on these endeavors and subsequently applied their experiences to real classroom settings. Furthermore, they were able to practice teaching, select suitable methods and techniques, and effectively utilize technology while crafting lesson plans (Doğan et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR27); Simsoft, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR75)). These multifaceted opportunities facilitated the comfort level of PSTs in terms of lesson planning and implementation and significantly contributed to the development of their skills in these areas through the SBL approach in teaching practicum (Kelleci et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR5)).

7 Conclusion
------------

This study examined the influence of a SBL approach in teaching practicum on PSTs’ teaching skills, in particular TPACK, classroom management, and lesson planning skills. The study was grounded in the design and implementation of a structured SBL-based course framework embedded within the teaching practicum. Within this framework, an iterative cycle of learning, virtual practice, real classroom practice, and feedback was incorporated into the teaching practicum process. The SBL approach was implemented in a way that brought pre-service teachers, teacher educators, and mentor teachers together within the same instructional process, supporting alignment between teaching practicum and school-based practice. Through repeated engagement with simulated classroom scenarios and guided feedback, PSTs were supported in connecting theoretical knowledge with practice-oriented teaching experiences. The findings of this study provide robust empirical evidence regarding the pedagogical value of virtual classroom simulations as a complementary component of teacher training. PSTs who participated in the teaching practicum with the SBL approach demonstrated significantly greater improvements in TPACK, classroom management, and lesson planning skills than those in the regular practicum. Theoretically, this research contributes to the literature by illustrating the interconnected development of these core teaching skills through a structured, SBL-based practicum design. strengthens PSTs’ ability to systematically align instructional planning decisions with student needs and technology tools before entering real classroom settings. Taken together, these findings support a holistic view of teaching competence development, in which planning, enactment, and classroom management are jointly strengthened through structured simulated experiences embedded within the practicum. From a practical standpoint, the integration of virtual classroom simulations into teaching practicums offers a viable way to safely bridge the gap between pedagogical theory and the complexities of real-world classroom practice, while addressing professional learning needs that are often only partially supported in traditional school-based models. In conclusion, the SBL-based teaching practicum framework developed and empirically tested in this study represents an effective approach for strengthening teacher education programs and preparing future teachers for technology-enhanced learning environments. The findings of this study suggest that integrating the SBL-based teaching practicum as a complementary component of teacher training programs can offer low-risk, feedback-rich opportunities for PSTs and provide practical guidance for teacher educators and curriculum designers in strengthening practicum design.

8 Limitations and future directions
-----------------------------------

This study on teaching practicum with the SBL approach does have some limitations. The process of the teaching practicum was conducted by different mentor teachers and teacher educators. Similarly, in real practice, the students and schools where PSTs teach vary. While this situation provides an advantage, such as a lower pre-service teacher-to-mentor teacher ratio and a lower pre-service teacher-to-teacher educator ratio, allowing for better monitoring of the process, it also introduces a limitation in terms of the discrepancies between the feedback provided by mentor teachers and teacher educators that cannot be controlled.

Similarly, while the involvement of various variables such as the school, teacher educator, mentor teacher, and students in the implementation process provided researchers with the opportunity to increase the number of participating PSTs, the large number of participants prompted researchers to rely on self-reported instruments. In future studies, direct measurements could be conducted, or performance-based research could be carried out with smaller groups. Alternatively, if self-report instruments are preferred, larger samples are needed to enhance the generalizability of the study results.

In addition, the simulation environment used in this study was limited to the use of the “SimInClass Standard” and “SimInClass MP” versions of the SimInClass. The options for scenarios and teaching environments provided by the simulation are limited. The simulation offers the scope to work with different classroom settings, student groups, teaching materials, and teaching objectives, enabling PSTs to gain experience in various instructional environments and address different learning scenarios. Similarly, there are shortcomings in terms of student diversity. The simulation could provide customized scenarios tailored to different student profiles and needs, facilitating interactions with diverse student groups and helping PSTs learn to meet various learning requirements. Lastly, the simulation also lacks in-depth analysis capabilities for classroom interactions and learning outcomes. By offering stronger data analysis and reporting tools, the simulation could enable PSTs to assess their performance and adjust their strategies as needed. Future studies could focus on the feasibility and effect of the SBL approach in virtual and augmented reality environments.

This study restricted its focus to PSTs’ TPACK, classroom management, and lesson planning skills. It is therefore strongly recommended that future studies test the SBL in teaching practicum in terms of other teaching skills. Furthermore, while the implementation was found to be significantly effective in enhancing each of the study variables, it produced varying effect sizes. To further examine the specific aspects and magnitudes of their influence, qualitative studies could be conducted to delve into these variables more deeply. This study contributes to the literature that indicates SBL in higher education improves complex skills (Chernikova et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13951-z#ref-CR15)) by focusing on the effects of simulation-based teacher education on the acquisition of TPACK, classroom management, and lesson planning skills. Future studies could test models that explore the interactions between TPACK, classroom management, and lesson planning skills variables. Future research may expand the scope of SBL by designing lesson plans on different topics related to their subject within the simulation content, thereby enhancing its applicability across diverse teacher education contexts. Moreover, future research can build upon these findings by examining the long-term impact of SBL on in-service teaching performance and investigating how different simulation designs affect specific dimensions of teaching competence.

Finally, another limitation of this study concerns the uneven distribution of participants across demographic variables such as gender and academic department. This imbalance primarily stems from inherent structural differences in enrollment patterns across various teacher education programs in Türkiye. However, since the present study focused on examining the overall influence of the SBL approach on PSTs’ teaching skills rather than conducting department or gender-based comparisons, this distribution was considered an acceptable limitation within the scope of the research. Nevertheless, future studies on simulation-based teacher education may benefit from employing more balanced samples to enable systematic comparisons across demographic variables, thereby allowing a more nuanced examination of how the effects of the SBL approach may vary across different teacher education programs and participant characteristics.
