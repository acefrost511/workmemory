Abstract
--------

This study examined the relationship between teachers’ 21st-century technological pedagogical content knowledge (TPACK-21) and their self-efficacy for online measurement and evaluation (SESOME). Additionally, it investigated how these two dependent variables differed based on independent variables, such as gender, age, school level, subject field, professional experience, and general daily technology use. This quantitative research utilized descriptive and correlational designs. The sample consisted of 337 secondary and high school teachers working in 25 public schools in the Kocaeli province. Data were collected using a personal information form, the TPACK-21 Scale, and the SESOME Scale. The results revealed a moderate, positive, and significant relationship between teachers’ TPACK-21 and SESOME total scores. Furthermore, TPACK-21 scores differed significantly based on gender, school level, subject field, and general daily technology use, whereas no significant differences were found for age or professional experience. Conversely, SESOME scores differed significantly according to age, subject field, and professional experience; however, no differences were observed based on gender, school level, or general daily technology use.

1 Introduction
--------------

The 21 st century is defined as the age of information and technology, with the rapid and continuous development of science and technology. These developments have led to significant changes in every field, from the economy to communication and from health to education. The rapid increase in the amount of information and the fact that information loses its relevance in a short time require learners and education systems to adapt to this change. In this process, abilities defined as 21st-century skills or lifelong learning skills have gained particular importance. These skills help individuals understand, analyze, and direct the era in which they live (Atış-Akyol & Aşkar, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR7)). However, as 21st-century skills do not have a set definition or category, similar skills have been redefined by different institutions and organizations (Çolak, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR20); Akcan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR1)). These include the OECD Skills Framework (Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development), ATC21 Skills Framework (The Assessment and Teaching of 21 st Century Skills), P21 Skills Framework (Partnership for 21 st Century Skills), NRC Skills Framework (National Research Council), NCREL Skills Framework (North Central Regional Education Laboratory), AACU Skills Framework (American Association of Colleges and Universities), and ISTE Skills Framework (International Society for Technology in Education National Educational Technology Standards), and the Turkey Qualifications Framework (Düzgüner et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR24)). In addition to these institutions and organizations, researchers such as Wagner ([2008](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR63)), Trilling & Fadel ([2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR59)), Finegold & Notabartolo ([2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR29)), Larson & Miller ([2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR45)), and Voogt & Roblin ([2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR62)) have also made classifications. Nevertheless, the P21 framework, developed in the United States, is the most extensively utilized and widely recognized (Cansoy, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR17)).

Although 21st-century skills such as creative and critical thinking, problem-solving, and productivity are often emphasized in education (P21, EnGauge, ATSC21, and ISTE), collaboration, communication, technological literacy, media literacy, and social and cultural competencies are also extremely important (Erten, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR27)). When the research literature is examined, it is evident that it is important and necessary to provide individuals with these skills through education and to include them in the education programs of countries (Anagün et al. [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR6); Ekici et al. [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR26); Selvi, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR55)). As a result of these changes, education programs in Turkey were first renewed in 2005, and within the scope of the Turkish Qualifications Framework, some skills such as *creative and critical thinking*, *research and questioning*, *problem solving*, *communication*, *entrepreneurship*, *use of information and communication technologies*, *and correct and effective use of Turkish* were added in 2005 (MEB, [2005](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR46)). In 2018, these competencies were updated again and changed to *communication in the mother tongue and foreign languages*, *mathematical competencies and basic competencies in science/technology*, *digital competencies*, *learning to learn*, *social and citizenship competencies*, *taking initiative and entrepreneurship*, *cultural awareness*, *and expression* (MEB, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR47)). In addition, the duties and responsibilities of teachers have changed in the 21 st century and have become a system that not only provides information but also encourages students to think critically and creatively, guides them in accessing information, provides resources, and directs them (Öğretir-Özçelik, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR49); Erten, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR27)). As a result of changing duties and responsibilities, the need for teacher competence that can improve students’ skills has emerged. For this reason, teachers must have skills such as pedagogical knowledge, cultural knowledge, professional knowledge, mastery of contemporary methods, and the use of technology in accordance with the needs of the age (Gürültü et al.,, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR32)).

Shulman ([1986](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR56)) examined teacher knowledge as subject matter knowledge, pedagogical content knowledge, and curriculum knowledge in 1986 and defined the distinction between the groups. Mishra and Koehler examined technological knowledge, pedagogical knowledge, and content knowledge together in 2006 and created the “Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge (TPACK)” model, which is also accepted in the literature. TPACK includes the knowledge and skills that enable course content to be delivered using the most appropriate technology in a way that students can understand and organize (Doğan, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR22)).

TPACK competencies are especially important for the distance education process to proceed smoothly and for teachers to use their technological knowledge efficiently and effectively (Taşkın-Ekici & Dereli, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR58)). Along with developments in distance education, considerable progress has been made in measurement and evaluation practices. However, measurement and evaluation are not given the necessary importance by distance education institutions, and they even view it as the last stage of the distance education process. When the literature is examined, it is evident that studies on online measurement and evaluation in distance education are limited (Baran, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR11)).

Measurement and evaluation activities are also used in face-to-face education to evaluate how well students have achieved the intended goals and how accurately they have acquired the information (Durmuş & Turgut, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR23)). Owing to these activities, the instructor must closely monitor the students during the teaching process, provide feedback, and support them to learn at their own pace and complete the evaluation activities effectively after the learning process is over (Gülbahar, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR31)). In distance education, measurement and evaluation activities are called “online measurement and evaluation” (Balta & Türel, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR8); Kocatürk-Kapucu & Adnan, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR40)). Balta ([2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR9)) defines online measurement and evaluation as the measurement and evaluation process conducted in a digital environment through information and communication technologies. According to this method, online measurement and evaluation activities should be used not only in distance education but also in educational environments where technological competence is available. It is assumed that in this way, learning experiences will be enriched and a more effective learning environment will be created. However, for online assessment and evaluation activities to be conducted effectively, instructors are expected to have self-efficacy for these activities. Bandura ([1986](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR10)) defines self-efficacy as the ability of an individual to successfully accomplish a goal and the belief that they have gained in this regard.

In this study, online measurement and evaluation is positioned in line with contemporary models of teacher assessment literacy. Assessment literacy is conceptualized as a multidimensional construct that encompasses not only technical assessment knowledge but also teachers’ efficacy beliefs and contextual constraints that shape assessment practice (Al-Bahlani & Ecke, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR3); DeLuca et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR21); Pastore, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR52)). Within Xu and Brown’s TALiP framework, assessment literacy is understood as an interaction of cognitive (knowledge and skills), affective/belief‑based (self‑efficacy, value‑laden orientations), and sociocultural components (Al-Bahlani & Ecke, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR3)). The scale and its subscales used in this study, which address the concept of “self-efficacy” for online measurement and assessment, can be interpreted as digital, assessment-specific examples of this emotion/belief dimension and focus on teachers’ confidence in performing assessment tasks in online environments. (Al-Bahlani & Ecke, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR3); Estaji et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR28)). Research on assessment literacy indicates that assessment training shapes both teachers’ assessment‑related knowledge/skills and their assessment self‑efficacy, although the two constructs do not always align strongly (Kruse et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR43); Nguyen & Habók, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR48); Husain, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR33)). Differentiating multiple self‑efficacy facets within SESOME therefore enables an examination of which types of online assessment self‑efficacy are more closely associated with broader assessment literacy, particularly in technology‑rich or distance education contexts (Estaji et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR28); Nguyen & Habók, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR48)).

With the rapid advancement of contemporary technologies and the evolving understanding of education, teachers have significant responsibilities in equipping students with 21st-century skills. However, teacher competencies and knowledge are also considered important for acquiring these skills. For this reason, teachers are expected to develop their TPACK competencies within the scope of 21st-century skills. In the context of this study, 21st-century skills are defined through the lens of the TPACK framework introduced by Valtonen et al. ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR61)). This framework synthesizes traditional TPACK with the core competencies of the 21 st century, primarily focusing on learning and innovation skills (often referred to as the 4Cs: critical thinking, communication, collaboration, and creativity), digital literacy, and life and career skills. Therefore, when evaluating teachers’ TPACK within the scope of 21st-century skills, this study specifically refers to teachers’ capacity to pedagogically integrate digital tools to foster these multifaceted student competencies, rather than merely using technology for basic instructional delivery. In addition, the rapid development of technology has increased online environments and tools and moved traditional measurement and evaluation activities to online environments in accordance with information and communication technologies (ICT). The use of online measurement and evaluation activities in the course depends on the teacher’s self-efficacy as well as technological possibilities. A review of the existing literature reveals that there are studies examining the relationships between teachers’ TPACK skills and different variables. However, studies examining the attitudes and self-efficacy perceptions of prospective teachers towards online measurement and evaluation tools seem to be limited, and there are no studies examining both TPACK and online measurement and evaluation together. In addition, the research conducted is considered original in terms of examining the relationship between teachers’ TPACK competencies within the scope of 21st-century skills and their self-efficacy levels for online measurement and evaluation; up to date in terms of the development of science and technology, the rapid increase in the amount of information and the loss of its relevance in a short time, learners and education systems adapting to this change; necessary in terms of the transformation of learning environments from traditional learning environments to online learning environments, the spread of online environments, the frequent use of these environments in education; the proper implementation of measurement and evaluation processes used in distance education, increasing the quality in education, and guiding researchers who want to develop the self-efficacy levels of teacher candidates for online measurement and evaluation in teacher education.

This study aimed to reveal the TPACK competencies and self-efficacy levels of online measurement and evaluation of today’s teachers within the scope of 21st-century skills and to examine the differentiation of the relationship between these two competencies in terms of various variables. This study sought to answer the following questions:

1. 1.
   
   Is there a relationship between teachers’ TPACK competencies within the scope of 21st-century skills and their self-efficacy levels for online measurement and evaluation?
2. 2.
   
   Do teachers’ TPACK competencies within the scope of 21st-century skills differ according to gender, age, level, field, professional experience, and the duration of technology use?
3. 3.
   
   Do teachers’ self-efficacy levels for online measurement and evaluation differ according to gender, age, level, field, professional experience, and the duration of technology use?

2 Method
--------

This section provides information on the research model, universe and sample, data collection tools, data collection, and data analysis.

### 2.1 Research model

This study was conducted using descriptive and relational quantitative research methods. While descriptive research methods are used to describe a situation completely and meticulously, correlational research methods are used to examine the statistical association and degree of relationship between two or more variables without implying causality. Correlational studies are divided into two categories: predictive and exploratory. Exploratory relational research is used to understand an event based on the relationship between variables (Büyüköztürk et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR15)). Therefore, this study was conducted by adopting the exploratory relational research model. In the context of this research model, the dependent variables were the teachers’ technological pedagogical content knowledge (TPACK-21) scores and their self-efficacy for online measurement and evaluation (SESOME) scores. The independent variables examined were the demographic and professional characteristics of the participants, specifically gender, age, education level, subject field, professional experience, and general daily technology usage time.

### 2.2 Population and sample of the study

The sample group of the study was formed using convenience sampling, one of the non-random sampling methods. The appropriate sampling method allows seeing events and logical connections between events without loss of time, money, and labor (Büyüköztürk et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR15)). The sample of the study consisted of 337 secondary and high school teachers working in 25 schools affiliated with the Ministry of National Education in the Kocaeli province. Since formal online learning environments and systematic measurement and evaluation activities are not primarily structured at the first level of education according to the new curriculum arrangements of the Ministry of National Education (MEB, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR47)), secondary and high school teachers were included in the sample group. In addition, the fields of teachers were grouped as verbal, numerical, and other; in the verbal field, foreign language, Turkish language and literature, Turkish, social studies, geography, history, religious culture and moral knowledge and philosophy, and departments of, were included, while in the numerical field, science, biology, physics, chemistry, and mathematics departments were included; and in the field grouped as other, physical education and sports, music, information technologies and software, visual arts, technology design, psychological Counselling and guidance, special education, and vocational courses departments were included. Demographic information of the research group is provided in Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#Tab1).

**Table 1 Demographic information of the research group**

Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#Tab1) presents the distribution of age, gender, and years of professional experience. Of the 236 (70.0%) female teachers participating in the study, 101 (30.0%) were men; 65 (19.3%) were 30 years old or younger, 199 (59.1%) were between 31 and 40 years old, and 73 (21.6%) were 41 years old or older. Regarding general daily technology usage time, 82 teachers (24.3%) stated that they used technology for 2 h or less, 214 (63.5%) for 3–5 h, and 41 (12.2%) for 6 h or more.

### 2.3 Data collection tools

The data collection tools of the study were the “Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge Scale within the Scope of 21 st Century Skills (TPACK-21)” developed by Valtonen et al. ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR61)) to TPACK competencies within the scope of 21 st century skills and then adapted into Turkish by Alpaslan et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR4)), the “Self-Efficacy Scale for Online Measurement and Evaluation (SESOME)” developed by Koç et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR39)) to measure self-efficacy levels for online measurement and evaluation, and the “personal information form” prepared by the researcher was used to access teachers’ demographic information.

The TPACK-21 scale used in the study, developed by Valtonen et al. ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR61)), was adapted into Turkish, and its validity and reliability were examined. In the Turkish adaptation study of the scale conducted by Alpaslan et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR4)), data were collected from 339 pre-service teachers studying at the faculty of education of a state university in western Türkiye via convenience sampling. The construct validity of the scale was evaluated using confirmatory dimensional analysis, and Rasch analysis was used for validity and reliability. The Rasch person and item reliability coefficients obtained for TPACK were approximately.90. It was also observed that there was a strong relationship between the dimensions. The scale consists of 38 items divided into seven sub-dimensions defined by Koehler & Mishra ([2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR41)): pedagogical knowledge, technological knowledge, content knowledge, pedagogical and content knowledge, technological and pedagogical knowledge, content and technological knowledge, and TPACK. All correlations between the factors were found to be at medium and high levels. When the fit values ​​between the items were examined, the fit values ​​were determined as 0.98. This value indicates a strong agreement with the data. In the current study, the internal consistency of the TPACK-21 scale was examined based on data collected from 337 participating teachers. Cronbach’s alpha reliability coefficient was calculated as 0.972, indicating an exceptionally high level of reliability for this sample.

SESOME was developed by Koç et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR39)) to reveal the self-efficacy levels of distance education instructors for online measurement and evaluation. For this purpose, 70 items were written and submitted to the evaluation of nine experts, and content validity was attempted to be ensured. As a result of the content validity, it was deemed appropriate to reduce the scale to 49 items and to distribute it to lecturers and teachers online on a voluntary basis for completion. After collecting 316 data, an exploratory factor analysis was performed, and as a result of the analysis, it was decided to separate the items into two components, consisting of 25 items. Confirmatory factor analysis was performed on 202 data to evaluate the final version of the scale, and it was found that the item loadings varied between 0.43 and 0.87, all t values ​​were significant, and the fit indices were at an acceptable level. After the transformation of the scale items, when the factor loadings and the meanings of the factor items were considered, it was observed that the items belonging to the first sub-factor were positively expressed, and the items belonging to the second sub-factor were negatively expressed. For this reason, the sub-factor consisting of 13 positive items was named “Competences, ” and the sub-factor consisting of 12 negative items was named “Deficiencies.” Furthermore, to confirm the reliability of the SESOME instrument within the current sample of secondary and high school teachers, internal consistency was analyzed. The Cronbach’s alpha reliability coefficient for the current study was found to be.941, demonstrating excellent reliability.

### 2.4 Analysis of data

The data collected in the research were analyzed using the SPSS 25.0 program. Descriptive statistics were used to determine the distribution of TPACK-21 and SESOME total scores. To decide which tests to use in the data analysis, scores were calculated based on the answers given by the teachers, and the normality level of the independent variables was determined by examining the skewness and kurtosis values. Initially, data were collected from 350 participants. During the preliminary data screening process, 13 cases were excluded from the dataset. The exclusion of these cases was not to force normality but rather because they contained incomplete responses (missing data) or were identified as extreme multivariate outliers (assessed via Z-scores and Mahalanobis distance) that violated the fundamental assumptions of the parametric analysis. Consequently, the final statistical analyses were reliably conducted with a sample of 337 participants. The skewness and kurtosis values of the teachers’ TPACK-21 and SESOME total scores are presented in Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#Tab2).

**Table 2 TPACK-21 and SESOME Skewness and Kurtosis values**

[Full size table](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6/tables/2)

Tabachnick & Fidell ([2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR57)) assume that a normal distribution is present if the skewness and kurtosis value ranges in the normality distribution are between − 1.5 and + 1.5. Accordingly, when Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#Tab2) is examined, it is seen that the TPACK-21 and SESOME values ​​do not deviate significantly from the specified normal distribution range and show a normal distribution. The relationship between the total scores of the scales was examined by calculating the Pearson correlation coefficient. Since the data were normally distributed as a result of the normality analysis, a t-test was applied for unrelated samples to determine the differences in teachers’ TPACK-21 and SESOME total scores according to gender and grade variables; one-way analysis of variance was applied for unrelated samples to determine the differences according to age, field, professional experience, and general daily technology use variables. While the t-test for unrelated samples is used to determine the significance of the difference between the means of the scores of two unrelated groups, the ANOVA test is used to determine the significance of the difference between the scores of more than two unrelated groups. In addition, if the variances were equal, Tukey and Scheffe tests were used as post-hoc tests during the analysis, but if they were not equal, Tamhane’s T2 test was used (Büyüköztürk et. al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR15)).

In this study, the effect size was assessed to determine the magnitude of the difference between the null and alternative hypotheses (Özsoy & Özsoy, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR51)). The effect size is characterized by the magnitude of the difference between the null and alternative hypotheses (Özsoy & Özsoy, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR51)). According to Cohen ([1988](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR19)), an effect size of 0.2 is considered small, 0.5 is regarded as medium, and 0.8 is interpreted as large.

3 Results
---------

This section presents the results and comments of the research. Before addressing the main research questions, descriptive statistics were calculated to present an overview of teachers’ scores regarding the main constructs and their respective subscales. The minimum, maximum, mean, and standard deviation values for the TPACK-21 and SESOME scales are presented in Table [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#Tab3).

**Table 3 Descriptive Statistics for TPACK-21, SESOME, and Subscales**

[Full size table](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6/tables/3)

As presented in Table [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#Tab3), the descriptive statistics indicate that the participating teachers possessed a relatively high level of technological pedagogical content knowledge, with an overall mean score of 172.98 (SD = 27.77). Similarly, their overall self-efficacy for online measurement and evaluation (SESOME) was found to be at a moderate-to-high level, with a mean score of 1677.89 (SD = 398.32) out of a maximum possible 2500 points. When examining the SESOME subscales, it is noteworthy that teachers reported higher levels of efficacy in the ‘Competences’ dimension (Mean = 944.10) than in the ‘Deficiencies’ dimension (Mean = 735.73). Overall, these findings suggest that the participants feel generally competent and confident in integrating technology into their pedagogical practices and online assessment processes.

### 3.1 Findings regarding the relationships between teachers’ TPACK-21 and SESOME scores and their sub-dimensions

To answer the first question of the study, “Is there a significant relationship between teachers’ 21st-century TPACK-21 competencies and their self-efficacy levels for online measurement and evaluation?“, Pearson ’scorrelation coefficients were calculated. To provide a deeper and more transparent understanding of these relationships, the analysis was conducted not only between the total scores but also across all sub-dimensions of both scales. The comprehensive correlation findings are presented in Table [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#Tab4).

### 3.3 Findings regarding multivariate analysis of the variables (general linear model)

To address the potential overlapping effects of demographic variables (e.g., the natural correlation between age and professional experience) and to control for the inflation of Type I errors caused by running multiple separate mean comparisons, a general linear model (univariate GLM) was conducted. In this comprehensive model, the total SESOME score was assigned as the dependent variable. The participants’ demographic and professional characteristics (sex, age group, school level, subject field, professional experience, and general daily technology use) were entered as fixed factors, while the total TPACKex-21 score was included as a continuous covariate.

As presented in Table [14](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#Tab14), the results of the GLM indicated that when all variables were evaluated simultaneously, the main effects of the demographic and professional variables on SESOME scores were no longer statistically significant (*p* >.05 for all demographic factors). However, the model clearly revealed that teachers’ TPACK-21 scores were a highly significant and robust predictor of their self-efficacy for online measurement and evaluation (F(1, 233) = 166.938, *p* <.001, ηp² = 0.417). This pivotal finding demonstrates that regardless of demographic variations, technological pedagogical content knowledge is the primary and most determining factor in teachers’ self-efficacy for online assessment practices.

4 Conclusion and recommendations
--------------------------------

The study concluded that there was a moderate, positive, and significant relationship between the total scores of TPACK-21 and SESOME in teachers. Based on this finding, it can be concluded that there is a significant positive association between teachers’ technological pedagogical content knowledge competencies and their self-efficacy levels for online measurement and evaluation. To support this finding, Kul et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR44)) stated that as TPACK levels increase, Web 2.0 levels also increase. Chai et al. ([2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR18)) stated that a lack of technology knowledge directly affects the level of TPACK-21. Sarı et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR54)) examined the relationship between the TPACK competencies of teachers in different fields and their attitudes towards information and communication technologies; consequently, a positive relationship was found, but no differentiation was found regarding the fields of teachers.

The literature reveals studies with comparable results (Akgün, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR2); Karademir, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR36); Çam, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR16)), as well as contradictory studies (Karakuyu & Karakuyu, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR37)). Karademir ([2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR36)) found a positive moderate relationship between self-confidence in TPACK and attitudes towards educational technologies. However, Karakuyu & Karakuyu ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR37)) concluded that there was no significant relationship between self-efficacy and the TPACK levels of prospective teachers; thus, self-efficacy did not have statistical significance in predicting the TPACK levels of prospective teachers.

When the differences in teachers’ TPACK-21 competencies within the scope of 21 st century skills were examined according to gender, age, level, field, professional experience, and duration of using technology, it was determined that the differences in TPACK-21 total scores depending on age and professional experience were not statistically significant. In addition, the fact that the teachers’ TPACK-21 total scores did not differ according to the age variable is similar to the results of other studies (Sabo & Archambault, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR53); Burmabiyik, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR14); Bilici & Guler, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR13)). This indicates that teachers’ TPACK levels do not vary according to age and professional experience. Yalçın-İncik ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR64)), on the other hand, did not find any difference in the 21 st century skills of teachers in terms of professional experience factor. However, Kozikoğlu & Özcanlı ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR42)) found in their study that teachers’ use of 21 st century skills differed according to their professional experience.

It was concluded that there were significant differences in the total scores of teachers obtained from TPACK-21 according to their gender, level of duty, field of duty, and general daily technology usage time. This indicates that teachers’ TPACK-21 competencies may vary depending on their gender, level of duty, field of duty, and general daily technology use time. In her thesis study, Altunoğlu ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR5)) found that science teachers’ TPACK competencies were similar according to the gender variable but differed according to the professional experience variable. Orhan-Göksun & Aşkım-Kurt ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR50)) concluded in their study that the use of 21st-century teaching skills differed according to teachers’ gender. Contrary to the study, the results of other studies (Gömleksiz & Fidan, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR30); Jang & Tsai, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR34); Bilici & Güler, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR13)) do not show any difference according to gender. A similar result was encountered by the findings of Gürültü et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR32)), who found that the level of use of 21st-century skills by teachers working in different fields was high, but there was no differentiation according to the gender variable. Uyar & Çiçek ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR60)) revealed that the 21st-century skills of teachers in different fields did not differ significantly according to gender. Eğmir & Çengelci ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR25)) concluded that teachers’ 21st-century skills differed significantly according to the school level at which they worked. Kaçan ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR35)) found that physical education and sports teachers’ use of 21 st century skills did not differ according to the level.

Thus, these findings do not require consideration of factors such as age and professional experience in determining and developing teachers’ TPACK levels; instead, they should be evaluated based on gender, level of duty, field of duty, and general daily technology use duration.

When the differences in teachers’ self-efficacy levels for online measurement and evaluation according to gender, age, level, field, professional experience, and duration of using technology were examined, it was shown that the differences in the total scores of SESOME depending on gender, level of work, and duration of daily technology use were not statistically significant. This indicates that teachers’ self-efficacy levels for online measurement and evaluation do not differ significantly according to gender, level of work, or duration of general daily technology use. Similarly, Yaralı ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR65)) found in her study that there was no difference in teacher candidates’ perceptions of measurement and evaluation competence in terms of gender. When general daily technology usage times were examined, Kiler ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR38)) found that there was no difference between the daily internet usage times of Turkish teacher candidates and their attitudes and self-efficacy towards online measurement and evaluation tools. However, it was concluded that the total scores of teachers obtained from SESOME differed significantly according to their age, field, and professional experience. These results showed that teachers’ self-efficacy levels for online assessment and evaluation may vary depending on certain factors, such as age, field, and professional experience. Studies that show a difference between teachers’ self-efficacy in measurement and evaluation and their professional experiences (Başkonuş, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13974-6#ref-CR12)) support these results.

Considering the multivariate general linear model (GLM) results, which were conducted to control for the overlapping effects of demographic variables, a critical overarching conclusion was reached: When variables such as age, gender, subject field, and professional experience are evaluated simultaneously, their individual effects on online assessment self-efficacy lose their statistical significance. Instead, technological pedagogical content knowledge (TPACK) emerges as the singular and most powerful predictor of this self-efficacy. This indicates that teachers’ capability and confidence to conduct online assessments are fundamentally independent of their demographic backgrounds and are almost entirely driven by their targeted pedagogical and technological competencies. Therefore, it is highly recommended that educational policymakers and school administrators focus their resources directly on professional development programs designed to enhance teachers’ TPACK skills, rather than tailoring interventions based on age or professional experience.

5 Limitations and future research
---------------------------------

First, the use of a convenience sampling method restricted to a single province (Kocaeli) limits the external validity and generalizability of the findings to the broader population of teachers in Türkiye. Second, the ‘daily technology use’ variable measured in this study reflects teachers’ general daily screen time (including personal use) rather than exclusively instructional or professional use. Therefore, inferences linking this general technology use directly to specific classroom assessment practices should be made with caution. Third, it is important to acknowledge the inherent methodological challenges of measuring complex constructs such as TPACK and self-efficacy relying exclusively on self-report surveys. Extensive literature suggests that self-reported measures can be susceptible to method effects, social desirability, and subjective overestimation of one’s own skills, which often leads to high inter-factor correlations. Therefore, the moderate positive correlation (r =.607) found between TPACK-21 and SESOME scores in this study should be interpreted with caution, as it reflects teachers’ perceptions of their competencies rather than their objectively observed skills. To mitigate these self-report biases, future studies should consider triangulating survey data with objective performance-based assessments, classroom observations, or mixed-methods approaches to validate these self-reported competencies more rigorously. Based on the findings, it can be said that factors such as gender, level of duty, or general daily technology use are not necessary to consider when determining and developing teachers’ self-efficacy levels for online measurement and evaluation; however, certain factors such as age, field, and professional experience are important. This information can play a key role in planning and implementing teacher training and professional development programs. According to the research results, teachers’ self-efficacy in online measurement and evaluation can be increased through studies aimed at increasing their TPACK-21. It was concluded that TPACK-21 competencies differ according to variables such as gender, stage, field, and general daily technology use duration, and self-efficacy for online measurement and evaluation differs according to variables such as age, field, and professional experience. By considering the variables that differ in the planning and implementation of teacher education and professional development programs to develop TPACK-21 competencies and self-efficacy for online measurement and evaluation, teachers’ TPACK-21 competencies and self-efficacy for online measurement and evaluation can be increased. The sample size can be increased with teachers working in different cities and schools. A more extensive study, including teacher candidates and faculty members, could be conducted instead of restricting the research sample to teachers at the secondary education level. In future studies, similar studies examining different variables can be conducted. This study was analyzed using quantitative research methods. Qualitative and mixed research methods can be used in future studies to obtain more detailed findings. There are limited studies in the literature on self-efficacy for online measurement and evaluation. It is recommended that new research be conducted on this topic.
