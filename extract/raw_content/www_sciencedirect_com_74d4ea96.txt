## Abstract
Recent developments in artificial intelligence (AI) have revolutionized the field of education as its presence and benefits enable new methods for teaching and learning. Therefore, many empirical studies have been conducted to evaluate the effectiveness of using AI on students' academic achievement. The purpose of this meta-analysis study was to examine the overall effect of AI on students' academic achievement in relation to a set of moderator variables including educational level, role of AI, intervention duration, sample size, learning strategy, subject area, and type of AI. Following the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines, a total of 29 empirical studies were selected from six databases (Scopus, Web of Science, APA PsycINFO, Education Full Text; Education Source, ERIC, and Social Sciences Full Text). These studies, comprising a sample of 2,657 participants across different educational levels, met the inclusion criteria before being coded, calculated, and analyzed. Overall, we found a significant positive effect of AI on students’ academic performance, with an effect size of 0.924. This study validates the efficacy of AI and provides a critical foundation for future research to identify and leverage specific conditions under which AI can most effectively enhance student learning.

## 1. Introduction
With the rapid advancement of Artificial Intelligence (AI), its potential to reshape the educational landscape is becoming increasingly evident ([Salas-Pilco, 2020](#bib61); [Sasikala & Ravichandran, 2024](#bib63)). In recent years, a growing body of research has explored the integration of AI into education, highlighting its ability to enhance learning processes and outcomes. Various AI techniques and applications, such as adaptive learning systems, AI-driven assessment tools, natural language processing, artificial neural networks, machine learning, intelligent tutoring systems, automated tutor, Chatbot, are widely regarded as transformative tools that can improve traditional educational methods and enhance learners’ academic achievement and performance ([Adiguzel et al., 2023](#bib1); [García-Martínez et al., 2023](#bib28), [Lee & Hwang, 2022](#bib46); [Sari et al., 2024](#bib62)).
Prior research has conflicting research findings regarding the relationship between AI and academic achievement. Some research focuses on whether specific AI tools, such as Chatbot or intelligent tutoring system, can directly enhance academic achievement (e.g., [Chang et al., 2022;](#bib12)[Huang et al., 2023](#bib37)), while others investigate how integrating AI into existing platforms might impair or hinder educational learning experiences ([Hamamra et al., 2024](#bib30); [Khalkho et al., 2024](#bib43)). Some researchers even found that there was no significant effect on students' learning performance ([Yin et al., 2021](#bib78)). Furthermore, the diversity of study designs, such as sample sizes, contexts, and disciplines, make it difficult to draw consistent conclusions from existing research. Since AI in education is rapidly developing in recent years, we conducted an updated meta-analysis of the articles to provide updated and holistic knowledge about the relationship between AI and students’ academic achievement in formal learning settings.
## 2. Literature review
### 2.1. Artificial intelligence in education
A rapid development in computing and information communication technologies has led to increased interest in AI, resulting in a growing body of literature within the field of education. The term “artificial intelligence,” introduced by Emeritus Professor John McCarthy of Stanford University in 1955, is defined as a system that uses science and engineering to create intelligent machines” ([McCarthy et al., 2006](#bib56)). Nowadays, AI is the simulation of human-like intelligence through computer systems that use algorithms and data, enabling machines to perform tasks that typically require human cognition ([Fetzer & Fetzer, 1990](#bib26)). This includes activities such as learning, reasoning, problem-solving, and perception ([Kumar & Thakur, 2012](#bib45)). AI technologies can be regarded as machine-based techniques with algorithms for making predictions, decisions, or recommendations ([Herrmann & Weigert, 2024](#bib31); [Hwang et al., 2020](#bib40)). AI-based tools, technologies, and platforms have emerged and are being used in educational settings.
AI can provide new opportunities, potential, and challenges for educators to enhance instruction across various educational levels and contexts. For example, AI can help educators deal with different educational tasks, such as content creation, automated grading and assessment, and learning analytics for students' behaviors ([Chen et al., 2020](#bib16)). AI, specifically chatbots like ChatGPT, can enhance students' engagement and interaction through transforming conventional teaching and learning processes by simulating human interactions and generating human-like text based on input from natural language ([Adiguzel et al., 2023](#bib1); [Azamatova et al., 2023](#bib5); [Karaman, 2024](#bib42); [Li, 2023](#bib49)). Furthermore, AI holds the promise of personalized learning, adapting instructional content to meet individual students’ needs and preferences ([Alneyadi ＆ Wardat, 2023](#bib4); [Drissi et al., 2024](#bib22); [Huang et al., 2023](#bib37)). This adaptability enables educators to implement AI across disciplines, such as geography ([Almelweth, 2022](#bib3)), language learning ([Lin & Chang, 2020](#bib60)), nursing ([Chang, Kuo, & Hwang, 2022](#bib12)), math ([del Olmo-Muñoz et al., 2022](#bib20)), biology ([Iku-Silan et al., 2023](#bib41)) or computer science ([Çakir, 2019](#bib10); [Lu et al., 2021](#bib52)).
A growing body of research suggests that AI can positively contribute to student learning outcomes and academic performance ([Almelweth, 2022](#bib3); [Lee & Hwang, 2022](#bib46)). Although AI has the potential to transform and facilitate education, some researchers still have concerns. For instance, it may hinder the development of critical thinking and deep understanding ([Wu, 2023](#bib74); [Yan et al., 2024](#bib76)) and reduce students' learning motivation and engagement ([Silitonga et al., 2023](#bib65)). Based on these controversies, there is a need for us to synthesize diverse findings, resolving inconsistencies and providing a clearer picture of the overall effects or trends on whether AI can improve or hinder students’ academic achievement.
### 2.2. AI and academic achievement
Academic achievement is a core indicator of educational success, reflecting students' understanding, knowledge retention, and application of skills ([Lemberger et al., 2012](#bib48)). Academic achievement can describe academic outcomes that indicate the extent to which a student has achieved their learning goals. Several definitions of academic achievement have been applied in prior studies. For instance, it can be considered an important outcome of formal educational experiences and serve as a key indicator of educational success for individuals ([Tian & Sun, 2018](#bib70)). It is one performance outcome of instruction and an important factor for shaping a person's outlook on life ([Steinmayr et al., 2016](#bib67)). Academic achievement can be assessed through a variety of indicators, including course grades, rubric, surveys and knowledge tests. These measures can provide unique insights into student performance and learning outcomes, aiding educators in making data-driven decisions to support student success ([Godwin et al., 2015](#bib29); [Mulhall et al., 2002](#bib58)).
AI technology has been proven to be an effective tool or approach to improve learners' academic achievement within formal educational settings. For instance, [Bachiri et al. (2023)](#bib6) employed a gamified AI-based approach, integrating automatic question generation to assess learning outcomes among 100 fifth-grade students. Their findings indicated that this gamified AI strategy substantially improved students’ learning outcomes when compared to traditional instructional methods. Similarly, [Azamatova et al. (2023)](#bib5) investigated the impact of digital tools and AI on academic achievement, specifically in the context of foreign language courses. Their study revealed that AI-driven instructional interventions positively influenced students' language acquisition, underscoring the effectiveness of projected learning models within AI-enhanced environments. However, recent research revealed that there was no significant difference in learning performance between the chatbot group and the control group ([Yin et al., 2021](#bib78)). Considering these conflict results, we conducted this meta-analysis. To be noted, this meta-analysis was restricted to measures of academic achievement with a criterion-oriented reference standard, such as grades from knowledge tests, in order to ensure comparisons across studies.
### 2.3. Existing meta-analyses on AI and academic achievement
To better understand the relationship between AI and academic achievement, many researchers have attempted to synthesize empirical studies by using meta-analysis. For example, [Wang and Zhao (2024)](#bib73) conducted a meta-analysis of 50 experimental studies published from 2010 to 2023. Although this study provided a comprehensive understanding of the impact of AI on learning outcomes, it only considered four moderators: different dimensions, types of AI, educational level, and disciplines. More moderators should be considered and analyzed. [Zheng et al. (2023)](#bib80) conducted a meta-analysis to explore the effectiveness of AI on learning achievement and perception, with 24 articles from 2001 to 2020. This study reported an overall effect size of 0.812 for AI on learning achievement, showing a high effect size. Additionally, this study analyzed the effect sizes of 13 moderator variables, which provided in-depth insights into future experimental design. However, the small number of included studies and fewer databases limit the validity of conclusions. There are also some meta-analyses focusing on specific AI related products or specific learning group, like the effectiveness of using AI Chatbot and learning outcomes ([Wu & Yu, 2024](#bib75)), the use of generative AI and college students’ academic achievements ([Sun & Zhou, 2024](#bib69)), the effects of using AI chatbot in Korean EFL education, the effects of AI-powered virtual agents on learning outcomes ([Dai et al., 2024](#bib19)), the use of intelligent tutoring systems on learning outcomes ([Ma et al., 2014](#bib53)). Those studies all showed a positive correlation. Although prior empirical studies and meta-analyses have investigated the relationship between AI and academic achievement across various contexts and participants in the past decade, we aim to provide updated research and an expanded investigation in the databases on the relationships between AI and academic achievement.
## 3. Research purpose and questions
A meta-analysis was conducted to quantitatively synthesize the empirical findings of prior research published between 2019 and 2024 in order to gain a deeper understanding of recent articles. We also performed a moderator analysis of learning strategy, subject area, education level, roles of AI, types of AI, intervention duration, and sample size to identify the effect of AI on students’ academic achievement in formal learning environments. Our research questions are as follows.
\* RQ1: What is the overall effect size of the effectiveness of AI on students' academic achievement?
\* RQ2: How do various moderator variables (learning strategy, subject area, education level, roles of AI, types of AI, duration of intervention, and sample size) influence the effects of AI on academic achievement?
## 4. Methodology
Guided by the principles of the PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) ([Moher et al., 2009](#bib57)), we conducted this meta-analysis to synthesize the results of previous studies related to the impact of AI on academic achievement.
### 4.1. Search strategy
We developed and conducted a comprehensive search of published studies using a wide range of scientific databases, including Scopus, Web of Science, and EBSCO (including APA PsycINFO, Education Full Text; Education Source, ERIC, Social Sciences Full Text). We targeted articles published between January 1, 2019, and December 31, 2024.
The identified keywords are categorized into two thematic groups. The AI-related keywords encompass terms such as “Artificial Intelligence”, “machine learning”, “ChatGPT”, “Chatbot”, “intelligent tutoring systems”, “intelligent”, and “automated tutor”. The academic achievement-related keywords include “learning performance”, “learning engagement”, “learning accomplishment”, “learning success”, “academic attainment”, “achievement∗”, and “outcome∗”. These keywords were systematically selected to ensure comprehensive coverage of relevant literature in the domain of AI's impact on academic achievement. The search string was created following the Boolean logic. We used a simple “OR” operator between these keywords. We also implemented the use of “∗” after some words so that we could search for multiple variations of the words. Moreover, the use of quotation marks (“ ”) over some terms was intended to search only for that specific term.

### 4.3. Data extraction and quality assessment
All the studies were screened and evaluated by three researchers working independently. As shown in [Fig. 1](#fig1), we used a PRISMA plot to present our data extraction process. We have three rounds of review and evaluation. In the first round, 5825 studies were retrieved and imported. After removing the duplicates, 5241 studies remained for screening. We focused on the title and abstract of each article to determine their suitability. After excluding non-relative studies, 313 full-text studies were extracted for eligibility. In the second round, we carefully read the full text of each article and excluded 283 studies. Finally, 30 studies came to the third round for data extraction and coding.

### 4.4. Coding of studies
An initial codebook containing the following information was developed: learning strategy (e.g., gamified approach, personalized learning, self-regulated learning and other), sample size (e.g., 1–50, 51–100, 101–300), subject area (e.g., language learning, math, computer science, nursing and other), intervention duration, roles of AI (e.g., learning support, feedback and recommendation, assessment and evaluation, or intelligent tutor), education level (e.g., higher education, junior/senior high school), types of AI (e.g., Chatbot, intelligent tutoring system, gamified AI). Three coders were trained and initially coded two articles together to ensure consistency and accuracy in the coding process. Then, two coders were randomly assigned to independently code the remaining studies. In total, 30 studies were coded, resulting in 0.92 inter-rater reliability. Finally, coders discussed and resolved the conflicts together, reaching 100 % agreement.
### 4.5. Statistical analysis
In this study, we incorporated statistically independent effect sizes to preserve the validity of the meta-analytic findings ([Scammacca et al., 2014](#bib64)). Dependence in meta-analysis can arise when studies use multiple outcome measures on the same participants or when two treatment groups are compared to the same control group. To avoid any possible dependent effect sizes, only one effect size was retrieved from each of the 30 extracted studies.
Since 30 effect sizes were retrieved from eligible studies, Hedge's g was chosen for the study instead of Cohen's d. Cohen's d tends to provide a biased population effect size for small samples, while Hedge's g applies a correction to reduce this bias. The correction formula is as follows ([Borenstein, 2009](#bib9)):Hedges’g=Cohen’sd×(1−34×(Nexp+Ncontrol)−9)
This adjustment makes Hedge's \*g\* more suitable for the current study. Additionally, a random effects model was employed as the analytical framework to synthesize the impact of AI on students' achievement scores. The selection of a random effects model over a fixed effects model was justified by conducting the Q test for heterogeneity, calculating the I2 statistics to quantify inconsistencies among study results and estimate the between-study variance (τ2). A forest plot was employed to visualize the effect sizes and their confidence intervals. Additionally, we conducted several moderator analyses to explore whether and how specific variables might influence the effectiveness of the AI-related interventions. The software used for all the analyses is SPSS 29.0.0.0 (241).

Given the potential to overly affect the results in an unrepresentative way, thus leading to a distorted interpretation, this study was excluded from the final analysis. All studies used AI in the intervention groups and traditional learning in the control groups. Among them, 1360 participants were in the control group and 1297 participants were in the experiment group. The majority of studies published after 2019 were conducted in higher education settings (70.0 %), followed by studies in junior and senior high schools (17.2 %), primary schools (10.3 %), and one study involving mixed educational levels (0.05 %). In terms of role of AI in the studies, learning support role accounted for the largest portion of all studies (34.5 %), other roles included assessment and evaluation role (24.1 %), feedback and recommendations (20.7 %), intelligent tutor (10.3 %), decision making (6.9 %) and problem solving (3.5 %). In terms of intervention duration, more than half of the experiments lasted between 1 and 4 weeks (51.7 %), followed by durations of 5–8 weeks (24.1 %), and more than 8 weeks (17.2 %). Two studies did not specify the duration of their interventions. Regarding sample size, most studies involved 51–100 participants (62.0 %), followed by those with 1–50 participants (20.7 %), 101–300 participants (13.8 %), and more than 300 participants (3.5 %). Complementary learning strategies varied across studies. Personalized learning was the most commonly employed strategy (20.7 %), followed by gamification (10.3 %), self-regulated learning (6.9 %), and project-based learning (6.9 %). Other strategies, such as repetitive practice, prompt-based learning, and review and feedback, were each used in a single study and were categorized collectively as “other.” In addition, we also investigated the subject area in each study, and found that language learning has 7 studies, followed by computer science (n = 6), And math, nursing and geography each has 2 studies. We also categorized the rest of the studies as “others”, as each only represented a different subject. Finally, AI interventions were classified into four main categories: Chatbot (n = 17), intelligent tutor system (n = 4), gamified AI (n = 3) and other AI-related intervention (n = 5). The following sections presented results and discussion.
### 5.2. Overall effect size of AI on students’ academic achievement
The overall pattern of effect sizes and the effects of individual study was presented in the forest plot ([Fig. 2](#fig2)). The analysis result yielded a statistically significant positive effect, with a weighted average effect size of 0.924 (95 % CI [0.612, 1.235], p < .01). This positive effect was aligned with prior meta-analysis and related systematic review (e.g., [Sun & Zhou, 2024](#bib69); [Zheng et al., 2023](#bib80)). It suggests that students engaged in the learning process with the support of AI technologies had better academic achievement than their peers in traditional learning environments by an average of 0.924 standard deviations.

A heterogeneity analysis revealed substantial variability across the 29 effect sizes (Q[28] = 233.384, p < .01, I2 = 93 %), indicating that 93 % of the observed variance in the effect sizes could be attributed to between-study differences rather than sampling error, suggesting differences in research outcomes between studies. This high level of heterogeneity suggests significant variation across the studies included in the meta-analysis, likely due to differences in the interventions, populations, and methodologies used. The variability is illustrated in the forest plot ([Fig. 2](#fig2)), where each study's effect size is plotted with its 95 % confidence intervals. The horizontal lines represent the confidence intervals for individual studies, with wider lines indicating greater uncertainty in their effect size estimates. The size of the squares reflects the weight assigned to each study in the analysis, with larger squares representing studies with greater precision, typically due to larger sample sizes. The diamond at the bottom represents the overall pooled effect size and its 95 % confidence interval, calculated using a random-effects model, which accounts for between-study variability.
The random-effects model, which accounts for this between-study variability, was used for the analysis. While the overall distribution of studies was well-balanced, study 22 emerged as a potential outlier with a relatively large effect size. Despite this, as further review confirmed the study's methodological rigor, it was retained in the analysis.
The forest plot also highlights the substantial variability in effect sizes across studies, as evidenced by the broad and overlapping confidence intervals. This variability contributes to the high heterogeneity observed (I2 = 93 %). The plot visually reinforces the importance of using a random-effects model, which accounts for such variability and ensures that the pooled effect size reflects the average across a distribution of varying effects rather than a single fixed effect.

#### 5.3.1. Educational level
The studies were categorized into four educational levels: primary school, junior/senior high school, higher education, and mixed education levels. A significant between-group difference (\*χ\*\*2\* = 11.30, \*df\* = 3, \*p\* < .01) was found, indicating that the impact of AI varies across different education levels. The mean effect size (Hedge's \*g\*) was the highest for mixed education levels (\*g\* = 1.32, \*SE\* = 0.22, \*k\* = 1). This suggests that AI interventions are most effective in mixed education settings, possibly due to the diverse implementation approaches that cater to different learning needs. However, there is only one study involving mixed education levels. Therefore, more studies should be done to verify the results. Except this, the following educational levels are higher education (g = 0.97, SE = 0.22, k = 20), junior/senior high school (g = 1.00, SE = 0.28, k = 5), and primary school (g = 0.49, SE = 0.15, k = 3). This finding is different from prior research ([Zheng et al., 2023](#bib80)), which reported the largest effect size in studies on junior and senior high school students, followed by those in higher education. The reason for this discrepancy is that their study was built on the studies published from 2001 to 2020, while most of our selected studies were published after 2020, with a greater proportion conducted in higher education institutions. According to these findings, AI technologies may be particularly beneficial for students at secondary and higher education levels, possibly due to their greater cognitive abilities and ability to interact with more complex AI applications. The findings are consistent with previous research, which indicates that older students can benefit more from technology use, as they are better equipped to understand and utilize the benefits of AI in classroom settings ([Su & Alberth, 2023](#bib68)). Based on this, there is a need for future studies to assess the suitability of AI tools for younger learners and how to assist them in interacting with AI more effectively.
#### 5.3.2. Role of AI
The analysis of AI's role in educational interventions revealed no significant moderating effect on student achievement (\*χ\*\*2\* = 3.01, \*df\* = 5, \*p\* = .70), suggesting that AI's role does not significantly influence student achievement as a moderator. The lack of significant variation suggests that the overall effectiveness of AI-driven interventions may depend more on other factors rather than the specific functional role AI serves. While certain roles, such as intelligent tutoring and decision-making, exhibited larger effect sizes, their higher variability indicates that these applications may be more sensitive to contextual or methodological differences. Breaking down the results by specific AI roles offers additional insights. For instance, AI's role in assessment and evaluation yielded a moderate effect size (0.62) with relatively low variability (Q = 39.14, df = 6, p < .001), suggesting consistent outcomes across studies. Similarly, feedback and recommendation systems demonstrated a higher effect size (0.81) but also displayed significant variability (Q = 37.30, df = 5, p < .001), indicating potential variability in the design and implementation of these systems. The role of intelligent tutors stood out with a high effect size (2.10) and substantial variability (Q = 62.51, df = 2, p < .001), reflecting the potential of AI to provide impactful personalized learning experiences but also pointing to significant differences in the effectiveness of these systems. Meanwhile, roles such as decision-making (1.20) and learning support (0.85) showed moderate-to-high effect sizes with varying degrees of variability, reinforcing the need for further exploration. Notably, problem-solving, represented by a single study, had a smaller effect size (0.57) but lacked sufficient data for a robust analysis. These findings highlight the need for more nuanced investigations into how specific AI roles can be optimized to maximize their educational impact.
#### 5.3.3. Intervention duration
The duration of AI interventions did not significantly impact AI effectiveness (\*χ\*\*2\* = 8.85, \*df\* = 5, \*p\* = .12), though interventions lasting more than 8 weeks showed a relatively higher effect size (\*g\* = 1.82, \*SE\* = 0.75, \*k\* = 5) compared to shorter implementations. This finding showed that longer intervention duration may be associated with larger effect size, although not all the effect sizes are significant. However, there is not a unified result regarding the intervention duration, but most of studies showed there is no significant difference in the effect of using AI on students’ academic achievement in different experimental periods. For example, [Wang et al. (2024)](#bib72) found a significantly larger effect size for AI-related intervention lasting less than two weeks, while [Zheng et al. (2023)](#bib80) showed the effect size was the largest when studies were implemented for 2–4 weeks. Overall, there is a potential advantage of longer durations for better integration and adaptation of AI tools for students. Therefore, when designing and implementing AI, educators should consider conducting in-depth and long-term intervention to optimize learning outcomes.
#### 5.3.4. Sample size
The sample sizes of the studies were classified into four categories: 1–50, 51–100, 101–300, and greater than 300. Significant between-group heterogeneity was observed (\*χ\*\*2\* = 26.84, \*df\* = 3, \*p\* < .001). The highest effect size was observed in studies with a sample size of 1–50 participants (\*g\* = 1.43, \*SE\* = 0.33, \*k\* = 6), followed by those with 51–100 participants (\*g\* = 0.94, \*SE\* = 0.22, \*k\* = 18), 101–300 participants (\*g\* = 0.44, \*SE\* = 0.18, \*k\* = 4), and studies with over 300 participants (\*g\* = 1.04, \*SE\* = 0.11, \*k\* = 1). The findings indicated that there was a significant difference in effect size among the various sample sizes. Therefore, sample size can influence the effectiveness of AI technologies on learning achievement, which is aligned with prior findings ([Zheng et al., 2023](#bib80)). Furthermore, the largest effect size was found in the smallest sample group (1–50 participants). This pattern suggests that studies with small sample sizes tend to have larger effect sizes than those with large samples as illustrated by [Slavin and Smith (2009)](#bib66). This may be due to smaller studies being conducted in more controlled experimental environments, where interventions might be more closely tailored to participants' specific needs and characteristics ([Nelson et al., 2015](#bib59)). This finding also highlights AI's emphasis on personalized learning, but achieving higher academic gains may be challenging in larger sample sizes, possibly due to greater variability in student characteristics and learning environments.
#### 5.3.5. Learning strategy
For the effect size of each learning strategy, the higher effect value is gamified approach (\*g\* = 0.99, \*SE\* = 0.36, \*k\* = 3), followed by personalized learning (\*g\* = 0.88, \*SE\* = 0.32, \*k\* = 6), project-based learning (\*g\* = 0.61, \*SE\* = 0.17, \*k\* = 2), and self-regulated learning (\*g\* = 0.56, \*SE\* = 0.17, \*k\* = 2). The analysis of variance found the significance level of \*p\* > .05, indicating no statistical difference based on the learning strategy. This suggests that while different strategies may exhibit varying levels of effectiveness in specific contexts, the overall impact on academic achievement does not differ substantially across these strategies within the scope of this analysis.
However, when comparing the effect values of various learning strategies, students' academic achievement appears to improve more when instructors implement gamified approaches, such as game-based learning and gamification. This finding aligns with previous research ([Zeng et al., 2024](#bib79)). The integration of gamification while using AI reveals demonstrates a positive influence on students' academic performance and learning effectiveness ([Yang et al., 2023](#bib77)), because gamification can offer students a more immersive and engaging learning experience while also boosting their motivation to learn ([Li et al., 2023](#bib50); [Lim et al., 2021](#bib51)). Additionally, AI-powered personalized learning supports students’ academic achievement by enhancing their engagement and addressing their individual learning needs ([Mahmoud & Sørensen, 2024](#bib55)). However, other learning strategies included in the analysis were repetitive practice, prompt-based learning, review and feedback strategies, and other methods. However, these were represented by only one study each, limiting the generalizability of their results. Due to the small sample size for these strategies, their effect sizes were not analyzed in detail. Therefore, the impact of these strategies requires further exploration in future research.
#### 5.3.6. Subject area
Results showed subject area emerged as a significant moderator (\*χ\*\*2\* = 39.48, \*df\* = 5, \*p\* < .01), with nursing education showing particularly strong effect (\*g\* = 2.10, \*SE\* = 0.23, \*k\* = 3). Geography (\*g\* = 1.33, \*SE\* = 0.74, \*k\* = 2) and language learning (\*g\* = 1.02, \*SE\* = 0.58, \*k\* = 7) also demonstrated robust effectiveness, while mathematics and computer science showed more moderate effects (both g ≈ 0.47). These findings suggest that AI interventions are particularly effective and supportive in nursing areas, where they can provide personalized, interactive, and context-specific learning experiences ([Chang, Kuo, & Hwang, 2022](#bib12); [Hwang et al., 2024](#bib39)). Furthermore, AI intervention is effective in language learning related subjects, especially English learning (e.g., [Chen et al., 2024](#bib17)). Further research could explore how AI can be adapted to maximize its effectiveness across a wider range of subjects.
#### 5.3.7. Type of AI
The type of AI intervention did not significantly influence its overall effectiveness (\*χ\*\*2\* = 1.09, \*df\* = 3, \*p\* = .78). Type of AI implementation demonstrated consistent effectiveness across different approaches. Among them, chatbots have revealed the strongest effects (\*g\* = 1.05, \*SE\* = 0.25, \*k\* = 17), followed by gamified AI (\*g\* = 0.92, \*SE\* = 0.44, \*k\* = 3), intelligent tutoring systems (\*g\* = 0.74, \*SE\* = 0.20, \*k\* = 4), and any unspecified AI-related intervention (\*g\* = 0.72, \*SE\* = 0.32, \*k\* = 5). Chatbots, especially ChatGPT, was widely used in recent years, which can be considered an effective tool for enhancing students’ academic achievements and have been proved in many studies (e.g., [Ait Baha et al., 2024](#bib2); [Chang, Kuo, & Hwang, 2022](#bib12)). Further, the result is also consistent with the prior study ([Lu et al., 2024](#bib82)), which stated gaming interventions have a high level of positive impact on AI education. However, it is worth noting that we only include three studies related to gamified AI, which may lead to biased results.
### 5.4. Publication bias
Sensitivity analysis for publication bias was assessed using a funnel plot ([Fig. 3](#fig3)), which showed slight asymmetry, indicating a small degree of potential bias. There was a modest overrepresentation of studies with larger effect sizes on the right side of the plot, while fewer studies with smaller or non-significant effects appeared on the left. Although this asymmetry suggests some bias, it was not large enough to have a significant impact on the overall findings. A few studies with higher standard errors fell outside the expected range, but their influence on the meta-analysis appears limited. Most studies clustered near the pooled estimate, which supports the reliability of the findings. Therefore, the results remained robust and provided meaningful insights into the relationship under investigation.

## 6. Conclusions and practical implications
This meta-analysis provides robust evidence of the positive impact of AI on students’ academic achievement and underscores the variability in the effectiveness of AI across different contexts. It emphasizes the need for further exploration of moderator variables. The significant moderation effects of educational level and sample size in this study offer valuable insights for educators and policymakers seeking to optimize AI integration in curricula. In addition, this study validates the efficacy of AI and provides a critical foundation for future research to identify and leverage specific conditions under which AI can most effectively enhance student learning.
Based on these findings, the implications of AI in education for learners' academic achievement are summarized as follows: Firstly, given the positive effects of AI interventions across all educational levels, the integration of AI in school curricula should be encouraged. Particular emphasis should be placed on higher education, where AI can be effectively utilized as a powerful learning tool. Second, the results indicate that studies with smaller sample sizes (e.g., 1–50 participants) demonstrated better outcomes compared to larger sample sizes. Future research should carefully consider and control sample sizes when investigating the impact of AI-based learning tools to optimize students’ learning outcomes. Third, experimental studies in diverse subject areas have demonstrated the potential of AI as an educational tool for enhancing learning. While AI should continue to be used in various fields, such as nursing, geography, and language learning, future research should also explore its application in less-studied disciplines in order to broaden its impact. Finally, future studies should explore additional moderator variables to gain deeper insights into the effects of additional moderator variables. Additionally, further research could examine how AI fosters metacognitive skills and enhances inclusivity in education, providing a more comprehensive understanding of its broader educational implications.
## 7. Limitations
However, this study also has some limitations. First, this study focused on seven primary moderator variables. As other potential moderators, such as theoretical support, were represented in only one or two studies, which may not provide significant results, Therefore, we did not include them in our study. Second, we only focus on peer-reviewed journal articles, so future studies should expand the scope of articles, like dissertations, conference proceedings. Additionally, peer-reviewed articles may introduce publication bias, as studies with positive or significant findings are more likely to be published. This could skew the results, limiting the generalizability of the conclusions. Third, we did not account for studies that include multiple measures for academic achievement. For instance, some AI studies related to language learning tend to measure speaking and listening separately (e.g., [Chien et al., 2022](#bib18)), which may pose challenges in calculating the results. Fourth, a limitation of this meta-analysis is the lack of longitudinal studies and long intervention durations in the dataset, primarily due to the limited number of studies with such data and methodological discrepancies that did not meet our eligibility criteria. As a result, insights into the long-term effects of AI interventions on students' academic achievement are limited. Finally, we only focused on the articles published between 2019 and 2024, the analysis may overlook earlier foundational research.

\* ### [A systematic literature review of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) literacy in schools](/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001274)
2025, Computers and Education Artificial Intelligence
Show abstract
Given the rapid integration of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) technologies, such as large language models, into educational contexts, fostering students’ GenAI literacy has become essential. However, previous AI literacy frameworks may inadequately reflect specific competencies necessary for proficient GenAI use. To address this gap, this study aimed to conceptualise a GenAI specific literacy framework tailored explicitly for educational settings and systematically examine recent research trends concerning GenAI literacy. Employing a systematic literature review approach, 51 empirical studies published in 2023 and 2024 were selected and analysed based on five identified competencies of GenAI literacy: (1) know and understand GenAI, (2) use and apply GenAI, (3) evaluate and incorporate GenAI, (4) GenAI ethics, and (5) attitudes towards GenAI. The findings indicate that students demonstrated moderate understanding of GenAI concepts but frequently faced challenges in prompt engineering and critical evaluation of AI-generated outputs. Ethical considerations, particularly related to academic integrity, privacy, and data security, were highlighted as significant concerns. Furthermore, positive student attitudes towards GenAI, including curiosity and self-efficacy, emerged as vital components enhancing engagement with GenAI tools. A five-step interaction model was proposed to help in fostering students' GenAI literacy, emphasising iterative and dynamic engagement with GenAI tools. This study underscores the necessity of explicitly integrating GenAI-specific competencies into educational practices and recommends clear institutional policies, and further empirical research to support the responsible, effective, and reflective use of GenAI in school settings.
