Abstract
--------

**Purpose:** 

The objective of this research is to compile a thorough review of existing literature, highlighting how artificial intelligence and personalized learning have shaped emerging research opportunities.

**Research methodology:** 

This study employs the PRISMA review protocol alongside a meta-literature review to analyze pertinent works sourced from the Scopus database, spanning the years 2013 to 2025.

**Findings:** 

The study examines the progress and deficiencies in the integration of artificial intelligence and personalized learning in education. It underscores a transition in global research priorities from dominant regions such as China, USA and Europe to broader Asia, signaling new opportunities for educational improvement. However, the findings reveal that the swift expansion of AI, combined with persistent concerns about educational standards in developing countries, may create additional institutional pressures that influence the effectiveness of education.

**Implications:** 

The findings from this review present significant implications for research, practice, and policy. For educators and academic institutions, the results highlight the necessity of professional development that goes beyond technical proficiency, emphasizing pedagogical integration and digital literacy. Furthermore, policymakers are urged to develop ethical frameworks for AI implementation in education, addressing critical issues such as data privacy, algorithmic bias, and unequal access.

**Originality:** 

The significance of this study lies in its effort to bridge a gap in the existing literature by systematically analyzing and reviewing research on Artificial Intelligence and Personalized Learning using PRISMA and meta-literature review methodologies. This comprehensive analysis offers researchers a clearer understanding of key developments and emerging trends within the field. Moreover, it identifies promising avenues for future inquiry and serves as a foundational reference for subsequent investigations.

1 Introduction and literature review
------------------------------------

The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence (AI) technologies has fundamentally transformed the landscape of educational research and practice, with personalized learning emerging as one of the most promising applications of AI in educational settings. Recent comprehensive analyses of the artificial intelligence in education (AIED) field demonstrate the exponential growth and diversification of research priorities, with systematic reviews mapping over 10 literature reviews that collectively illustrate the field’s evolution toward more sophisticated, data-driven approaches to individualized instruction ([Mustafa et al., 2024](#ref27)). As educational institutions worldwide grapple with the challenge of accommodating diverse learner needs, backgrounds, and learning preferences within increasingly heterogeneous student populations, AI-driven personalized learning systems have gained substantial attention as a potential solution to enhance learning outcomes while maintaining educational scalability and accessibility ([Zhang et al., 2024](#ref45)). The convergence of machine learning algorithms, educational data analytics, and adaptive instructional design has created unprecedented opportunities to tailor educational experiences to individual learners, including students with disabilities and those requiring specialized support, with recent meta-analytic evidence demonstrating medium positive effects of AI interventions across diverse learner populations ([Zhang et al., 2024](#ref45)). Contemporary developments in AI-enabled assessment systems and dialogic tutoring platforms exemplify how advanced technologies such as sequence mining, natural language processing, and automated feedback generation are enabling truly personalized educational experiences that adapt to individual learning patterns and preferences in real-time ([Chen et al., 2020](#ref9); [Wang et al., 2025](#ref42)).

The conceptual foundation of AI-enabled personalized learning rests on the premise that effective education must account for individual differences in cognitive abilities, learning styles, prior knowledge, motivation and contextual factors that influence learning processes. Traditional educational approaches constrained by practical limitations of classroom instruction and resource availability have historically struggled to provide truly individualized learning experiences on a scale. However, the emergence of sophisticated AI technologies, including machine learning algorithms, natural language processing, computer vision, and adaptive systems has enabled the development of intelligent educational platforms capable of analyzing vast amounts of learner data in real-time and making dynamic adjustments to instructional content, pacing and delivery methods based on individual learner characteristics and performance patterns.

The historical evolution of AI in personalized learning can be traced through several distinct phases, each characterized by technological innovations and pedagogical insights that have progressively enhanced the sophistication and effectiveness of personalized learning systems. The earliest implementations emerged from rule-based intelligent tutoring systems (ITS) developed in the 1970s and 1980s, which utilized expert systems and predetermined decision trees to provide basic adaptive feedback and content sequencing. These foundational systems established the core architectural components that continue to underpin contemporary AI-driven educational technologies: student models for tracking learner progress and characteristics, domain models for organizing educational content and learning objectives and tutoring models for determining appropriate instructional strategies and interventions.

The subsequent evolution in the 1990s and early 2000s witnessed the integration of more sophisticated data collection mechanisms and statistical modeling approaches, enabling systems to capture and analyze learner interactions with greater granularity and precision. This period coincided with the emergence of learning analytics as a distinct research discipline, focusing on the systematic collection, analysis and interpretation of educational data to improve learning outcomes and institutional effectiveness. The proliferation of online learning platforms and digital educational resources during this era generated unprecedented volumes of learner data, creating both opportunities and challenges for personalizing educational experiences through computational methods.

Contemporary AI-driven personalized learning systems, which have emerged and rapidly evolved since the 2010s, are characterized by the adoption of advanced machine learning techniques, including deep learning, neural networks, reinforcement learning and ensemble methods, which have dramatically enhanced the predictive accuracy and adaptive capabilities of educational technologies ([Figure 1](#fig1)). Modern personalized learning platforms leverage sophisticated algorithms, such as collaborative filtering, content-based recommendation systems, and hybrid approaches, to analyze multiple data streams simultaneously. These data streams include clickstream data, assessment results, time-on-task metrics, physiological indicators, and social interaction patterns, enabling the creation of comprehensive learner profiles that inform personalized instructional decisions.

Recent developments in generative artificial intelligence and large language models have further revolutionized the personalized learning landscape, enabling systems to provide contextually appropriate feedback, generate customized learning materials and engage in sophisticated natural language interactions with learners. These technologies have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in understanding learner queries, providing explanations tailored to individual comprehension levels, adapting communication styles to match learner preferences, and creating personalized educational content across diverse subject domains and learning contexts. The integration of conversational AI agents and intelligent tutoring capabilities has made it possible to create virtual mentors and educational assistants that can provide personalized support at scale, addressing one of the fundamental challenges in traditional educational settings. In this review, generative AI is operationalized as AI systems capable of producing novel, context-sensitive outputs (e.g., text, feedback, learning materials, or dialogue) based on probabilistic language and content models, including large language models and related generative architectures, that support adaptive and interactive learning processes ([Chen et al., 2020](#ref9); [Guettala et al., 2024](#ref15); [Shen et al., 2025](#ref35)).

Despite the substantial technological advances and promising applications of AI in personalized learning, the current research landscape reveals significant gaps in understanding of the effectiveness, implementation challenges and broader implications of these technologies. Existing literature demonstrates considerable heterogeneity in research methodologies, outcome measures, implementation contexts and theoretical frameworks employed to study AI-driven personalized learning systems. This methodological diversity, while reflecting the multidisciplinary nature of the field has resulted in fragmented evidence that limits ability to draw definitive conclusions about the effectiveness of different AI approaches for personalized learning across various educational contexts and learner populations.

Meta-analytic evidence suggests that AI-enabled adaptive learning systems can produce medium to large positive effects on learner cognitive outcomes, with reported effect sizes ranging from *g* = 0.50 to *g* = 0.70 across different studies and contexts. However, these aggregated effects are moderated by numerous factors, including study design quality, implementation duration, adaptive system features, educational domain, learner characteristics and geographic context, indicating that the effectiveness of AI-driven personalized learning is highly dependent on specific implementation conditions and contextual factors.

The current body of research also reveals significant gaps in understanding of the mechanisms through which AI-driven personalization affects learning processes and outcomes. While numerous studies document positive effects of personalized learning interventions, fewer investigations have examined the underlying cognitive, motivational and social processes that mediate these effects. This limitation is particularly important given the complexity of learning processes and the potential for AI systems to influence multiple aspects of the learning experience simultaneously, including cognitive load, motivation, engagement, self-regulation and social interaction patterns.

Methodologically, the field is characterized by considerable diversity in research approaches, ranging from randomized controlled trials and quasi-experimental designs to case studies, design-based research and mixed-methods investigations. While this methodological pluralism reflects the complexity of educational phenomena and the need for multiple perspectives, it also creates challenges for synthesizing evidence and establishing robust conclusions about the effectiveness of AI-driven personalized learning approaches.

The geographic and contextual distribution of research in AI-driven personalized learning reveals significant disparities, with most high-quality studies conducted in developed countries with advanced technological infrastructure and well-established educational research traditions. This geographic concentration raises important questions about the applicability of research findings to diverse cultural, linguistic, and socioeconomic contexts, particularly in developing countries where educational challenges are more acute and technological resources are more limited. Furthermore, most existing research has focused on specific educational domains, such as mathematics, computer science, and language learning, with limited attention to other subject areas and interdisciplinary applications.

Ethical considerations surrounding AI-driven personalized learning have received increasing attention in recent literature, with researchers highlighting concerns related to data privacy, algorithmic bias, transparency, accountability and the potential for AI systems to perpetuate or exacerbate existing educational inequalities. Studies have documented instances of algorithmic bias in educational AI systems, particularly affecting students from underrepresented groups or those with diverse learning needs. Privacy concerns have been raised regarding the extensive data collection required for effective personalization, including sensitive information about learning difficulties, socioeconomic status and personal characteristics that may be inferred from behavioral data.

The implementation of AI-driven personalized learning systems in real-world educational contexts faces numerous practical challenges that have been inadequately addressed in existing research. These challenges include technical infrastructure requirements, teacher training and support needs, integration with existing educational systems and curricula, cost considerations and institutional change management processes. Research has identified teacher acceptance and trust as critical factors influencing the successful implementation of AI-driven educational technologies. However, few studies have systematically examined the factors that promote or inhibit teacher adoption of these systems.

Current literature reviews and systematic syntheses in the field of AI and personalized learning have made important contributions to understanding but also reveal significant limitations that justify the need for additional comprehensive reviews. Existing meta-analyses have typically focused on specific types of AI systems or educational domains, limiting their scope and generalizability. Many reviews have included studies of varying methodological quality without implementing rigorous quality assessment criteria, which may compromise the reliability of their conclusions. Furthermore, most existing reviews have not adequately addressed implementation factors, contextual moderators, and ethical considerations that are crucial for understanding the broader implications of AI-driven personalized learning.

The rapid pace of technological development in AI has also created challenges for maintaining a current and comprehensive understanding of the field. Many existing reviews were conducted several years ago. They may not adequately reflect recent advances in AI technologies, particularly in areas such as deep learning, natural language processing, and generative AI. The emergence of large language models and conversational AI systems has created new possibilities for personalized learning that are not well-represented in existing systematic reviews, highlighting the need for updated syntheses that incorporate these technological developments.

The theoretical foundations underlying AI-driven personalized learning research also reveal important gaps and inconsistencies. While numerous studies reference established learning theories and educational frameworks, there is often a limited explicit connection between theoretical principles and the design and evaluation of AI systems. This disconnect between theory and practice limits the ability to understand why certain AI approaches are effective and under what conditions, hindering the development of more robust and generalizable personalized learning systems.

To address this theory practice gap, emerging studies increasingly emphasize the need to explicitly align AI-driven personalized learning features with established pedagogical theories. For instance, adaptive content sequencing, learner-controlled pacing, and feedback personalization commonly implemented in AI systems are conceptually grounded in constructivist learning theory, which views learning as an active process of knowledge construction shaped by prior experience and contextual interaction ([Chen et al., 2020](#ref9); [Castro et al., 2024](#ref8)). Similarly, AI-supported dialogic tutoring systems, collaborative recommendation engines, and social feedback mechanisms reflect principles of social learning theory by facilitating observation, interaction, and knowledge co-construction within digitally mediated learning environments ([Wang et al., 2025](#ref42); [Mustafa et al., 2024](#ref27)). Explicitly mapping AI functionalities to such pedagogical foundations is essential for ensuring that technological innovation meaningfully supports learning processes rather than operating as a purely technical intervention.

The measurement and evaluation of personalized learning outcomes present additional challenges that have been inadequately addressed in existing research. Many studies rely on traditional academic achievement measures that may not fully capture the benefits of personalized learning, such as improved motivation, self-regulation, metacognitive awareness, and long-term retention. Furthermore, the complexity of customized learning systems makes it difficult to isolate the effects of specific AI components or features, limiting the understanding of which aspects of personalization are most important for different learners and learning contexts.

Given these identified gaps and limitations in the current research landscape, there is a compelling need for a comprehensive systematic literature review that synthesizes high-quality evidence on AI-driven personalized learning from peer-reviewed sources indexed in major academic databases. Such a review would provide valuable insights into the current state of knowledge, identify robust evidence for the effectiveness of different AI approaches, examine moderating factors that influence outcomes, and highlight priority areas for future research and development.

The proposed systematic literature review aims to address these gaps by focusing specifically on high-quality peer-reviewed articles published in journals indexed in Scopus that meet rigorous peer-review standards. This approach will ensure that the review synthesizes the most reliable and methodologically sound evidence available, providing a solid foundation for evidence-based recommendations regarding the implementation and further development of AI-driven personalized learning systems.

The systematic approach will enable comprehensive identification and synthesis of relevant studies while maintaining transparency and reproducibility throughout the review process. By implementing rigorous inclusion and exclusion criteria, quality assessment procedures, and systematic data extraction protocols, the review will provide a more robust and reliable synthesis of evidence than has been available in previous reviews. The focus on both quantitative outcomes and qualitative implementation factors will provide a comprehensive understanding of not only whether AI-driven personalized learning is effective, but also how and under what conditions it works best.

This systematic literature review will contribute to the field by providing educational researchers, practitioners, policymakers, and technology developers with evidence-based guidance for implementing AI-driven personalized learning systems effectively and ethically. The review findings will inform future research priorities, guide the development of more effective AI technologies for education, and support evidence-based decision-making regarding the adoption and implementation of personalized learning systems in diverse educational contexts. Furthermore, the systematic identification of research gaps and methodological limitations will help establish priorities for future investigations and contribute to the continued advancement of the field.

The urgency of this systematic review is underscored by the rapid adoption of AI technologies in educational settings worldwide, often without sufficient empirical evidence to guide implementation decisions. Educational institutions are increasingly investing in AI-driven, personalized learning platforms; however, the lack of comprehensive, high-quality evidence synthesis limits their ability to make informed choices about which technologies to adopt and how to implement them effectively. This review will address this critical need by providing a rigorous, evidence-based foundation for decision-making in the implementation of AI-driven personalized learning.

2 Methodology
-------------

The methodology is divided into two main parts: the review protocol and the meta-literature review. The systematic approach incorporates four elements: the review protocol (PRISMA), the development of research questions using the PICo framework, a comprehensive, inclusive search strategy and the analysis of findings.

### 2.1 The review protocol—PRISMA

The Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) framework was adopted for conducting the systematic review of literature ([Ikram and Kenayathulla, 2023](#ref18); [Rethlefsen et al., 2021](#ref32); [Saleem et al., 2024](#ref34)). PRISMA provides structured guidelines that support authors in determining inclusion and exclusion criteria while also enhancing the transparency and quality assessment of reviews. Although originally developed for systematic reviews of randomized controlled trials, it has been widely recognized as applicable to other types of systematic reviews ([Moher et al., 2009](#ref24)). According to [Sierra-Correa and Cantera Kintz (2015)](#ref37), PRISMA offers distinct advantages: it refines research focus, sets clear boundaries for study selection, and facilitates efficient analysis of large datasets. More recently, [Page et al. (2021)](#ref28) emphasized that PRISMA enables a comprehensive and systematic exploration of terms and concepts, including those related to policies on artificial intelligence in higher education. Given these strengths, the PRISMA methodology is particularly well-suited for investigating the intersection of Artificial Intelligence and Personalized Learning in this study.

In line with PRISMA 2020 guidance, the framework was applied to guide study identification, screening, eligibility assessment, and transparent reporting of the search process, while quality appraisal was conducted separately using the Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool (MMAT). This approach is consistent with prior systematic reviews that employ PRISMA for reporting structure and MMAT for methodological rigor in heterogeneous evidence bases ([Moher et al., 2009](#ref24); [Page et al., 2021](#ref28); [Mustafa et al., 2024](#ref27)).

### 2.2 Formulation of research question

This study employed the PICo framework to construct its research question, as it is a widely recognized approach for generating systematic review queries ([Dhir and Gupta, 2021](#ref12)). The framework incorporates three main components: Population, Interest, and Context. Accordingly, this research identified existing literature as the Population, personalized learning as the Interest, and artificial intelligence as the Context, thereby framing the research question effectively.

### 2.3 Systematic search strategies

This study employed a three-step systematic search comprising identification, screening, and evaluation of eligibility.

#### 2.3.1 Identification

For this research, a substantial number of potentially relevant documents were identified through a systematic process carried out in three stages. The first stage involved identifying keywords, followed by the inclusion of analogous terms and synonyms drawn from various sources and existing literature. These keywords were then used to construct a comprehensive search query within the Scopus database, recognized for its high-quality standards and suitability for systematic literature reviews ([Mehmood et al., 2022a](#ref21),[b](#ref22); [Wider et al., 2025](#ref44)). Scopus was selected as the sole database due to its broad multidisciplinary coverage and strong representation of peer-reviewed journals in education and computer science, making it suitable for trend-focused systematic reviews ([Rethlefsen et al., 2021](#ref32)). The final search string (see [Table 1](#tab1)) targeted document titles containing the most relevant keywords. In this initial step, the Scopus search yielded a total of 219 documents.

4 Discussion on research topics in artificial intelligence and personalized learning
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Across the reviewed studies, notable methodological variation was observed in research design, outcome measures, and implementation contexts, contributing to differences in reported effectiveness of AI-driven personalized learning. While several quantitative and mixed-methods studies reported positive learning gains, others highlighted contextual constraints, ethical risks, and inconsistent outcomes, underscoring the need to interpret findings in light of methodological quality and study context ([Mustafa et al., 2024](#ref27); [Zhang et al., 2024](#ref45); [Castro et al., 2024](#ref8)) as reflected in the descriptive patterns summarized in [Figures 3](#fig3)–[6](#fig6).

### 4.1 Personalized learning pathways and student engagement

Across the literature, one of the most prominent themes is the capacity of AI to construct personalized learning experiences tailored to students’ needs. Research highlights how AI facilitates real-time feedback, adjusts content flexibly, and encourages both creativity and motivation ([Abrar et al., 2025](#ref1); [Baba et al., 2024](#ref6); [Baltezarević and Baltezarević, 2024](#ref7); [Rajesh et al., 2024](#ref31); [Tapalova and Zhiyenbayeva, 2022](#ref41)). Reported outcomes include increased vocabulary retention, more accurate syntax, a deeper understanding of complex concepts, and heightened student engagement ([Aravind et al., 2025](#ref5)). Furthermore, AI-driven gamification and continuous evaluation enhance learner-focused approaches, resulting in better academic outcomes than traditional instruction. Overall, the findings imply that AI-enabled personalization benefits both academic performance and students’ motivation to take an active role in their learning journey.

### 4.2 Predictive analytics, assessment, and early intervention

A second major theme concerns the role of AI in predictive analytics and customized assessment. Evidence suggests that AI systems are capable of projecting students’ academic progress, grouping them according to risk levels, and enabling early intervention strategies ([Huang et al., 2025](#ref16); [Shoaib et al., 2024](#ref36)). Innovations such as AI-supported oral assessments ([Dong et al., 2024](#ref13)) and simulation-based instruction ([Rajesh et al., 2024](#ref31)) illustrate how these tools improve both assessment validity and inclusivity. Predictive algorithms and recommendation models not only enhance accuracy and engagement but also equip educators to develop targeted strategies that reduce dropout rates and support struggling learners. Collectively, these findings signal a movement toward data-driven and preventative approaches in education.

### 4.3 Teacher training, digital competence, and pedagogical innovation

Another theme concerns the need for teacher readiness and digital competence to ensure AI is integrated effectively into teaching and learning. According to [Castro et al. (2024)](#ref8), [Moslemi and Arani (2024)](#ref25), and [Wang et al. (2024)](#ref43) both pre-service and practicing teachers require adequate preparation to adopt AI for personalized education. Similarly, [Rajesh et al. (2024)](#ref31) and [Junruang and Kanjug (2025)](#ref19) argue that teachers must scaffold computational and active learning approaches to cultivate problem-solving and creativity among students. The evidence indicates that although AI tools provide substantial opportunities, their effectiveness relies heavily on pedagogical alignment. Consequently, teacher agency and digital literacy emerge as pivotal factors in realizing equitable and impactful AI integration.

These findings further indicate that effective AI integration depends on making pedagogical assumptions explicit, particularly by aligning system design features with constructivist and social learning principles that guide how learners actively engage, collaborate and regulate their learning processes ([Castro et al., 2024](#ref8); [Wang et al., 2024](#ref43)).

### 4.4 Ethical, governance, and data privacy concerns

The prominent cross-cutting theme identified is the importance of ethics and governance in AI adoption. Concerns frequently cited include data privacy, bias in algorithms, unequal access, and questions of epistemic trust ([Al-Omari et al., 2025](#ref4); [Castro et al., 2024](#ref8); [Mulaudzi and Hamilton, 2025](#ref26); [Sunmboye et al., 2025](#ref38)). Learners have voiced apprehension about inaccuracies and the misuse of AI-generated information, particularly within fields such as medicine, where errors carry serious consequences ([Shen et al., 2025](#ref35)). Scholars propose measures such as robust governance structures, transparency protocols, and regular audits to manage these risks. In addition, educators favor hybrid models that integrate AI with conventional methods instead of fully replacing them ([Agatova and Latipova, 2025](#ref2)). Overall, these studies emphasize that ethical and transparent AI practices are essential for equitable and trustworthy educational outcomes.

### 4.5 Generative AI and education 5.0 transformation

Within this review, Education 5.0 is operationalized as a learner-centered educational paradigm that integrates advanced digital technologies, including generative AI, with humanistic values, inclusivity, and societal relevance, emphasizing personalized learning, ethical governance, and human-AI collaboration rather than automation alone ([Castro et al., 2024](#ref8); [Ronksley-Pavia et al., 2025](#ref33); [Guettala et al., 2024](#ref15)). The role of generative AI is widely recognized as pivotal in advancing education toward the vision of Education 5.0. Research points to its ability to deliver adaptive instruction, personalize content creation, and cater to learners with unique needs ([Guettala et al., 2024](#ref15); [Ronksley-Pavia et al., 2025](#ref33); [Shen et al., 2025](#ref35)). In professional disciplines such as nursing and medicine, prompt engineering and AI tutoring have proven effective in strengthening both analytical and clinical skills ([Tao and Pan, 2025](#ref40)). Similarly, its integration into inclusive pedagogies supports twice-exceptional and neurodivergent learners through tailored, strengths-based approaches ([Ronksley-Pavia et al., 2025](#ref33)). These insights underline that generative AI represents more than a supportive tool; it is a catalyst transforming educational inclusivity and learning design.

### 4.6 Equity, inclusivity, and socio-cultural impact

A key theme emerging across studies is the role of AI in promoting inclusivity and cultural responsiveness. Evidence highlights its capacity to broaden access in areas such as language learning, early childhood education, and distance learning, while also recognizing the importance of tailoring AI tools to specific cultural contexts ([Aldhilan and Rafiq, 2025](#ref3); [Dong et al., 2024](#ref13); [Moslemi and Arani, 2024](#ref25)). Research further indicates that adoption patterns vary according to learners’ characteristics, including age, gender and academic field, with these factors influencing perceptions and concerns ([Sunmboye et al., 2025](#ref38); [Tapalova and Zhiyenbayeva, 2022](#ref41)). Furthermore, AI promotes multilingual and intercultural competencies, which help break down cultural divides and foster equity ([Moslemi and Arani, 2024](#ref25)). Collectively, these findings position AI as a catalyst for equitable and globally adaptable education.

5 Future direction of artificial intelligence and personalized learning
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

### 5.1 Advancing predictive analytics and data-driven models

A recurring theme across the literature is the advancement of predictive analytics and machine learning to forecast student outcomes more accurately. [Shoaib et al. (2024)](#ref36) emphasize the importance of testing diverse classification methods, incorporating demographic variables, and developing standardized datasets to improve predictions of academic failure and dropout risks. In a similar vein, [Deng et al. (2024)](#ref11) emphasize the value of dynamic recommendation algorithms and incremental learning in adapting to students’ changing needs, while [Huang et al. (2025)](#ref16) stress the importance of validating high-performance computing (HPC) models on larger datasets. Collectively, these studies underscore the pivotal role of scalable and robust predictive systems in facilitating early intervention and delivering personalized educational support.

### 5.2 Generative AI and emerging technologies in education 5.0

The literature frequently highlights the transformative impact of generative AI and related technological innovations on reshaping education, as defined in this review through its emphasis on personalized, inclusive and ethically governed human-AI learning environments. [Guettala et al. (2024)](#ref15) and [Dong et al. (2024)](#ref13) stress the potential of integrating generative AI with adaptive platforms, IoT applications, and personalized feedback systems. [Ronksley-Pavia et al. (2025)](#ref33) extend this by introducing synthetic learner profiles to address the needs of twice-exceptional learners, while [Coban et al. (2025)](#ref10) advocate for further exploration of LLM use in AR/VR contexts to enhance learner focus and outcomes. Together, these contributions emphasize the necessity of advancing inquiry into generative and immersive technologies as essential enablers of Education 5.0, supporting inclusivity and adaptability in learning environments.

### 5.3 Ethics, governance, and trust in AI integration

The theme of ethics and governance has become central in research on AI in education. [Castro et al. (2024)](#ref8) and [Al-Omari et al. (2025)](#ref4) emphasize the importance of addressing transparency, algorithmic bias, and socioeconomic inequality in the adoption of AI. Long-term investigations into academic integrity and the role of AI-detection tools are recommended by [Mulaudzi and Hamilton (2025)](#ref26). Meanwhile, [Tanchuk and Taylor (2025)](#ref39) emphasize the importance of epistemic trust and accountability in AI tutors. Additional concerns raised by [Sunmboye et al. (2025)](#ref38) and [Shen et al. (2025)](#ref35) include safeguarding accuracy, combating misinformation, and ensuring that innovation complements humanistic skills. Collectively, these studies indicate an increasing demand for ethical and governance frameworks that ensure privacy, equity, and epistemic reliability in educational contexts.

### 5.4 Teacher training, digital literacy, and human–AI collaboration

A key research direction focuses on equipping educators with the skills and strategies necessary for effective AI integration. Studies by [Moslemi and Arani (2024)](#ref25), [Wang et al. (2024)](#ref43), and [Agatova and Latipova (2025)](#ref2) emphasize the importance of teacher training, digital competence, and hybrid instructional approaches that integrate AI with established teaching practices. [Rajesh et al. (2024)](#ref31) and [Junruang and Kanjug (2025)](#ref19) highlight the necessity of scaffolding computational thinking and active learning, ensuring that technology enhances rather than displaces pedagogy. In addition, [Aravind et al. (2025)](#ref5) suggest exploring collaboration between AI and human teachers to create well-balanced learning ecosystems. Collectively, these directions demonstrate that teacher readiness and pedagogical innovation are pivotal to leveraging AI for personalized learning.

### 5.5 Enhancing cognitive, creative, and higher-order learning outcomes

Future research is increasingly focused on exploring the influence of AI on higher-order cognitive and creative skills. [Baba et al. (2024)](#ref6) call for extending inquiry to advanced levels of Bloom’s taxonomy, while [Parkavi et al. (2024)](#ref30) and [Panwale and Vijayakumar (2025)](#ref29) recommend refining swarm intelligence and personalisation techniques to enhance interpretability, creativity, and communication. [Fletscher et al. (2025)](#ref14) and [Tao and Pan (2025)](#ref40) highlight AI’s contribution to developing analytical and problem-solving competencies, particularly within medical and STEM disciplines. [Junruang and Kanjug (2025)](#ref19) further introduce the importance of fostering computational and creative thinking among younger learners. Collectively, these studies signal a shift in emphasis from basic personalization toward cultivating critical, creative, and complex intellectual growth through AI.

### 5.6 Inclusivity, equity, and socio-cultural contexts

The final theme focuses on the role of AI in promoting inclusivity and addressing equity across diverse educational settings. Research by [Lyu and Salam (2025)](#ref20) and [Wang et al. (2024)](#ref43) emphasizes the need for cross-cultural validation and the inclusion of heterogeneous learner groups, while [Aldhilan and Rafiq (2025)](#ref3) stress the importance of early childhood and socio-emotional dimensions. [Moslemi and Arani (2024)](#ref25) highlight the broader societal benefits of multilingual education and equitable access to AI resources, whereas [Junruang and Kanjug (2025)](#ref19) caution that overlooking socio-cultural differences in early education may compromise equity goals. Taken together, these studies underscore that personalization in AI must be carefully adapted to demographic and cultural contexts to promote inclusivity and avoid deepening educational inequalities.

6 Conclusion and implications
-----------------------------

### 6.1 Conclusion

This review explores emerging patterns, methods and focal points in current AI and personalized learning research. The findings reflect a dynamic and interdisciplinary field that is still evolving but already shows promising directions. The dominance of qualitative studies suggests that researchers are prioritizing the exploration of lived experiences and nuanced insights into how AI is shaping education. With China and India emerging as leading contributors, the geographical distribution suggests that this field is reaching diverse educational settings worldwide.

The subject area distribution further underscores the cross-sectoral interest in this topic, with Social Sciences, Computer Science and Engineering all contributing significantly. Key themes, such as personalized learning pathways, predictive analytics, teacher readiness, ethical governance and inclusive AI-driven education, collectively indicate that AI is not just a technological add-on but a transformative agent in reimagining how learning can be delivered and experienced. Overall, this body of research confirms that AI holds great potential in transforming education, but it also calls for thoughtful integration that respects pedagogical principles, learner diversity and social equity.

While this review applied systematic protocols and relied on Scopus-indexed peer-reviewed studies, it excluded gray literature and non-English publications. In addition, although a formal quality appraisal using the Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool (MMAT) was conducted to ensure methodological rigor, quality ratings of included articles were not quantitatively weighted, as the review aimed to provide a thematic and descriptive synthesis rather than a statistical meta-analysis. Although PRISMA reporting standards were followed, the review adopts a descriptive synthesis, and variations in study design, methodological quality and implementation context may partly explain divergent or conflicting findings across the included literature ([Page et al., 2021](#ref28); [Mustafa et al., 2024](#ref27)). These constraints may limit the generalizability of findings.

### 6.2 Implications

The review findings offer several important implications for research, practice and policy. First, the predominance of qualitative studies suggests that future research should aim for methodological diversity, combining rich narrative insights with scalable, data-driven assessments. Second, the global distribution of studies indicates opportunities for collaborative research across regions, particularly in adapting AI tools to diverse educational settings. For educators and institutions, the evidence suggests a need for professional development that encompasses not only technical skills but also pedagogical integration and digital literacy. Additionally, policymakers must focus on establishing frameworks for the ethical use of AI in education to address concerns such as data privacy, algorithmic bias, and access disparities. Lastly, the emergence of generative AI and its role in Education 5.0 signals a shift in how to design learning environments toward systems that are not only intelligent and adaptable but also inclusive, culturally sensitive and learner focused.
