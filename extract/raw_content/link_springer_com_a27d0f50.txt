Abstract
--------

Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) holds promise as a tool to assist teachers in addressing specific instructional design and implementation tasks. This study explores how teachers in a master’s-level elementary science methods course used Large Language Model AI, as one type of GenAI, to design lessons fostering students’ environmental science agency (ESA). The course introduced ESA as one of pedagogical constructs that emphasize the importance of science learning relevant and consequential to students’ lives, and encouraged teachers to use GenAI for their ESA-focused lesson design projects. Data sources included surveys, lesson design documents, GenAI chat logs, and field notes. Findings, derived through constant comparative analysis, highlight GenAI’s utility in three areas: (1) deepening teachers’ conceptual understanding; (2) developing detailed content of lesson components; and (3) integrating ESA into science instruction. Teachers also reported the challenges they encountered and addressed mainly in two ways: (1) ensuring the quality of GenAI-generated responses and (2) aligning these responses with their specific lesson visions. These findings were discussed in terms of teacher perspectives on ESA integration and their effort to take authorship and responsibility in their project process and outcomes. The study concludes by calling for follow-up teacher education practice and research focused on creating science learning opportunities, including those with proper and ethical use of GenAI, that go beyond knowledge enhancement to fostering civic participation aimed at justice-oriented and transformative changes, as envisioned by the concept and practices of ESA.

Introduction
------------

The advent of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) brought about a paradigmatic shift in the production and communication of human intellectual endeavors. GenAI, as a subset of AI systems, uses machine learning algorithms for creating new content through the interactive and collaborative process between users and AI (Cain, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR5); Cooper, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR7); Moundridou et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR13)). This study focuses on the utility of one type of GenAI tool: Large Language Models (LLMs) such as OpenAI’s GPT and Google’s Gemini. In this study, the term GenAI broadly refers to LLMs employed by this study’s teachers.

Along with its powerful utility across various domains, GenAI demonstrates significant potential in educational sectors (Tang, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR18)). In science education specifically, studies have explored AI’s contribution to science teaching, learning, and assessment (Jantaraweragul et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR10); Weckel et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR21); Zhai et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR23)). As GenAI continues to evolve, its integration into science education presents new opportunities for reimagining instructional practices and innovative learning experiences (Almasri, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR3)).

This study, in particular, focuses on GenAI’s utility in support of teachers’ one of the most critical tasks: instructional design. Over the years of teaching elementary and secondary science methods courses, I have focused on helping teachers learn, design, and practice comprehensive components of science instruction to support students’ academic engagement while addressing critical and consequential matters such as environmental and climate issues. Central to this effort, the course introduces the concept of environmental science agency (Ballard et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR4)) and features ESA-focused instructional design as its signature project. Teachers who took the course demonstrated strong commitment to these projects, though some teachers reported challenges in integrating ESA. Elementary teachers, in particular, often expressed lower confidence compared to secondary teachers in articulating some scientific concepts and identifying relevant environmental and climate issues for lesson integration.

To address these challenges, I encouraged teachers in my spring 2024 elementary science methods course to use LLMs, as one type of GenAI, for their ESA-focused science lesson design. This study aims to analyze how teachers utilized GenAI in this context and what challenges they encountered and addressed. Two research questions guide this study:

> (1) How did teachers use GenAI for their ESA-focused science lesson design projects?
> 
> (2) Subsequently, what challenges did teachers address in using GenAI for the lesson design process?

Answering these questions drawing on a constant comparative analysis of data generated from a science methods course, this study uniquely contributes to the field by specifically examining the application of GenAI tools to support the creation of environmentally relevant and consequential learning experiences, a critical intersection not extensively explored in prior research on GenAI in instructional design.

Literature Review
-----------------

This section situates the study’s research questions by first reviewing the literature on GenAI as a tool for supporting teachers instructional learning, design, and practices. Followingly, the concept of ESA is articulated as the instructional theme as well as the conceptual reference for data analysis.

### Using GenAI for Instructional Practices

A growing body of research highlights GenAI’s role in support of teacher instructional practices. Recent studies report that teachers use AI as a technological assistant for preparing student activities, conducting assessments, and predicting academic performance (Almasri, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR3); Zhai et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR23)). For teacher assessment tasks, machine learning has been applied to evaluate students’ argumentation skills, offering actionable suggestions for educators (Zhai et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR23)). In addition, AI itself is recognized as a discipline to be taught through interdisciplinary curriculum (Lin & Brummelen, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR12)) and through the discussion comparing AI and science as epistemic systems (Kim, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR24); Kim & Rachmatullah, [2025](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR26)).

Lesson design encompasses teacher practices listed above, ranging from predicting student learning experiences, developing assessment plans, and creating instructional materials. Prior research highlighting GenAI’s support in teacher practices, combined with its generative language processing capabilities, demonstrates its potential to make complex, multidimensional tasks like lesson design be more effective and efficient. Moundridou et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR13)) explored GenAI tools useful for inquiry-based learning lesson plans and identified multiple tasks that can be facilitated such as capturing students’ attention, addressing gaps in understanding, introducing topics in detail, checking student comprehension of new content, designing student activities, and facilitating communication and reflection.

The findings of Moundridou et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR13)) and other literature discussed above provides the varied utility of GenAI in teacher practices, and thus I refer to them concurrently during the data analysis of this study. At the same time, this study extends the prior work by paying attention to actual teacher approaches, prompting, and perspectives. Teachers in this study were tasked with designing lessons that align with state standards and school curricula while ensuring relevant and meaningful learning experiences for students. And, I seek to document the in-the-middle design process evidenced by teachers’ iterative interactions with GenAI and the resulting project of lesson design.

To facilitate teachers’ effective iteration in creative tasks like lesson design, it is essential to establish and share norms for the effective and responsible use of GenAI. The literature particularly emphasizes the importance of prompt engineering—crafting instructional texts for GenAI—for generating high-quality, relevant, and accurate outputs (Cain, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR5)). By shaping their interactions with specific requests and anticipated outcomes, this process shifts teachers from passive recipients of GenAI responses to active authors of their work. Authorship in such creative processes requires critical thinking skills and information literacy, both of which are fundamental for the effective and responsible use of GenAI tools (Cain, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR5)).

Ethical and practical concerns surrounding GenAI also warrant attention and should be integral to establishing shared norms for its responsible use within educational communities (Tang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR19)). In intellectual and creative tasks where authorship and originality hold critical value, it is essential to ensure rigorous epistemic practices such as academic integrity and the critical evaluation of AI-generated responses (Funer, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR8)). This includes fostering discussions on how to critically assess the quality, reliability, and appropriateness of AI-generated content to ensure it aligns with the intended standards of scholarship and creativity (Kim, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR24); Sadler et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR15)). Moreover, ethical considerations should extend to acknowledging the systemic biases inherent in the training data used to develop GenAI models (Zhai, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR22)), necessitating careful scrutiny and mitigation strategies to promote fairness and inclusivity (Tang & Cooper, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR18)). Additionally, users should be aware of the risk of carelessly sharing privacy data during their interactions, and pay additional attention to the ethical handling of content shared in the user interface platform.

Incorporating these considerations into the design and use of GenAI not only safeguards ethical standards but also fosters a culture of critical engagement. By empowering teachers to navigate the complexities of GenAI with awareness and responsibility, teachers and their communities can leverage its transformative potential while minimizing its risks. To support teachers in effectively and ethically using GenAI, scaffolded learning opportunities are needed to facilitate discussions and solutions within their professional learning communities. This study recognizes these issues of ethics, academic integrity, and professional responsibility, and integrates them into a workshop designed as part of the method course, detailed in the Methods section.

### Environmental Science Agency as a Construct for Informing Lesson Design

Teachers in this study were encouraged to use GenAI for designing science lessons in ways to foster students’ ESA. This study assumes that how teachers understand and interpret ESA into their lesson context will inform and be informed by their interactions with GenAI. Environmental Science Agency (ESA) is a form of critical agency that uses science to understand environmental concepts and address issues crucial to students and their communities (Harris & Ballard, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR9)). ESA involves three dimensions: knowledge and inquiry practices, positioning students as experts, and participatory actions for systemic change (Ballard et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR4)).

Teachers can foster ESA by focusing on the knowledge and inquiry practices dimension, which emphasizes developing students’ understanding of environmental science content and engaging them in investigations. Ballard et al. ([2017](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR4)) documented students contributing to rigorous environmental data collection and analysis processes as the basis for connecting science concepts to environmental issues.

The second dimension, positioning students as experts, involves creating opportunities for students to take ownership of their learning by identifying and investigating community-relevant issues and sharing their findings. Schenkel and Calabrese Barton ([2019](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR16)) described students designing light-up circuits to address school sustainability concerns. Positioning students as problem-solvers and knowledge creators fosters agency and responsibility, integrating traditional expertise with diverse cultural and experiential knowledge.

The third dimension, participatory actions for systemic change, focuses on supporting students as they apply their environmental science knowledge to bringing communal and social changes toward just ends. For example, Kim ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR25)) illustrates how a teacher and their students explored the environmental impact of dietary habits, which led to the school-wide vegetarian lunch project that raised awareness of climate-friendly food choices by soliciting the participation of their school members from the principal, culinary experts, and teachers to students and parents. This project empowered students to connect knowledge with environmentally conscious actions to bring community changes.

Despite the potential of these three dimensional ESA in science learning contexts, little research exists on how teachers intentionally design lessons to promote it. Harris and Ballard ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR9)) highlight the dual role of classrooms in enabling or constraining ESA, depending on how instructional practices align with student agency. Teachers, recognizing the dual role, should be prepared to anticipate and amplify unexpected expressions of agency while providing the resources and flexibility to support diverse inquiries. As one effort toward that direction, I encourage teachers in methods course contexts to integrate ESA from their lesson design phase so as to ensure purposeful and thoughtful scaffolding for ESA. Accordingly, I draw on the literature discussed thus far to analyze how teachers integrated ESA and how GenAI supported this process.

Method
------

This study was conducted in an elementary science methods course during Spring 2024, offered by a teacher education program at a university in a West Coast state of the United States. The course aimed to engage teachers in designing and implementing science instruction for elementary and middle school classrooms. Beyond covering the general science instruction components aligned with the State’s teacher education expectations, I emphasized the importance of why science learning matters to students and their communities as the central criterion for developing instructional storylines. To support this focus, I introduced the concept of ESA and its integration into lesson design.

### Participants

A total of 23 teachers enrolled in the course. At the conclusion of the course, after completing all grading and course evaluation, I inquired about their interest in offering their consent to collect and analyze their course products for this study, and all of them agreed. Sixteen teachers were student or intern teachers with 0.5 to 1.5 years of teaching, while seven were teachers with 2 to 6 years of independent teaching. At the time of the course, grade levels they taught spanned from kindergarten to 6th grade. Regarding GenAI experiences, most teachers identified themselves as beginners or intermediate users with modest usage frequency. Insights from the pre-course survey, including teachers’ positivity to using GenAI for coursework, were reflected to design the in-class workshop. Refer to Supplementary Materials for additional teacher information details.

### In-class Workshop: GenAI Use for the Course and Lesson Design

Building on the first three prior classes focused on the overall science instruction components aligned with the state’s credentialing requirements, the fourth session shifted gears to teacher practices and reflections, emphasizing equitable and justice-oriented instruction. It began with introducing teachers to the course signature project, ESA-focused lesson design, and the workshop followed (1.5h out of the total 3h per session).

### Data Source

This study utilized four data sources: course surveys, ESA-focused lesson design documents, GenAI chat logs, field notes. Each source contributed to examining how teachers used GenAI for ESA-focused lesson design (RQ 1) and the challenges they encountered and addressed (RQ 2).

#### Surveys

Two surveys were administered: a course-opening survey and a course-ending survey. The course-opening survey included general questions about the course and teachers’ prior experiences with GenAI (See Supplementary Material). These responses also helped me design the GenAI workshop details as outlined in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#Fig1) above.

Course-ending survey provided data directly tied to research questions. For RQ 1, one multiple-choice question asked teachers to select all areas where GenAI supported their lesson design projects beyond proofreading. I provided five options drawing on our prior workshop discussion: (1) understanding instructional concepts comprising the lesson design, (2) understanding scientific concepts covered by the lessons, (3) developing lesson design content, (4) ESA integration beyond addressing environment-related science concepts, and (5) other uses. For RQ 2, one open-ended question asked teachers to identify challenges they faced and addressed while using GenAI for lesson design.

#### ESA-Focused Instructional Design Document

The course signature project, an ESA-focused lesson design, represents teachers’ culminating learning outcome and showcases how they applied course concepts to their instructional practices. Each teacher independently developed a lesson segment consisting of four sections: (1) a description of students’ academic and cultural assets and learning needs; (2) an overview of the lesson segment, including ESA integration plans; (3) lesson details featuring teaching strategies, student activities, and assessment prompts and how ESA were actually integrated in these lesson components; and (4) a self-reflection essay of their lesson design and GenAI usage. I focused primarily on teachers’ post-project reflection essays to see how they used GenAI to support specific components of their ESA-focused lesson design (RQ1) and what questions, critiques, and suggestions they raised drawing on their project experiences (RQ2). I also reviewed the detailed lesson plans themselves as the actual first-hand evidence showing teachers’ integration of ESA into their lessons.

#### GenAI Chat Logs

**Fig. 3**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0/figures/3)

Example of GenAI chat log

As the focus of this study is teachers’ usage pattern, not the exploration of GenAI tools’ features, I encouraged teachers to choose any type of LLM that they would feel comfortable with. While they had access to educator-tailored GenAI tools such as MagicSchool, teachers chose OpenAI’s Chat GPT 3 (*N =* 20) or Google’s Gemini (*N =* 3) for their lesson design projects. Teachers maintained a dedicated GenAI chat thread for their lesson design projects and submitted the link to the chat when they submitted their final project (Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#Fig3)). I extracted individual teachers’ prompts from the chat logs into a separate electronic spreadsheet. These logs offered primary evidence of how teachers interacted with GenAI, including the prompts they crafted, and the responses they received and incorporated into their lessons.

#### Instructor Field Note

I recorded field notes during and after each session to document salient moments of class discussions, activities, and reflections, including those related to GenAI use–in particular those during the workshop session. These notes occasionally included verbatim teacher statements that highlighted key moments of discussion. The instructor notes provided contextual data that complemented and added depth to the three primary data sources above.

### Data Analysis

I employed three phases of a constant comparative analysis (Charmaz, [2002](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR6)). This approach allowed me to answer the two research questions by exploring the meanings emerging from data while concurrently referencing the literature.

#### Phase 1. Open Coding

The first phase focused on identifying the varying ways teachers used GenAI in ESA-focused lesson design (RQ 1) and their challenges (RQ 2). During this phase, I collaborated with an independent coder, a secondary teacher with a doctoral degree unaffiliated with the course. I invited the coder due to his expertise in AI integration as a computer science teacher. After introducing the study to the coder, we conducted the initial coding together. The codes were organized in an electronic spreadsheet, which included detailed descriptions and examples, such as teacher statements.

Initial codes were derived from triangulating the course-ending survey responses with teacher reflections in their lesson design documents as well as field notes. For RQ 1, the five multiple-choice options from the survey served as entry points for coding GenAI use. Survey responses showed that many of them used GenAI for multiple purposes. In response to the question that allowed the selection of multiple options, all 23 teachers used GenAI for developing overall lesson design components, 11 teachers for developing ideas for explicit ESA integration beyond enhancing environmental concepts, 9 teachers for understanding scientific concepts covered by the lessons, or 5 teachers for understanding instructional concepts comprising their lesson designs. This broad trend was further divided into more detailed usage codes based on teachers’ open-ended responses (in survey) and reflections (in lesson design), which led to 41 initial codes for teacher GenAI usage. For RQ 2, I analyzed teachers’ responses in two open-ended questions (in survey) and reflections section (in lesson design), which led to 12 initial codes for teacher experienced challenges.

#### Phase 2: Analysis of Individual Teachers’ Lesson Design Documents and GenAI Chat Logs

The initial codes from the prior phase were triangulated to analyze each teacher’s lesson design documents and chat logs. In addition to the teacher coder (phase 1), a graduate coder joined in this phase to ensure consistency and reliability of coding. The graduate coder had a solid understanding of ESA-focused lesson design as she took the secondary science method course, independent from the one focused by this study. Together, three of us reviewed a subset of 5 randomly selected teachers’ lessons and chat logs to establish a shared understanding of the code definition. We collaboratively assigned codes (those established from phase 1) to these data. The remaining data from the other 18 teachers were evenly divided among the three coders, with each providing a second review of the data coded by the other two. Over this collaborative coding, some initial codes were refined (split or merged) as necessary. Any disagreements were resolved through discussion until consensus was reached. Inter-rater reliability among the three coders was calculated using Krippendorff’s kappa (Krippendorff, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR11)), with resulting values of 0.88 indicating a high level of consistency across all three coders. From this process, the 41 initial codes for GenAI usage were refined and reached the final 33 codes, and the 12 initial codes for challenges were refined and reached the final 8 codes.

#### Phase 3: Iterative Code Categorization

In this phase, the codes refined in phase 2 were iteratively grouped into categories and themes. To ensure coherence and relevance for this thematic grouping, I referred to the literature on GenAI use, ESA, and its three dimensions, which provided a theoretical basis for grouping codes into categories and key themes that answer research questions. Also, my field notes helped identify and confirm themes by referencing the verbatim statements I documented during course discussions and after individual conversations with teachers. I derived categories by grouping codes according to their shared purposes of GenAI usage (RQ 1) or the shared reasons of concerns (RQ 2). I further identified themes by combining categories that reflected overarching ideas. As a result, three key themes answering RQ 1, and 2 themes for RQ 2 were identified as elaborated in the Findings section below. See Supplementary Materials for the comprehensive list of themes, categories, and codes identified.

### Limitations and Clarification

This exploratory, small-scale study, primarily involving early-career teachers with beginner-to-intermediate level GenAI experience, inherently limits the generalizability of findings. The observed patterns of GenAI usage may not reflect the practices of more experienced educators or those with advanced GenAI proficiency. Furthermore, the focus on individual ESA-centered lesson design within a specific science methods course context might not align with all teacher education programs, potentially limiting transferability to different educational contexts. Despite these constraints, this initial investigation offers insights into GenAI’s potential for supporting instructional design, particularly for tasks emphasizing instructions for consequentially relevant science learning.

I declare that ChatGPT 4o was used during the writing process of the current manuscript between August 2024 and December 2024. The tool was used to edit sentences for improved phrasing, flow, and word choice (e.g., ChatGPT prompt: edit focusing on flow, grammar, and word choice: [sentence]). I take full responsibility for the manuscript in thorough review of edited texts (Perkins & Roe, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR14); Tang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR19)).

Findings
--------

This section presents the findings aligned with the respective research questions. To support and illustrate these findings, I reference teachers’ statements from data sources using the code structure TN (e.g., T1).

### RQ 1. How Did Teachers Use GenAI for their ESA-Focused Science Lesson Design Projects?

In the context of ESA-focused lesson design projects, teachers utilized GenAI primarily for three purposes: (1) deepening conceptual understanding, (2) shaping lesson components, and (3) integrating ESA into lesson components. Below, I illustrate these findings with seven teachers’ cases: the first purpose with T1 and T2, the second with T2, T3, T4, and the third with T5, T6, T7. While some teachers focused on a single purpose and iteratively refined their tasks, most actively used GenAI for multiple purposes (e.g., T2). The chat logs further revealed that when teachers engaged in follow-up dialogues with GenAI, their prompts became more evaluative and specific (e.g., T3, T6, and T7), when compared to unidirectional interactions limited to providing prompts and receiving responses without follow-up.

#### Using GenAI To Deepen Teachers’ Conceptual Understanding

9 out of 23 teachers used GenAI to enhance their own understanding of instructional and scientific concepts. Although this kind of usage does not directly address tasks for lesson design, it assists teachers in articulating concepts crucial for instructional planning and implementation. Teachers requested GenAI for the explanation of instructional concepts covered in the course such as ‘epistemic’, ‘nature of science’, and ‘crosscutting concepts.’ Regarding their ESA-focused lesson design projects, teachers sought GenAI’s help in understanding ESA as a concept and identifying integration examples (“Explain environmental science agency to a 5th grader in simple terms. And some examples of activities suitable for elementary science classroom” from T1’s GenAI chat logs). GenAI was also used to deepen teachers’ grasp of specific science concepts and phenomena integral to their lessons (“Explain extreme cold as a phenomenon of global warming with detailed mechanism,” from T2’s GenAI chat log).

#### Using GenAI To Shape Lesson Components

17 out of 23 teachers used GenAI to refine the lesson component details. For this purpose, ESA was not always the central focus of teacher-GenAI interactions. Instead, teachers focused on completing lesson components (e.g., learning goal statements, student activities, assessment questions), and creating instructional materials for lesson implementation. Some teachers explained that their lessons would automatically foster ESA as they saw their lesson topics like weather, climate, ecosystem, and plant life are inherently environmental.

When outlining lesson flows, teachers provided GenAI with lesson topics or NGSS performance expectations. For example, while T3 and T4 both designed their 2nd-grade lessons on the topic of recycling, their initial prompts to GenAI differed:

> T3: Can you help me design three 45-minute science lessons for 2nd graders on recycling? I’m looking for ideas on learning goals, engaging student activities, and ways to assess their understanding.
> 
> T4: Suggestion for a 2nd-grade science unit that aligns with the NGSS performance expectation of communicating solutions to reduce human impact on the environment?

Some teachers, like T3, began their chats by briefly presenting the core concepts or phenomena of their lessons. Others, like T4, used NGSS performance expectations as a broader reference point to guide GenAI’s response. GenAI responded to these prompts accordingly, and produced the overall lesson structure and content.

However, no teachers automatically used the content as their final version for lesson design. Instead, they critically evaluated and refined it further. As such, teachers also actively used GenAI to create specific lesson content. For example, after outlining the recycling lesson as mentioned earlier, T3 continued by asking a series of questions to refine teacher strategies and assessment methods:

> T3: Great! Let’s use the 5E model for the three lessons you outlined. [5E was explained in detail] Can you revise the first lesson to focus on Engage and Explore, the second on Explain, and the third on Elaborate? Make sure Evaluate is included at the end of each lesson.
> 
> T3: Your assessment questions are somewhat broad. Make them more relevant to the recycling bingo game to check their understanding of recycling rules.

While refining lesson details, T3 guided GenAI with additional instructions drawing on specific anticipated outcomes in mind. These prompts exemplify teachers’ iterative dialogue with GenAI informed by their evaluation of its response. In addition to supporting the elaboration of teacher strategies, student activities, and assessment methods as illustrated by T3, GenAI also assisted teachers in selecting anchoring phenomena and creating accommodation plans for diverse learners.

Teachers further utilized GenAI to support lesson implementation beyond lesson documentation. For example, they prompted GenAI to draft lesson opening statements, translate student handouts for emerging bilingual students, and develop safety instructions. A particularly unique use was observed in T2’s lesson on climate change. T2 assigned GenAI the role of their 5th-grade students, using it to rehearse discursive interactions as part of their lesson preparation:

> T2: You’re my 5th-grade students learning about climate change. Let’s have a teacher-student discussion. Ask me questions or share comments one at a time, and I’ll respond as I would in class. Sounds good?
> 
> T2: [After GenAI’s response] Thanks. Look at the attached graph [showing CO2 concentration and temperature]. What questions would you have when first reading it? My students understand basic graph-reading but might not know terms like ppm. I also want to address the misconception that the occurence of extreme cold weather disproves global warming.

Subsequently, T2 engaged in a conversational thread with GenAI, simulating an interaction similar to what might occur in a classroom. I highlight this interactive teacher-student role-play as a key benefit of using GenAI—not only for lesson documentation but also for practicing lesson implementation. Although this type of usage was not discussed during our in-class workshop, T2 discovered it by providing GenAI with a detailed description of the lesson topic, students’ grade levels, students’ prior learning content, and specific comments T2 intended to make during the interaction.

#### Using GenAI To Integrate ESA into Science Instruction

ESA integration served as the overarching context for using GenAI across three key purposes. For the first purpose—deepening conceptual understanding—some teachers prompted GenAI to explain ESA as a concept and instructional approach. For the second purpose—developing lesson components—many teachers chose topics inherently linked to environmental themes.

In contrast to the prior two, the third purpose of using GenAI features intentional exploration of ESA as a core component to shape their science lesson content, activities, and learning outcomes. 11 out of 23 teachers illustrate this approach. They used GenAI to adjust lesson components to foster ESA, create ESA-focused instructional prompts and materials, and investigate environmental issues suitable for their lessons.

3 out of 11 teachers introduced the concept of ESA or environmental issues (those beyond concepts) early in their chats to help shape lesson components. For example, T5 began their chat by requesting to design lessons focused on ESA:

> T5: You are my fellow designer for 3rd-grade science lessons on the topic of ‘force and magnet.’
> 
> - I think 3 to 5 lessons… each will last 40 min.
> 
> - I selected one NGSS performance expectation for the lessons: 3-PS2-3. [full text omitted]
> 
> - I want to integrate Environmental Science Agency into the lessons. What do you think ESA means? How can I make the lesson address at least some of it?

In addition to outlining the overall lesson structure, T5 inquired about the concept of ESA and how their lesson could incorporate it. Some teachers went further, requesting GenAI to suggest participatory and community-engaged activities to explicitly address ESA.

7 out of 11 teachers used GenAI by adjusting already developed lesson content to address ESA. They introduced GenAI to the concept of ESA and intentionally asked it to revise their lessons to foster ESA. For example, T6 independently developed 3-day 5th-grade lessons on the hydrosphere and water cycle and then attached it to the chat, prompting GenAI with a series of questions:

> T6: Read the attached lesson plan. Help me revise it to have components fostering an environmental science agency. How can I?
> 
> T6: These ideas are good but feel a bit general. Could we start with a phenomenon students experience in their daily lives? For example, what about asking, Where do you see water at home? or something similar?
> 
> T6: Our school is located in [area information], a State that has the water scarcity issue. Do you have any ideas we could add? I’m open to including another lesson focused specifically on an action project.

T6’s prompt exemplifies how teachers engaged in iterative discussions with GenAI to comprehensively revise lessons for explicitly addressing ESA. Teachers revised opening questions to incorporate anchoring phenomena, modified learning goal statements, and added new activities to their pre-established lesson flows, continuously evaluating and refining GenAI responses.

In comparison, T7 primarily used GenAI to explore specific environmental issues, which they then used as a basis for generating lesson ideas. In their post-project reflection essay, T7 explained that they wanted to incorporate GenAI as a topic in their science lessons, inspired by their prior observations of students’ interest in digital devices and AI, as well as their own curiosity about whether GenAI could positively or negatively impact carbon emissions. Examples of T7’s chats include:

> T7: Are you [ChatGPT] aware of the environmental burdens you enormously add to the world, particularly the electricity consumption wise?
> 
> T7: Can you calculate your, your AI species/families, carbon emission amount per day? Compare it with an estimated carbon emission of fossil-fueled cars a day.
> 
> T7: But, I heard that GenAI also can help slow down climate change. How do you think it will be possible?

T7’s chats demonstrate the unique intersection of using GenAI as an instructional tool and examining its environmental implications, particularly in terms of energy consumption. Building on their exploration of the GenAI energy consumption issue, T7 transitioned to the lesson design discussion:

> T7: Let’s develop 5th grade science lesson ideas building on our chat thus far. Consider NGSS standard 5-ESS3-1. What about having students guess how AI affects carbon emissions, then work in groups to discuss its pros and cons??
> 
> T7: [in response to GenAI suggestion] I like that idea, but AI’s energy concerns go beyond what individuals can address. What efforts are happening at the social or policy level? Could you suggest an activity plan to help students call for action from those responsible for AI-driven carbon emissions?

I highlight this prompt as it sought to revise student activities to address ESA by fostering student empowerment within science learning contexts. When implemented, such activities can encourage students to think critically and take meaningful action to advocate for systemic change beyond individual behavioral changes–as envisioned by ESA integration.

### RQ 2. What Challenges Did Teachers Address in Using GenAI for the Lesson Design Process?

In the section above, I have elaborated on the three primary purposes teachers utilized GenAI in the context of ESA-focused science lesson design projects. While most teachers acknowledged the benefit and effectiveness of using GenAI for their projects, teachers also shared the challenges they encountered and addressed particularly regarding the second (developing lesson components) and third purposes (ESA integration). The challenges are (1) ensuring the quality of GenAI responses and (2) aligning these responses with their specific lesson visions. These challenges drew on teachers’ reflection essays within their lesson design documents and open-ended responses to their course-ending survey.

#### Ensuring the Quality of GenAI Responses

By quality, I mean how teachers sought to meet their original expectations for lesson outputs and ensure the GenAI responses’ accuracy. One of the most frequent challenges teachers mentioned was the need for long, iterative prompting that required elaborate backward design processes. While GenAI provided a general structure for lesson design, refining its suggestions to achieve satisfactory specificity and alignment with NGSS standards often necessitated multiple rounds of tailored inputs.

In this regard, teachers noted the importance of thorough outlining before engaging with GenAI to streamline the collaboration process and achieve desired results more efficiently. T4 mentioned that if they would do this project once more they would first do a “thorough backward design in advance” to “ease prompting and ordering the prompts.” This comment highlights the need for instructional scaffolding to guide teachers in effectively interacting with GenAI for complex tasks like lesson design.

Additionally, when teachers used GenAI to explore current environmental issues and develop related instructional materials, evaluating the reliability of GenAI’s responses emerged as a challenge. For example, T7, while exploring GenAI’s contribution to carbon emissions, found it necessary to cross-check the reliability of GenAI-generated responses, such as estimated carbon emissions and comparisons with other sources. T7 ended up spending significantly more time conducting online searches, without finding sources reliable enough from the publicly available domain. Given the on-going nature of current environmental issues, other teachers also carried out additional online searches to verify content reliability, which often required considerable extra time.

#### Aligning GenAI Responses with their Specific Lesson Visions

Another frequently mentioned challenge was aligning GenAI responses with teachers’ specific visions of lessons, particularly considering their students’ learning needs, standard expectations, and the curricular schedules of their schools. While the processes of contextualizing and ensuring relevance are meaningful and represent critical practices necessary for teachers, they also pose challenges that require deliberate effort to address.

For example, when GenAI suggested T2 the student activity on the topic of weather and climate patterns, T2 saw it still broad and it needed to modify these suggestions to align with students’ cultural and environmental knowledge and experiences while meeting standard expectations and fitting within the curricular schedule. In their reflection essay, T2 noted, “It was in the end my responsibility to decide whether and to what extent to adapt this GenAI content to fit my instructional vision.” T2 further reflected that iteratively prompting GenAI felt like an additional task they could skip since teachers already have instructional visions in mind, raising questions about the fundamental utility of GenAI for lesson design tasks.

This skepticism was observed from several teachers, who used GenAI sparingly for their projects. In their reflection essays, they expressed reservations about using GenAI responses, mentioning that the suggestions were often too broad to be directly applicable to their classrooms. They also emphasized the additional steps required to contextualize these responses, which they found cumbersome.

Taken together, these challenges in ensuring content quality and relevance highlight the critical role of teachers in critiquing, accepting, revising, and contextualizing AI-generated content. Moreover, these challenges suggest that GenAI does not inherently simplify tasks. When users approach GenAI with clear expectations for outcomes, it requires a critical and evaluative stance to effectively engage with its responses. I interpret these challenges as tasks that emerged as teachers sought to take the responsibility as lesson authors, striving to ensure the reliability, validity, and relevance of their lesson content to foster meaningful student learning experiences.

Discussion
----------

Within the ESA-focused lesson design projects, teachers employed GenAI mainly for conceptual understanding, lesson component refinement, and explicit ESA integration. In the process, teachers reported challenges in ensuring the quality and relevance of GenAI-assisted content outputs. The utility of GenAI and experienced challenges not only confirm the literature on AI application in the education sector but also add nuance to existing research on AI-assisted teaching practices. On the one hand, teachers’ GenAI usage patterns documented in this study are aligned with those in prior work, such as preparing activities and assessments (Almasri, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR3); Zhai et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR23)) and designing engaging activities and facilitating reflective discussions (Moundridou et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR13)).

On the other hand, this study extends the prior findings by showcasing how teachers used GenAI not only to improve lesson structure but also to simulate teacher-student interactions as illustrated by T2. T2 practiced classroom discussions by assigning GenAI the role of a student, demonstrating how such simulations could refine discursive strategies. It also adds to the literature by documenting how GenAI can function as a useful assistant for making science lesson content more relevant and meaningful through iterative prompting and guiding, as showcased by T5, T6, and particularly T7.

T7’s critical engagement with GenAI went beyond the use of GenAI as an instructional tool to consider the inherent environmental costs associated with AI technologies themselves. This perspective directly connects broader ethical debates surrounding AI–including energy consumption, resource utilization, and carbon footprint–to the very environmental issues that science agency seeks to address (Van Wynsberghe, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR20)). By being mindful of the environmental impact of the tools they employ, T7 demonstrated critical consciousness on the ethical and environmental implications of AI. This nuanced perspective of AI as an instructional assistant and a system having environmental tolls suggests a potential to explicitly integrate discussions on the ethical and environmental costs of AI within the framework of instructions for ESA.

The unique contribution of this study is further highlighted by its examination of GenAI use through the lens of ESA. For the first and second purposes, teachers primarily leveraged GenAI to grasp the knowledge and inquiry aspect of ESA by seeking their own better understanding of ESA and designing content around these concepts. These trends speak to some teachers’ perspective on ESA integration that if they cover environmentally relevant concepts, they are automatically supportive of ESA without explicitly planning for student agency or action. In contrast, the third purpose, intentionally exploring and revising lesson plans for ESA integration, directly targeted the ESA’s aspect of action. These teachers proactively sought to incorporate all three ESA aspects, emphasizing student agency in investigating environmental issues and including actionable components. These trends highlight a critical area for teacher education and professional development: explicitly guiding teachers on how to move beyond the assumption of inherent environmental relevance of a topic to intentionally design learning experiences that cultivate all three aspects of ESA, aimed at student empowerment as agents of change through informed action.

Future investigations in this line should also account for the challenges reported by this study’s teachers regarding GenAI use in real classroom settings. Teachers took significant time for the additional steps that required iterative refinement–as T2 put, the steps they might not need to undergo if they did not use GenAI. This finding, highlighting the time-consuming and cognitively demanding nature of ensuring quality and relevance, directly contradicts the widespread perception of GenAI as a tool for simplifying and accelerating tasks. Similarly, the long-term integration of GenAI into already demanding teaching workflows may face significant resistance. Therefore, on the one hand, this might suggest the need for future research and technology addressing these usability concerns to facilitate more seamless and efficient integration into teachers’ real-world classroom practices.

On the other hand, these reported challenges can be understood as the positive evidence of teachers’ active exercise and information literacy in consuming and modifying, therefore, taking responsibility for the contents generated in collaboration with GenAI. As Cain ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#ref-CR5)) highlights, engaging critically with GenAI allows and prompts teachers to transition from passive recipients to active authors, fostering a sense of ownership and responsibility in the creative process–even though this could make them invest more time than expected. Still, the fact that teachers perceived the iterative evaluation process as challenges should be noted as the tasks future teacher education programs and research projects should take into account so as to support in lowering the burden of using GenAI for teachers’ instructional purposes. Particularly, support for teacher learning is needed given that most teachers, despite the challenges in time put to iterative design process, expressed in course-ending survey their willingness to use GenAI for the instructional brainstorming.

For example, the teacher learning sessions such as the in-class workshop conducted in this study’s course can incorporate the challenges reported by this study’s teachers. While this study’s workshop indeed has the areas of improvement based on this study’s findings, it was purposefully designed to mitigate some of the issues anticipated from teacher GenAI usage. It provided a scaffolding space for exploring ethical use, effective prompting techniques, and the evaluative norms necessary for GenAI-assisted teaching as indicated by the workshop content (Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#Fig1)), shared norm statement we co-developed (Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s11165-025-10262-0#Fig2)), and the teachers’ positive views on the workshop stated in their course-ending survey responses. Looking ahead, future research might also investigate the long-term impacts of such interventions on teaching practices (e.g., lesson design) and their impacts on student outcomes (e.g., lesson implementation), particularly in fostering ESA-focused learning experiences. By addressing both the utility and the challenges of GenAI, teachers will be able to maximize its potential to create transformative and meaningful classroom experiences.

Conclusion
----------

This study highlights that while GenAI offers significant support for ESA-focused lesson design, its use is not without challenges. Teachers’ expectations for rigor and relevance demanded time, effort, and critical engagement to tailor GenAI responses to their specific instructional needs. Moving forward, teacher educators should prioritize explicit instruction and collaborative learning opportunities focused on developing effective GenAI prompting strategies tailored for rigorous and relevant ESA-focused lesson design. Curricula could integrate frameworks that guide teachers in evaluating GenAI outputs critically and efficiently, emphasizing iterative refinement techniques that align with sound pedagogical principles. It is the role of teacher educators and researchers who partner with teachers in addressing these challenges while maximizing the effectiveness in GenAI-assisted lesson design. I also hope that this study has contributed to the broader notion of science that matters through its teacher education practices aimed at encouraging teachers to integrate ESA as one effort to expand science learning into justice-oriented civic participation for bringing transformative changes.
