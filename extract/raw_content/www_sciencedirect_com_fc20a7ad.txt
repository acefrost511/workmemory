## Abstract
This study investigates the application of predictive analytics and machine learning models to enhance student achievement in educational settings. The experiment involved a dataset of 24,005 student records collected from institutional academic records at Wollo University and the Kombolcha Institute of Technology, spanning the years 2017–2022. The data were systematically gathered from student demographic information, academic performance metrics, and contextual features such as studied credits, entrance results, and number of previous attempts. Unlike prior works, this study proposes a novel hybrid architecture that combines Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Random Forests with XGBoost as a meta-learner, achieving superior accuracy (88 %) compared to individual models such as Random Forest (85 %). Accuracy and other performance metrics (precision, recall, F1-score, and AUC-ROC) were calculated using a hold-out validation approach, with 80 % of the data used for training and 20 % for testing. This architecture effectively captures complex feature interactions and provides actionable insights for educators. Additionally, key predictive factors such as studied credits, entrance results, and regional differences were identified, offering a comprehensive understanding of student performance. The study addresses gaps in feature diversity and demonstrates the applicability of hybrid models in educational settings, paving the way for targeted interventions and improved resource allocation.

## 1. Introduction
The advancements in technology in education hold significant promise for enhancing student achievement through predictive analytics. Using historical and current data, predictive analytics can anticipate future events, offering an innovative approach to enhance education. The education sector is constantly evolving, driven by technological advancements that enhance student success with new methods. Forecasting future outcomes in education is made possible through predictive analytics, which analyzes previous and current data ([Datnow & Hubbard, 2016](#bib14)). This approach highlights trends and patterns in educational data that educators might overlook because of the data's vast quantity. Predictive analytics enhances academic results by being proactive in meeting students' requirements.
At the same time, machine learning, which is a branch of artificial intelligence, has shown great promise in analyzing and understanding intricate educational information. Machine learning algorithms can acquire knowledge from data and generate predictions or choices without needing to be explicitly instructed to carry out a particular function. Methods like decision trees, neural networks, and ensemble techniques have proven highly successful in educational settings. These models can examine different factors impacting student achievement, offering educators valuable information to customize teaching and support to meet each student's specific needs ([Silvola et al., 2021](#bib38)).
Incorporating predictive analytics and machine learning in education goes beyond enhancing student grades; it aims to establish a more tailored and efficient learning atmosphere. Educators can identify students who may struggle to offer timely assistance and ensure their academic success. Furthermore, predictive models can help assess the effectiveness of teachers, leading to more focused professional development and allocation of resources. In parallel, immersive technologies such as Virtual Reality (VR) have also demonstrated potential in reshaping higher education when grounded in educational theory, offering innovative and engaging approaches to learning environments ([Shah et al., 2024](#bib36)). This study's goal is to delve into these uses, comparing various machine learning models and investigating how hybrid models can improve predictive accuracy.
Machine learning (ML) and educational analytics have recently become very popular and have increasingly used for educational data analysis and application. At this point, machine learning models, especially those used for predictive analytics, have greatly advanced capabilities of profiling students’ learning behaviors, predicting academic performance, and personalizing learning. Notably, techniques like deep learning and reinforcement learning have helped improve student outcome prediction accuracy by being able to extract complex patterns from a large dataset.
In the field of educational analytics, progress in data-driven decision-making has allowed institutions to implement more efficient interventions for at-risk students and tailor curriculum design. Educational systems can now generate insights from vast amounts of student data including demographics, past performance and interaction with learning materials, that were impossible to detect otherwise.
### 1.1. Motivation
The motivation for this research stems from the pressing need to address the diverse challenges faced by modern educational systems. The reason for conducting this research is the pressing necessity to address the diverse difficulties encountered by contemporary educational systems. Modern classrooms have a growing variety of student backgrounds, learning styles, and abilities which render traditional teaching techniques inadequate. Frequently, students who are considered at risk struggle to keep up in school because they do not receive personalized attention and timely intervention. Predictive analytics and machine learning provide an innovative answer by allowing educators to identify at-risk students in advance and personalize interventions to cater to their specific requirements. This proactive strategy enhances student retention and performance while fostering a more inclusive and supportive learning atmosphere.
Moreover, conventional ways of evaluating teacher performance are frequently subjective and restricted, not fully grasping the intricate aspects of teaching. Schools can use predictive analytics to improve teaching methods by implementing data-driven strategies. This change may result in more focused professional growth opportunities, guaranteeing that educators get the assistance and materials required to succeed. This research aims to connect data science with education to offer useful insights that can enhance student results and teaching effectiveness.
This study is also motivated by the overall influence of enhanced educational results. Higher academic performance results in enhanced job opportunities, increased long-term income, and better overall social welfare. This study seeks to enhance knowledge of how technology can help solve educational issues by using advanced machine learning models. The end objective is to establish a structure for teachers to apply predictive analytics and machine learning in their specific situations, making these powerful tools more accessible and promoting a culture of ongoing enhancement in education.
### 1.2. Problem statement
Despite the potential for transformation in education, the effective adoption and implementation of predictive analytics and machine learning face several significant challenges. Conventional approaches to assessing teacher performance and student success are frequently responsive, based on personal opinions, and do not offer the level of accuracy required to cater to the varied requirements of contemporary classrooms. Handling diverse educational information like academic progress, socio-economic status, attendance, and behavior patterns is not efficiently achieved through conventional approaches.
Multiple machine learning models such as linear regression, support vector machines (SVM), XGBoost, decision trees, and convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have been created. Nonetheless, it is necessary to conduct a methodical evaluation of their ability to make predictions in educational settings. Furthermore, the potential of hybrid modeling, stacking, and ensemble learning to improve predictive performance has not been fully explored. Merging various models can harness their unique strengths and provide more reliable and precise predictions in comparison to singular models. However, the efficiency of combining hybrid and ensemble methods to forecast educational results has not been fully examined.

\* •

## 2. Literature review
### 2.1. Predictive analytics in education
Predictive analytics in education has evolved significantly over the years. Historically, educational institutions relied on basic statistical methods to analyze student data ([Ali, 2024](#bib5)). Early efforts were primarily focused on descriptive statistics, which provided a snapshot of student performance without delving into the underlying causes or future predictions. However, with the advent of more sophisticated data analysis techniques and the proliferation of educational data, predictive analytics has become a critical tool for educators and policymakers ([Howard, 2017](#bib21)).
In contemporary settings, predictive analytics leverages advanced algorithms and machine learning models to analyze vast amounts of data and forecast future outcomes ([Huang et al., 2017](#bib22)). This shift has enabled more proactive and personalized approaches to education. For example, predictive analytics can identify at-risk students before they fail, allowing for timely interventions that can significantly improve their chances of success. Additionally, these techniques can optimize resource allocation, ensuring that support is directed where it is most important ([Furquim et al., 2019](#bib16)). Recent systematic reviews ([Guizani et al., 2025](#bib17); [Shahzad et al., 2025](#bib37)) also highlight how large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 are transforming predictive education analytics, not only through automation and personalization but also by addressing key concerns such as data privacy and ethical integration in higher education settings.
Many important studies over the past years have shown how effective predictive analytics for education are. For instance, by using logistic regression models in the prediction of student dropout rates in higher education ([Lam et al., 2024](#bib25)), were able to find that early alerting of students who are at risk of dropping out significantly lowers the number of cases of dropout by giving the said student support.
Another notable contribution is the development of the student-Per formulator, a hybrid deep neural network model that utilizes BiLSTM with an attention mechanism to predict student academic performance. This approach has demonstrated superior accuracy compared to traditional methods by leveraging the ability of deep learning to automatically extract high-level features from educational data ([Yousafzai et al., 2021](#bib39)).
The other one was conducted by ([Hlosta et al., 2022](#bib20)) to predict performance in online courses through the use of decision trees and neural networks, which yielded accurate results compared to those coming from the traditional methods. The study of ([Bond et al., 2024](#bib11)) reviewed the effects that can be derived from predictive analytics on personalized learning. They pointed out that machine learning models can be used to predict student learning paths and recommend personalized content for improving study outcomes. Moreover, recent contributions ([Shahzad et al., 2025](#bib37)) propose structured theoretical frameworks for embedding AI-based predictions into educational decision-making, thus elevating the strategic role of machine learning beyond technical forecasting into pedagogical planning. Taken together, these studies underline the transformative potential of predictive analytics in education.

One common and straightforward method for predictive modeling is linear regression. The methodology involves the application of a linear equation to construct a model that depicts the correlation between the independent and dependent variables (Ahmed, 2024). Previous studies in education have used variables including socioeconomic status, study habits, and attendance to forecast grades for pupils (Orji & Vassileva, 2022). A very effective classification method called Support Vector Machines (SVM) finds the best hyperplane to divide data into discrete categories. Teachers have used it to group pupils into various performance levels according to test scores as well as additional behavioral and academic traits (Dervenis et al., 2022). ### 2.3. Applications of Educational Data Mining (EDM)
Educational Data Mining (EDM) employs advanced data mining techniques to extract relevant insights from educational data, aiming to improve educational outcomes. According to Baker et al. (2016), EDM encompasses methods such as association rule mining, grouping, classification, and regression, which enable educators to derive meaningful patterns from large datasets.
Clustering techniques, for instance, group students with similar characteristics, aiding in the design of targeted interventions. By identifying groups of students with similar learning styles, clustering supports the development of personalized learning plans tailored to individual needs ([He et al., 2016](#bib19)).
Classification techniques, including decision trees and Support Vector Machines (SVM), are employed to classify students into predefined categories, such as high-risk or low-risk, based on their academic and behavioral data. This facilitates the early identification of at-risk students and the implementation of preventive measures to improve their academic outcomes ([Albreiki et al., 2021](#bib4)).
Regression techniques, such as linear regression and XGBoost, predict continuous outcomes like student grades or test scores. These predictions provide valuable insights into potential future performance, enabling educators to adjust instructional strategies proactively ([Bokonda et al., 2020](#bib10)).
Finally, EDM techniques help identify relationships between variables in educational data. For example, these methods can uncover associations between study habits and academic performance, offering educators a deeper understanding of the factors that contribute to student success ([Khasanah, 2017](#bib24)).
### 2.4. Hybrid models in machine learning
Hybrid models combine multiple machine learning techniques to leverage their strengths and improve overall predictive performance. By integrating different models, hybrid approaches can capture a broader range of patterns and relationships in the data, leading to more accurate and robust predictions ([Oppong, 2023](#bib32)).
Research in hybrid models, whether in the field of healthcare, finance, or marketing, has proven its performance over individual models. For instance, a hybrid model using neural networks and decision trees greatly improved the prediction accuracy for medical diagnoses. For example, the hybrid models developed by integrating SVM and XGBoost have shown better performance in predicting stock prices under finance ([Doleck et al., 2020](#bib15)). Stacking involves training multiple models (base learners) and combining their predictions using a meta-learner. This approach can improve predictive performance by capturing different aspects of the data([Metawa et al., 2022](#bib27)). Ensemble learning techniques, such as bagging and boosting, combine the predictions of multiple models to form a stronger overall model. For instance, XGBoost, an ensemble method, combines the predictions of multiple weak learners to achieve high accuracy ([Nawang et al., 2021](#bib30)).
### 2.5. Research gap
Despite notable advancements in predictive analytics and machine learning applications in education, several critical gaps remain unaddressed. This study identifies the following key areas where existing research is limited:
\* •
Incorporation of Longitudinal Data: Most studies rely on static datasets that provide a snapshot of student performance, which limits the ability to assess long-term trends and sustained impacts of interventions. The lack of longitudinal data hampers efforts to track changes in academic performance over time.
\* •
Feature Diversity: Current research often focuses on limited factors such as studied credits and entrance results. Few studies incorporate socio-economic background, extracurricular activities, and psychological metrics, which are crucial for understanding the broader determinants of student performance.
\* •
Real-Time Predictive Models: Existing models predominantly focus on retrospective analysis, offering little value for real-time intervention. The potential for real-time predictive analytics to enable proactive decision-making during critical academic periods remains largely unexplored.
\* •
Scalability and Generalizability: Many studies are confined to specific institutions or regions, limiting the scalability and generalizability of their findings. There is a pressing need for models that can be adapted to diverse educational environments globally.
\* •
Ethical and Operational Challenges: Although some studies have addressed ethical considerations, there remains insufficient discussion on mitigating algorithmic bias, ensuring data privacy, and achieving equitable access to predictive tools, particularly in under-resourced settings.
\* •
Advanced Hybrid and Ensemble Techniques: While hybrid models, such as BiLSTM-based architectures, have shown promise ([Yousafzai et al., 2021](#bib39)), there is limited exploration of advanced stacking and ensemble methods specifically tailored to educational datasets. These techniques could significantly enhance predictive performance but are underexplored in the context of education.
The present study explicitly addresses these gaps by:
\* •
Introducing a hybrid model architecture that leverages advanced stacking techniques tailored for educational datasets, thereby addressing the need for domain-specific ensemble methods.
\* •
Demonstrating the importance of integrating diverse features such as studied credits, regional differences, and number of previous attempts while advocating for the inclusion of socio-economic and behavioral metrics.
\* •
Highlighting the potential of predictive models for real-time applications, setting the stage for operationalizing these models in practical academic settings.
\* •
Employing a dataset from Wollo University and the Kombolcha Institute of Technology, representing diverse student profiles, to ensure the scalability and generalizability of the proposed approach.
\* •
Proposing actionable strategies for using predictive insights to enhance student outcomes, optimize teacher development programs, and allocate resources effectively.
\* •
Discussing ethical considerations and operational challenges, ensuring the responsible deployment of predictive models in education.
By addressing these gaps, this study significantly advances the field of predictive analytics in education, providing a framework that is robust, generalizable, and ethically sound.
## 3. Methodology
### 3.1. Research design
The models were evaluated under two scenarios: 1) \*\*Baseline Performance\*\*—where the models were trained using the original set of features to establish baseline performance metrics, and 2) \*\*Enhanced Feature Set\*\*—where additional domain-specific features were incorporated to assess their impact on model performance and prediction accuracy. These scenarios allowed for a comparison of model effectiveness under both basic and enhanced conditions, directly addressing the research objectives.
The workflow in the student performance predictive analytics goes through several phases. It begins with the collection of data, where the CSV files are pre-loaded with relevant information about the students. The Data Cleaning step makes sure that the collected data is without error and inconsistency for further analysis. In the Feature Engineering stage, new features are created to enhance the predictive power of the dataset. Model Selection occurs after the data preparation and involves picking diverse machine learning models—such as regression, classification, and neural networks and then proceeding to further analysis. The picked models will, after this, go through the training process, where the implementation of the preparation of data takes place. Trained models are then evaluated based on their performance and accuracy during the Model Evaluation step. Hyperparameter Tuning is performed in an attempt to achieve better predictive abilities. After optimization, the models proceed to the Deployment stage, where they are put into practice. In the final Monitoring & Maintenance stage, constant supervision ensures that the models remain effective and accurate over time. This strong systematic workflow aims at leveraging predictive analytics in education to the ultimate benefit of student outcomes. All participants gave their informed consent for inclusion before they participated in the study.

#### 3.2.3. Normalization
It is an essential step to avoid features with different scales to disproportionally influence the machine learning models which would result in better model performance. Normalization is scaling all the numerical attributes in a common range or distribution so that all attributes can be compared and does not make any particular feature dominate the learning process.
Furthermore, studied\\_credits and num\\_of\\_prev\\_attempts have significant scale difference where the values vary and hence need to be normalized. The scale of num\\_of\\_prev\\_attempts (e.g., 0 to 10) greatly differs (e.g., 0 to 240) from that of the studied\\_credits attribute. This is because studied\\_credits are very big relative to the num\\_of\\_prev\\_attempts, which otherwise may not be an important factor, and if not normalized, the model would pay more attention to the large scale and not find any meaningful relationships.
This normalization assists in standardizing these features so the model can pay equal importance to each feature, make the convergence better and increases the model's predictive reliability. This step prevents some features from having undue impact on the model, which is vital when building a model that consists of two different types of features such as numeric and categorical ones.
The common ways to do it include Min-Max Scaling and Z-score normalization. Former scales the features to [0, 1] range while latter normalizes with zero mean and unit variance. For the studied\\_credits and num\\_of\\_prev\\_attempts attributes, both the methods were applied on the datasets so that the model can learn better and accurately.

Our dataset presented a combination of numerical and categorical variables, which required careful curation. Using the random forest algorithm as our primary tool, we were able to identify the most informative characteristics within this diverse dataset. This algorithm is highly effective for feature selection as it can handle a wide range of feature types with great efficiency. We aimed to train a random forest model iteratively using a 5-fold cross-validation setup. This approach not only aids in evaluating the model's performance but also enables us to identify the ideal number of features. Using 5-fold cross-validation is a smart and efficient approach. This method entails dividing the dataset into five subsets or "folds" and utilizing four of them for training purposes while keeping the fifth one aside for validation. The process is repeated five times, with each fold taking a turn as the validation set ([Albreiki et al., 2021](#bib4)).
The smaller number of folds is well-suited for our dataset, ensuring efficient computation while still yielding reliable insights. In addition, using a small number of folds has its benefits as it enables each fold to accurately represent a significant portion of the data. With a dataset of 32,005 students, this approach guarantees that each iteration captures a varied and representative sample, enhancing the overall reliability of the model's performance evaluation.

### 3.4. Hybrid model design
To enhance the predictive performance and robustness of our machine learning models for predicting student achievement, we have designed a hybrid model utilizing ensemble learning techniques, specifically stacking. Stacking integrates multiple machine learning models to leverage their strengths, thereby improving overall prediction accuracy and robustness.
#### 3.4.1. Feature selection and data preparation
The initial dataset for a hybrid model included columns such as id\\_student, gender, region, entrance\\_result, num\\_of\\_prev\\_attempts, studied\\_credits, disability, and final\\_result. The most informative features were selected based on feature importance analysis using the Random Forest algorithm. The id\\_student column was removed as it did not provide predictive value for the model. Existing features were preprocessed for model training, ensuring the quality and suitability for modeling. Missing data was addressed by imputing values or removing records with substantial missing information. Numerical features were normalized using min-max scaling, and categorical variables were encoded using one-hot encoding. The dataset was split into training (70 %), validation (15 %), and test (15 %) sets to facilitate robust model evaluation. This thorough preprocessing ensured the data was clean, consistent, and ready for use in the hybrid modeling process ([Baashar et al., 2021](#bib7)).
#### 3.4.2. Stacking architecture
The stacking architecture comprises two levels. The first level, known as the base learners (Level-0), includes individual models that are trained on the dataset. In this hybrid model, we use a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) and a Random Forest as base learners. The CNN was chosen for its ability to capture complex, non-linear feature hierarchies and deep interactions within the dataset, which is particularly useful for extracting latent patterns from tabular educational data. The Random Forest provides robust predictions by aggregating the outputs of multiple decision trees, thereby reducing overfitting and improving generalization ([Abu-Qdais et al., 2023](#bib1)). Random Forest was selected for its interpretability, ensemble strength, and ability to handle feature variance and categorical diversity.
The second level, known as the meta-learner (Level-1), is trained on the outputs (predictions) of these base learners and makes the final prediction. XGBoost was selected as the meta-learner due to its computational efficiency, regularization capabilities, and ability to boost weak learners effectively—leading to enhanced generalization performance and robustness. For this role, we have selected XGBoost due to its powerful boosting capabilities and efficiency in handling the combined predictions from the base learners, further enhancing the accuracy and robustness of the final model ([Mucherino et al., 2009](#bib28)).
#### 3.4.3. Implementation process
Training the base learners starts with the Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) and the Random Forest. The CNN structure is created with TensorFlow, including numerous convolutional layers, pooling layers, and fully connected layers. Dropout and batch normalization techniques are used to avoid overfitting. The CNN receives training using data augmentation for increased data diversity and regularization to enhance generalization. Grid search is used to optimize hyperparameters like the number of trees and maximum depth in the Random Forest model configured with Scikit-learn. This model is trained on the identical training set, with a main focus on maximizing accuracy and robustness.
For generating base predictions, 5-fold cross-validation is used to create out-of-fold predictions for both CNN and Random Forest. This involves splitting the training data into five subsets, training the models on four subsets, and validating on the fifth subset. The predictions from each fold are stored, forming a new training set for the meta-learner.
The meta-learner is trained by combining the out-of-fold predictions from the base learners to create a new dataset, which includes the predictions of CNN and Random Forest as features and the true labels as the target. The XGBoost ([Cui, 2020](#bib13)) model is then trained on this new training set, with hyperparameters optimized using grid search or Bayesian optimization. For the final prediction, the trained base learners (CNN and Random Forest) generate predictions on the test set. These predictions are then fed into the trained XGBoost meta-learner, which produces the final prediction for the test set. This comprehensive process ensures robust and accurate predictive modeling.
### 3.5. Classification and clustering
Classification and clustering are fundamental techniques in the realm of educational data analytics. Classification models, such as Support Vector Machine (SVM), Decision Tree, and Random Forest, are employed to categorize students into predefined groups based on features like gender, region, entrance\\_result, num\\_of\\_prev\\_attempts, studied\\_credits, and disability. SVM ([H. Alamri et al., 2020](#bib18)) functions by identifying the best hyperplane to divide the data into distinct categories, enabling the classification of students into performance groups such as 'Pass', 'Fail', 'Withdrawn', and 'Distinction'. With their hierarchical layout, Decision Trees divide data into groups based on feature values, making them simple to understand and efficient for foreseeing student results and pinpointing at-risk students. Random Forest works by combining numerous decision trees to improve predictive accuracy and robustness in dealing with the intricacies and fluctuations of educational data effectively.
Alternatively, clustering methods like K-means are used to categorize students with alike traits, helping in creating specific interventions and individualized learning strategies. K-means ([Alsariera et al., 2022](#bib6)) divides the data into separate clusters according to similarity in features, allowing for the detection of groups of students who have similar learning styles, academic achievements, or behavioral tendencies. For instance, clustering can help educators recognize students from similar regions who share analogous entrance results and numbers of previous attempts, facilitating the development of personalized learning strategies for these groups. Together, classification and clustering techniques provide a comprehensive approach to understanding and enhancing student performance, allowing educators to implement tailored interventions and support mechanisms effectively.
### 3.6. Training and evaluation
Training and evaluation are critical components of the predictive modeling process in educational data analytics. For training individual models, the process begins with data preprocessing, which involves handling missing values, removing duplicates, and normalizing numerical features such as studied\\_credits and num\\_of\\_prev\\_attempts. Categorical variables, including region, gender, entrance\\_result, and disability, are encoded appropriately, often using one-hot encoding. Next, the data is divided into training, validation, and test subsets, usually in a ratio of 70:15:15. Cross-validation methods like k-fold cross-validation are used to guarantee the model performs well on new data sets. Hyperparameters are adjusted through techniques such as grid search or random search to enhance the model's effectiveness. After being trained, models go through a series of assessments that involve metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and the AUC-ROC curve to determine their predictive ability and reliability ([Baldi et al., 2000](#bib9)).
In hybrid models, the training process includes utilizing base learners like Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) and Random Forests. These base learners are trained on the dataset, and out-of-fold predictions are generated using cross-validation. These predictions serve as input features for a meta-learner, such as XGBoost, which aggregates the base learners' predictions to make the final prediction. This stacking architecture, which includes a two-level structure with base learners at Level-0 and a meta-learner at Level-1, is optimized through hyperparameter tuning using grid search or Bayesian optimization. The comprehensive training and evaluation processes ensure that both individual and hybrid models are robust, accurate, and capable of providing reliable predictive analytics, thereby enhancing the ability to design effective educational interventions and support mechanisms.

#### 4.3.3. Novelty of the hybrid architecture
The proposed hybrid architecture demonstrates novelty not only through performance enhancement but also in its architectural synergy. The model integrates three complementary learning paradigms: (i) the convolutional neural network (CNN), which excels at capturing high-order non-linear dependencies; (ii) Random Forest, known for its robustness and interpretability in handling diverse feature spaces; and (iii) XGBoost, a powerful gradient boosting algorithm that refines ensemble decisions at the meta-learning stage. This layered integration enables superior generalization across complex and heterogeneous educational data. Unlike conventional single-model approaches, the stacking-based hybrid structure dynamically balances feature sensitivity, model bias, and prediction variance. This design choice was guided not only by empirical accuracy but also by the potential to support modular upgrades, improved transferability to other domains, and better error tolerance, which are all essential in real-world educational analytics systems (see [Table 3](#tbl3)).

## 5. Discussion
### 5.1. Interpretation of results
Based on the detailed evaluation of different machine learning models, Random Forest demonstrated its robustness and effectiveness in predicting educational outcomes. Its high accuracy (85 %), precision (81 %), recall (84 %), and AUC-ROC (0.88) underscore its ability to handle diverse features and capture complex interactions. This validates Random Forest as one of the most reliable standalone models for educational predictive tasks.
This study revealed several actionable areas to improve educational outcomes based on its findings. Firstly, feature engineering can be improved by incorporating domain specific features first which can further improve the accuracy of the model and predictive performance. Second, hybrid models or ensemble methods may be employed to improve the reliability of the predictions so as not to invoke wrong decisions for educational settings. Finally, given an ability to predict student outcomes using the models, at risk students can be identified early, and then utilized by personalized intervention or targeted support strategies that improve overall learning experiences and outcomes.
Modern deep learning models such as CNNs, Decision Tree, SVM and Random Forest also performed well, with CNN achieving an accuracy of 85 % and an AUC-ROC of 0.87. These models excel at capturing high-level feature representations, making them suitable for complex datasets. However, their computational requirements and sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning pose challenges for scalability in resource-constrained settings. Traditional models like SVM and Decision Tree, while interpretable and easy to deploy, exhibited lower performance, with SVM achieving 82 % accuracy and Decision Tree reaching 79 %. These results reflect the limitations of simpler algorithms when applied to high-dimensional and diverse educational datasets.
The hybrid model, combining CNN and Random Forest as base learners with XGBoost as the meta-learner, emerged as the best-performing approach. Achieving an accuracy of 88 % and an AUC-ROC of 0.90, it effectively addressed the shortcomings of individual models by leveraging their strengths. CNNs contributed to feature extraction, Random Forest ensured robustness, and XGBoost optimized the ensemble through gradient boosting. This stacking-based architecture highlights the potential of hybrid models to advance the state-of-the-art in educational predictive analytics.
Clustering analysis further enhanced the study by categorizing students into three distinct performance groups: high-performing (Cluster 1), mixed outcomes (Cluster 2), and low-performing (Cluster 3). This categorization provides actionable insights for educators, enabling targeted interventions. For instance, students in Cluster 3, characterized by low studied credits and high previous attempts, may benefit from additional support, while high-performing students in Cluster 1 could be offered advanced learning opportunities. Cluster 2, representing mixed outcomes, presents an opportunity to design personalized strategies that address specific challenges.
These results demonstrate the potential of predictive analytics to transform educational practices. Early identification of at-risk students, personalized learning plans, and optimized resource allocation are just a few examples of how these models can improve educational outcomes. Furthermore, integrating AI tools like ChatGPT for interactive learning and personalized feedback complements these approaches, fostering innovation in teaching strategies, as discussed in prior studies ([Al Shloul et al., 2024](#bib3)). The hybrid model's interpretability is reinforced by feature importance outputs from the Random Forest base learner and SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values generated by XGBoost at the meta level, enabling educators to understand why certain predictions were made. This facilitates data-driven decisions and builds trust among stakeholders.
### 5.2. Limitations
Despite its strong performance, the proposed hybrid model is not without limitations. One significant limitation is the computational complexity of the architecture, which combines CNNs, Random Forest, and Decision Tree. This complexity increases training time and resource requirements, making real-time applications challenging, particularly in underfunded educational institutions or resource-constrained environments. Another limitation lies in the dependency on high-quality, curated datasets. The model's effectiveness may decline when applied to datasets with missing, imbalanced, or noisy data, which are common in real-world educational settings. While preprocessing steps like normalization and data balancing were applied in this study, further work is needed to enhance the model's robustness to imperfect data.
Moreover, this study primarily focused on model development and evaluation rather than real-world implementation. While the hybrid model achieved superior performance in a controlled environment, its ability to forecast student outcomes and drive interventions in live educational contexts remains untested. This represents a key area for future research. Finally, ethical considerations, including privacy concerns, algorithmic bias, and fairness, have not been fully addressed in this study. The deployment of predictive models in education must account for these issues to ensure equitable access and prevent unintended consequences. Addressing these ethical challenges is crucial for the responsible use of predictive analytics.
While this study provides valuable insights into the performance of the evaluated models, there are several limitations to consider. The findings are based on a single dataset, which may contain biases or limitations in feature selection that could affect the generalizability of the results. Additionally, the models evaluated in this study may not perform as effectively in other educational contexts without further validation on more diverse datasets. Future research should explore the application of these models across different datasets and educational settings to better understand their scalability and robustness.
### 5.3. Recommendations for future research
Future research should prioritize the real-world application of the hybrid model to validate its practical utility. Deploying the model in live educational contexts will allow for real-time prediction of student outcomes, enabling proactive interventions for at-risk students and personalized support for average and high-performing learners. Real-time analytics opens possibilities for dynamic, data-driven decision-making in instructional design and student support services.
This operationalization will provide valuable insights into the model's scalability and effectiveness in diverse settings. To support large-scale deployment, researchers should explore integration with scalable infrastructures such as Apache Spark for distributed data processing and TensorFlow on Kubernetes for elastic deep learning workloads. These platforms will ensure the model remains performant when applied to large, heterogeneous datasets across institutions.
Incorporating additional features, such as socio-economic background, psychological metrics, and extracurricular activities, is essential for enhancing the model's predictive accuracy and comprehensiveness. These features will capture the multifaceted nature of student performance, enabling deeper insights into the factors influencing educational outcomes. Longitudinal studies tracking students over multiple years could provide a better understanding of how predictive analytics impact long-term outcomes. Such studies would also help refine early intervention strategies by identifying sustained effects over time.
Further research should explore more efficient hybrid architectures that maintain high accuracy while reducing computational complexity. Approaches such as knowledge distillation, model pruning, or federated learning can be investigated to make the model more lightweight and privacy-aware. These innovations could significantly enhance accessibility in resource-constrained educational environments, where computational infrastructure may be limited.
Ethical considerations must remain central. Future work should investigate ways to mitigate algorithmic bias, ensure data privacy, and promote fairness in predictive models. Developing guidelines for responsible deployment and creating frameworks for equitable access will ensure that predictive analytics benefit all students, regardless of their background. As highlighted by [Khan et al. (2025)](#bib23), considerations around operational efficiency, privacy, and inclusivity are essential for promoting sustainable educational practices.
By addressing these areas, future research can unlock the full potential of predictive analytics, advancing both the science and application of educational technologies to improve student outcomes and institutional effectiveness.
While this study does provide some valuable insight, numerous further areas are left unexploited. In particular, evaluating the real-world effectiveness of interventions driven by predictive insights remains an open challenge. Models should not only predict but inform meaningful actions. Tracking the actual impact of model-guided interventions on retention, grades, and well-being will offer a more holistic view of their utility. Finally, cross-institutional collaborations could support benchmarking efforts and foster the development of universally adaptable educational AI systems.
## 6. Conclusion
This study explored the use of machine learning models, including SVM, XGBoost, decision trees, CNNs, and hybrid models, for predicting educational outcomes. Among the individual models, the Random Forest model demonstrated strong predictive performance with 85 % accuracy, 81 % precision, 84 % recall, 82 % F1-score, and 0.88 AUC-ROC. However, the hybrid model, which utilized a stacking architecture with CNN and Random Forest as base learners and XGBoost as the meta-learner, outperformed all individual models, achieving 88 % accuracy, 85 % precision, 87 % recall, 86 % F1-score, and 0.90 AUC-ROC.
The study identified key predictive factors influencing student outcomes, including studied credits, the number of previous attempts, entrance results, and geographical region. Additionally, clustering analysis revealed three primary student groups based on performance and background characteristics: high-performing students (Cluster 1), mixed-outcome students (Cluster 2), and low-performing students (Cluster 3). High-performing students exhibited high studied credits and low numbers of previous attempts, while low-performing students showed the opposite. Mixed-outcome students displayed moderate studied credits and an average number of prior attempts. These insights underscore the potential of predictive models to distinguish between varying levels of academic achievement and enable tailored interventions for each group.
The implications of these findings are significant for educational practices. Predictive analytics can help educators identify at-risk students early, allowing for timely and targeted interventions to improve their academic outcomes. Clustering analysis facilitates the design of personalized learning strategies for diverse student groups, enhancing learning experiences and success rates. Predictive models also inform resource allocation, enabling institutions to direct support to areas of greatest need. Moreover, these models can guide teacher development programs by providing insights into student challenges and areas for instructional improvement.
To further enhance the accuracy, reliability, and practical utility of predictive models, future research should incorporate additional data sources, such as socio-economic background, psychological metrics, and extracurricular activities. Longitudinal studies spanning multiple academic years could reveal long-term trends and the sustained impact of early interventions. Advanced hybrid models and real-time predictive analytics should also be explored to provide actionable insights during critical academic periods. Finally, scalability and implementation across varied educational contexts, along with ethical considerations such as privacy and fairness, are essential to ensure widespread applicability and acceptance.
This study provides practical evidence by utilizing a large-scale, real-world dataset from Wollo University and Kombolcha Institute of Technology, reflecting the diversity and heterogeneity of actual student populations. The demographic and behavioral features used mirror what is typically available in higher education systems, increasing the model's relevance for institutional deployment.
Future implementations should explore deploying the hybrid architecture on scalable platforms like Apache Spark for distributed data processing or TensorFlow on Kubernetes for scalable deep learning tasks. These infrastructures can ensure that the proposed system remains efficient when used with larger and more diverse educational datasets.
By addressing these areas, future research can build on the findings of this study, advancing the field of predictive analytics in education and improving outcomes for students across all levels of academic performance.

\* ### [Modelling of enhanced predictive analytics and decision-making approach using recurrent autoencoder with attribute subset reduction for students’ academic performance](/science/article/pii/S2090447926000262)
2026, Ain Shams Engineering Journal
Show abstract
Education plays a vital part in society as it helps economic growth through human capital, decreases crime, and enhances common well-being. Currently, predictive modelling plays a crucial role in decision-making procedures in each branch of action. Conventional techniques for recognising at-risk students often rely on reactive events that occur only after academic complexities have already been demonstrated, usually too late to avert failure or withdrawal. The development of predictive analytics powered by artificial intelligence (AI) provides a paradigm shift in proactive intervention tactics that can recognise students at risk of academic failure or dropout before vital thresholds are attained. Predictive analytics has developed as a transformative method in educational technology, leveraging AI and machine learning (ML) models to recognise at-risk students, forecast academic performances, and recommend targeted interventions before failure happens. ML for predictive analytics is altering data-driven decision-making through industries by leveraging enormous datasets and innovative techniques to discover hidden patterns and estimate future tendencies. In this manuscript, a Predictive Analytics and Decision-Making Using Recurrent Autoencoder with Dimensionality Reduction (PADM-RAEDR) approach is presented in the education sector. The aim is to develop an effective framework that enhances predictive analytics and supports effective decision-making in the education domain. At first, the data pre-processing stage is done by applying Z-score standardisation. For an effective feature selection process, the PADM-RAEDR model employs mutual information (MI), symmetric uncertainty (SU), and minimum redundancy maximum relevance (mRMR) to remove irrelevant and redundant data. At last, the long short-term memory with auto-encoder (LSTM-AE) method is employed for classification. The comparison analysis of the PADM-RAEDR model demonstrated a superior accuracy value of 98.61% over existing methods under a benchmark dataset.
