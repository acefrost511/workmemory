Introduction
------------

The *Futures Dialogue on ‘AI and the Futures of Education’* took place on **22 October 2025**, following [**UNESCO’s Digital Learning Week (DLW)**](https://www.unesco.org/en/weeks/digital-learning), held in Paris on 2-5 September 2025 on the theme “AI and the Future of Education: Disruptions, Directions and Dilemmas”.

The session opened with key insights from the DLW, delivered by **Shafika Isaacs,** Chief of Section for Technology and AI in Education, UNESCO. She positioned the event as a timely pulse-check of the global sentiment on AI’s role in education, creating a platform for critical reflection on the disruptions, dilemmas and directions that are emerging. The accompanying publication: [*AI and the Future of Education*](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-and-future-education-disruptions-dilemmas-and-directions?hub=83250)was intentionally curated to include divergent perspectives. It echoes UNESCO’s call for a global commons for dialogue and action on the rights-based, equity and inclusion agenda on AI in Education. This was followed by contributions from six panelists spread over two panels: (1) Overall reflections on ‘AI and the Future of Education: Disruptions, dilemmas and directions’ and a specific focus on (2) Anticipated futures of curriculum, futures of digital citizenship, and futures of open educational resources. Finally, there was a rich open dialogue.

Artificial intelligence as experimentation
------------------------------------------

Across the world classrooms are increasingly becoming spaces for AI experimentation, often without sufficient understanding of its long-term consequences. As **Wayne Holmes** cautioned, “Until we know, we’re effectively experimenting on children,” stressing the ethical implications of untested technologies in learning environments. He underscored the lack of independent evidence for AI’s educational effectiveness, noting that most existing research is produced by the technology developers themselves. Despite its growing influence, there remains no shared framework or consensus on what constitutes valid, independent evidence of AI’s impact in learning environments.

**Helen Beetham** went a step further and observed that “we’re in a space where what learning outcomes mean is entirely thrown up in the air,” raising urgent questions about how to define and measure meaningful learning in AI-mediated environments. This sense of experimentation extends beyond pedagogy to the broader structures of education itself, prompting calls for transparency, accountability and interdisciplinary collaboration in understanding AI’s consequences for learners, teachers and education systems. Given that AI now permeates many aspects of our lives, addressing its role in education requires engagement from diverse stakeholders, including students, teachers and communities, as echoed by participants **Emmanuel Blanchard** and **Sondi Muluyadi.**

Top-down approaches: Governance, regulation and normative frameworks
--------------------------------------------------------------------

Panelists agreed that ethical governance frameworks are essential to ensure AI serves the common good and protects the rights of learners and educators. **Felisa Tibbitts** emphasized the need for ethical norms rooted in human rights to guide decision-making throughout all stages of AI design and implementation. She referenced UNESCO’s new publication, [*AI and Education: Protecting the Rights of Learners*](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-and-education-protecting-rights-learners), as a crucial tool to advance this agenda. In the context of education, regulation should safeguard the well-being of learners and educators. **Nastassja Pugliese** emphasized that global AI norms must be shaped not only through top-down regulation, but also bottom-up consultation with teachers, students, and communities. Participant **Daniel Baril**further highlighted the opportunity to engage with current deliberations relating to the normative framework on the right to education. Together, these three perspectives underscored the urgency to embed ethical, inclusive, and protective principles into the governance of AI in education. 

Bottom-up approaches: AI literacy, teacher agency and local contexts
--------------------------------------------------------------------

There was broad consensus in the discussion that top-down governance and regulatory frameworks must be complemented by bottom-up and participatory approaches that involve educators, learners and communities. Teachers, in particular, must be recognized as key agents when it comes to curriculum transformation and the ethics of AI, rather than passive recipients of technological change. As **Janeth Kigobe** emphasized, teachers should be at the centre - to lead, to create and to be ethical guardians of what students learn. **Felisa Tibbitts** reinforced this point by asserting that educators should co-create policy and curriculum, not merely implement tools and standards designed elsewhere.

While there is general agreement that AI literacy is a key strategy for navigating AI in education, panelists stressed the need to unpack what AI literacy truly entails and to equip learners and educators with the right tools. **Wayne Holmes** argued that AI literacy must go beyond technical proficiency to include social and human dimensions such as empowerment, child rights and human rights. Participant **Abhilasha Singh** highlighted the difficulties of reconciling “AI-driven education” with humanistic values in education, while **Nastassja Pugliese**cautioned against cognitive dependency that might emerge from overreliance on AI systems, emphasizing the need for learners to cultivate agency, reflection and critical thinking.

Participant **Helen Beetham** stressed that developing critical AI literacies should begin with an understanding of AI’s geopolitical and economic dimensions, enabling individuals and communities to recognize where they possess agency for questioning and resistance. In multicultural settings, **Nurul Akqmie Badrul Hisham**added, AI fluency intersects with identity and inclusion, requiring young people to become “digital negotiators,” capable of navigating the complex interplay between technology, culture and self. 

Openness and open-washing
-------------------------

Panelists examined the risks of opaque AI systems, often presented as “black boxes,” which threaten transparency, accountability and accuracy. They can also lead to cognitive dependency. As **Nastassja Pugliese** explained, most users have little understanding of how these systems function, and large language models exhibit ‘AI sycophancy’ – they are trained to please the user. They often mirror user expectations—even when those responses are inaccurate or misleading. This lack of transparency is compounded by the misuse of the term “open,” which is frequently co-opted for commercial gain. 

**Tel Amiel** warned against “[open washing](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/open-education-principles-resisting-metrics-ai-black-boxes?hub=81942),” where proprietary technologies are marketed as open despite keeping essential mechanisms under tight control. Genuine openness, he argued, must be rooted in public, transparent and collaborative systems that treat knowledge as a common good rather than a commodity. Highlighting the urgent need for ethical scrutiny and structural reform in how AI is developed and deployed, Amiel proposed three pathways forward: 1) Promoting the use of free and open software; 2) Expanding open educational resources and practices; and 3) Ensuring the active participation of all educational actors in the development and governance of AI tools.

Active agents and contributors to policy and practice
-----------------------------------------------------

Participants consistently emphasized the importance of empowering all actors within education systems to become active agents and co-creators in AI in education—teachers, students, researchers, policymakers and communities alike. **Wayne Holmes** noted that “AI has escaped from the laboratory and now impacts all of us,” underscoring the need for wider engagement from scholars across the humanities, social sciences, and education in defining responsible and context-sensitive uses of AI.

**Tel Amiel** and **Janeth Kigobe** argued that genuine transformation requires moving beyond consultation toward co-creation and shared responsibility. Teachers and learners should not only adapt to change but actively shape it, influencing how AI tools are designed, governed, and evaluated. This involves participatory governance, collaborative curriculum design, and inclusive dialogue that values local knowledge, cultural perspectives and lived experience.

Conclusion
----------

In conclusion, **Shafika Isaacs** highlighted the shared commitment by panelists, participants, and UNESCO to collaboratively address the complex ethical, social, and educational challenges of AI by promoting more equitable, inclusive, socially just, and open, approaches to digital learning.
