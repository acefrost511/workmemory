Abstract
--------

This study evaluates the effectiveness of a higher education initial teacher education (ITE) programme in preparing primary school preservice teachers (PSTs) to integrate information, communication, digital, and robotics technologies (ICDRTs) into classroom teaching. While situated in an ITE programme, the methodology, based on a Rasch-calibrated self-audit, offers an actionable approach to evaluating PSTs’ digital confidence and learning needs across higher education disciplines. Using a Rasch-validated 77-item self-audit instrument, responses from 490 final-year PSTs were analysed to identify curriculum gaps and assess the programme’s capacity to develop digital pedagogical readiness. Regression analysis examined associations between readiness and socio-demographic variables (sex, age group, and qualification) to explore equity-related differences. Age and sex were associated with digital readiness. Mature-age PSTs showed higher readiness in workplace-associated tools, while school-leavers were more confident in classroom applications. The association among sex and readiness varied across digital readiness indices. The findings also revealed consistent shortfalls in ICDRT readiness related to spreadsheets, robotics, and commonly used classroom technologies, aligning with broader concerns about digital capability in initial teacher preparation. These results offer actionable evidence for curriculum redesign and institutional evaluation within higher education and demonstrate the value of structured student self-audits as institutional evaluation tools. The study contributes to equity-focused, data-driven approaches to programme improvement in higher education.

1 Introduction
--------------

In the higher education context, initial teacher education (ITE) programmes face persistent challenges in preparing preservice teachers (PSTs) to effectively integrate information, communication, digital, and robotics technologies (ICDRTs) into classroom practice (Adnan et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR4); Redmond et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR47)). These competencies are increasingly critical, particularly for supporting students’ future engagement with Science, Technology, Engineering and Mathematics (STEM) fields. Yet, PSTs’ confidence and preparedness to use these technologies vary considerably (Wang et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR51); Wilson, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR53)). To address this variability, ITE providers must gain a deeper understanding of PSTs’ self-perceived confidence in technology integration, not only to gauge readiness for the profession but to inform and improve the design of their curricula.

This study examines variation in PSTs’ ICDRT confidence using a Rasch-calibrated self-audit instrument (Martin et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR11)), designed to capture self-perceived readiness across a broad set of specific tools and pedagogical practices. Measuring PSTs’ technology-related capabilities through established frameworks such as the Technological, Pedagogical, Content Knowledge (TPACK) (Mishra & Koehler, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR40)) framework and contextual knowledge (XK) (Mishra, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR39)) allow ITE programs to transition from generalised (one size fits all) approaches to more targeted, equitable, and responsive curricula. In line with recommendations from Brianza et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR21)) and Mishra ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR39)), we also analyse how socio-demographic factors of sex, age group, and highest qualification relate to PSTs’ confidence in using specific ICDRTs. Importantly, this study analyses each ICDRT item individually rather than relying on aggregated composite scores. This item-level approach enables a more granular understanding of where support is needed, revealing confidence patterns that may otherwise be obscured.

Beyond ITE, this study demonstrates how student self-assessment can inform higher education evaluation. While the study is situated in an ITE programme, the Rasch-validated self-audit tool used here offers broader relevance for tertiary education. The empirical item-category measures generated by Rasch analyses provide institutions with fine-grained, actionable data to support student learning and curriculum development. Self-assessment tools are widely recognised for encouraging PST reflection and supporting formative assessment practices in higher education (Serratos & Parkman, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR48)). When coupled with Rasch modelling, these tools become powerful instruments for evidence-based evaluation of learning priorities (Boone et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR20)).

2 Pre-service teacher confidence in using technologies
------------------------------------------------------

In Australia, the national curriculum includes the Technologies learning area, taught from Foundation to Year 10, which focuses on developing students’ skills in digital systems and computational thinking (ACARA, 2025a, [2025b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR1)). The curriculum also features a Digital Literacy General Capability, emphasising data management, digital communication, and safety (ACARA, [2025c](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR2), [2025d](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR3)). Therefore, PSTs must be confident in meeting these professional requirements upon completing their ITE programmes (AITSL, 2011, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR5), Blannin et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR18)).

To contextualise this study, we draw upon a systematic literature review of PST technology integration during teaching practice (Anwar et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR6)). By analysing studies published between 2017 and 2022, Anwar et al. synthesised trends, challenges, and best practices in ICDRT integration within ITE programmes. The review concluded that there was a need “for a shift in focus towards the development of models and frameworks that delineate the specific technology and technology-related courses required within teacher preparation programs” (Anwar et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR6), p.11). Common barriers to effective technology use identified by the review included the need for targeted interventions to bridge gaps in technology-related skills and the variability in PSTs’ confidence across ICDRT competencies.

ITE programmes must identify and address gaps in PSTs’ technological and pedagogical skills, as many PSTs, despite being tech-savvy in daily life, lack the depth of understanding needed for educational purposes (Redmond et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR47)). Confidence, which is often linked to self-efficacy, is an important trait for teachers to possess to successfully adopt specific teaching practices and content knowledge (Adnan et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR4)). Higher confidence correlates with a greater likelihood of using technology-based learning and teaching methods (Bai et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR14); Joshi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR30); Pozas & Letzel, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR44)). However, PSTs often struggle with integrating ICDRTs into their practice (Valtonen et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR50)). Therefore, it is essential for teacher educators to understand their PSTs’ ICDRT confidence to support the design of effective learning experiences (Adnan et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR4); Backfisch et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR13), Wilson et al., 2023).

3 Theoretical background
------------------------

### 3.1 Preservice teachers’ self-efficacy beliefs

Self-efficacy, from Bandura’s social cognitive theory (1977, 1997), refers to an individual’s belief in their ability to perform tasks successfully. In teaching with digital technologies, PSTs’ self-efficacy influences their confidence, engagement, and quality of integrating ICDRTs (Joshi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR30); Kapici & Akcay, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR31); Redmond et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR47)). Self-efficacy develops through mastery experiences, vicarious learning, verbal encouragement, and emotional/physiological states (Bandura, [1997](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR16); Pfitzner-Eden, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR43)). Thus, ITE programs can strengthen PSTs’ technology-related self-efficacy by offering sustained experiential learning. Such learning might include: (a) direct success using ICDRTs with peer/tutor support; (b) observing effective demonstrations; and (c) receiving encouragement during informal tech-development sessions.

There is evidence to suggest that scaffolded learning environments that promote pedagogical competence and teacher agency enhance PSTs’ confidence (Carey et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR10)). Strategies like instructor modelling, authentic teaching practice, and structured peer collaboration support meaningful ICT integration (Martin et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR9); Joshi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR30)). As an additional resource, a Rasch-validated self-audit tool, used diagnostically before coursework, enables PSTs to reflect on their confidence with various ICDRTs. This approach aligns with Bandura’s theory, helping PSTs recognise areas of mastery and challenge. Reflection on prior experiences supports self-awareness, motivation, and goal setting in digital teaching development.

### 3.2 Situating the validated self-audit tool within the TPACK model and contextual knowledge (XK)

The Rasch-validated self-audit tool is grounded in the TPACK framework (Mishra & Koehler, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR40)), which identifies the essential teacher knowledge domains for effective integration of ICDRTs: technological (TK), pedagogical (PK), and content knowledge (CK), along with their intersections—TPK, TCK, and PCK. These domains collectively form the TPACK construct, with the framework also suggesting that technology integration is most effective when these knowledge types are synthesised.

Expanding this framework, Mishra ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR39)) introduced Contextual Knowledge (XK) as a critical outer domain (see Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#Fig1)), arguing that meaningful and sustainable technology integration also requires teachers to understand the specific educational contexts in which they operate. Without XK, the application of TPACK can become inconsistent or ineffective (Nguyễn, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR42); Willermark, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR52)). Of relevance to XK, traditional self-report assessment tools often fail to capture how teachers confidently apply ICDRTs in diverse pedagogical contexts (Voogt et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR55); Mishra, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR39)).

By embedding the self-audit tool within the expanded TPACK + XK framework, the study recognises the vital role of context in shaping PSTs’ pedagogical decisions and confidence with ICDRTs. The tool prompts PSTs to reflect not only on what they know, but also on how their knowledge aligns with the realities of teaching in primary schools. The Rasch measurement model strengthens this process by ensuring precise, psychometrically valid comparisons across competencies. Through item-level alignment with specific TPACK domains situated within XK (Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#Tab1)), the tool enables targeted insights into PSTs’ digital capabilities and supports more granular, reliable self-evaluation.

Self-report items should be contextual and specific and able to produce responses from PSTs that minimise misinterpretation and are sensitive enough to reliably measure variation in PST confidence. In a previous study (Carey et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR10)) we reported the Rasch-validated self-audit tool’s utility in measuring the effect of an ICDRT intervention, and PSTs’ use of the self-audit survey results to identify their areas of strength, and areas in need of further development. In the current study we focus on identifying how the socio-demographic factors of sex, age group, and highest qualification are associated with PSTs’ self-reported ICDRT confidence.

4 Equity challenges in the development of digital competencies
--------------------------------------------------------------

Digital competency development is increasingly viewed as an equity issue, not merely a technical or skills-based matter. Raihan et al. ([2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR45)) emphasise that disparities in access, confidence, and effective use of digital technologies stem from intersecting socio-demographic and structural factors such as age, gender, education, and income. Digital inequity is thus a multi-dimensional issue, not a singular deficit, and it significantly limits individuals’ capacity to build and maintain digital competencies over time.

Teachers’ competence plays a crucial role in shaping the learning environment, particularly influencing female students’ engagement with educational technologies in the classroom (Msambwa et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR41); Tellhed et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR49)). Therefore, it is important to examine the extent of sex-based differences in ICDRT confidence among primary PSTs. If female teachers generally have lower confidence, this imbalance could perpetuate gender stereotypes, further reinforcing sex-based disparities among primary school students through the influence of role models.

Previous research across various cultural contexts has shown mixed results regarding sex differences in confidence, with some studies finding lower confidence in females in Zambia (Bwalya & Rutegwa, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR22)) in Estonia (Ibrohim et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR29)) and in Spain (Gómez-Trigueros & de Yáñez, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR28)) and others reporting no significant difference in China (Li, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR33)) in Germany (Pozas & Letzel, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR44)) and in Australia (Redmond & Peled, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR46)). One study in India found higher self-reported confidence in women (Basargekar & Singhavi, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR17)). Further research in such diverse international settings is required to better understand the basis of these sex differences in PST ICDRT confidence. A more nuanced understanding of sex differences in confidence for specific items of ICDRTs is warranted, because most prior research has involved the analysis of confidence using broad classifications.

Regarding age, studies on ICDRT confidence often report lower levels in older PSTs and in-service teachers compared to younger ones. For example, ICDRT confidence has been shown to decrease as age increases among Singaporean science teachers, especially females (Lin et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR34)). Similarly, Indonesian teachers under 40 years show higher confidence than those over 40 (Kumala et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR32)). However, in Australia and Israel, PSTs older than 26 years exhibit higher confidence (Redmond & Peled, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR46)). While higher qualifications (e.g., master’s degrees) are linked to greater digital confidence (Ibrohim et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR29); Luik et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR36)), the digital divide literature cautions against using qualification level as a proxy for competence. Foteinou ([2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR26)) notes that gender disparities persist even among highly educated women, driven more by structural and institutional inequities than by access or skills.

5 The present study
-------------------

Despite a growing body of research on digital capability in teacher education, much of the existing literature relies on aggregated measures of technology confidence, often obscuring the ways in which PSTs engage with specific tools and practices in primary school teaching. Building on this body of work, the present study adopts a more granular, item-level approach to examine PSTs’ confidence in using a wide range of ICDRTs aligned to teaching in a primary school context. This study explores the associations between sex, age group, highest qualification, and PSTs’ confidence in specific ICDRT competencies. It also aims to identify priorities for ITE programme design and delivery using detailed analyses from a highly specific PST self-audit survey tool.

The research questions are:

1. 1.
   
   What curriculum development priorities can be identified through a self-audit of PSTs’ digital readiness?
2. 2.
   
   Do differences in confidence across socio-demographic groups (sex, age group, and highest qualification) suggest equity challenges that ITE programmes should address in the development of digital competencies?

6 Materials and methods
-----------------------

### 6.1 Participants

This quantitative study utilised convenience sampling to recruit PST participants who were in their last year of a four-year Primary School Bachelor of Education degree at a university in Australia during the period 2021 to 2023. Over the duration of five consecutive semesters, 490 PSTs volunteered to complete the 77-item online survey (Appendix 1); a response rate of 95%. The high response rate was due to the survey being one of five discussion board tasks that had a combined assessment weighting of 10% in the course. The survey was made available to the participants at the start of their technologies education course. Prior to enrolling in the technologies course, PSTs undertook coursework across their ITE degree in which digital technologies were integrated in limited and varied ways in relation to the three Australian Professional Teaching Standards for ICDRTs: *2.6-Information and communication technology; 3.4-Select and use resources; and 4.5-Use ICT safely*, *responsibly*, *and ethically* (AITSL, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR5)).

The study was approved by the university’s Human Research Ethics Committee (HREA201431) and conducted in accordance with all required ethics protocols. Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#Tab2) summarises the demographic information associated with the participants: their self-identified sex (Male/Female/Non-binary), age group, and the highest education qualification they had obtained. Participants all identified as either Male or Female.

### 6.2 Measures: The ICDRT confidence self-audit tool

The 77-item self-audit survey tool used in the current study was previously validated with PSTs through a rigorous Rasch model analysis (Martin et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR11)). Survey items were developed to represent 10 ICDRT components: *General Computing Skills* (7 items); *Email Skills* (8 items); *World-Wide Web Skills* (9 items); *Word Processing Skills* (6 items); *Presentation Skills* (7 items); *Spreadsheet Skills* (8 items); *Multimedia Skills* (7 items); *Coding and Robotics Skills* (6 items); *ICT and Technologies Pedagogy* (9 items); and, *Common Technologies used in Schools* (10 items). All outcome measures reflect perceived confidence rather than demonstrated competence or readiness. Participants responded to each item on a four-point scale: 1 = not confident at all; 2 = slightly confident; 3 = moderately confident; and 4 = very confident. One example survey item for each of the 10 ICDRT components is provided in Table [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#Tab3).

### 6.3 Approach to analysis

In this study, ordinal regression was used to examine the relationships between socio-demographic factors (sex, age group, highest qualification) and PSTs’ confidence across the items of the ICDRT confidence self-audit tool. Each of these items represents a unique aspect of ICDRT use as integral for understanding specific competencies within the broader framework. We analysed each of the items individually using ordinal regression to capture the specific effects of sex, age, and qualification on PSTs’ confidence, which would be lost in a parsimonious approach of grouping outcome scores. The analytic focus was intentionally item-level to support fine-grained curricular diagnosis; synthesis using Rasch person measures or multidimensional Rasch models is identified as a valuable extension for future work rather than a requirement for the present diagnostic aims.

The ordinal regression models were used to descriptively surface item-level patterns in perceived confidence across demographic groups rather than to support inferential hypothesis testing. Accordingly, formal reporting of proportional-odds assumption tests, model fit indices, and effect sizes (e.g., odds ratios with confidence intervals) was not prioritised, as the analyses were not intended to yield population-level estimates or causal interpretations. Findings are therefore interpreted contextually and in conjunction with Rasch model results, which provide the primary psychometric basis for scale validity, measurement invariance, and equity interpretation.

Rasch analysis was conducted prior to the regression analyses to confirm unidimensionality and item fit. This established that the survey items functioned as indicators of a single latent construct rather than as independent outcomes. On this basis, the subsequent item-level analyses were undertaken for diagnostic purposes within a single institutional context, rather than for inferential generalisation or formal hypothesis testing. Consequently, corrections for multiple comparisons (e.g., Bonferroni or false discovery rate adjustments) were not applied, as the analyses were intended to identify item-specific patterns relevant to curricular review rather than to test a global null hypothesis.

To further support the equity interpretation of item-level findings, exploratory Rasch differential item functioning (DIF) screening was conducted by sex, age group, and highest qualification using practical DIF contrast thresholds. This screening provided a robustness check on whether observed group differences were plausibly attributable to DIF rather than substantive differences in perceived confidence.

For the preliminary Rasch analyses of this current data set we used Winsteps Version 5.2.1 (Linacre, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR35)), to evaluate the construct validity and measurement accuracy of the survey. These analyses assessed how well the data fit the Rasch model, using fit statistics for both PST respondents (persons) and items, as well as the separation indices. Fit statistics use the patterns of participant responses to estimate how much misfit is evident, or “how likely that misfit is” (Bond et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR19), p. 36). We examined item-level fit indices to identify any items with sufficient level of misfit to be considered for removal in subsequent analyses. Expected fit is a value of 1. Fit statistics Infit and Outfit mean square values (MNSQ) from 0.6 to 1.4 are acceptable for rating scale measurements that are low stakes, such as the survey items used in this study, in comparison to high-stakes scales such as standardised test (Wright & Linacre, [1994](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR54)).

We examined Item Reliability and Person Reliability estimates to measure the precision and consistency of the survey scale, with the recommended threshold of acceptability being α ⩾ 0.9 for Item Reliability (Linacre, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR35)) and ⩾ 0.7 for Person Reliability (George & Mallery, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR27)). We examined Item Separation indices to determine the number of statistically distinct levels of item endorsement difficulty or person ability (confidence) within the data (Linacre, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR35)). Low Person Separation (< 2) implies, in the context of our self-audit survey, that there is great variability in individual responses, so some items may not be able to distinguish well between high and low confidence (Linacre, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR35)). Low Item Separation (< 3) implies that the person sample is not large enough to confirm item difficulty hierarchy (construct validity) of the instrument.

To address our first research question related to identifying curriculum priorities for ITE programmes, we examined the Rasch analysis output table *Empirical Item-Category Measures*, which shows the percentile ranks of the participants’ response selection for each item (not at all confident to very confident) and the order of the most difficult to the least difficult to endorse item. To address our second research question, we used SPSS to develop separate ordinal logistic regression models, one for each self-audit survey item, to examine how sex, age group, and highest qualification influenced PSTs’ reported confidence in specific technology skills. The analysis, using a proportional odds model, assumed consistent associations across all outcome categories. Pearson goodness-of-fit tests verified model adequacy, and final models were evaluated to determine that they significantly predicted ICDRT confidence beyond the intercept-only model.

7 Results
---------

### 7.1 Preliminary analyses

The Rasch analysis showed that the survey scale was fit for purpose after deleting two items. In the analysis of the 77 items, two items were misfitting:

1. 1.
   
   Item 9.4: ICT and Technologies Pedagogy - *I can use the SAMR model to select how ICT and other technologies will be used in teaching strategies* (infit MNSQ 1.66 outfit MNSQ 1.67).

These two ill-fitting items were not included in the following ordinal regression analysis.

Most importantly for this self-audit survey, after removing the two misfitting items, Item Reliability was strong (α = 1), and Item Separation values (16.36) were high (Linacre, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR35)), indicating that the items were consistently ordered in terms of endorsement levels. The Person Separation value was 4.97, indicating strong measurement precision, as values above 2 are generally considered acceptable (Linacre, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR35)).

This indicates that the survey distinguishes between PSTs with high and low confidence with five levels of separation. Lower Person Separation usually indicates that some items in the component are too easy or too difficult to endorse for most respondents. In this self-audit context, the Person Separation index is less critical because the primary goal of the self-audit survey is to provide individual feedback based on the item responses rather than to rank or compare individuals. Person Reliability (0.96) was above the acceptable recommended threshold of α ⩾ 0.7 (George & Mallery, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR27)). Detailed results for this Rasch analysis and the ordinal regression results are available in the OSF repository: https://osf.io/kxd5g/overview? view\_only=2576ed091b884a959ccaae963d2ca700.

### 7.2 PST confidence levels and potential curriculum priorities for ITE courses

In response to research question 1, we examined the Empirical Item-Category Measures in the Rasch model analysis of the survey instrument to determine the most difficult to the least difficult items for the PST participants to endorse (Appendix 1). The most difficult item to endorse was Item 8.5 - *I can explain the way in which binary digits are used to encode individual pixels of an image*, as part of the Coding and Robotics component, and the easiest was Item 4.9 - *I can change the page orientation from portrait to landscape*, from the Word Processing Skills component.

The PSTs expressed the least confidence in items within the following components: Common Technologies used in Schools, Spreadsheet Skills, and Coding and Robotics Skills. The component containing the highest number of items (eight) for which the PSTs expressed the least confidence was Common Technologies used in Schools. For instance, the PSTs were not confident when using technologies such as Makey Makey, Micro: bit, Minecraft Edu, Kodu, Scratch Jr, Scratch 3.0, and Microsoft Paint 3D, which are technologies commonly used in Primary school education.

In the Spreadsheet Skills component, the PSTs expressed the least confidence in six items, for example, using formulas, creating hyperlinks, creating graphs from tables, and sorting data alphabetically or numerically. The Coding and Robotics Skills component also contained six items for which PSTs expressed low confidence with four items identified as yielding the lowest level of confidence: (1) item 8.5 - *I can explain the way in which binary digits are used to encode individual pixels of an image*, (2) item 8.4 - *I can teach the binary number system*, (3) item 8.2 - *I can program a robot to perform a complex task using sensors*, and (4) item 8.3 - *I can explain the elements of a digital system*. The three components for which the PSTs expressed the least confidence, Common Technologies used in Schools, Spreadsheet Skills, and Coding and Robotics Skills, are related in the Australian context of this study to the Digital Technologies curriculum (ACARA, [2025b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR1)) and the Digital Literacy General Capability (ACARA, [2025d](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR3)) which are compulsory to teach in Primary school education.

### 7.3 Associations of ICDRT confidence with sex, age group, and highest qualification

To address research question 2, a cumulative odds ordinal logistic regression with proportional odds was run to determine the effect of sex, age group, and highest qualification, on PSTs’ confidence to use specific technologies for teaching and learning purposes. The assumption of proportional odds was met for all items (*p* >.05). The Pearson goodness-of-fit test indicated that the model was a good fit to the observed data (*p* >.05), and the final model significantly predicted the dependent variable over and above the intercept only model for 25 out of 75 items (*p* <.05). Out of these 25 ICDRT confidence items tested with regressions, 24 significant associations were found. The model fit and regression results tables for each of the survey items can be viewed and downloaded from the OSF data repository.

The sex, age group, and highest qualification variables that were significant predictors of PST confidence for each survey item are displayed in a coefficient forest plot (Appendix 2). #### 7.3.1 Male and female PSTs

The sex of PSTs significantly influenced confidence in 10 items while controlling for qualification and age. Males were 31% to 64% more confident in four items within General Computer Skills, 40% more confident in two World Wide Web Skills items, and 50% more confident in two Coding and Robotics items. Conversely, females were more than twice as likely to express higher confidence in two ICT and Technologies Pedagogy items, particularly those related to lesson planning and administrative tasks like creating classroom newsletters for parents (Table [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#Tab4); Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#Fig3)).

#### 7.3.2 School-leavers and mature-aged PSTs

Of the 75 items tested, 64 showed no significant differences in confidence between school-leaver and mature-age PSTs after controlling for sex and qualification. No age group differences were found in World-Wide Web Skills, Word Processing Skills, Presentation Skills, Multimedia Skills, Coding and Robotics Skills, and ICT and Technologies Pedagogy components. However, mature-age PSTs were significantly more confident in 13 items (Table [5](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#Tab5); Fig. [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#Fig4)), particularly in General Computer Skills (39% to 63% more likely) and Email Skills (39% to 55% more likely). In Spreadsheet Skills, mature-age PSTs were 56.8% more confident in tasks like print previewing. School-leavers were only more confident in setting up OneNote, being 78.2% more confident than mature-age PSTs.

#### 7.3.3 Highest qualification

Only one item showed a significant association with the highest qualification variable, over and above the associations of sex and age group. PSTs with a bachelor’s degree were 61.3% more likely to be confident in their ability to set up parameters to automatically move messages into folders (Item 2.10 - Email Skills component) than those with a pre-tertiary qualification (B = − 0.49, SE = 0.23, *p* <.05, OR = 0.613, 95% CI [−0.95, − 0.03]).

8 Discussion
------------

Building on the item-level analysis, this discussion evaluates how socio-demographic characteristics, namely sex, age group, and highest qualification, reveal important patterns in PSTs’ digital readiness. By using a validated self-audit survey and Rasch modelling, the study provides empirical evidence that can guide institutional decision-making in ITE programmes, especially regarding digital capability development. The PSTs involved in this study had not yet completed their formal instruction in technologies content and pedagogy, providing a strategic window to assess baseline readiness. These insights allow for an authentic evaluation of how well ITE programmes are preparing PSTs prior to final-year technologies coursework, and where redesign or targeted intervention is warranted.

While frameworks such as DigCompEdu provide valuable benchmarks for digital competence, the present instrument complements these approaches by offering a Rasch-validated, item-level self-audit aligned with TPACK + XK, and is readily extensible to incorporate emerging AI-related pedagogical concerns, including tool selection, ethical use, and accuracy awareness.

### 8.1 PST ICDRT confidence and curriculum priorities for ITE

The Rasch-validated self-audit survey employed in this study was an effective tool for evaluating the extent to which the teacher education program equipped PSTs to meet the digital demands of modern classrooms. Rather than relying on aggregate scores, this tool enabled a detailed, competency-level analysis. This granularity enhanced its value for informing curriculum design, allowing targeted improvements aligned with specific areas of need.

The Rasch model analysis produced clear priorities for curriculum improvement. In response to research question 1, the least-endorsed items, primarily related to Spreadsheet Skills, Coding and Robotics, and Common Technologies used in Schools, were identified as critical gaps in digital readiness. These findings align with Anwar et al. ([2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR6)), who highlight persistent issues in PSTs’ preparedness to teach with emerging technologies, despite general digital literacy. Consistent with Chen et al. ([2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR23)) and Milutinović ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR38)), PSTs in this study showed high confidence in general productivity tools (e.g., email, word processing, internet research), but significantly lower confidence in integrating more specialised, pedagogically relevant technologies. This suggests digital skills exist, but pedagogical use needs explicit support. Building on Mason and Rich ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR37)), the findings stress the importance of sustained, hands-on engagement with classroom technologies like Makey Makey and Scratch, which support computational thinking and align with curriculum standards to prepare PSTs for technology-rich teaching.

Accordingly, research on digital skills development and equity suggests that peer-supported learning structures can play a key role in addressing uneven confidence profiles within cohorts. Collaborative tasks, mixed-experience groupings, and peer modelling have been shown to support confidence development in areas such as data handling and computational thinking, particularly where prior exposure varies across learners. Embedding peer-learning approaches within spreadsheet, coding, and robotics activities may therefore offer a practical mechanism for addressing the specific confidence gaps identified in this study.

Item-level difficulty rankings (Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#Fig3), Appendix 1) offer actionable insights for program design. Specifically, time should be spent working with overemphasised general tools and more time dedicated to specific underdeveloped digital competencies. This positions the self-audit tool as both a reflective aid and a valid instrument for curriculum evaluation and redesign, enabling targeted improvements based on PSTs’ confidence levels.

Table [6](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#Tab6) outlines how key digital skills can be embedded across curriculum learning areas, promoting contextual, repeated exposure. For instance, integrating spreadsheets in Mathematics supports conceptual learning and transferable data skills. Embedding digital learning across courses, rather than isolating it, ensures more consistent, authentic preparation throughout the ITE program.

At the program level, findings reveal a structural issue. Placing the technologies education course late in the degree limits iterative skill development. Concentrating ICDRT instruction in a single fourth-year course restricts practice and reinforcement. A distributed model that involves embedding ICDRTs across multiple courses and placements better supports progressive skill-building and aligns with Bandura’s ([1977](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR15)) self-efficacy theory, which emphasises mastery experiences as key to developing PSTs’ confidence (Adnan et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR4); Backfisch et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR13); Wilson, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR53)).

### 8.2 Equity in digital preparedness: Socio-demographic influences

The second research question investigated whether demographic factors revealed inequities in digital readiness among PSTs. From an institutional perspective, these findings carry implications for equity-focused evaluation of teaching programmes. In Rasch measurement, differential item functioning (DIF) is a core diagnostic for examining equity and measurement invariance across groups. The absence of substantively meaningful DIF in this study supports the interpretation that observed group differences reflect differences in perceived confidence rather than item bias within the studied cohort, strengthening the equity-based interpretation of the findings.

Age group emerged as the most significant predictor: mature-age PSTs reported higher confidence in spreadsheet, email, and general computer tasks, likely due to prior workplace exposure. This aligns with Joshi ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR30)), who found that PSTs with prior exposure to technology through work or school showed greater confidence and readiness to integrate it into their teaching. Conversely, school-leavers showed higher confidence with school-oriented tools like Microsoft OneNote, possibly reflecting post-pandemic high school experiences (Cuskelly et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR25)). This distinction suggests that ITE programmes must recognise and address differential entry points to technology preparedness.

Sex differences also pointed to systemic issues. Male PSTs reported higher confidence in coding and technical components (e.g., digital systems), while female PSTs were more confident in pedagogical applications. These findings mirror gendered patterns in STEM confidence and participation (Cimpian et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR24); Bwalya & Rutegwa, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR22)), and reinforce the institutional responsibility to design inclusive, stereotype-challenging learning experiences, especially in female-dominated primary education courses.

Interestingly, highest qualification had minimal impact on overall confidence, suggesting that traditional academic entry points may not predict technology preparedness. Instead, programmes should employ ongoing formative evaluation tools, such as self-audits, throughout the curriculum to track growth and provide differentiated support. Collectively, these findings highlight areas where ITE programmes can take a data-driven approach to promote equity in digital pedagogy development and ensure all PSTs graduate with the necessary skills to meet professional standards.

Bandura’s self-efficacy theory (1997) offers a valuable lens for interpreting these findings, highlighting the role of mastery experiences, vicarious learning, and encouragement in building PSTs’ confidence to integrate ICDRTs. To foster equitable self-efficacy, ITE programs should embed scaffolded, practice-based experiences throughout. For instance, mature-age PSTs with workplace experience could lead group tasks to support intergenerational learning, while younger PSTs could benefit from early digital literacy workshops and guided, hands-on practice (Martin et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR9); Joshi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR30)). Instructor modelling and structured opportunities for application can help bridge experience gaps.

To address gender disparities, ITE providers must go beyond access, recognising that the digital divide stems from structural inequities (Foteinou, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8#ref-CR26)). Strategies include embedding technical skills (e.g., coding) within authentic classroom contexts, increasing visibility of female instructors, and fostering inclusive learning environments. Critical digital literacy tasks can also prompt PSTs to examine how policies, systems, and social norms shape digital access and usage.

### 8.3 Limitations and future directions

This study was conducted within a single institution using a convenience sample and does not claim generalisability beyond the focal context. Findings are therefore interpreted as programme-specific diagnostics rather than population estimates. Although data were collected across multiple semesters, analyses were not intended for inferential estimation across cohorts; modelling cohort or semester effects through longitudinal or multilevel designs is identified as an important direction for future multi-institutional research.

The integration of technology into teaching practice is shaped by numerous contextual factors, including institutional infrastructure, access to digital tools, and evolving pedagogical expectations. While this study provides important insight into PSTs’ perceived digital confidence, a self-audit does not measure actual performance or longitudinal development. Accordingly, findings are interpreted as indicators of perceived confidence rather than demonstrated competence.

Although exploratory Rasch DIF screening did not indicate substantively meaningful item-level bias within this cohort, future research using more balanced samples and multi-institutional designs could examine measurement invariance with greater precision and extend equity analyses across diverse contexts.

Confidence levels reported in this study are also shaped by local institutional conditions, including the availability of instructional technologies and the sequencing of technologies coursework within the degree. Cross-national and large-scale EdTech readiness studies highlight the role of such contextual factors in shaping digital confidence and preparedness. As a result, findings should be interpreted as context-dependent, and future cross-national or multi-institutional research would be required to examine how variations in infrastructure and institutional support influence PSTs’ digital confidence more broadly.

Future studies should triangulate self-audit data with performance-based tasks, classroom observations, or digital portfolios to validate self-perceptions and to examine how confidence aligns with demonstrated competence post-graduation and in professional settings. Targeted interventions, particularly for under-confident groups, could also provide stronger causal evidence to inform programme design.

Follow-up experimental studies using pre/post-intervention designs with the Rasch-validated self-audit tool would enable evaluation of the impact of targeted curriculum changes on confidence with specific ICDRTs (e.g., robotics or spreadsheet literacy). Although the present study retained an item-level focus for diagnostic purposes, future work could incorporate component-level summaries, Rasch scale scores, or visualisations (e.g., heatmaps) to support programme-level benchmarking and communication of patterns across items.

To further align with institutional evaluation frameworks, ITE programmes might embed this Rasch-validated self-audit tool at multiple stages of the degree, providing both PSTs and programme leaders with developmental trajectories, student-developed action plans, and evidence for curriculum accreditation or renewal. Future studies could also trial the tool across diverse tertiary education contexts (e.g., business, health sciences, engineering), incorporating artefact-based or performance-based validation alongside self-report data to examine its cross-disciplinary utility.

9 Conclusion
------------

This study evaluated the effectiveness of an ITE programme in building PSTs’ digital capabilities using a Rasch-validated self-audit tool. Unlike prior research that relies on domain-level ICDRT confidence ratings, our item-level analysis provided specific, actionable insights that enable programme-level diagnosis of gaps in technological pedagogical readiness. Three key areas emerged as consistent points of PST weakness: spreadsheets, coding and robotics, and technologies commonly used in primary schools. These findings can directly inform curriculum priorities for ITE course redesign, ensuring alignment with national frameworks such as the Australian Curriculum: Technologies and the Digital Literacy general capabilities.

Crucially, the study identified the socio-demographic factors of age and sex as predictors of digital readiness. This has significant implications for equity within teacher education programmes. Mature-age PSTs showed higher readiness in workplace-associated tools, while school-leavers were more confident in classroom applications. The persistent gender-based confidence gap in technical areas reinforces the need for inclusive pedagogical strategies that challenge stereotypes and scaffold diverse learners’ needs. The Rasch-validated self-audit tool demonstrated clear utility not only for PST self-reflection but also for institutional self-evaluation. Its deployment can support evidence-based programme improvement, track progression over time, and guide targeted intervention.

In a rapidly evolving educational landscape, institutions must move beyond assumptions about generational digital fluency and instead adopt structured evaluation frameworks. By doing so, they will be better positioned to prepare all PSTs for digitally enabled teaching, to contribute to broader educational equity, and meet the standards of effective teaching in the 21 st century. While based in ITE, the methodological approach, especially the integration of self-assessment with Rasch modelling, holds promise for wider application in tertiary education. This framework offers institutions a way to gather actionable data to inform curriculum priorities and support evidence-based programme enhancement.

**Fig. 6**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13948-8/figures/6)

Sex, age group, and highest qualification as significant predictors of confidence level (in logits) for specific survey items, shown here in the coefficient forest plot on the left with their associated component and item label on the right. Error bars are 95% confidence intervals. *Notes*: The coloured shapes illustrate the proportional impact of each covariate on the PSTs’ confidence level (coefficient estimate). The horizontal lines indicate the 95% confidence intervals for one or more significant predictors for each item, which is shown in the figure with its corresponding component label. The vertical dashed line at coefficient zero denotes the *line of no effect*; the predictors are presented with confidence intervals that do not cross the vertical line at coefficient = 0. Predictors to the left of the vertical dotted line are the reference categories; they represent significant responses from males (blue), mature-aged (yellow) PSTs and those, for one item only, who have a bachelor’s degree (green) as their highest qualification. Predictors to the right of the vertical dotted line represent significant responses from females (red), and school-leavers (purple). Higher co-efficient values in the negative direction represent higher confidence levels for the related predictors on the left and higher co-efficient values in the positive direction represent higher confidence levels for the related predictors on the right side of the line of no effect. For instance, for the item, *I can zip and unzip files on the computer* (General Computer Skills component), confidence levels were higher for male PSTs and for mature-aged PSTs, with a slightly stronger effect for the age group variable
