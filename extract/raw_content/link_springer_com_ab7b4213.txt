Abstract
--------

Role-play tasks have long been used by researchers and practitioners to observe L2 (Second language) speaking performance. This social-situated simulation allows students to employ their language skills to converse about real-life themes. While role-plays are highly plausible to actively engage students in interactive learning environments, it has been challenging to determine whether students perform at an adequate level of speaking proficiency with an appropriate learning approach. Nevertheless, the emerging technology-supported role-play tasks employing AI (artificial intelligence) could enhance competencies, enabling students to perform better in role-plays. Therefore, to enhance and support students’ performance in role-plays, English speaking skills, and their interaction mindset, we integrated an AI-based speaking learning app in the Generalization, Formulation, Correction, Appreciation (AI-GFCA) learning model. A quasi-experiment was conducted in a university with a total of 45 students. One class was randomly assigned to apply the AI-GFCA learning model as the experimental group, while the other was the control group (AI-C). The findings indicated that the AI-GFCA learning model could significantly enhance students’ role-play performance, English speaking skills, and interaction mindset. Furthermore, students produced fewer L2 errors and perceived a better learning experience than the AI-C group. It is noted that with the support of AI learning speaking through role-play tasks, students received sufficient corrective feedback, which encouraged them to establish a positive and motivating learning interaction, thus benefiting their academic performance.

Introduction
------------

Communicating in a second (L2) or foreign language is increasing globally. Role-play is widely accepted as a method for learning communication across domains (Lin et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR28)). Stokoe ([2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR45)) argued that training people to communicate relies on role-playing in simulated real-life situations. In language learning assessment, role-play enables practitioners and researchers to generate students’ interaction while at the same time affording control measurement over that interaction (Kasper & Youn, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR21)). High-stakes assessments in language learning, such as TOEIC (the Test of English for International Communication), are delivered in the form of oral tests that closely examine spoken language ability. A role-play component is often included in the interview to elicit speaking opportunities targeting a certain proficiency level. Consequently, there is a practical need for L2 learners to successfully participate in the role-play task as it assesses whether they possess an adequate and necessary L2 speaking ability through scaled-level descriptions such as rubrics that can describe learners’ language skill performance.

Although role-play pertains to social action-in-interaction between L2 learners, the ability of role-plays to "simulate the talk" has been criticized, with the claim that they produce inauthentic and unnatural dialogue (see Al-Gahtani & Roever, [2012](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR2)). The authenticity of using English as a second language (ESL) during role-play is a concern due to the aim of accomplishing the role-play as a tangible form of social practice. Prioritizing role-plays to create opportunities for communicative interaction between L2 learners requires feedback to give language input and an overview of what learners have learned and applied to support their learning performance (Shinogaya, [2012](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR44)). During role-play interactions, learners are exposed to L2 errors and have opportunities to provide corrective feedback to each other (Sato & McDonough, [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR42)). The provision of peer corrective feedback is pedagogically important to overcome the concern of the role-play task’s inauthenticity. However, supposing that the provision of corrective feedback is encouraged, learners may hesitate to provide corrective feedback due to their low ability to correct (Philp et al., [2010](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR38)), the salience of the L2 errors, and their peer relationships (Xu et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR54)).

In correlation with the learners’ challenges in corrective feedback provision, the rapid development of emerging technology-assisted language learning environments such as artificial intelligence (AI) might offer broader opportunities to enhance L2 learners’ proficiency. Hwang et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR4)) stated that AI technologies have been increasingly used in the field of language learning to stimulate learners’ knowledge and provide personalized support. For instance, automated writing feedback supported by AI could improve L2 learners’ writing skills and their self-regulated learning (Liu et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR5)), and a spoken dialogue system could be an effective way to practice English speaking and generally elicit learners’ performance (Timpe-Laughlin et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR49)).

Despite the great potential of AI technologies in the field of L2 learning, none of the prior studies, to the best of our knowledge, have integrated AI technologies with effective learning strategies to provide proper scaffolding stages as opportunities for corrective feedback provision and reception. In particular, for role-play tasks, prior studies have argued that corrective feedback facilitates learners’ state of cognitive comparison of what they currently know and what the acceptable form is in English language speaking (Loewen & Sato, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR30)). However, corrective feedback is only effective if learners are instructed to notice their L2 errors. Their inability to notice their L2 errors may be due to their primary focus being on the meaning of the role-play dialogue. Consequently, they may not notice the L2 errors that their peers produce, leading to the scarcity of peer corrective feedback during the learning process (Bryfonski & Sanz, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR6); Peace, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR36)). Thus, it is necessary to employ effective scaffolding stages to empower multiple affective and social factors, such as confidence in L2 learners’ feedback accuracy and perceived positive appraisal, which affect their social impact through feedback (Philp et al., [2010](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR38)). AI technologies might be an effective tool to aid students with real-time and accurate feedback, allowing L2 learners to gain confidence and fostering interaction among peers.

Researchers have introduced learning strategies as scaffolding processes to enhance learners’ peer feedback. For example, O’Donnell and Topping ([1998](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR34)) proposed objective and criteria stages to ensure clear expectations from peer feedback. They argued that learners need to generalize the expectation to maintain their focus to achieve their learning goals. This argument justifies our decision to include a "generalization" stage in the scaffolding process of the present study. It was expected that when "generalization" occurs, students would be encouraged to work collaboratively due to peer feedback provision depending on the learners’ prior knowledge, which varies from one student to another. Meanwhile, technology-assisted collaborative learning could help students reduce their cognitive load and actively participate in the peer feedback process. Integrating technology and collaboration into the learning task enables students to make plans to complete the task, and hence could enhance their readiness for collaborative tasks (Guo & Stevens, [2011](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR18)); therefore, a "formulation” stage is included in our approach. Following that, learners are instructed to conduct corrective feedback, implying the need to include a "correction" stage. As indicated by Storch ([2008](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR46)), the provision and reception of corrective feedback could lead learners to higher learning engagement. In addition, prior studies mentioned the significant hesitation of learners to provide peer corrective feedback, implying the need to foster a safe and friendly learning environment in which learners receive a positive appraisal (Alemdag & Yildirim, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR1)); therefore, an "appreciation" stage is included in this study to strengthen peer relationships.

Accordingly, the current study integrated an AI-based speaking app to support L2 learners in their role-play by proposing the "Generalization, Formulation, Correction, and Appreciation" (AI-GFCA) model to enhance role-play performance, English speaking skills, and an interaction mindset. The latter key term was included because the interaction mindset can predict the occurrence of feedback in the role-play task, an indicates a disposition towards the role-play task during the interaction. Moreover, the model was proposed to reduce learners’ L2 errors and increase their L2 error reporting.

Literature review
-----------------

### Technology-supported role-play task

Role-play is argued to be an effective method to create socially situated interactions through an authentic target language environment in the language learning context. Conducting role-play in the learning process aligns with Dewey’s "learning by doing" theory (Dewey, [1923](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR9)), which advocated learners’ opportunities to acquire learning through real-life practice. In other words, involvement in role-play practice could integrate learners’ language skills into the actual context. More likely, critical and creative thinking occurs in collaborative role-play tasks, and cultivates learners’ collaboration and interaction skills through communication (Liu & Carless, [2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR29)).

Researchers designing role-play practice have paid attention to designing stimulus learning activities with the support of technologies (see Cho et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR7); Manabe et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR32); Shek et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR43); Timpe-Laughlin et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR49)). Recently, several technologies have been widely applied to support learners in role-playing. For instance, Cho et al. ([2015](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR7)) employed the pre-service teachers’ affordance of virtual reality (VR); in their study, it was found that the learners’ positive beliefs about technology-supported role-playing significantly influenced their academic performance. Besides students’ age and epistemological beliefs, beliefs about the nature of knowledge and the knowing process were argued to influence their affective behaviors when using VR. Therefore, students’ individual differences should be considered when choosing and designing technology-assisted learning. Later, Manabe et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR32)) employed mobile-assisted learning platforms to provide learners with an authentic English language environment, revealing that the technology integration significantly improved their learning behavior (e.g., annotation, using text-to-speech and translation function) and learning achievement (English language speaking skill) in English role-plays. Although some technologies show potential benefits for learners, providing learners with opportunities to practice speaking and receive feedback, not to mention the linguistic and language function in the oral role-play task, the proposed systems might be limited as they take time and consume resources. For example, in VR-supported role-play, users, or learners in this case, interact with virtual peers, as it was designed to be a closed role-play or so-called "oral discourse completion task" (e.g., Félix-Brasdefer, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR12)). In a closed role-play task, learners are involved in a limited real-life situation with minimal interaction. On the contrary, open role-play is predominantly used in conventional role-play tasks, or so-called "impromptu" role-play, where learners can communicate in an unscripted way and achieve their goals based on their interests (see Huth, [2010](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR20)). Even though the open role-play task allows learners to engage in impromptu planning, it may not be suitable for L2 learners with limited knowledge of the target language. Thus, the current study used semi-scripted role-plays, which combined technology-supported closed role-play practice with the flexibility of open role-play. We employed AI to support authentic interaction and spontaneous communication, whereas students’ collaborative role-play tasks facilitated certain moves in various real-life dialogues, which are often unpredictable.

### The AI-based dialogue system

The rapid development and availability of emerging technologies such as AI have enabled practitioners to conduct language learning innovations (Lin et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR27)). In L2 learning, some automated human–machine interactions have been employed to improve L2 communicative performance. For instance, Sydorenko et al. ([2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR48)) employed automatic speech recognition (ASR) to allow students to have different conversation pathways, and Ockey and Chukharev-Hudilainen ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR35)) built a spoken dialogue system (SDS) that performed natural language processing (NLP) to evaluate paired oral tasks based on oral communication rating scales.

In terms of providing feedback to learners, few studies have reported how an AI-based dialogue system could offer dialogue practice and non-formulaic forms (e.g., pronunciation and spelling) as feedback to L2 learners (Timpe-Laughlin et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR49); van Doremalen et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR50)). They argued that AI-based dialogue systems greatly benefit L2 learners’ speaking practice and low-stakes assessment. In particular, L2 learners could value the systems’ oral practice and real-time feedback due to their authenticity which allows learners to mimic real-life interactions. For example, van Doremalen et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR50)) investigated an AI-based dialogue system to provide oral practice and speaking performance feedback to Dutch L2 learners. It was found that both teachers and learners positively perceived the human–machine interaction through the AI-based dialogue system, which has the potential to help L2 learners improve their oral proficiency. However, we should notice that teacher preferences and L2 learners’ needs might differ from one another. The content of an AI-based system is a crucial factor that somehow varies the users’ opinions (e.g., the dialogue topic, the usage of the target language, and required lexical use). By way of examples, the deployment of an AI-based system might be limited to fitting the pedagogical needs of L2 learners. Thus, it is important to take into account how and when the AI-based dialogue system can be employed to address L2 learners’ errors naturally when designing teaching plans.

Along the lines above, there is no one-size-fits-all AI-based system to aid L2 learners in improving their oral proficiency. Therefore, the current study referred to previous studies that recommended incorporating the AI-based dialogue system into effective learning scaffolding. For example, instead of entirely relying on the AI-based dialogue system to practice the role-play task and give feedback, we designed intergroup corrective feedback to guide students to clarify their L2 errors from peers’ perspectives (internal monitoring) as well as getting real-time feedback regarding their speaking quality from the AI-based system.

### Corrective feedback in the scaffolding

In terms of second language acquisition, corrective feedback has been argued to be beneficial and necessary for the development of L2 learners (Ellis, [2017](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR11); Lyster et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR31); Nassaji, [2012](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR33)). To some extent, Reynold and Teng ([2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR39)) found the plausibility of a situational native-speaking environment for L2 learners to speak in which the provided corrective feedback can be more effective in terms of improving grammatical accuracy. For instance, involving native speakers in the conversation or technology tool allows L2 learners to receive corrective feedback from native speakers.

Previous studies have examined the frequency and patterns of corrective feedback in various L2 teaching and learning contexts (e.g., Gholami, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR16); Ha, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR19); Ko, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR22); Reynold & Teng, [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR39); Sato & McDonough, [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR42)). Gholami ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR16)) and Ha ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR19)), for example, conveyed that teachers’ corrective feedback provision is still needed to constitute a reaction and to draw attention to the students’ initial utterance in the corrective feedback. In contrast, Ko ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR22)) believed that technology tools such as personalized correction systems have significantly benefited learners’ autonomy development due to the corrective feedback. Nevertheless, it is notable that previous studies indicated that L2 learners provided and conducted corrective feedback with limited scaffolding stages and primarily relied upon their prior knowledge of the target language. However, many of these studies did not include various learning strategies that allowed students to engage in collaborative higher-level thinking. In this matter, a meaningful scaffolding stage is a sine qua non for achieving L2 development in meaning-making and linguistic accuracy.

In this study, the perceptual monitoring theory unveiled the meaningful scaffolding to apply the nature of peer corrective feedback (Levelt, [1983](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR26)). This theory argued that a speaker could notice self-errors either before or after producing them due to the utterance being heard by the speaker through a particular speech-comprehension system. Related to peer corrective feedback, monitoring errors can be triggered by an external factor. Kormos ([2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR23)) added that this application could be used in the L2 acquisition process where checking target language production occurs internally and externally, leading to a noticing gap between L2 learners and the automatized-correction system.

Sato ([2017](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR41)) drew on the perceptual theory of monitoring with the L2 acquisition scenario to interpret the findings of corrective feedback. The drawing proposed that the corrective feedback serves dual functions, internal and external monitoring, to benefit the receivers and providers of L2 development. As depicted in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#Fig1), we modified the drawing by aiming to aid L2 learners in role-play tasks supported by the AI-based speaking learning app. In order to make corrective feedback happen, (a) the L2 learners needed to detect L2 errors made by their peers in the role-play performance. By receiving the detection results, it is expected that the learners would be encouraged to participate in intergroup communication and discussion. By doing so, (b) the L2 learners must notice whether their peers employed the target language correctly or produced L2 errors as the gap between their actual production and the target language production. (c) During the intergroup discussion process, each L2 learner might compare the errors they found themselves and their peers’ findings, which would help them correct the errors internally, and they may possibly detect other errors they missed due to their limitations in the target language. Furthermore, (d) with the help of the AI-based speaking learning app, L2 learners could clarify their peers’ errors and push to contribute to the intergroup discussion as a consolidation of external monitoring. Ultimately, these cognitive processes contribute to the L2 knowledge reconstruction, triggering L2 learners to notice the errors and modify the original utterance for the next and better role-play tasks. Along the same lines, the present study not only intended that students provide and receive corrective feedback with the support of the AI-based speaking learning app, but also encouraged students to analyze and create role-play performances that would enable them to effectively communicate meaning and improve their linguistic skills through the scaffolding stages.

A dual model of corrective feedback proposed by Sato ([2017](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR41))

To examine the effects of the AI-GFCA learning model, the current study modified and set the model as the framework. Moreover, while there are few studies on AI-enhanced language learning, even fewer have examined the integration of AI-supported English role-play tasks with the pedagogical theory. This study therefore critically explored the proposed learning environment (AI-GFCA) and the AI-supported conventional learning (AI-C) in which the AI was not integrated with the pedagogical theory. We aimed to discuss the effects of both learning models on students’ role-play performance, English speaking skills, noticing of L2 errors, interaction mindset, and learning experiences through the proposed learning environment. Thus, we proposed the following research questions:

1. (1)
   
   Do students in the AI-GFCA group perform better in the role-play than those in the AI-supported conventional learning (AI-C) group?
2. (2)
   
   Do students in the AI-GFCA group perform better in English speaking skills than those in the AI-supported conventional learning (AI-C) group?
3. (3)
   
   Do students in the AI-GFCA group produce fewer L2 errors compared to students in the AI-supported conventional learning (AI-C) group?
   
   1. a)
      
      Which L2 errors in the role-play task are the most reported by students in their corrective feedback?
4. (4)
   
   Do students in the AI-GFCA group have a higher interaction mindset than those in the AI-supported conventional learning (AI-C) group?
5. (5)
   
   What are students’ learning experiences in the AI-GFCA and AI-supported conventional learning (AI-C) groups?

AI-supported GFCA learning environment
--------------------------------------

The present study proposed the GFCA learning model with the support of AI-speaking learning to promote students’ task engagement and performance, sense of responsibility and respectability, and cooperative learning through peer corrective feedback in the role-play task. This task-based language learning encourages students to manage interaction and perform the task collaboratively, thereby developing students’ engagement and self-esteem to achieve the goal (Ellis, [2009](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR10)). As shown in Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#Fig2), the study employed generalization, formulation, correction, and appreciation strategies by integrating AI speaking learning by proposing the AI-GFCA learning model to scaffold students’ performance in role-play tasks, English speaking skills, L2 errors, and interaction mindset.

**Fig. 2**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6/figures/2)

The AI-supported GFCA learning model

The learning instruction starts with the generalization stage. Students are expected to comprehend the task goals and success criteria in this stage. For instance, in this study, providing descriptions of the role-play tasks in the target language (i.e., English) aims to remind the students that they need to use the target language while performing the role-play. The generalization stage is derived from O’Donnell and Topping ([1998](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR34)), who proposed objectives and criteria to clarify the assessment expectation. Also, the students’ concerns are essential as they are encouraged to be involved in the development and elaboration process through the generalization stage. In the second stage, the formulation is proposed to stimulate students’ desire to be involved in the collaborative learning process in which social interaction precedes the cognitive development in the previous stage. This stage is based on Guo and Stevens ([2011](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR18)) who implied that the awareness of using technology-assisted collaborative group tasks can enhance students’ readiness to participate in and take responsibility for their own learning processes. In the current study, formulating their tactics and strategies within group learning ensures that each student has a specific role to play in completing the group’s assignment; the role-play performance becomes the primary goal of this stage. In the third stage, a correction is proposed by conducting the intergroup corrective feedback, which similarly regards the engagement in the language task-based interaction (Philp & Duchesne, [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR37)). Storch ([2008](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR46)) referred to the correction stage and found that elaborating engagement is essential as students and their peers could instantly deliberate and discuss language items and acknowledge the suggestions through correction activities. As peer corrective feedback is one of the current study’s primary purposes, the repairing correction issue is significant for achieving the expected learning goals. Rolin-Ianziti ([2010](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR40)) suggested implementing systematic organization in language correction, including turn-taking correction between teachers, students, and peers. In the current study, internal and external monitoring of students’ role-play performance is conducted during the correction stage where the corrective feedback task is the main goal while monitoring students’ role-play performance. In the fourth stage, appreciation is proposed to praise and discuss peers’ role-play performance. This stage aims to foster a safe and comfortable learning environment where students can give and receive positive appraisals from their peers to reduce tension (Alemdag & Yildirim, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR1)). Each student is expected to provide positive comments on peers’ role-play performance.

Experimental design
-------------------

We investigated the effects of the AI-supported GFCA learning model by collecting quantitative and qualitative data to examine students’ role-play performance, English language speaking skills, students’ production in L2 errors and how they reported their L2 errors, students’ interaction mindset, and their learning experience in the proposed learning environment.

### Participants

A total of 45 first-year university students in Taiwan participated in this quasi-experimental study. They were from two required EFL (English as a Foreign language) classes offered for students with a range of A1-B1 levels of English proficiency based on the Common European Framework of Reference for Language (CEFR). Each student was enrolled in these two classes since the early academic term. The two classes were then chosen randomly to be treated as either the AI-GFCA group (the experimental group) or the AI-C group (the control group). The AI-GFCA group consisted of 23 students, while the AI-C group had 22 students. They were between 18 and 20 years old. Their first languages (L1) were Chinese Mandarin (73.3%), Vietnamese (8.9%), Chinese Cantonese (6.7%), Thai (6.7%), and Korean (4.4%). The two classes were taught by the same instructors (*N* = 2) who had been teaching EFL for at least 10 years.

### Experimental procedure

The two classes were taught for 100 min each week. As shown in Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#Fig3), the first week began by introducing the proposed learning environment and the corrective feedback initial training to ensure that students had equivalent knowledge to follow either the AI-GFCA or AI-C learning environment and the competence to use the learning tools. Students were guided to construct their cognitive understanding of the role-play activities and each type of corrective feedback. Moreover, we also introduced the AI-based speaking app (<https://elsaspeak.com/>) to the students, including its learning features and learning tasks such as a multi-turn spoken dialogue system (SDS), pronunciation checker, and fluency reporting (see Fig. [4](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#Fig4)).

In the following 3 weeks, students were assigned to form small groups (3 to 4 people) to perform the first role-play task (T1). A situational dialogue in the tourist information center was chosen as the role-play topic in T1. Some target language, such as giving directions and making recommendations, was set to be the standard of the student role-plays. Their role-play performances were recorded and launched in the e-formative assessment platform (<https://app.formative.com/>). Students conducted their reports and feedback through the platform. The learning stages were re-assigned for students to perform the second role-play task (T2) in the next 3 weeks. Students were instructed to perform situational dialogue at a traditional English restaurant, the role-play topic in T2, by setting various characters as customers and using different politeness expressions. Once they had completed the learning activities and the role-play tasks, a survey of interaction mindset was filled out online, and an English-speaking assessment through the AI-based speaking learning app was taken in the 8th week. Students in the AI-C group followed the steps of the AI-GFCA group in the 1st week and the last 2 weeks. However, to compile their role-play performance tasks (T1 and T2), students in the AI-C group only followed the "correction" stage by reporting their own group’s L2 errors.

The interview session was conducted in the 9th week to understand students’ experiences and perceptions of the learning environments. A total of 10 students from the AI-GFCA group (*n* = 5) and the AI-C group (*n* = 5) were willing to participate in the interviews. To understand their perceptions and experiences, we invited a few translators with the same L1 as the students (e.g., Chinese Mandarin, Thai, and Vietnamese) to help with the interviews.

As for further detailed procedures, we provided scenes and descriptions in each stage. First is the generalization stage. As seen in Fig. [5](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#Fig5), students were given role-play scenarios and characters, including the target language, in each group. The rubric criteria gave them a clear expectation of the learning goals. Students discussed and assigned roles to each member while checking the language items in the target language through the AI-based speaking learning app.

Second, is the formulation stage. As seen in Fig. [6](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#Fig6), students set their own goals in the role-play task by being involved in scriptwriting based on specific communication skills. The script was used to practice their role-play task. Also, students were encouraged to employ the AI-based speaking learning app autonomously to check their speaking fluency, intonation, and pronunciation. Their role-play performance was recorded for the delayed correction.

Each group was assigned to give corrective feedback on another group’s performance. Each group received the notices and used them as reflection which enabled them to improve their performance for the next role-play task.

### Instruments

This study used measuring tools which comprised the post-test of role-playing performance and English speaking assessment, students’ L2 errors (by experts and students), the post-survey of interaction mindset, and interview questions.

The post-test of role-play performance was derived from the scoring rubrics of role-play performance. Two experienced EFL teachers designed the rubrics by referring to Youn ([2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR56)). The rubrics have five dimensions (presentation, role-play character, achievement of purpose, use of non-verbal cues, and imagination and creativity) with a 4-point score (4 = *excellent*; 1 = *limited*). Students’ role-play task videos were watched and evaluated by the two experts. The Kendall’s ω of the grading of the five dimensions was 0.86, 0.83, 0.91, 0.83, and 0.87 (*p* < 0.001), respectively. This indicated that the grading of the two experts was significantly correlated. Thus, the experts’ grading was the rating average of role-play performance for each student (see Table [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#Tab1)).

**Table 1 Scoring rubrics for role-play performance**

[Full size table](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6/tables/1)

The post-test of English speaking assessment was collected from the students’ log profile in the AI speaking learning app. It consisted of 15 sentences which had to be read and were automatically recorded by the platform. The AI speaking learning app reported scores from four assessed variables: pronunciation, intonation, fluency, and IELTS (International English Language Testing System) speaking predictor (see Fig. [9](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#Fig9)).

**Fig. 9**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6/figures/9)

A sample scene and students’ log profile in the AI-based speaking learning app

L2 errors were identified from students’ role-play tasks (at T1 and T2). Two EFL teachers met and watched students’ performances. Upon discussion of the findings, three L2 errors were mainly identified and correlated to the general issues of Asian L2 students. First, the morpho-syntactic error (ME) was selected since grammatical structures are predominantly challenging for L2 students to acquire (see Gass et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR15); Lee, [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR24)). For instance, it is argued that L1 Chinese speakers are not sensitive to plural *–s* and so such errors in plurality commonly occur. Second, the lexical error (LE) was selected due to the high-frequency band in the Corpus of Contemporary American English (COCA) and remained challenging for Asian L2 learners to acquire, such as nouns. Noun usage is not clearly specified by Asian L2 learners, leading to different meanings in English (Wu & Garza, [2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR53)). The phonological error (PE) was selected due to its L1-specific developmental pattern. For instance, vowel and consonant errors are frequently made by L2 learners; examples include errors in unstressed syllables (e.g., *catch air → catcher*) or in stressed syllables (e.g., *butter → better*).

In addition to the L2 error analysis, we extended our analysis by collecting students’ reports of the L2 errors from their role-play tasks. We aimed to examine students’ noticing frequency of each type of L2 error. We followed the previous discussion by compiling ME, LE, and PE that they reported through an online formative assessment platform.

The questionnaire on interaction mindset was adapted based on a study by Sato and McDonough ([2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR42)). A total of 15 items were comprised of five variances: peer interaction (4 items; α = 0.69), collaboration (4 items; α = 0.68), form of orientation (3 items; α = 0.66), provision of corrective feedback (3 items; α = 0.63), and reception of corrective feedback (3 items; α = 0.63). Each item used a 6-point Likert scale from 1 (*strongly disagree*) to 6 (*strongly agree*).

For the interview analysis, we slightly modified seven questions from Hwang et al. ([2009](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR3)) to ensure that all questions followed the context of this study. For example, in our first question, we asked, "How was performing role-play with this learning strategy different from what you have experienced before?" and in our third question, we asked, "What did you gain the most from this learning strategy? Which stages did you like the most?". The initial questions were translated and asked by the translators. All interview sessions were recorded and translated back into English to be transcribed. We employed ATLAS.ti 9, computer-assisted qualitative data analysis software (CAQDAS) for the coding process, and used grounded theory to identify themes (Creswell, [2013](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR57)). We started by coding both interview recordings and transcriptions. We examined each code to ensure that all coding reached our consensus. We then grouped the codes into code groups we used as the themes. We ran the code-document table analysis and counted coding in absolute frequencies, which resulted in the groundedness of each code. The coded quotations were used to demonstrate the number of occurrences in the interviews.

Results
-------

### Role-play performance

Regarding grading students’ role-play performance, we employed the Mann–Whitney U test, a non-parametric statistical analysis, to examine the result due to the small sample size of this study. Besides, the Shapiro–Wilk test shows that our sample had non-normal distribution.

Table [2](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#Tab2) shows the result of the Mann–Whitney test which indicated that the AI-GFCA group (Mdn = 3.6) performed better in the first role-play task (T1) than the AI-C group (Mdn = 3.4) with *U* = 139.50, *z* = − 2.622, *p* = 0.009, and *r* = 0.546. Also, the AI-GFCA group (Mdn = 3.8) outperformed the AI-C group (Mdn = 3.3) in the second role-play task (T2) with *U* = 142.00, *z* = − 2.559, *p* = 0.011, and *r* = 0.545. This finding implies that in role-play performance, students who adopted the AI-supported GFCA learning environment performed better than those who adopted the AI-supported C learning environment.

### English-speaking skills

We applied the Mann–Whitney U test analysis to the post-test scores on students’ English speaking assessment. Similar to the previous variable, we chose non-parametric statistical analysis as we found that our sample size was small and the Shapiro–Wilk test resulted in a non-normal distribution.

As shown in Table [3](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#Tab3), students in the AI-GFCA group (Mdn = 63) had better pronunciation scores than students in the AI-C group (Mdn = 55) with *U* = 168.00, *z* =  − 1.934, *p* = 0.05, and *r* = 0.28. In terms of fluency skill, the AI-GFCA group (Mdn = 66) had better fluency scores than the AI-C group (Mdn = 53.5) with *U* = 138.00, *z* =  − 2.613, *p* = 0.009, and *r* = 0.39. Moreover, when the AI speaking learning app showed the result of IELTS speaking predictor scores, students in the AI-GFCA group (Mdn = 5.5), again, outperformed students in the AI-C group (Mdn = 4.5) with *U* = 142.50, *z* =  − 2.566, *p* = 0.01, and *r* = 0.38. Thus, it can be concluded that students who adopted the AI-supported GFCA learning environment had better pronunciation and fluency skills and achieved higher scores in the IELTS speaking predictor than those who adopted the AI-C learning environment.

Regarding the T2 result, students in the AI-GFCA group (Mdn = 1, 2) produced fewer morpho-syntactic errors and lexical errors compared to students in the AI-C group (Mdn = 0, 1) with *U* = 145.00, *z* =  − 2.569, *p* = 0.01, and *r* = 0.38, and *U* = 158.00, *z* =  − 2.396, *p* = 0.017, and *r* = 0.35, respectively. We further explored the overall number of students’ reports on their L2 errors. We employed the Friedman test, the non-parametric analysis, as an alternative to the parametric version of repeated measures analysis.

Overall, students’ reports showed a significant difference depending on which L2 errors were reported as corrective feedback to either their own group or other groups with X2(2) = 41.847, *p* = 0.000. For the post-hoc analysis with the Wilcoxon signed-rank test, we applied the Bonferroni adjustment, resulting in the alpha level of 0.017 (0.05/3). We sought this level of significance as the threshold. The median perceived L2 errors report level for morpho-syntactic, lexical, and phonological errors was 2, 2, and 1, respectively. The Wilcoxon signed-rank test detected that students reported morpho-syntactic errors more than lexical errors (*Z* = − 4.752, *p*adjust = 0.000), morpho-syntactic errors more than phonological errors (*Z* = − 5.728, *p*adjust = 0.000), and lexical errors more than phonological errors (*Z* = − 2.861, *p*adjust = 0.004).

### Interview

As shown in Table [7](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#Tab7), both groups positively perceived the employment of the AI speaking learning app in the learning environment. The learning benefits most mentioned by students were the opportunities to practice their communicative speaking in the role-play tasks. The AI-GFCA group, in particular, expressed their perceptions and experiences predominantly in each theme we coded.

#### Providing autonomous learning opportunities

The positive attitude toward the AI speaking learning app was the most mentioned in this theme. S04114 mentioned, "The second benefit is self-learning opportunity; I had more time to practice my English speaking with the app and got real-time feedback." This benefit was strongly supported by S05164, who recommended the AI-based speaking learning app, "Most of us are beginners in English speaking. Using the application to practice our English speaking is important since our pronunciation is not as good as native speakers." Autonomous learning appeared in the students’ perceptions of the AI-based speaking learning app. S07208 shared the moment when the platform was needed, "I like the app the most because it gave me many models of conversation for when I had no idea what to say or how to respond to the English expression. It helped me prepare the conversation model for my role-play task." The AI-based speaking learning app allows students to choose the types of situational dialogues they want to practice—for example, a conversation at a job interview or meeting a new person in the neighborhood.

Despite the positive attitude shared by students, the quality of the AI-based speaking learning app was also discussed by both groups. For instance, S03198 reported, "The voice recognition is sometimes inaudible, so I have to record back my voice." S02232 added, "Some steps in the app are confusing, I sometimes do not know what to do." Although the AI-based speaking learning app was found to be irritating in a few classroom activities, students still praised its potential to provide feedback for their English speaking quality. S03197 conveyed, "It sometimes makes errors, but in terms of giving review/feedback for my English speaking, it is fine".

#### Improving English speaking skills

Both groups responded that employing the AI-based speaking learning app to support their role-play tasks was such an opportunity to practice their communicative speaking. S06184 mentioned, "Performing in the role-play benefited me the most because this activity let me talk in English confidently," while S08193 added, "In other classes (traditional L2 classroom), we normally do not speak English; instead, we only responded to our teachers’ questions. This class gave me the advantage of having more time to practice my English speaking, which helped me sharpen my English pronunciation." S03235 reported, "The class was alive. Unlike other English classes, we actively participated in the speaking activity".

In terms of being immersed in pronunciation practice, the AI speaking learning app assisted students in performing the nature of L1 speaking. S05163 revealed, "As we do not have too many opportunities to speak English, I enjoy using the app to practice my speaking naturally." S07202 extended, "If I do not know how to express my idea in English, I will also not know how to pronounce it. Therefore, the AI-based speaking learning app is useful for me to check my pronunciation." To some extent, students found the learning environment intriguing to sharpen their English listening. S05141 said, "Looking at every role-play performance is interesting and fun, and I can practice my listening and find many new words and phrases from that scenario".

#### Promoting peer corrective feedback and interaction

Since the AI-GFCA group completely adopted the four scaffold stages (generalization, formulation, correction, and appreciation), students in this group enjoyed their learning experience. S04122 expressed, "This is the first time I was encouraged to give feedback to others before and after my speaking task. In the past, I was extremely nervous when having oral performances in the English classroom. By doing this activity, I have less anxiety and get more interaction with other students." In further findings, students found that the role-play task group is a way to form positive interactions among them, particularly for foreign students who have limited ability in Chinese Mandarin. S04120 revealed, "As a foreign student, I do not talk too much with the local students. This speaking task helps me to interact with them and make some new friends." S05118 added, "I did not talk to them before, as I struggled to speak their language. With the group task I had, I have many opportunities to talk to them, which is great".

In contrast, students in the AI-C group experienced difficulties as they were only instructed to do the "correction" stage. S01228 expressed, "We are unsure how to give feedback to our own group because it probably was not good feedback." Another student, S051108 added, "Giving feedback to my own group is difficult because my English ability is poor, and they know that".

Although students perceived positive encouragement to give and get peer corrective feedback, some students preferred providing immediate feedback instead of delayed feedback. S04181 stated, "Instead of watching the recording performance again, I think it might be better to give them corrective feedback while my classmates were performing their role-play tasks." On the other hand, several students expressed their learning enjoyment in the proposed learning environment by sharing their valuable experiences. S04157 shared, "When the instructor divided us to form the role-play task groups, I felt a little worried since we were not close enough, but then our tasks were executed smoothly, and we are getting closer now." S05112 expressed, "I only hope we have more time to discuss while having peer corrective feedback. I think that moment is significant to identify our errors in real-life English speaking".

#### Clarifying the importance of role-play activities

Based on the two groups’ interview results, role-play activities were found effective in terms of sharpening students’ English speaking skills. S0122 confessed, "I think role-play is important to promote better English communication." S02245 added, "The best thing about role-play tasks is we can practice using uncommon words or phrases that we rarely use in daily conversation. For example, in the role-play job interview, we were targeted to use several professional English phrases." In another view, students were eager to do the role-play task as it offered them various English language activities such as English scriptwriting. S03226 noted, "I like the role-play task because I can make a role-play script, and we have a great time discussing and arranging our script together. It can improve my English language skills (not only speaking skills)." S01225 agreed by mentioning, "I think the most benefit is scriptwriting because I can produce various and more specific vocabulary for the real-life situation. Also, I can mark my actual emotion and intonation on the script which helps me during the role-play performance".

However, students expressed concern about the limited time given for role-play preparation. S06188 revealed, "If we had much more time to discuss and rehearse before performing our role-play tasks, our performance would be much better. A couple of hours was too short for me." S041101 suggested, "Two things need to be improved. One is the role-play preparation time. The time was a bit short to prepare our role-play’s content, and perhaps we need more time to discuss and revise our content".

Despite their concern in the role-play preparation time, students praised the learning environment which allowed them to reduce their language speaking anxiety, communication barriers, and difficulties in public speaking, as S02243 shared, "At first, I thought it would be difficult to perform an English role-play since we never trained to speak English for such a long period of learning time. However, as time went by, it became easier as we got corrective feedback and positive comments from others, not to mention that we can talk to each other now. Our difficulties were helped by the AI learning speaking, peers, and instructor" (Table [8](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#Tab8)).

Conclusion and Suggestions
--------------------------

Corrective feedback is pedagogically important for L2 learners in second language acquisition practice such as role-plays. Ellis ([2017](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR11)) argued that both practitioners and researchers should be concerned with whether correcting learners’ language production is necessary and, if so, at which stages and how the correction should be treated in the learning process. Accordingly, the AI-GFCA learning model was proposed and employed in the oral English courses at a university. The current study employed an AI-based speaking learning app integrated into the GFCA learning model to support and enhance students’ role-play performance, English speaking skills, L2 error reports, and interaction mindset. In addition, the AI-GFCA group supported L2 learners to produce fewer L2 errors compared to their peers in another group.

Regarding students’ role-play performance, the AI-GFCA group significantly outperformed the AI-C group at both measured times (T1 and T2). The findings indicated that the GFCA learning stages could enhance students’ overall role-play performance based on the scored criteria such as presentation, role-play character, achievement of purpose, use of non-verbal cues, and imagination and creativity. Also, it showed that students who conducted the role-play task in the generalization stage could clarify the learning expectations, which significantly benefited their learning goals (Guo & Stevens, [2011](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR18)). Our findings align with Sydorenko ([2015](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR47)), who found that role-play tasks promote creativity and pragmatic interaction, but often inappropriate language and content among peers. Thus, using the AI-based speaking learning app could support students’ role-play rehearsal which appeared to be naturalistic interaction.

As for English speaking skills, the AI-GFCA group outperformed the AI-C group in terms of pronunciation, fluency, and IELTS speaking predictor. However, the AI-GFCA group did not significantly differ from the AI-C group in intonation. This finding echoes the previous studies, which indicated that authentic role-play conversation with the aid of an AI-based speaking system could enhance students’ linguistic complexity, such as fluency and pronunciation (Floare Bora, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR13); Timpe-Laughlin et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR49)). Then, why did the students’ intonation in the AI-GFCA group not show significance compared to the AI-C group? A closer inspection of this issue might be related to the participants’ L1. It is generally claimed that Chinese Mandarin speakers have difficulties using appropriate English intonation. In a similar previous study, Jiang and Chun ([2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR58)) identified that although technology tools had been employed to train English intonation, the L1 Chinese Mandarin students continued to face challenges in tone choice which naturally pressed them to use many fillers, pause in the wrong position, produce choppy and flat speech, and not notice the words’ stress. Chinese Mandarin’s prosodic system is vastly different from that of English. Therefore, it is suggested to teach and train exclusively English intonation to L2 learners whose L1 prosodic systems are distinctive (Wong et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR52); Yengkimaleki & Van Heuven, [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR55)).

Regarding the students’ L2 errors, students in the AI-GFCA groups outperformed the AI-C group in reporting morpho-syntactic errors (ME) in both measured times (T1 and T2). Moreover, the AI-GFCA group performed better in reporting phonological errors (PE) in T1. However, there was no significant difference in T2; in contrast they outperformed the AI-C in lexical errors (LE) in T2 but there was no significant difference in T1. In further findings, students in both groups mainly reported ME compared to the LE and PE, where PE were the least L2 errors reported by students. Without referring to the previous research on L2 learners’ attitude in errors input, regarding the two findings, we presume two possible reasons. First, students in many countries or areas where English is taught as a foreign language are trained to focus on grammatical issues in the English language. This training is supported by a typical English language teaching method which adopts the conventional grammar-translation approach in the learning process.

Consequently, students tend to choose the grammar-structural issues, or ME, as their prerequisite for L2 language development. Yet, less attention is paid to issues related to lexis (LE) or phonology (PE). Second, ME was mainly reported based on the general development patterns, while LE and PE were reported based on the issues of L1-specific development. For example, the error of "I interesting …" (correct: I am interested …) was reported much more than an error of "The desert is …" (correct: The dessert is …) or "the thief" (pronounced as ‘thəf). In this matter, students might be more exposed to those L2 general development patterns than to those L1-specific errors in their English-speaking interaction, which led them to report fewer PE and LE than ME (Goldschneider & DeKeyser, [2005](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR17)).

In response to the finding of students’ interaction mindset, students in the AI-GFCA group outperformed students in the AI-C group regarding peer interaction, collaboration, form of interaction, and provision of peer corrective feedback. In contrast, the reception of peer corrective feedback did not show any significant difference between the two groups. With this finding in mind, it is indicated that the provision of peer feedback has the most significant effect size, which is highly related to the previous finding in the English language skills. According to prior studies, students’ provision of peer feedback is commonly influenced by cognitive factors, including students’ L2 proficiency (Dao & McDonough, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR8); Lesser, [2004](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR25); Watanabe & Swain, [2007](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR51)). For instance, Watanabe and Swain ([2007](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR51)) found that higher proficiency students could attend the form of interaction more frequently than their peers with lower proficiencies. It also greatly affected their peer interaction while conducting the collaborative oral task. In doing so, this finding supports the prior studies that peer feedback provision allows higher proficiency students to have more spaces devoted to language form. In other words, students in the AI-GFCA group, who performed better in the English language skills, gained more confidence, regardless of their language proficiency level, to provide feedback to peers. On the contrary, the possibility of a non-significant difference in the peer feedback reception was that undervaluing peers’ correction of their L2 errors might have been the reason for this issue. This finding is related to the interview result that some participants felt hesitant to correct their peers’ L2 errors due to their low ability to correct and the salience of L2 errors (Philp et al., [2010](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR38); Xu et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR54)).

Regarding the students’ learning perceptions and experiences, the two groups agreed that the AI-supported role-play task was a great opportunity to practice English communicative skills in authentic situations. For example, students enjoyed using AI-speaking learning for natural listening comprehension and immersion in pronunciation practice. This finding followed Timpe-Lauglin ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR49)), who found that students enjoyed interacting with an AI-based dialogue system before and after completing their human-to-human dialogue tasks. Similarly, we found that the students from two groups extended their positive perceptions of role-play activities. However, students in the AI-GFCA group shared that the proposed learning treatment could reduce their foreign language anxiety and maximize the role-play preparation time. This verified that the AI-GFCA learning model could bring more positive values for students to learn English language speaking through role-play practice, as a previous study reported on how guided role-play tasks revealed students’ L2 improvement in pronunciation accuracy and syntactic complexity in particular (Floare Bora, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR13)). The interview result also revealed another benefit of the AI-GFCA learning model: the autonomous learning opportunity. Students indicated that AI-speaking learning allowed them to practice speaking autonomously with real-time feedback. This finding implies that the students could draw the various types of English-speaking learning and practice to respond strategically to the upcoming role-play performance. It is a similar finding to Liu et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR5)), who reported that an AI-based English language learning system could potentially cultivate autonomous L2 learners. In the meantime, students positively perceived the peer corrective feedback in the AI-GFCA learning model as it highly promoted peer interaction and systematic instructional scaffolds. However, few students expressed concern about the peer feedback timing, which may have affected the peer interaction. For instance, immediate feedback, which refers to students receiving feedback immediately after performing role-play, or delayed feedback, which refers to giving feedback after completing some stages of the learning process, bring different effects to the students. Fu and Li ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10453-6#ref-CR14)) argued that the conceptualization of feedback timing could be found through the skill acquisition theory embedded in explicit instruction timing. Thus, feedback timing can be considered a limitation of this study.

Besides the limitation in the feedback timing, we have to point out that the sample size of this study was not large enough to conduct a more reliable statistical analysis, which might have allowed us to find whether the proposed learning environment could be suitable for role-play tasks emphasizing specific pragmatic features used by students. Furthermore, we did not proceed with the information related to how exactly the frequency of each type of L2 error occurred during the students’ performance. Also, there was insufficient investigation of the AI-generated feedback from each student’s log profile.

To sum up, the present study’s main contribution is to propose the AI-GFCA learning model in an authentic teaching context to show the effectiveness of this model for L2 learners. Based on the limitation, some future directions are provided as follows:

1. (1)
   
   The AI-GFCA learning model can further investigate whether immediate and delayed feedback can have different effects on L2 learners.
2. (2)
   
   Due to the small sample size, it is suggested that the proposed learning environment could be employed in the classroom where a large number of students participate in the role-play tasks.
3. (3)
   
   We encourage further studies to explore AI-generated feedback for the students to see how effectively the feedback supports students’ role-play performance.
4. (4)
   
   It is also suggested that studies explore the impact of this learning model in terms of students’ pragmatic features while performing their role-plays, for example, whether the proposed learning model could affect students’ interactional features such as the length of interaction and type of engagement tokens (i.e., acknowledgment, confirmation, and making a suggestion) produced during role-play tasks.
5. (5)
   
   Future studies might also investigate the number of various types of L2 errors produced by L2 learners to better understand how this proposed learning environment might affect each type of L2 error differently.
