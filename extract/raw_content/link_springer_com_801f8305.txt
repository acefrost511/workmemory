Abstract
--------

Traditional manual essay grading methods struggle to evaluate student writing quickly and impartially. Automated essay scoring (AES) systems use NLP to assess text, yet overlook behavioral context and lack fairness in real-time feedback. The research presents DeepGrade, an IoT-enhanced automated essay scoring system for context-aware English essay grading based on deep learning. Score accuracy and bias can be enhanced by combining semantic text interpretation with real-time behavioral information, such as keystroke dynamics and writing behaviors. A transformer-based language model (BERT) encodes essay content, and LSTM-based spatiotemporal encoders handle IoT-derived data in the hybrid method. These two feature streams are combined and scored by a fairness-aware scoring brain to produce holistic, rubric-aligned scores. In experiments using the ASAP 2.0 dataset and synthetically produced IoT-like behavioral traits to simulate writing interactions, the researchers achieve a QWK of 0.89. Though intriguing, these results are proof-of-concept and need real-world behavioral data validation for adoption. Score bias across demographic categories is reduced by 9.6% using the model. Adding IoT behavior boosts rubric-level scoring reliability and prediction precision by 12%. Economically disadvantaged groups’ score variance drops 15.4% due to a reduction in fairness awareness. For accurate, fair, and real-time essay assessment in smart educational contexts, DeepGrade: IoT is scalable and intelligent. This conceptual prototype shows feasibility, but full-scale deployment requires greater validation in real-world educational settings.

1 Introduction
--------------

Educational systems globally must evaluate student writing. Everybody agrees that essays are the best approach to test students’ analytical thinking, vocabulary, and comprehension of complex ideas [[1](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR1)]. However, marking essays is difficult, especially in big educational settings, because writing is subjective and delivering timely, accurate, and consistent comments takes time [[2](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR2)]. In a multilingual classroom with fewer resources and more kids than teachers, it’s harder to provide each child the attention they require [[3](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR3)].

Automated essay scoring (AES) methods can overcome these constraints [[4](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR4)]. Machine learning and NLP are used in these systems to analyze textual content against standards. Their scalable approach swiftly evaluates thousands of papers, reducing human effort [[5](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR5)]. Traditional AES models are effective, but they rarely assess students’ originality, effort, and coherence. Their focus on linguistic features ignores behavioral elements that may illuminate writing [[6](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR6)].

Modern deep learning advances, particularly BERT and other Transformer-based architectures, have improved textual understanding [[7](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR7)]. These frameworks improve AES system performance by providing contextually sensitive evaluations. However, they remain independent of real-time data about essay writing, not just content. Student performance can be better understood with behavioral data such as keystroke time, typing pauses, and revision frequency [[8](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR8)]. The rise of smart classrooms and the IoT makes such integration possible.

Computerized evaluations in education also have bias and unfairness issues. Scoring discrepancies often result from socio-economic status, race, and gender [[9](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR9)]. Traditional AES models may overlook these changes, biasing outcomes. To overcome these biases, a scoring system should consider demographic awareness, behavioral equity, and textual quality [[10](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR10)]. This transition requires a more solid, coordinated deep learning and IoT approach.

### 1.1 Defining the research problem

Although computerized essay scoring has improved, it still struggles to assess student writing appropriately. The majority of current models use static textual analysis, which ignores the fact that behavioral data can significantly affect written answer authenticity and quality [[11](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR11)]. These systems without population heterogeneity raise fairness concerns when employed on a wide scale. AES technologies cannot perform context-aware assessments in real time, which makes them less useful in innovative learning contexts. A scoring mechanism that integrates real-time behavioral data and strong linguistic understanding is critically needed for accuracy and fairness [[12](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR12)]. The research Objectives are:

* To improve autonomous essay scoring, integrate deep learning models with IoT behavioral data for greater dependability and insight.
* To reduce demographic prejudice in essay evaluation, fairness-aware algorithms should be implemented to account for student background and circumstances.
* To generate real-time rubric-aligned feedback, a hybrid architecture integrating writing behavior tracking and semantic analysis is being implemented.

### 1.2 Methodology overview

With a hybrid architecture that combines behavioral and semantic data, the suggested framework, DeepGrade: IoT, scores essays. A transformer-based language encoder (BERT) is the brains of the operation, processing the essay text to glean linguistically rich information [[13](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR13)]. At the same time, a temporal encoder built on Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks is used to interpret real-time behavioral data acquired by Internet of Things (IoT) devices, such as keystroke dynamics, writing speed, and revision frequency [[14](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR14)].

A combined representation layer combines the two streams and feeds them into a scoring module that considers fairness. The goal of this module’s adversarial loss function-based scoring is to reduce scoring variance across demographic groups [[15](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR15)]. The ASAP 2.0 dataset is used for training and validation purposes, with the addition of simulated IoT signals to create an environment similar to a real classroom. To measure performance, the research utilizes indexes of demographic fairness and the Quadratic Weighted Kappa (QWK) index. The final results are accurate and fair because they are both context-aware and matched with the rubric. It is made possible by the overall architecture. The Research Contributions are:

* To present Deepgrade: IoT, an innovative multimodal framework for essay evaluation that combines behavioral insights with deep linguistic encoding.
* To improve context-sensitive assessment and bolster adaptable learning environments through the real-time application of writing pattern monitoring based on the Internet of Things.
* To use a scoring mechanism that takes fairness into account so that there is less variation in predicted scores based on students’ demographics.
* To show that Deepgrade: IoT’s scoring accuracy has improved, with a QWK of 0.89 compared to 0.82 in the baseline models.
* To verify the system’s practical suitability for deployment by validating it on a large-scale benchmark dataset (ASAP 2.0) using realistic behavioral simulations.

2 Related work
--------------

### 2.1 Automated english essay scoring systems (AES)

Mizumoto et al. [[16](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR16)] used the GPT-3 (text-davinci-003) model for automated essay scoring to ensure trustworthy and consistent evaluations of non-native English writing. For the TOEFL11 corpus, 12,100 essays from 11 native language backgrounds were marked with competence levels (low, medium, high). GPT-3 aligned well with benchmark scores across proficiency levels, exhibiting moderate-to-strong correlation values (*r* = 0.72 to 0.85). It occasionally failed to detect sophisticated linguistic faults and context-specific semantics, which reduced the precision of scores for lower-proficiency writers. Transformer-based language models can augment standard AES systems, especially when combined with linguistic feature analysis; however, high-stakes assessments still require improvement.

Wang et al. [[17](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR17)] This work presents an essay scoring method that utilizes ConvNet, LSTM, attention processes, Doc2Vec, and nine manually collected linguistic features to capture the semantic, thematic, and structural properties of student writing. It utilizes ASAP and CLC-FCE datasets with varying essay lengths and complexities. The model enhanced ASAP QWK by 1.13 points, as well as CLC-FCE Pearson (0.005) and Spearman (0.0337) scores. The complex structure and multi-model integration limit processing time and interpretability; however, the model excels in terms of feature diversity and performance.

Stephen et al. [[18](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR18)] used a keyword-based Automated Essay Scoring (AES) algorithm to assess 359 nursing students (2016) and 40 students (2018) on four constructed-response exam items. The dataset included written answers rated by two humans and AES. AES outperformed human evaluators in speed and consistency. Predefined answer keywords improved accuracy and processing time. However, small sample size and item design issues, particularly in one question, highlighted the need for expanded validation across varied nursing cohorts.

Almusharraf et al. [[19](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR19)] compared the Grammarly AES algorithm to human raters on 197 EFL student essays for error identification and grading. Using Corder’s (1974) error analysis and Pearson correlation, AES identified more errors, and people scored higher. The research found a moderate connection. Grammarly provides timely and detailed feedback, but it over-identifies tiny errors and lacks contextual judgment. The findings suggest that AES tools can supplement human assessment, but they should be used cautiously in high-stakes evaluations to strike a balance between accuracy and fairness.

Pack et al. [[20](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR20)] Four prominent large language models (LLMs)—Google’s PaLM 2, Anthropic’s Claude 2, and OpenAI’s GPT-3.5 and GPT-4—were tested on 119 English placement test essays authored by language learners for automated essay scoring (AES). Each algorithm rated essays twice and was compared to human raters for reliability and validity. GPT-4 demonstrated the highest intrarater reliability and validity, whereas GPT-3.5 showed the least reliability. Most models improved; however, the interrater reliability of GPT-4 and GPT-3.5 deteriorated somewhat, indicating that performance variability remained a problem, especially in longitudinal and high-stakes assessment settings.

### 2.2 **Deep learning in essay scoring**

Bernius et al. [[21](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR21)] introduced CoFee, a machine learning-based framework for open-ended engineering problem-solving feedback and grading, in this research. Topic modeling, segment-based analysis, language modeling, and hierarchical clustering enable CoFee to identify comparable text segments and provide targeted feedback. The framework was tested utilizing 34 exercises from three years (2019–2021) at the Technical University of Munich, involving up to 2,200 students per semester. Results revealed 92% feedback acceptance precision and 85% grading effort savings. However, 45% feedback coverage and reliance on historical evaluation data limit generalizability and scalability.

Li et al. [[22](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR22)]A Bidirectional GRU with Attention Pooling (Bi-GRU\_AP) is used to create an IoT-based automatic English grading framework and compare it to Bi-GRU, GRU, and a Bi-GRU variation with self-attention. In experiments on Chinese and English essay corpora, Bi-GRU\_AP outperformed the nearest competitor by 1.3% on Chinese and 2.2% on English, improving approximately 19% over vanilla GRU. After 20 iterations, the model shows excellent convergence and stability, although its controlled datasets may not fully replicate real-world student writing.

Lei et al. [[23](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR23)] proposed a deep learning framework that utilizes group LASSO with correntropy for feature selection and a multi-layer independently recurrent neural network (IRNN) regression to capture spatial-temporal patterns in brain MRI data, thereby predicting AD clinical scores. The model was trained and tested using ADNI clinical and longitudinal MRI data. Early diagnosis and treatment planning were enhanced by precise clinical score prediction. Limited generalizability across groups and dependency on high-quality MRI data require confirmation on larger clinical datasets.

Bhowmik et al. [[24](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR24)] The proposed deep learning framework predicts AD clinical scores using group LASSO with correntropy for feature selection and a multi-layer independently recurrent neural network (IRNN) regression to capture spatial-temporal patterns in brain MRI data. The model was trained and tested using ADNI clinical and longitudinal MRI data. Early diagnosis and treatment planning were enhanced by precise clinical score prediction. Limited generalizability across groups and dependency on high-quality MRI data require confirmation on larger clinical datasets.

Bano et al. [[25](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR25)] This research uses BERT for sentence-level embeddings and BiGRU for contextual dependencies to overcome BERT’s input length constraints in extractive summarization. The model outperformed previous approaches on the arXiv and PubMed datasets, achieving ROUGE-F scores of (46.7, 19.4, 35.4) and (47.0, 21.3, 39.7), respectively. The model summarizes long papers coherently and relevantly. However, computational complexity and training time may limit implementation in low-resource situations. Academic text summarization and other extractive summarization areas can benefit from this hybrid design.

### 2.3 Integration of internet of things (IoT) in educational assessment

Mircea et al. [[26](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR26)] examine IoT integration in higher education using a Structural Equation Modeling (SEM)-validated assessment approach based on six hypotheses. The collection includes Romanian university surveys on connection, data integrity, resource attraction, and the impact of education policy. The results show that IoT implementation boosts teaching efficiency, resource management, and institutional connectivity. Limitations include a focus on one national school system and the use of subjective poll data. This research supports the strategic planning of innovative universities and the revolutionary role of IoT in future education.

Shaqrah et al. [[27](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR27)] This research uses the UTAUT2 model to examine the uptake of education’s IoT, focusing on innovativeness and social support. Taibah University student survey data were analyzed using Smart PLS-SEM to determine IoET application acceptance. The strongest predictors of IoET usage were social support, facilitated conditions, innovativeness, and effort expectancy, while performance expectancy and perceived usefulness were weaker. However, perceived ease of use did not affect behavioral intentions. The findings suggest ways colleges might promote IoET adoption, although self-reported statistics and a single institution’s perspective limit them.

Ding et al. [[28](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR28)] This research compares IoT-enabled supply chains to traditional ones in terms of cost, carbon emissions, and market performance, using system dynamics (SD) and agent-based (AB) modeling. Simulated battery delivery chain data compares a two-tier IoT-supported supply chain against a three-tier chain. Optimization of storage and transport techniques reduces emissions by 60%-70% and boosts profitability by 30% in the IoT supply chain. However, extensive truck usage and a lesser market share under the same pricing strategy constrain it. IoT can increase supply chain efficiency, but scalability and market strategy are issues.

Khan et al. [[29](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR29)] employ the Entropy Weighted Method (EWM) and TOPSIS to evaluate SIoT (Social Internet of Things) technologies and their impact on human behavior in this research. The dataset evaluates nine essential elements and seven SIoT breakthroughs for improving human behavior and activity precision. Results reveal that MCDM-based SIoT technologies enhance decision-making accuracy, with ranking outcomes used to select the most effective solutions. Integrating various MCDM approaches can be complicated, and the application of real-world data is unclear.

Chung et al. [[30](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR30)] examine the psychological and operational effects of AIoT-enabled service robots on hotel personnel, including job expectations, resources, cognitive trust, and perceived behavioral control. The SPSS 21 and SmartPLS dataset comprises employee responses to self-efficacy, threat evaluations, and technological familiarity. Self-efficacy improves cognitive trust and power, but threat evaluations aggravate job insecurity. Trust increases with responsiveness and engagement, and anthropomorphic features affect control. Generalizability is limited by the concentration on hotel employees in a single industry. The research provides valuable tips for hospitality-related human-robot collaboration.

3 Materials and methods
-----------------------

The structure of DeepGrade, an intelligent system for grading essays that leverages deep learning and the Internet of Things, is illustrated in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#Fig1). The first step is to gather information from Internet of Things (IoT) sensors and the ASAP 2.0 dataset (for example, typing speed and pauses). Tokenization, lemmatization, Word2Vec, and TF-IDF are among the preparation procedures used to clean and transform the essays in this collection. The essay scoring module uses complicated models like BERT and BERT-LSTM to score papers based on sentiment and behavioral context for fairness and rubric alignment. Using IoT behavioral data, LSTM/BiGRU assesses student involvement. The last assessment and optimization module employs TOPSIS to rank models and fine-tune performance using accuracy, F1-score, and ROUGE. This hybrid system can be used in smart classrooms to improve equality, demographic bias, and score accuracy by 9.6%.

**Terminology and operational definitions**

* This research defines real-time as the system’s ability to process an essay and behavioral signal stream with a delay of ≤ 2 s from keystroke to score production.
* The context-aware intelligence model considers semantic content (text embeddings) and writing-behavior context (IoT keystroke features) to improve scoring decisions, resulting in better fairness and rubric alignment than text-only baselines.
* The conscious model uses adaptive self-monitoring, with an internal fairness module evaluating subgroup prediction disparities during inference and adjusting decision thresholds when they exceed a set tolerance (δ = 0.02 in experiments). Functional, not philosophical, definition of “consciousness” centered on operational bias detection and correction.

### 3.1 Data collection and preprocessing

To develop DeepGrade: IoT, a complex essay grading system, users must collect, clean, and preprocess diverse datasets to ensure high-quality and reliable submissions. This technique involves collecting semantically essay materials from the ASAP 2.0 dataset and integrating simulated IoT-based behavioral cues like keystroke dynamics and writing patterns. Preprocessing includes aligning behavioral data with essays, normalizing text, and mapping rubrics. Cleaning removes irregularities, missing values, and noise to increase model accuracy. A structured data pipeline enables DeepGrade to utilize the hybrid BERT-LSTM architecture, producing more generalizable, fair, and real-time scoring outputs that meet rubrics.

**Text preprocessing**

The DeepGrade: IoT Automated Essay Scoring System requires text preparation to ensure clean and valuable input for the deep learning model. Three main alterations are made to raw essay texts using NLTK or spaCy. To arrange analysis, essays are tokenized into terms \(\:\:T=\:\{{t}\_{1},{t}\_{2},\dots\:..,{t}\_{n}\:\}\).). Next, eliminate stop-words, which are common but have weak semantics, from an article. Such phrases include “is,” “the,” and “and”. Last, lemmatization converts each token. \(\:{l}\_{i}=\:Lemma\:\left({t}\_{i}\right)\), which improves semantic consistency and accuracy in downstream natural language processing tasks like feature extraction, rubric alignment, and essay grading.

**Feature extraction**

DeepGrade: IoT scoring uses Word2Vec embedding and TF-IDF vectorization to improve essay score semantics. First, Word2Vec maps lemmatized tokens to maintain semantic linkages like synonyms and contextual similarities.\(\:{l}\_{i}\) ​ into a dense, continuous vector space \(\:{v}\_{i}\:\in\:{\mathbb{R}}^{d}\). The model can better detect criteria like coherence and grammar embedding. At the same time, TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency) measures how significant each term is in an essay in comparison to all of the writings in the collection. The TF-IDF score is determined by:

$$\:\text{T}\text{F}-\text{I}\text{D}\text{F}\:\left(\text{t},\text{d}\right)=\text{t}\text{f}\left(\text{t},\text{d}\right).\text{log}\left(\frac{N}{df\left(t\right)}\right)$$
(1)

In Eq. [1](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#Equ1), the frequency of term \(\:t\) in document \(\:d\:\)is denoted by \(\:\text{t}\text{f}\left(\text{t},\text{d}\right)\), the number of documents containing the term \(\:t\) is denoted by \(\:df\left(t\right)\), and the total number of documents is represented by \(\:N\). These methods, when combined, produce detailed semantic representations, which greatly enhance the AES model’s rubric-aware scoring accuracy.

TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency) is a statistical measure that evaluates the importance of a word within a specific document relative to a corpus. This analysis takes into consideration both the Term Frequency, which is a count of the number of times the term appears in a single document, and the Inverse Document Frequency, which is a count of the number of times the word appears in all of the papers. This helps it determine the weight to assign to each word. This method may reduce the number of infrequently occurring keywords in the corpus and place greater emphasis on phrases that are more significant to the text. TF-IDF produces high-dimensional, sparse vectors that are very useful for a variety of applications. Document classification and information retrieval are two practical uses.

In contrast, Word2Vec uses dense, low-dimensional vectors to capture the semantic relationships between words. As a basis for its operation, neural embeddings are used. It acquires the ability to represent words to improve its ability to deal with words that are comparable in meaning and that are included within the same context. To assist models in determining how words are related to one another, how they imply the same thing, and how they are similar in other ways, Word2Vec embeddings are pretty helpful. Because it can comprehend context and draw broad conclusions, it is the best option for increasingly sophisticated natural language processing applications.

While working together, TF-IDF and Word2Vec perform more effectively in natural language processing (NLP) applications, such as those that automatically evaluate essays. Adding the weights of the Word2Vec embeddings to the TF-IDF scores is the method by which you accomplish this goal. To put it another way, this is how the meanings of words are connected to their monetary worth. It can be used for a wide variety of activities, including sorting, assessing how people feel, capturing the uniqueness of language, and aligning rubrics.

By encoding complete essays into fixed-length vectors that contain both contextual flow and semantic meaning, Doc2Vec is used for sentiment and structure analysis in the DeepGrade: IoT system. This technique guarantees that the essay’s general coherence, tone, and argumentative structure are maintained by defining \(\:\:Doc2Vec\left(D\right)\:=\:d\:\in\:\:{\mathbb{R}}^{k}\), where is the essay and is the resultant vector. \(\:{\mathbb{R}}^{k}\) Means that each of the \(\:k\)components of vector \(\:d\) is a real number, i.e., \(\:d={d}\_{1},{d}\_{2},\dots\:,{d}\_{k}\)]where \(\:{d}\_{i}\in\:\mathbb{R}\). When it comes to aligning with rubric characteristics such as organization, relevance, and clarity, Doc2Vec is superior to word-level embeddings because it considers paragraph- and document-level semantics. It, in turn, improves the scoring model’s understanding.

**Combined representation layer architecture**

Together, the BERT encoder’s semantic embedding vector and the LSTM/BiGRU encoder’s behavioral embedding vector are L2-normalized and then combined to create a joint feature vector, as represented in Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#Fig2). This vector is run through a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) that has two completely linked layers, activation with ReLU, and a dropout rate of 0.4 between them. The layers range from 512 units to 256 units. The next step is to send the fused representation to the scoring module that considers fairness.

The DeepGrade: IoT framework uses a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network to handle behavioral patterns derived from keyboard logs, such as typing speed (\(\:{v}\_{t}\)) and backspace frequency (\(\:{b}\_{f}\)) as sequential data. Hidden state from the previous time step \(\:{h}\_{t-1}\). The LSTM is trained with each behavioral input at time \(\:t\), represented as \(\:{x}\_{t}\). To update the hidden state \(\:{h}\_{t}\). According to the recurrence in Eq. [2](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#Equ2):

$$\:{h}\_{t}=LSTM({x}\_{t},{h}\_{t-1}\:)$$
(2)

By capturing the temporal dependencies in writing behavior, the model can learn the correlation between behaviors, such as typing fluency or revisions, and essay quality. **Multimodal IoT embedding strategy**

All behavioral modalities derived from the Internet of Things (e.g., typing speed, backspace frequency, pause duration) are initially normalized and time-aligned using the proportional mapping that was previously defined. After that, a single LSTM/BiGRU encoder is fed these features, which are stacked into a multivariate time series of dimensions (time steps × number of modalities). The recurrent network can now capture modalities’ interdependencies and temporal dynamics in one cohesive vector within this architecture. In a further step, the combined representation layer joins this embedding with the BERT encoder’s semantic representation.

The ASAP 2.0 dataset uses synthetically generated IoT behavioral features to mimic actual writing patterns, simulating IoT behavioral signals. While discrete events like backspace counts follow Poisson or binomial models, typing speed and inter-key delays are pulled from normal or log-normal distributions. To prepare the multivariate time series for integration into the LSTM/BiGRU behavioral encoder, they are first aligned to the duration of each essay’s composition and then cleaned using Z-score or IQR filtering.

**Temporal alignment and synchronization strategy**.

Using a timestamp-based proportional mapping, DeepGrade: IoT connects IoT behavioral features to essay content. The essay is divided into tokens or phrases, and each simulated signal (such as typing speed, pauses, or backspaces) has a time index. To guarantee that the LSTM/BiGRU encoder’s semantic and behavioral characteristics are in perfect sync, behavioral signals are either resampled or interpolated to correspond to these periods.

Word2Vec and TF-IDF are typically used during preprocessing and feature extraction to identify semantic connections between words and to determine the importance of each term relative to the dataset. Word2Vec gives you continuous word vector embeddings that show how related words are in context, while TF-IDF shows how often key terms show up. They make a rich semantic picture together. Doc2Vec turns whole essays into fixed-length vectors (paragraph/document-level embeddings) that capture how well the essay flows, its tone, its structure, and how well it organizes its arguments. The scoring module uses an adversarial fairness-aware loss function for fairness. This design has two parts:

1. 1.
   
   Use the scoring network \(\:{f}\_{s}(\cdot\:;{\theta\:}\_{s})\) to predict the rubric-aligned essay score.
2. 2.

To maintain high scoring accuracy without encoding demographic information in the score representation. Using \(\:y\) as the ground truth essay score, \(\:\widehat{y}\) As the anticipated Score, and ddd as the demographic label (e.g., gender, socio-economic status), the Main scoring loss in Eq. [3](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#Equ3):

$$\:{\varvec{L}}\_{\varvec{s}\varvec{c}\varvec{o}\varvec{r}\varvec{e}}\left({\varvec{\theta\:}}\_{\varvec{s}}\right)=\frac{1}{\varvec{N}}\sum\:\_{i=1}^{N}{({y}\_{i}-{\widehat{y}}\_{i})}^{2}\:$$
(3)

When one engages in adversarial learning training, one will acquire this knowledge.

Because this min-max-unfriendly target does not allow demographic information to be one of the elements that predict results, it is fair to all groups. The purpose of the fairness-aware learning objective is to minimize bias while maintaining accurate scoring. The Contextual Fairness Index (CFI), the Demographic Parity Difference (DPD), and the Equalized Odds Difference (EOD) are three methods for assessing fairness. They illustrate how this strategy promotes greater fairness and reduces disparities in scores across demographic groups.

5 Performance analysis
----------------------

Results should be seen as conceptual proof of the approach’s potential rather than confirmation of operational success in actual classroom situations, as they are based on studies with simulated behavioral aspects. In the single-institution deployment, (i) raw essay text and keystroke telemetry are gathered under IRB with de-identification, (ii) bandwidth and client heterogeneity are non-binding, and (iii) rubric-aligned QWK is the goal, not cross-silo compatibility.

### 5.1 Contextual fairness index (CFI)

To determine the effectiveness of the scoring method for various student demographics (such as socio-economic status, native language, or typing speed), the Contextual Fairness Index is employed. To reduce the possibility of bias, CFI measures the variation in score consistency between these groups in DeepGrade: IoT, which incorporates behavioral data derived from the Internet of Things into the essay scoring process. For example, if the CFI is low, it indicates that the model is biased and does not accurately represent all students, whereas a high CFI suggests that the system is fair and inclusive of all students. As shown by the 9.6% improvement reported in the research, the statistic aligns with minimizing demographic-based scoring bias.

**Standard fairness metrics**

The suggested CFI and BRAS are evaluated with the algorithmic bias literature’s fairness measures, Demographic Parity Difference (DPD), Equalized Odds Difference (EOD), and Subgroup Calibration Error. DPD measures the absolute difference in demographic group positive prediction rates. TPR and FPR differences between groups are calculated by EOD. SCE measures subgroup deviations between projected probability and empirical result frequencies. These measurements compare automated scoring performance to fairness-aware methods.

[![]()](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w/figures/6)

Contextual fairness index (CFI) comparison across AES models

[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w/figures/6)

Five different Automated Essay Scoring (AES) systems are shown in Fig. [6](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#Fig6): GPT-3 [[16](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR16)], ConvNet, LSTM, Doc2Vec [[17](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR17)], UTAUT2 [[27](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR27)], and the new DeepGrade. The fairness of each model’s scores across demographic groups, including gender, socio-economic status, ELL status, handicap status, and ethnicity, is measured by CFI. The formula is used to compute the CFI in Eq. [11](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#Equ10):In this formulation,

$$\:CFI=1-\left(\raisebox{1ex}{${\left(\frac{1}{N}\sum\:\_{g=1}^{N}{\stackrel{-}{(s}}\_{g}-{\stackrel{-}{s}}\_{all}\right)}^{2}$}\!\left/\:\!\raisebox{-1ex}{${\sigma\:}\_{baseline}^{2}$}\right.\right)$$
(11)

In the given Eq. [11](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#Equ10),\(\:\:{\stackrel{-}{(s}}\_{g}\)Represents the mean predicted Score for demographic group g, \(\:{\stackrel{-}{s}}\_{a}\)ˉ stands for the overall mean projected Score for all groups, and \(\:N\)is the total demographic group count. \(\:{\sigma\:}^{2}The\:\)variance of the group means in the baseline model, such as text-only AES, is one divided by \(\:{\sigma\:}\_{baseline}^{2}\)To ensure consistent comparisons, the denominator normalizes the numerator’s measurement of score discrepancy between groups to the baseline system’s variance. A lower relative disparity and a higher CFI indicate greater demographic fairness. The fairness-aware scoring system in DeepGrade significantly improves upon previous models, as it achieves 89.7% CFIs compared to preceding models’ 80% or lower. This model enhances score consistency for underprivileged groups by 15.4% and reduces demographic bias by 9.6% by integrating behavioral data via LSTM and semantic analysis using BERT. With the addition of simulated Internet of Things signals, such as keystroke dynamics, the ASAP 2.0 dataset is enhanced. The outcomes demonstrate that DeepGrade can accurately, inclusively, and in real-time score essays in intelligent education systems.

### 5.2 Behavioral-aware rubric alignment score (BRAS)

Model final essay scores are compared to predetermined grading rubrics using the Behavioral-Aware Rubric Alignment Score, which considers both behavioral signals (such as keyboard speed or revision patterns) and semantic content. This measure in DeepGrade: IoT indicates if behavioral data improves rubric adherence by capturing the full integration of writing activities with rubric criteria, such as organization, coherence, and grammar. A higher BRAS score surpasses the capabilities of standard NLP-based analysis, demonstrating that behavioral variables derived from the IoT are making a substantial contribution to accurate, rubric-consistent scoring.

Behavioral-aware rubric alignment score (BRAS) comparison across AES models

The Behavioral-Aware Rubric Alignment Score (BRAS) was compared amongst six Automated Essay Scoring (AES) systems: GPT-3 [[16](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR16)], ConvNet, LSTM, and Doc2Vec [[17](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR17)], UTAUT2 [[27](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#ref-CR27)], and the DeepGrade model (Fig. [7](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#Fig7)). By combining semantic information with behavioral signals (such as keystroke dynamics and revision trends) from IoT devices, BRAS is a new evaluation metric that determines how well each model matches rubric-based criteria. Given that they are text-only, traditional models like GPT-3 (75.5%) and Doc2Vec (70.1%) exhibit poor adherence to the rubric. On the other hand, DeepGrade achieves a BRAS of 90.1%, which is significantly higher than the 80% criterion, suggesting that the rubrics are more closely aligned. The calculation for the metric in Eq. [12](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00731-w#Equ11):

$$\:BRAS=\left(\frac{1}{K}\sum\:\_{K=1}^{K}\frac{{S}\_{k}^{pred}\bullet\:{R}\_{k}}{{\left|\left|{R}\_{k}\right|\right|}^{2}}\right)\times\:100\%$$
(12)

where \(\:K\) is the number of rubric features, \(\:{R}\_{k}\) Is the ground-truth rubric vector for the -th dimension (e.g., grammar, organization), and \(\:{R}\_{k}\) It is the projected rubric vector. This normalized dot product captures the consistency between semantic and behavioral aspects. Proving its efficacy for intelligent educational systems, DeepGrade utilizes the ASAP 2.0 dataset, supplemented with simulated IoT signals, to produce real-time, equitable, and rubric-consistent assessments.

### 5.3 Real-time scoring efficiency ratio (RSER)

An evaluation of the time-accuracy trade-off in essay grading using the DeepGrade: IoT system is conducted using the Real-Time Grading Efficiency Ratio. By comparing the executed processing time to the obtained Quadratic Weighted Kappa (QWK) score, it determines the system’s accuracy and speed in producing dependable essay ratings. Scalable machine learning implementations that require immediate input require this. In real-time educational monitoring, an RSER upgrade enables the system to deliver excellent grades on time while maintaining fairness and rubric alignment.

6 Conclusion and future work
----------------------------

The research investigation introduced DeepGrade: IoT, a new Automated Essay Scoring system. Real-time essay grading is more efficient, accurate, and fair using deep learning and IoT behavioral data. Unlike typical AES systems that use semantic text features, DeepGrade uses LSTM-driven behavioral modeling and BERT-based semantic encoding to capture students’ writing style and content. DeepGrade outperforms current systems in BARS, RSER, and CFI on the ASAP 2.0 dataset with simulated behavioral data. Because of its fairness-aware architecture, the model reduced demographic bias by 9.6%, rubric adherence by 12%, and scoring variance by 15.4% among disadvantaged groups.

The long-term goal is to implement DeepGrade in actual classrooms and use real-time behavioral IoT data (such as eye-tracking and typing latency) to improve customization. The fairness assertions are strengthened by including DPD, EOD, and SCE alongside the proposed CFI and BRAS. Metric consistency and statistical significance testing strengthen equity improvements. Integrating explainable AI (XAI) modules for transparent feedback, adding support for multilingual essay grading, and ensuring DeepGrade complies with data protection requirements (such as GDPR) will all contribute to its scalability and reliability. This research paves the way for future work in educational AI that is both contextually aware and human-centered. The study employs controlled datasets; therefore, the outcomes are proof-of-concept rather than field-ready. Integration into learning management systems, real-time classroom experiments, and privacy and scalability issues are coming.

The option to deploy FL in the future. Future work should evaluate the utility-robustness trade-offs under these defenses. Still, in multi-institution scenarios where data locality/privacy constraints are dominant, it could move DeepGrade: IoT to an FL training regime that uses robust aggregation (such as geometric median/RFA, Krum variants, γ-mean) and aggregation-rule randomization to decrease the risk of poisoning. Synthetically generated behavioral variables (such as keystroke timing, backspace frequency, and pause duration) are used to imitate real writing habits in this rtesearch. These simulations allow us to evaluate the multimodal fusion method in a controlled environment, but they don’t represent the real thing when it comes to student interactions; they don’t adequately account for the noise, variability, and authenticity. Consequently, assertions concerning the behavioral context, fairness, and the performance of real-time scoring have only preliminary empirical backing. Consequently, this research should be considered a proof-of-concept in theory. For more robust empirical validation, future research will focus on gathering and analyzing real-life datasets of keystrokes and behavioral data from genuine writing sessions conducted with informed consent.
