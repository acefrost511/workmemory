### Tech Outlook 2026: What Education Leaders Expect this Year

* By [Rhea Kelly](https://thejournal.com/forms/emailtoauthor.aspx?AuthorItem={2D7E57D7-17DE-4E78-935C-82BB7E2F788F}&ArticleItem={36CB1661-2693-486A-8320-97F13EF7E44E})
* 01/29/26

### Key Takeaways

* Workforce readiness and career pathways are central themes for 2026: Leaders cited increased emphasis on career and technical education (CTE), industry certifications, micro-credentials, hybrid learning models for CTE, simulation/extended reality tools, and stronger connections between classrooms and careers.
* AI use in K–12 is described as moving from novelty to embedded use and broader strategies: Predictions included AI becoming a norm, districts standardizing on approved AI ecosystems inside existing platforms, increased focus on ethical use and data privacy, training for teachers and administrators, and institutional AI strategies that define governance, compliance, and evaluation.
* Leaders highlighted shifts in data integration and cybersecurity expectations: Responses pointed to interoperability standards and connected data flows, concerns about reduced federal data collections due to budget cuts, “context-aware” dashboards, ransomware defense alongside quantum-readiness gaps and staffing shortages, AI-enabled data exfiltration, centralized app/platform reviews tied to procurement policies, and expectations of AI governance and monitoring.


[![]()](https://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/jump?iu=/5978/eof.the&t=item%253d36cb1661_2693_486a_8320_97f13ef7e44e%26pos%253dbox_c1%26Topic%253dOpinions_and_Expert_Commentary%252cStrategic_Planning%252cFeatures_and_Cover_Stories%252cCentral_IT%252cIT_Leadership%252cARTICLE_TYPE%252cAUDIENCE&sz=300x250|640x481&tile=4&c=123456789)



  

In an open call last month, we asked technology leaders in education for their predictions on how the tech landscape will change for schools and districts in the coming year. Responses covered topics ranging from AI and data integration to cybersecurity and workforce readiness. Here's what they told us.

### Career Skills Will Be a Priority

"In 2026, workforce readiness will no longer be seen as someone else's responsibility, but will become a collective mission. Schools, employers, families, and policy-makers will increasingly work together to connect students' strengths to real opportunities. Career and technical education (CTE) and industry certifications will move to the center of the conversation as districts rethink graduation requirements to prioritize alignment between student aptitudes and workforce demand. The goal will shift from 'graduation' to *readiness.* Students don't lack ambition; they lack connection between what they're good at and where those talents are needed. When education, industry, and community align, that connection becomes clear. The result? A generation that enters the world not just credentialed, but confident and capable." *— Edson Barton, CEO & co-founder, [YouScience](https://www.youscience.com/)*

"In the coming year, the most impactful trends will be those that tighten the bond between classrooms and careers. AI-driven personalization will move from experimentation to expectation. Adaptive platforms will help instructors differentiate instruction in welding, health science, IT pathways, and many others. This will meet students where they are while maintaining industry standards. When implemented properly and used effectively, AI will support teachers, not replace them. Use of simulation and extended reality tools for CTE will continue to grow in the next year. Virtual labs and augmented reality overlays will allow students to practice high-risk or high-cost skills before ever touching real equipment, improving safety, confidence, and access. Credentialing will become more granular and portable. Ed tech platforms that align coursework with industry-recognized micro-credentials will help students build résumés while still in school, giving employers clearer signals of job readiness. Education and workforce systems are under growing pressure to demonstrate value for learners and employers. Access is not enough; outcomes matter now more than ever. The year ahead is not about more technology; it is about smarter and more intentional use that prepares learners for real work, real wages, and real futures." — *Dr. Lotoya West, director of Career & Technical Education, [Escambia County Schools](https://www.escambiaschools.org/)*
