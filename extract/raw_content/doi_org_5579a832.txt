## Abstract
This study summarizes the predictive effects of individual-level information and communication technology (ICT) and country-level factors on students’ learning outcomes, including cognitive, affective, and behavioral domains. Moreover, we synthesized how country-level factors shape the predictive effects of student-level ICT factors on learning outcomes. We systematically reviewed empirical studies that conducted secondary analyses of the \_Programme for International Student Assessment\_ (PISA) data incorporating country-level factors into their analyses. The analysis showed that the most frequently studied topics in PISA-derived ICT research were student-level ICT attitude and ICT use, followed by country-level factors directly related to ICT. The association between student-level ICT adoption and academic achievement varied depending on the educational setting, such as academic domains and instructional designs. Meanwhile, student attitude was consistently positively associated with learning outcomes. Country-level factors, such as national ICT access in education and human capital indicators, were generally positively associated with learning outcomes. Conversely, national ICT infrastructure and economic indicators showed mixed or negative results. Furthermore, country-level factors moderated the relationship between student-level ICT factors and learning outcomes, exhibiting facilitating, buffering, diminishing, or exacerbating effects. This study highlights that the educational effect of ICT is highly context-dependent, meaning it requires pedagogically aligned integration within learning contexts and institutional support at the broader institutional and national contexts to ensure effective learning outcomes.
## Introduction
As information and communication technology (ICT) has become integral to modern life, ICT literacy, the ability to utilize ICT, has emerged as a critical skill. Many countries have integrated ICT into education to foster students’ digital competence (OECD, 2016). Consequently, research interest in ICT’s impact on learning outcomes has grown (Hopfenbeck et al., 2018). Reflecting this, the \_Programme for International Student Assessment\_ (PISA), which significantly influences educational policy (Domínguez et al., 2012), has consistently included ICT-related assessments.
ICT’s effect on students’ learning outcomes has been widely expected, with research indicating inconsistent effects depending on context. For example, Scherer and Siddiq (2019) conducted a meta-analysis and found gender differences in ICT literacy, suggesting individual background matters. Additionally, Petko et al. (2017) analyzed cross-sectional data across 39 countries and reported cross-national variation in the predictive effect of ICT use on academic achievement. Moreover, ICT use at home for entertainment was positively associated with reading in Turkey and Russia, negatively associated with reading in Austria and Korea, and non-significantly associated with reading in the Netherlands and Italy. Such inconsistencies highlight the role of environmental contexts.
To better understand how ICT is associated with learning outcomes, this review systematically analyzed PISA-based studies. Many studies have shown that student-level ICT factors, including access, use, and attitude, predict students’ learning outcomes (Guzeller & Akin, 2014, Wang et al., 2023). Going beyond Odell et al. (2020), who focused on student-level ICT factors and academic outcomes in mathematics and science, this study expands the scope to include both student- and country-level factors across cognitive, affective, and behavioral outcomes. Several studies showed that national contexts also explained a substantial proportion of the variance in student outcomes (e.g., Deng & Sun, 2022; Loh et al., 2023; Park & Weng, 2020). By applying ecological systems theory, this study examines how national contexts not only predict student learning outcomes directly but also shape the relationship between student-level ICT factors and achievement. This study also identifies gaps in domains that remain underexplored, particularly affective and behavioral outcomes. For policymakers, the findings guide on when and in what contexts ICT use tends to be educationally effective.
