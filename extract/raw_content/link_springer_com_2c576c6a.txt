Abstract
--------

Traditional grading systems in higher education face significant challenges, including time inefficiency, subjective bias, and scalability issues, necessitating innovative solutions. This narrative review synthesises literature from 2018 to 2025, examining AI-powered grading and feedback systems, analysing 77 core studies across multiple databases. AI technologies, particularly machine learning, natural language processing, and computer vision, demonstrate significant potential in automating assessment processes, providing consistent grading, and delivering personalised feedback. Benefits include reduced educator workload, faster turnaround times, and enhanced learning experiences. However, critical challenges persist, including algorithmic bias, data privacy concerns, lack of transparency, and the need for human oversight. While AI-driven assessment tools offer transformative potential for higher education, successful implementation requires careful integration with human expertise, robust ethical frameworks, and continuous validation to ensure equitable and effective educational outcomes.

1 Introduction
--------------

Assessment methods in higher education can be transformed through powerful tools such as AI. Innovative AI assessment tools can score tasks, provide comprehensive feedback, and continuously monitor student development [[1](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR1)]. The evolution of assessment technology has undergone significant transformations, particularly with the integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) into grading systems. Initially, assessments relied heavily on traditional manual grading methods, which posed challenges such as subjectivity, inconsistency, and time inefficiency. These limitations prompted the search for more reliable and objective assessment methods, leading to the incorporation of technology into education [[2](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR2)]. By employing natural language processing (NLP) and machine learning (ML), AI can assess both fundamental knowledge and advanced cognitive skills, including critical thinking and problem-solving abilities [[3](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR3), [4](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR4)]. The rise of AI tools like ChatGPT has further accelerated this shift, as they offer opportunities for personalised feedback and formative assessments. Educators have begun to recognise the potential of AI to provide immediate feedback, allowing them to focus on instructional strategies rather than administrative tasks. This adaptability is critical in a fast-evolving educational landscape, where continuous learning and the integration of new technologies are paramount for effective teaching [[2](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR2), [5](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR5), [6](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR6)]. AI technologies have been acknowledged as transformative educational instruments, particularly in higher education [[7](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR7)]. Integrating AI into educational assessment processes facilitates grading automation, which enhances efficiency and mitigates biases associated with human grading [[8](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR8)]. For example, Mahmudi et al. [[9](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR9)] highlight that AI can tailor educational materials and deliver automatic feedback, creating a more personalised and practical learning experience for students. When incorporated into education, AI-driven tools can revolutionise current practices through assessments and feedback, enabling consistency, efficiency, and scalability [[10](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR10)]. Automated grading systems can manage large volumes of student submissions with considerable accuracy and speed, alleviating the workload for educators while improving turnaround times. These systems utilise large language models for grading student work, providing consistency and objectivity, thereby minimising human bias. Such systems streamline grading processes and foster a more equitable assessment environment by reducing the subjective biases inherent in traditional grading practices [[11](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR11)]. Gnanaprakasam et al. [[12](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR12)] emphasise that digital technology can render assessment methods fair and comprehensive, constituting one of the most essential elements of modern educational assessment. AI-driven grading systems also pave the way for real-time feedback, vital for student learning. Torres [[13](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR13)] elaborates on how online teaching strategies incorporating AI can improve student feedback quality, promoting a more engaging and responsive learning environment.

One more area where AI holds immense potential is personalised feedback. Individual assessments analysed by AI systems can identify student strengths and weaknesses and provide specific personalised feedback [[14](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR14), [15](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR15)]. Such tailored support will enable students to understand their learning achievements better and guide them towards areas requiring more attention and development. Palanisamy et al. [[16](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR16)] propose a vision for a Personalised Adaptive Learning and Assessment (PALAS) system that employs AI to aggregate data on students’ prior knowledge and academic performance, facilitating feedback that addresses individual learning gaps. AI-driven adaptive learning frameworks can significantly enhance student performance by customising educational experiences to meet individual learning needs [[17](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR17), [18](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR18)].

The advantages of AI in educational assessment extend beyond grading and feedback. AI-powered tools can also improve the learning experience by providing insights into student behaviour and learning patterns [[19](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR19), [20](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR20)]. Adiguzel et al. [[21](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR21)] explain that multimodal data analysis offers a comprehensive understanding of the learning process, enabling educators to devise effective teaching strategies. Moreover, AI systems’ capacity to analyse vast amounts of data and deliver insights into student performance can empower educators to make informed decisions that enhance learning outcomes [[22](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR22)]. In this way, a collaborative effort between AI and human insight may lead to even better decision-making, improving educational results.

However, the introduction of AI into educational assessment faces challenges. Several issues must be thoughtfully considered; these include data privacy, algorithmic transparency, and the need for human oversight [[23](#ref-CR23),[24](#ref-CR24),[25](#ref-CR25),[26](#ref-CR26),[27](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR27)]. As highlighted by Spreadborough and Glasser [[28](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR28)], the analysis of Learning Management System (LMS) data reveals the necessity to consider the types of research questions being addressed carefully and the analytical techniques employed. The potential for over-reliance on automated systems raises concerns about losing human oversight in the grading process, which could lead to issues of fairness and transparency [[29](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR29)]. Sghir et al. [[30](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR30)] emphasise that these issues must be prioritised to ensure that AI technologies are used ethically and proven efficient. Practical methods for merging AI with expertise involve collaboration, meaning AI tools should support and not replace human judgement [[31](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR31)]. Educators can utilise AI-generated data to inform their teaching practices while incorporating their expertise and intuition to guide student learning. Collaboratively integrating AI and human insight optimises effectiveness in assessment for valid and plausible evaluation following proper education [[32](#ref-CR32),[33](#ref-CR33),[34](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR34)]. Integrating AI in educational assessment aligns with broader trends in digital transformation within higher education. As Zhang [[35](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR35)] noted, the transformative power of educational technology is reshaping online learning environments, making them more interactive and personalised. Thus, AI-driven grading and feedback mechanisms can be a part of a more significant movement towards dynamic and responsive educational practices prioritising student engagement and success [[36](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR36)].

Overall, AI transforms educational assessment by enhancing grading efficiency, personalising feedback, and imparting valuable lessons learned from teaching. By addressing the challenges and leveraging AI’s strengths, educators can foster a cyclical environment of continuous improvement and pave the way towards a more effective and equitable educational ecosystem. This review paper covers significant advancements in AI-based grading and personalised feedback systems.

2 Methodology
-------------

This study employed a narrative literature review approach to examine AI-powered grading and feedback systems in higher education. The review methodology followed these systematic steps:

Search Strategy: Literature searches were conducted across multiple databases, including Scopus, Web of Science, IEEE Xplore, and Google Scholar, from January 2018 to March 2025.

Keywords Used: “artificial intelligence education”, “automated grading”, “AI feedback”, “machine learning assessment”, “NLP education”, “computer vision grading”, “personalised feedback”, “educational technology”.

Inclusion Criteria: Peer-reviewed articles, conference proceedings, and reports published in English from 2018 to 2025 that explore AI applications in educational assessment, specifically within higher education. The articles with a focus on research related to grading automation, feedback systems, or assessment technologies are considered.

Exclusion Criteria: Studies focused solely on K-12 education, non-English publications, Opinion pieces without empirical backing, and Studies older than 2018.

Selection Process: Initial search yielded 197 articles. After removing duplicates and applying inclusion/exclusion criteria, 156 relevant studies were selected for detailed review. Following rigorous application of inclusion and exclusion criteria, 77 studies were included in the final qualitative synthesis.

### 2.1 Traditional grading system

Traditional grading systems used to be the backbone of educational assessment, allowing a systematic way to determine student performance.

**Fig. 1**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0/figures/1)

Key challenges of traditional grading in higher education

Educators most often grade students’ work manually based on the fulfilment of predefined criteria. While this method is simple to implement, several drawbacks discourage it, motivating the development of more advanced, automated processes. Figure [1](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#Fig1) summarises the main limitations of manual assessment methods, including time consumption, susceptibility to subjectivity and bias, and scalability issues in large classes.

* *Time-Consuming Traditional Methods*: The time-consuming nature of traditional assessment methods presents one of the significant challenges. Traditional educational assessment methodologies require extensive time and effort from educators; for instance, they manually grade exams and assignments, often at the expense of instruction and content development. This process usually occurs individually for each student, which can be even more cumbersome in larger classes. Furthermore, it is prone to inconsistencies, as different assessors may interpret the grading criteria differently [[37](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR37)].
* *Subjectivity and bias in grading*: Subjectivity and bias in grading remain persistent issues that educational assessment must address, as the concepts of fairness and validity in student evaluations are at risk. The December 2018 study found that E-rater tended to give students from mainland China lower scores for grammar and mechanics, while African Americans tended to get low marks for grammar, style, and organisation, receiving below-average scores compared to expert human graders [[38](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR38)]. This inconsistency can undermine the fairness and reliability of the grades awarded, potentially affecting students’ academic trajectories. These biases may arise from factors such as a grader’s mood, preconceived notions about a student’s abilities, or unconscious biases linked to a student’s background or identity [[4](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR4), [39](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR39), [40](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR40)].
* *Scalability issue*: In higher education, class sizes can be significant, and traditional grading methods often struggle to remain manageable with so many assessments to grade. The challenges associated with scalability become more pronounced in large classes and online education, where the student-to-teacher ratio may be disproportionately high. Bauer et al. [[41](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR41)] underscore the inherent obstacles in these situations, such as ensuring equitable and uniform evaluations for a diverse and extensive student body. There is a need for robust solutions to manage resources in education and effectively tackle the issues presented by large classroom environments and online educational platforms.

3 Emergence of AI in education
------------------------------

The rise of AI in education is seen as a significant milestone in the evolution of educational assessment. AI technologies have facilitated drastic changes, especially in automated grading and personalised feedback, which have the potential to revolutionise higher education [[42](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR42)]. These tools are now implemented to enhance grading processes across three dimensions: efficiency, consistency, and scalability. They can swiftly handle large volumes of student work accurately, allowing educators to focus on more instructional activities. For instance, automated grading systems strongly correlate with manual grading, demonstrating their reliability and efficiency in assessing student performance [[37](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#CR37)]. This efficiency benefits STEM education, where timely feedback is crucial for student learning and development.

The integration of AI in educational assessment has demonstrated significant quantitative improvements across multiple dimensions of efficiency, accuracy, and learning outcomes. Recent studies using ChatGPT for automated essay scoring found strong correlations with human assessors (*r* = 0.829, *p* < 0.001) for well-structured assessments, demonstrating the potential reliability of AI systems in educational [[43](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR43)]. Large language models in automated essay grading achieved substantial agreement with human markers, with Quadratic Weighted Kappa scores of 0.68, while automated writing evaluation systems achieved agreement levels of 4.9 out of 5 with human assessments [[44](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR44)]. Meta-analytical evidence from 51 studies indicates that AI systems like ChatGPT demonstrate significant positive impacts on learning performance (g = 0.867) and moderate positive effects on learning perception (g = 0.456) and higher-order thinking development (g = 0.457) [[45](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR45)]. These quantitative findings provide substantial empirical support for the effectiveness of AI-driven assessment systems in improving both efficiency and educational outcomes.

Furthermore, AI provides personalised feedback that addresses the diverse needs of learners. This tailored approach will assist students in their learning journeys, contributing to improved educational outcomes. Conversely, integrating AI in feedback mechanisms ensures that the feedback is timely, highly relevant, and actionable to enhance learning [[46](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR46), [47](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR47)].

However, challenges and issues relating to AI ethics must be discussed and considered by those aiming to maximise its effectiveness and ensure enhancement rather than replacing human educators. With continued collaboration among stakeholders, the full potential of AI to drive transformation in large-scale educational assessment and improve students’ outcomes can be realised [[12](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR12), [37](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR37), [40](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR40), [46](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR46), [48](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR48)].

4 AI-powered technologies in grading systems
--------------------------------------------

Most AI systems use three different technologies: Machine Learning (ML) Algorithms, natural language processing (NLP), and computer vision (CV), as shown in Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#Fig2). This schematic illustrates how ML, NLP, and CV operate within automated assessment frameworks, highlighting their roles in processing student work, generating feedback, and supporting educator decision-making. Algorithms such as NLP and deep learning have greatly improved the ability to interpret and assess complex textual responses. In practice, these technologies often work synergistically: for instance, a system designed to grade handwritten lab reports may use computer vision to digitise the handwriting and diagrams, natural language processing to evaluate written explanations, and machine learning to integrate rubric-based scoring trained on historical data [[49](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR49), [50](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR50)]. These technologies have made educational assessments more accurate and efficient [[12](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR12), [51](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR51)]. For example, NLP techniques can assess grammar, vocabulary, and pronunciation to provide detailed and constructive feedback to learners [[49](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR49), [51](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR51)].

**Fig. 2**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0/figures/2)

Core AI technologies applied in grading systems

* *Machine Learning (ML) Algorithms*: Machine learning (ML) algorithms are central to AI grading systems, which learn from historical grading data to improve their performance over time. These algorithms can be trained to comprehend what constitutes a high-quality response and adapt to various grading rubrics, providing flexibility in assessment criteria [[1](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR1)]. The advantages of ML in grading include enhanced scalability and the capacity to identify trends in student performance, thereby assisting educators in tailoring their teaching strategies [[1](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR1), [5](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR5)]. In addition to boosting grading efficiency, ML algorithms support the development of auto-grading technologies. These systems can automatically evaluate written assessments by comparing student responses to predefined criteria and benchmarks [[52](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR52)].
* *Natural Language Processing (NLP)*: NLP algorithms enable machines to interpret and analyse text for grammar, syntax, and semantics, assessing the coherence and relevance of arguments in essays. This technology facilitates automated essay scoring, ensuring consistent grading standards and providing immediate, constructive feedback to students, essential for improving their writing skills [[1](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR1), [5](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR5)]. The statistical methods, classification techniques, and neural network approaches allow the system to evaluate essays automatically with significantly less intervention from human assessors and minimal scope for subjective bias [[3](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR3)]. This automation streamlines the grading process and guarantees a uniform yardstick of assessment across different submissions. Moreover, NLP assists in providing students with personalised feedback. Furthermore, the application of NLP in educational assessment extends beyond essay grading. AI-driven tools can also analyse short answer responses in online learning environments, showcasing the adaptability of these models [[33](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR33), [39](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR39), [53](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR53)]. According to Ouyang et al. [[37](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR37)], AI techniques are increasingly utilised in educational assessment, making the academic evaluation process more automated and convenient. The optimisation model structures formed with evaluation indicator systems will enhance the efficiency and accuracy of the assessment. Thus, NLP is a cornerstone of AI-driven grading systems that offer multiple advantages, such as increasing grading efficiency and consistency while providing personalised feedback.
* *Computer Vision (CV)*: Computer vision utilises advanced algorithms to interpret and analyse visual data, enabling unparalleled accuracy in assessing handwritten and printed student work. This system is particularly beneficial in university settings with high-class enrolments. Moreover, grading accuracy remains consistent, as computer vision removes the potential for subjective bias common in human grading. This results in a more standardised assessment process, resulting in fairer student outcomes [[12](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR12), [54](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR54), [55](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR55)]. Another advantage of employing computer vision for grading is its capacity to provide personalised feedback, ultimately creating a more supportive learning environment [[40](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR40), [55](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR55)].

ML, NLP algorithms, CV and other AI technologies, such as speech recognition and predictive analytics, can foster a more comprehensive approach to analysing student performance. Employing these systems to identify patterns and trends across multiple data points often uncovers insights that human assessors might overlook. This comprehensive approach enables educators to gain deeper insights into student learning and adjust their instructional strategies accordingly [[46](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR46), [51](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR51)]. However, it is vital to recognise the limitations and challenges of using ML and NLP algorithms in educational assessment. One fundamental limitation is the accuracy of the feedback delivered by the AI systems. Although these systems can process vast amounts of data and generate insights, they may occasionally yield inaccurate or contextually inappropriate feedback [[24](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR24), [46](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR46), [56](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR56)]. One of the primary concerns is the potential for bias in AI-driven systems. If the training data used for these algorithms is not representative of the diverse student population, then the models will likely perpetuate existing biases and inequalities. Therefore, it is equally crucial to ensure that the training data used to train algorithms is representative and inclusive [[46](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR46)]. Developers should also strive to create algorithms that are free from biases that may disadvantage specific groups of students [[57](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR57), [58](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR58)]. Nonetheless, there is a pressing need to address the challenges associated with AI-driven grading systems to ensure that such systems are fair, transparent, and beneficial for all students [[46](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR46), [51](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR51), [59](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR59), [60](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR60)].

5 Types of AI-driven grading systems
------------------------------------

This section will expand on the structured analysis of key types (Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#Fig3)) of AI-driven grading systems.

* *Automated Multiple-Choice Grading*: Automated multiple-choice grading is one of the most striking developments in the application of AI in educational assessment. Machine learning algorithms grade students’ responses to multiple-choice questions in this scheme. One of the primary benefits of automated multiple-choice grading is its ability to deliver near-instantaneous feedback, which can be instrumental in helping students identify and correct misconceptions early in their learning process [[34](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR34)]. This immediacy enhances the learning experience and encourages students to engage more actively with the material.

**Fig. 3**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0/figures/3)

AI-driven grading systems overview

* *Automated Essay Grading (AEG)*: Automated essay grading is a breakthrough in using artificial intelligence to assess student learning. This technology uses machine learning algorithms to score written essays and attempts to mimic the grading by human educators. The immediate benefit of AEG systems is that they have the potential to increase grading efficiency, consistency, and scalability. The advantage of AEG is that it can give students instant feedback. This fast response will allow the learners to identify their strengths and weaknesses early and change their learning strategies accordingly [[57](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR57)]. AI-driven continuous-assessment tools are helpful in this direction because they can establish a more interactive and responsive learning environment. AEG systems can handle large volumes of essays, so they are helpful in higher education, where class sizes tend to be large. Because these systems automate the grading process, they relieve educators of valuable time and enable them to pay greater attention to more personalised and qualitative aspects of teaching [[55](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR55)]. With this shift, not only does educator productivity improve, but the students’ educational experience also does. Besides grading more efficiently and consistently, AEG systems can offer an enhanced personalised learning experience. By analysing patterns in student writing, these systems can provide feedback and suggestions for improvement according to everyone’s needs, thus offering support for that kind of learning [[60](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR60), [61](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR61)]. This can significantly enhance students’ participation and results. However, there are also many challenges in AEG implementation. One of the most significant risks is the potential to manipulate AI systems and get misleading assessment results. The only way to ensure these systems are used ethically and effectively is by balancing artificial intelligence with human input. To continue to provide qualitative feedback, which AI cannot yet do, educators must remain actively engaged with the grading process.
* *Grading of Creative and Subjective Assignments*: Beyond text-based essays, early research is also exploring AI’s potential to evaluate creative domains such as design portfolios, music compositions, and video presentations. While these areas remain in their infancy, experimental systems combining pattern recognition, audio analysis, and multimodal machine learning are beginning to emerge, highlighting the opportunities and challenges of applying AI to diverse creative outputs in universities [[32](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR32), [34](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR34)]. Grading creative and subjective assignments presents unique challenges in AI-driven educational assessment. Traditional grading systems often struggle with the inherent variability and nuanced nature of assignments. However, AI-driven tools have shown promise in addressing these challenges by leveraging advanced algorithms and machine learning techniques.

For instance, automated essay scoring systems use hand-engineered feature-based methods combined with deep learning models to assess different aspects of writing, such as grammar, style, mechanics, and content development [[3](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR3)]. This allows the system to analyse vast volumes of text and provide consistent, objective evaluations that are key to fairness in grading subjective assignments. Most importantly, AI-driven feedback systems in English as a Foreign Language (EFL) learning have shown promise in quickening the process while alleviating performance evaluation anxiety. These systems would give immediate and accurate feedback, allowing students to continue practising and fine-tuning their skills. AI tools must consider the subjective nature of self-reported data to avoid biases and ensure that the provided feedback will be meaningful and relevant to the students’ contexts [[58](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR58)].

6 Implementation and integration
--------------------------------

Integration with Learning Management Systems (LMS) is critical for AI-driven grading systems in higher education. The seamless incorporation of AI tools within LMS platforms can enhance the efficiency and effectiveness of educational assessments. AI technologies, such as machine learning and natural language processing, can be embedded in LMS for automatic grading and personalised student feedback, thereby improving the learning experience [[62](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR62), [63](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR63)].

One of the primary advantages of integrating AI into LMS is the real-time feedback it can provide. AI-enabled tools can monitor student responses and offer instant, individualised feedback, essential for effective learning. This capability assists students in recognising mistakes and facilitates continuous learning [[60](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR60)]. Additionally, AI-driven systems can manage large volumes of data, making them particularly beneficial for scaling the grading process without compromising quality and consistency [[54](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR54)].

Several considerations are crucial for the successful integration of AI with LMS. First, the challenge of interoperability between the AI tool and existing LMS platforms necessitates the development of a standardised protocol and interface to enable different systems to communicate effectively [[37](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR37)].

### 6.1 Data security and privacy issues

Data security and privacy concerns are crucial in integrating AI-driven grading systems into educational settings. Incorporating AI technologies in grading and feedback mechanisms necessitates collecting and processing a considerable amount of student data, which raises significant privacy issues. Several high-profile incidents have highlighted the vulnerability of student data in AI systems. More recently, the Los Angeles Unified School District’s AI-powered assistant “Ed” was abruptly discontinued when the developing company faced financial difficulties, leaving uncertainty about the fate of collected student data [[64](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR64)]. The collection and use of data in AI-driven educational tools heavily impact ethical considerations [[21](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR21)]. highlight that integrating AI in education inevitably brings several ethical and practical considerations to the forefront, particularly relating to the legality and ethicality of data collection standards designed to safeguard students’ rights and privacy.

Furthermore, AI integration into educational assessment demands robust data governance frameworks. Such frameworks should outline clear policies regarding access, storage, and data sharing to ensure that students’ information is cared for. Learning analytics and educational data mining are vital components of AI in education, requiring strong data governance that guarantees the privacy of students’ data [[30](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR30)]. In addition to purely technical measures, educators and students should be educated on data privacy.

**Global implementation perspectives**: The global implementation of AI in educational assessment reveals significant disparities influenced by infrastructure, cultural, and socioeconomic factors. Samala et al. [[65](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR65)] provide a comprehensive analysis of emerging educational technologies across global contexts, highlighting how technological innovations face varying adoption challenges in different regions. Sub-Saharan Africa faces substantial infrastructure challenges, with internet penetration at only 36% as of 2021 and rural electricity connectivity at just 30.4%, compared to 99.9% in South Asia [[66](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR66)]. These limitations significantly impact AI adoption in education, as basic infrastructure like electricity remains lacking in many low-income countries, making widespread AI technological adoption difficult [[67](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR67)]. African educational institutions have responded to these challenges by developing innovative mobile-first solutions, such as SuaCode, an AI-powered app that enables students to learn coding using smartphones, specifically designed to address limited access to computers and expensive internet data [[68](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR68)]. Cultural considerations also significantly influence AI implementation in educational settings. Research examining automated essay scoring across different cultural contexts, such as Chinese students learning English, reveals that cultural and linguistic backgrounds significantly affect both system performance and student acceptance of automated feedback [[69](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR69)]. Studies have noted that cultural backgrounds, educational systems, and language proficiency levels vary significantly across contexts, potentially yielding different outcomes in AI-assisted educational applications [[70](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR70)]. These findings underscore the need for culturally adaptive AI systems that can accommodate diverse linguistic patterns and educational traditions rather than applying Western-developed models universally.

7 Comparative analysis between traditional and AI-driven grading
----------------------------------------------------------------

The differences between the methodologies, efficiencies, and impacts on educational outcomes are quite sharp between traditional and AI-driven grading. Table [1](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#Tab1) describes the significant differences between traditional and AI-based grading and feedback mechanisms. Traditional grading, usually performed by human educators, is heavily dependent on subjective judgment and thus can be influenced by various biases and inconsistencies. While it offers an interpersonal touch and great subtlety in assessing students’ work, it often becomes a victim of inconsistent grading standards and delays in providing feedback to students [[34](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR34), [59](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR59)]. On the other hand, AI-driven grading systems propose a much more uniform and time-sensitive approach—advanced algorithms, including natural language processing (NLP) and machine learning, power such systems to assess student submissions [[50](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR50)]. The most plausible advantage of AI-driven grading systems is their ability to process large amounts of data quickly, reducing time spent on grading and providing faster feedback to students [[12](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR12), [37](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR37)]. This quick feedback loop is critical in enhancing learning, allowing students to identify and correct their errors within a manageable timeframe [[34](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR34)]. Additionally, AI-driven grading systems are designed to mimic human judgment by identifying patterns and applying predefined criteria for evaluating student performance. These systems are particularly effective for subjects requiring objective evaluation, such as mathematics and computer science, where algorithmic solutions can be directly compared to correct answers [[37](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR37), [71](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR71)]. However, the application of AI in grading more subjective disciplines, such as literature and social sciences, remains challenging due to the complexity and nuances involved in evaluating written content. One key advantage of AI-driven grading is its ability to enhance fairness and reduce biases. AI systems trained on diverse datasets that include works from various cultural and educational backgrounds can offer fairer assessments. These systems also provide transparency about the basis of assigned grades, fostering trust and acceptance within the educational system [[12](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR12)]. However, human oversight is necessary to validate AI-generated grades and ensure they fairly and comprehensively reflect student work [[33](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR33)]. Although AI-driven systems raise concerns about computational resource consumption, they may simultaneously reduce the environmental footprint of traditional assessment methods by minimising paper use, printing, and physical transport of exam materials. This dual perspective underscores the need for a holistic evaluation of sustainability impacts [[7](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR7), [39](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR39)].

Moreover, more than AI is needed to achieve full effectiveness; it must work in conjunction with human expertise. Combining the efficiency and consistency of AI with the nuanced understanding and contextual knowledge of human educators creates a more holistic and practical hybrid approach to assessment.

8 Impact on educators
---------------------

Among the social implications of AI in education, the impact on educators is significant. AI-driven tools integrated into educational assessment may ultimately alter the role and responsibilities of educators beyond recognition [[72](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR72)]. First and foremost, one of the most notable impacts is a reduction in grading-related workload. This shift alleviates the burden on educators and enables them to devote more time to interactive and personalised instruction. Such approaches could significantly enhance students’ overall learning experiences.

Despite these challenges, the potential benefits of AI in educational assessment are substantial. By automating routine tasks and providing personalised support, AI can improve the effectiveness and efficiency of educational systems. Educators can leverage AI for deeper insights into students’ performance, identify opportunities for improvement, and deliver targeted interventions to foster student success [[54](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR54)]. This collaboration between AI and human educators will create a more dynamic and responsive educational environment, enhancing student and educator outcomes. Thoughtfully addressing ethical and technological concerns will ensure AI’s practical and responsible integration into education. With the ongoing development of AI, its role in educational assessment is likely to evolve further, opening new avenues to enhance teaching and learning processes [[21](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR21), [46](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR46), [59](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR59), [73](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR73)].

### 8.1 Student perceptions and acceptance

Student perceptions and acceptance of AI in educational assessment play a decisive role in its successful adoption. Many students recognise the potential value of AI-driven feedback, particularly its ability to deliver personalised and actionable suggestions for improvement. Pang et al. [[46](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR46)] found that a large majority of participants believed AI could significantly enhance the delivery of effective, individualised feedback.

Despite these benefits, some students express concerns about the impersonal nature of automated feedback, noting that a human touch remains essential for motivation and engagement. Memarian et al. [[58](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR58)] caution that fully automated assessment and feedback may feel inhumane to some learners, leading them to prefer human feedback providers.

Speed and availability are also widely acknowledged advantages of AI systems. Sutrisno et al. [[51](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR51)] report that AI-driven feedback consistently outperforms traditional approaches in these areas, particularly in large educational settings where timely responses can make a substantial difference in student progress.

An additional strength valued by students is AI’s capacity to tailor learning experiences and provide targeted support. Onesi-Ozigagun et al. [[74](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR74)] highlight that AI can personalise learning for students regardless of their background or ability, offering a more effective alternative to one-size-fits-all approaches.

However, to fully realise these benefits, challenges remain. These include ensuring that feedback retains a human dimension, safeguarding fairness and equity, and guaranteeing that all students have equal access to the necessary technologies.

### 8.2 Future trends

The future of AI-driven grading and personalised feedback is set to evolve rapidly, shaped by advances in technology and the growing demand for individualised learning experiences. Anticipated future trends in AI-driven educational assessment

**Advancements in AI algorithms**: Ongoing improvements in AI algorithms will continue to enhance the accuracy and efficiency of grading. Machine learning techniques will leverage increasingly large and diverse datasets, improving the precision of automated assessments over time [[1](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR1)]. Natural language processing (NLP) will further strengthen AI’s capacity to interpret and evaluate student responses, enabling more nuanced and context-aware feedback mechanisms [[1](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR1)]. The use of culturally and linguistically diverse datasets will help mitigate bias, while continuous validation against human grading standards will ensure reliability and equity [[12](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR12), [39](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR39)]. Regular comparisons between AI-generated and human-assigned grades will highlight discrepancies, guiding iterative model refinement to maintain accuracy and trust.

**Increased personalisation**: Adaptive learning technologies will further individualise feedback, tailoring it to each student’s needs, learning styles, and performance history [[3](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR3)]. Such personalisation has the potential to boost engagement and motivation by providing learners with actionable insights aligned to their progress trajectories. Integration with student learning profiles will enable AI systems to adapt feedback dynamically, making interventions more targeted and effective.

**Data-driven insights**: The integration of predictive analytics will allow educators to anticipate performance trends and identify at-risk learners earlier [[1](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR1), [3](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR3)]. By analysing historical and real-time data, AI systems can deliver actionable recommendations that support timely interventions, inform curriculum adjustments, and optimise resource allocation.

**Emphasis on digital literacy**: As adoption grows, both educators and students will need stronger digital literacy skills to maximise the benefits of AI tools [[3](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR3)]. Training in interpreting AI outputs, understanding their limitations, and integrating them into teaching practice will be critical to effective use.

**Future research directions**: Emerging studies are likely to investigate factors that influence the relationship between AI tools and student engagement, such as motivation, learning styles, and institutional support [[75](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR75)]. Mixed-methods approaches combining quantitative and qualitative analyses will strengthen the evidence base [[3](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR3)]. Ethical considerations, accountability, data privacy, bias mitigation, and transparency will remain central [[73](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR73)]. Algorithmic enhancement for more accurate predictions of student success, absenteeism, and exam performance is another promising line of inquiry [[76](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR76)]. Additional exploration is also needed on how educators can best integrate AI into their practice so that it complements, rather than replaces, human judgment [[4](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR4), [73](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR73)]. Research into the effects of AI on students’ cognitive, emotional, and metacognitive skills, particularly in online learning communities, is essential. While still largely theoretical, the potential long-term role of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) in personalised education remains an intriguing research direction. Unlike near-term developments such as adaptive learning or predictive analytics, AGI should be considered speculative and long-term [[73](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR73)]. The potential role of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) in enriching personalised education remains an intriguing area for investigation [[4](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR4)].

Finally, investigation should be guided by internationally recognised ethical frameworks, including UNESCO and IEEE recommendations, to ensure that AI integration supports equitable learning environments and adheres to principles of fairness, accountability, and transparency [[73](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR73), [77](/article/10.1007/s44163-025-00517-0#ref-CR77)]. Together, these developments will help ensure that AI integration in education remains equitable, effective, and adaptable to diverse learning contexts.

9 Conclusion
------------

Artificial intelligence is no longer a speculative addition to higher education assessment but an operational reality capable of reshaping grading and feedback practices at scale. The reviewed evidence demonstrates that AI-powered systems can improve efficiency, consistency, and personalisation, particularly when supported by robust ethical safeguards and informed human oversight. For these benefits to be realised equitably and sustainably, institutional adoption must be strategic, transparent, and inclusive. This involves embedding ethical governance frameworks that prioritise data privacy, algorithmic transparency, and bias mitigation, ensuring that AI tools uphold fairness and accountability in every context. AI should be positioned as a collaborative partner rather than a replacement for educators, enabling them to focus on nuanced evaluation, mentoring, and student engagement while delegating repetitive grading tasks to automated systems.

Equally important is the need for diverse, representative datasets to train AI models, improving accuracy and cultural adaptability across varied educational environments. Capacity building through digital literacy initiatives will help both educators and students interpret AI outputs effectively and integrate them into teaching and learning practices. Continuous validation of AI-generated assessments against human benchmarks remains essential to maintain trust and reliability. From a research perspective, further work is needed to examine AI’s long-term influence on learning behaviours, cognitive skill development, and equity in educational outcomes, as well as to develop scalable frameworks that integrate AI feedback into adaptive learning pathways.

The integration of AI into educational assessment should be guided as much by pedagogical purpose as by technological capability. When implemented thoughtfully, AI can reduce administrative burdens, deliver timely and personalised guidance, and contribute to a more responsive, fair, and data-informed education system. The challenge and opportunity lie in ensuring that AI strengthens, rather than diminishes, the human elements of teaching and learning.
