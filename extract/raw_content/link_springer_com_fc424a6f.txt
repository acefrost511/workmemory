Abstract
--------

This study examines factors influencing teachers' intention to adopt Generative AI technologies in education by extending the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM). The proposed comprehensive model incorporates both external factors (exposure to AI information, information credibility, and institutional support) and internal factors (intrinsic motivation and self-efficacy). A survey of 400 teachers reveals that teachers’ exposure to credible AI information positively influences perceptions of Generative AI usefulness, which ultimately impacts their intention to use AI. Importantly, institutional support has both direct and indirect effects on teachers' intention to use AI, with the indirect effect mediated by the internal factors of intrinsic motivation and self-efficacy. This research complements TAM theory by integrating psychological and contextual factors, offering a nuanced framework for understanding Generative AI adoption in educational settings. The findings suggest that for educational leaders and policymakers, developing strategies that allocate resources for infrastructure, technical support, and professional development—such as Generative AI training programs—will be crucial in driving Generative AI adoption among teachers. By addressing both external and internal determinants, this study provides a comprehensive perspective of the dynamics behind technology acceptance in the classroom.

1 Introduction
--------------

Generative artificial intelligence (AI) has emerged as a transformative force in education (Mello et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR50)), revolutionizing classroom dynamics and creating both opportunities and challenges for teachers. A recent American survey revealed that 51% of teachers use ChatGPT, with 40% incorporating it into their routines and 10% utilizing it daily (Government Technology, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR29)). Various other Generative AI tools—such as Canva, Gamma, Gemini, Heygen, Padlet, Leonardo, and Ping— are also being used in classrooms (Kyrpa et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR41)) for various purposes, such as administrative tasks, content creation, and personalized learning. Each of these tools has more specialized benefits, such as enhancing creativity (e.g., Gamma, Canva), improving student engagement (e.g., Padlet, Leonardo, Heygen, ChatGPT, Curipod), and streamlining classroom management (e.g., MagicSchool, Eduaide). While use of Generative AI improves teaching methods and fosters interactive learning environments (Limna et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR47)) these tools also come with noticeable limitations, including the generation of inaccurate information and limited free access (Kyrpa et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR41)).

Successful implementation of Generative AI in education depends on teachers' awareness and willingness to adopt these tools. Not all teachers use Generative AI in their teaching methods (Kyrpa et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR41)), and AI integration in educational platforms is progressing slowly in many schools or is entirely absent in many others worldwide (Cukurova et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR18)). This highlights the importance of understanding factors influencing teacher acceptance and use of Generative AI. While existing research has explored various aspects of AI in education, including applications (Zawacki-Richter et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR73); K. Zhang & Aslan, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR75)) and implementation challenges (Ji et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR33)), a significant gap remains in understanding the drivers of teachers' perceptions and adoption of AI tools in school teaching. Furthermore, while some research on AI adoption in higher education exists (Chatterjee & Bhattacharjee, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR13)), studies examining AI integration in K-12 settings are scarce. This gap in the literature calls for a comprehensive study to examine the internal and external factors that impact teacher acceptance and use of AI tools in school teaching environments.

Internal factors, such as self-efficacy and intrinsic motivation, are recognized in other contexts as key drivers of behavior (Ajzen, [1991](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR3); Lee & Lee, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR43); Ryan & Deci, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR59)). However, research specifically addressing how these factors shape teachers' adoption of AI in educational settings is limited (Bai et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR9)). Self-efficacy, in particular, may determine teachers’ confidence in navigating AI technologies, while intrinsic motivation could increase their willingness to adopt these tools, driven by personal interest or a commitment to enhancing student outcomes.

Similarly, external factors that influence teachers' perceptions and adoption of AI—such as information sources and institutional support—remain largely underexplored. Credible information sources can play a vital role in enhancing teacher awareness and understanding of the benefits of AI (Al Darayseh, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR5)). Moreover, institutional support, including professional development opportunities and resource allocation, is essential for fostering an educational environment conducive to the successful implementation of generative AI technologies (Kemp et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR34); Sánchez-Prieto et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR60)). Addressing these internal and external determinants is critical to providing a holistic understanding of the factors that drive or hinder AI adoption among teachers.

Thus, the purpose of this study is to investigate the internal and external factors affecting teachers' adoption of Generative AI in educational settings, using the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) as a theoretical framework. While TAM has been widely applied to study technology adoption in education (Granić & Marangunić, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR31); Legris et al., [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR44)), previous research has focused primarily on the acceptance of technology in general rather than in the specific context of Generative AI in teaching (C. Zhang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR76)).

This integrated approach of examining internal and external motivating factors complements the TAM framework and provides a more comprehensive understanding of AI adoption in educational contexts. By doing so, it has the potential to inform strategies for effectively promoting AI integration in educational settings. Additionally, this study provides valuable insights that decision-makers, stakeholders, and teachers can apply for effectively fostering AI integration in educational settings through differentiated support strategies while also promoting a culture of innovation and continuous learning.

2 Theoretical framework and literature review
---------------------------------------------

### 2.1 Generative AI in education

Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to the simulation of human intelligence by machines, enabling interaction with humans to influence behavior and assist in accomplishing tasks beyond individual capabilities (Chintalapati & Pandey, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR15); Holmes & Tuomi, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR32); Uzir et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR68)). AI in education is called AIEd (Mello et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR50); Schiff, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR62)), and refers to technology designed to support teachers by automating routine tasks, providing actionable insights, and enhancing instructional planning (Feuerriegel et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR23); Kyrpa et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR41); Velander et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR69)). In the 1960s, ELIZA was one of the first attempts to simulate human-like conversation using simple pattern-matching techniques. ELIZA’s primary function was to engage users in dialogue, mimicking a therapeutic conversation (Al-Amin et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR7)). This early AI system laid the foundation for more advanced AIEd technologies by demonstrating how AI could facilitate learning through interaction. Over the decades, AI has evolved from basic systems to advanced applications, such as *generative AI*, which combines machine learning and natural language processing to power tools like ChatGPT, Canva, Eduaide, Gamma, and Gemini, transforming the way complex educational tasks are handled (Feuerriegel et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR23); Kyrpa et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR41); Velander et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR69)).

AIEd plays many roles and has many advantages, such as improving teacher productivity, offering support, and resolving issues (Bai et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR9); Cukurova et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR18); Wang et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR71)). Moreover, it includes tailoring learning experiences for students, streamlining assessment processes, and utilizing predictive analytics (Mello et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR50); Velander et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR69)). It reduces administrative burdens by managing student records, tracking attendance, and handling other logistical tasks, freeing teachers to focus on their core responsibilities (Feuerriegel et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR23); Kyrpa et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR41); Velander et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR69)). AIEd tasks involve analyzing student data, creating learning environments, and integrating resources (C. Zhang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR76)). It helps manage student records, evaluate assignments, and identify learning difficulties through automated analysis (Tuomi, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR67)). Its primary aim is to provide assistance that meets the needs of each student (Limna et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR47)) while allowing teachers to focus on other responsibilities (Tuomi, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR67)). In addition, teachers can enhance their skills through AI-powered teaching assistants and improve their productivity (Holmes & Tuomi, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR32)). AI tools improve classroom engagement by creating dynamic and interactive learning experiences that keep students motivated. Moreover, AI-enhanced e-learning platforms optimize efficiency by delivering task-relevant, real-time learning solutions tailored to the evolving demands of modern education systems and dynamically changing environments (Feuerriegel et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR23); Kyrpa et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR41); Mahmoud et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR48); Velander et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR69)).

However, these advantages come with some concerns about encouraging teachers to use AI in their work (Bai et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR9); Cukurova et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR18); Wang et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR71)). These concerns often involve teachers’ readiness and the potential impact on their teaching roles, stemming from a lack of familiarity with AI tools, uncertainty about their effectiveness, and fears of being replaced or overshadowed by technology, among other factors. Additionally, teachers may worry about the time and effort required to learn and integrate AI into their practices, as well as ethical considerations like data privacy, algorithmic bias, academic integrity, and student autonomy (Kyrpa et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR41)). Without sufficient institutional support, training, and alignment with teaching goals, these concerns can lead to resistance or superficial implementation (Gabby et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR27); Velander et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR69)). Overall, developing and implementing strategies involving AI is essential for meeting the needs of both teachers and students, and potentially improving performance (Limna et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR47)). The focus here is not on replacing teaching methods but rather enhancing them for the digital era (Flogie & Krabonja, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR25)).

### 2.2 AI acceptance among teachers

Integration of AI presents new challenges for teachers, especially those with less experience in technology (Limna et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR47)), and as with employees in other fields, their role in accepting or resisting change is common and crucial (Choi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR17); Du & Gao, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR21); Limna et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR47)). Yet, teachers differ from other professionals due to their significant instructional autonomy, which allows them to independently choose their teaching methods and the extent of technology integration (Chiu et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR16)). This autonomy makes teacher-specific factors critical to understanding their acceptance of new technologies like Generative AI. Analyzing these factors collectively is crucial for understanding the behavior of technology adoption (C. Zhang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR76)).

The literature in education provides valuable established theories to explain technology adoption behavior (Granić, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR30)), such as the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) (Davis, [1989](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR19)) and the subsequent Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) (Venkatesh et al., [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR70)). While UTAUT incorporates social influences and facilitating conditions that are more relevant in a collaborative or directive environment, TAM is preferable in this study because of its simplicity and its alignment with teacher-specific factors like pedagogical autonomy. Furthermore, TAM’s core principles focus on the individual level, which makes it particularly effective for studying technology adoption in autonomous settings like teaching (Al-Adwan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR6); Granić & Marangunić, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR31)).

According to the TAM framework, two crucial factors play a role in shaping a user's willingness to adopt a technology. The first factor is perceived usefulness, which reflects user belief that the technology will help them achieve their goals. The second factor is perceived ease of use, which involves user confidence that the technology is friendly and easy to understand and use (Davis et al., [1989](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR20)). Research has demonstrated that these core variables serve as fundamental drivers of teacher acceptance of learning technologies in educational settings (Granić & Marangunić, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR31); Sánchez-Prieto et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR60)). In the context of AI, a recent study has demonstrated that perceived usefulness and ease of use are important factors that influence intentions to use AI (C. Zhang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR76)). In line with these studies, it is suggested that TAM's core principles (perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use) relate to teachers' intention to use Generative AI. Therefore, the following hypotheses are proposed:

* **Hypothesis** **1** (**H1).** Perceived usefulness (PU) is positively related to teachers' intention to use AI.
* **Hypothesis** **2 (H2).** Perceived ease of use (PEU) is positively related to teachers' intention to use AI.

While TAM provides valuable insights, it may not fully capture the complexities that influence teacher adoption of AI technology, necessitating an examination of additional factors crucial for a comprehensive understanding of what motivates teachers to adopt AI in the classroom.

Addressing this complexity requires consideration of both internal and external factors that affect teacher willingness to adopt AI in their work (Limna et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR47)). The combination of both kinds of factors is important in the adoption process (Ajzen, [1991](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR3); C. Zhang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR76)). In addition to these factors, teacher awareness of AI plays a key role in making effective decisions about adopting AI. Awareness and exposure to credible information sources can shape teacher attitudes and behaviors toward AI adoption (Zimand-Sheiner et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR77)).

### 2.3 Credibility of AI information sources

Source credibility is the confidence or trusts that people have in a particular source, which refers to the extent to which the information in the message source is perceived to be competent, believable, accurate, fair, trustworthy, objective, and unbiased (Moran & Muzellec, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR52); Shin, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR65); Zimand-Sheiner et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR77)). Providing dependable scientific information is vital for making important decisions. People are more likely to adopt and rely on information from trusted channels which they believe to be objective and factual. Moreover, people evaluate the credibility of information based on source expertise and reliability (Moran & Muzellec, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR52); Zimand-Sheiner et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR77)). Therefore, when the source is seen as credible, it may have a stronger impact on teachers' willingness to use AI.

The diverse sources of information about AI significantly influence perceptions of its value and importance in integrating AI tools into daily activities, consumer behavior, and educational practices (Gerashi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR28); Kol et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR39); Moran & Muzellec, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR52); Uzir et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR68)). Consequently, this study employs credibility as a multidimensional variable, encompassing the perceived trustworthiness of four primary information channels that teachers typically encounter: authoritative sources, personal word-of-mouth, electronic word-of-mouth, and traditional mass media.

Authoritative sources are information providers, such as government agencies (e.g., Ministry of Education) or policymakers and institutional leaders (e.g., school principals), with the authority to influence decisions, shape policies, and guide practices within the AI domain. These sources play a crucial role in shaping the educational landscape and implementing policies. The Ministry of Education is not only responsible for creating national policy frameworks but also for disseminating guidelines, offering professional development programs, and allocating funding to ensure effective implementation of AI technologies (Miao et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR51)). School principals are responsible for ensuring seamless integration of new policies received from the Ministry of Education (Avidov-Ungar & Reingold, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR8); Gabby et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR27)). Information from these policymakers is generally perceived as credible, although building trust and credibility requires fairness, transparency, and accountability (Ajala, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR2); Flanagin & Metzger, [2007](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR24); Zimand-Sheiner et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR77)).

Word-of-mouth (WOM) communication, defined as oral, person-to-person communication within interpersonal networks (ShabbirHusain & Varshney, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR63); Zimand-Sheiner et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR77)), serves as another significant information channel. In education, peers provide comprehensive information and articulate their experiences and skills, enhancing the credibility of transmitted information (Nowak & McGloin, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR53)). Positive WOM recommendations from satisfied teachers facilitate increased awareness and adoption of new technologies (Zeqiri et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR74)).

Electronic word-of-mouth (eWOM), which involves sharing peers' opinions online (Kol & Levy, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR40); Kol & Lissitsa, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR38)), significantly influences perceptions and decision-making regarding the adoption of AI tools (ShabbirHusain & Varshney, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR63); Shin, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR65)). eWOM encompasses social media platforms, blogs, forums, and review sites that allow teachers to exchange knowledge on a larger scale. However, the anonymity of eWOM can raise doubts about information credibility (Cheung & Thadani, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR14)), potentially affecting teachers' willingness to adopt AI technologies in their teaching (ShabbirHusain & Varshney, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR63)). Despite these challenges, eWOM is a valuable platform for teachers to exchange knowledge and provide mutual support in integrating AI into education, emphasizing the importance of transmitting credible and trustworthy information with full transparency and objectivity (Kol et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR39)).

Mass communication, encompassing both traditional channels (television, radio, print media) and modern digital platforms (online news sites), represents the fourth major information source (Ajala, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR2); Zimand-Sheiner et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR77)). Mass media plays a vital role in disseminating information about artificial intelligence (AI) and enhancing public understanding and acceptance (Li et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR46)). Emphasizing the crucial nature of media credibility and delivering factual information to a broad audience influence public opinion (Ajala, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR2); Zimand-Sheiner et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR77)), and may shape teachers’ expectations about AI.

In short, the general credibility of AI information formed from these four information sources may directly affect teachers’ consideration of adopting AI tools in their teaching methods (Moran & Muzellec, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR52)). The Ministry of Education, through its authoritative role, provides the foundational trust required for large-scale adoption of AI by offering clear guidelines, accessible resources, and ongoing training opportunities. These efforts shape teachers' perceptions of AI tools’ usefulness in their work. Similarly, the influence of credible mass media, WOM, and eWOM amplifies the accessibility and perceived reliability of this information. Furthermore, research indicates that information credibility relates to the perceived usefulness of examined technology (Kol et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR39)) and may specifically impact teachers’ perception of AI usefulness and its feasibility in their work. Therefore, the following hypothesis is proposed:

* **Hypothesis 3** (**H3):** The credibility of AI information sources (authoritative sources, mass sources, and word-of-mouth) is positively related to a teacher's perceived usefulness of AI.

### 2.4 Exposure to AI information

Teachers are exposed to information through many channels, including traditional and digital sources, professional discussions, workshops, and collaborative educational projects (Francke & Sundin, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR26)). This exposure to diverse sources highlights the importance of critical thinking and the ability to evaluate information and its credibility, thus enabling teachers to address the challenges of distinguishing between reliable and unreliable sources (Schemm, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR61); Shin, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR65)).

In the context of AI, access to credible information is particularly significant in shaping teachers’ awareness and understanding of AI tools and their potential benefits in education (Bai et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR9); Cukurova et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR18); Wang et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR71)). Credible information refers to data or content from trusted familiar sources that teachers recognize and have experience with, ensuring reliability (Al Darayseh, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR5); Moran & Muzellec, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR52)). Research supports the idea that individuals are more likely to adopt information from sources they perceive as trustworthy, objective, and factual (Moran & Muzellec, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR52); Zimand-Sheiner et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR77)).

Greater exposure to credible sources can enhance teachers' ability to identify consistent, evidence-based information, which is vital for informed decision-making regarding AI adoption in education (Moran & Muzellec, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR52); Shin, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR65)). Furthermore, such exposure not only builds teachers’ knowledge but also enables them to critically evaluate AI tools and distinguish between those supported by scientific evidence and those that are not. Professional interactions, such as workshops, AI-focused training, and collaborative projects, further contribute to teachers' capacity to critically evaluate information (Francke & Sundin, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR26)). This relationship between exposure and credibility is especially relevant in the context of AI, as it affects teachers' ability to evaluate and trust information. Therefore, the following hypothesis is proposed:

* **Hypothesis 4** (**H4)**: Higher exposure to AI information sources is positively related to the credibility of AI information.

### 2.5 Internal factors

Factors such as intrinsic motivation and self-efficacy are crucial internal factors that help understand what drives teachers to adopt AI tools (Sánchez-Prieto et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR60)). Intrinsic motivation and self-efficacy are closely related (Tabernero & Hernández, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR66)), reinforcing each other in the context of AIEd adoption. While intrinsic motivation drives teachers to explore AI tools out of interest and satisfaction, self-efficacy reflects the confidence and capability teachers feel to integrate these tools effectively.

#### 2.5.1 Intrinsic motivation

*Intrinsic motivation* refers to the internal drive to engage in an activity for its inherent satisfaction, interest, or personal value, rather than for external rewards or pressures (Ryan & Deci, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR59)). According to Cognitive Evaluation Theory (CET) and Self -Determination Theory (SDT), intrinsic motivation is a psychological factor that is influenced by self-direction, independence, and the feeling of choice (Bieg et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR11); Khun-Inkeeree et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR35); Reiss, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR58)) It plays a vital role in shaping human behavior and attitudes (Shahzad et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR64)).

In the context of AIEd, intrinsic motivation emerges as a critical driver encouraging teachers to explore and adopt AI tools. This motivation stems from their desire to employ innovative teaching strategies and the personal satisfaction derived from enhancing student learning outcomes (Yang, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR72)). Research suggests that intrinsic motivation significantly influences teachers' intentions to integrate AI tools, particularly when these tools align closely with their professional goals and teaching objectives (Bukhari et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR12); ORAN, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR55)). Moreover, the impact of intrinsic motivation is amplified when paired with a supportive and encouraging learning environment and acknowledgement of teacher achievements, thus fostering greater engagement and promoting active learning (Lai et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR42)). Intrinsic motivation fosters a sense of autonomy and competence, which are foundational elements of self-efficacy (Tabernero & Hernández, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR66)). However, intrinsic motivation to adopt AI tools challenges teachers to perceive themselves as capable of understanding and effectively using these technologies (Khun-Inkeeree et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR35)).

Teachers with high intrinsic motivation are particularly receptive to the potential benefits of adopting AI technologies, including personal growth, professional satisfaction, skill development, and relevance to their goals. This openness enhances their willingness to embrace tools that support autonomy, competence, and meaningful connections (Bieg et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR11); Bukhari et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR12); Khun-Inkeeree et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR35)). By nurturing intrinsic motivation, institutions can indirectly foster the acceptance and effective utilization of AI technologies in educational settings. Building on this foundation, the following hypothesis is proposed:

* **Hypothesis 5** (**H5).** Intrinsic motivation is positively related to perceived usefulness (PU).

#### 2.5.2 Self-efficacy

Bandura defines self-efficacy as an individual's awareness of their belief in their ability to organize and perform tasks successfully (Al Darayseh, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR5); Bandura, [1994](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR10); Lee & Lee, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR43)). Simply put, it reflects a person's confidence in their ability to accomplish tasks and achieve goals (Akram et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR4)).

In education, teachers' confidence in their ability to integrate new technology is crucial. Studies show that belief in oneself could be more important than skills and knowledge for teachers using technology in classrooms (Ertmer & Ottenbreit-Leftwich, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR22)). Self-confidence is often built on the basis of achievements, overcoming obstacles, and determination. Accomplishing tasks successfully increases one's confidence in managing challenges. Failure can reduce self-belief, but determination and sustained effort also play a significant role in enhancing self-efficacy (Al Darayseh, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR5)). Teachers who feel skilled in using AI tools are more motivated and open to welcoming them into their teaching methods. Teachers with high self-efficacy and confidence can find AI integration easier and will be more prepared to deal with any obstacles that come with adopting it until they master the process (Afari et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR1); Al Darayseh, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR5); Lee & Lee, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR43)).

Teachers can nurture their self-efficacy by engaging in collaborative knowledge practices such as co-creation and sharing experiences, and by seeking guidance or support from others. This is vital for increasing confidence and belief in the application of new ideas. Learning from peers and receiving support from colleagues helps to contend with the barriers and challenges encountered when adopting new practices. In addition, participating in new technology in real classroom settings provides opportunities for mastery experiences that strengthen and enhance self-efficacy (Ley et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR45)). Contrarily, emotional states such as stress, anxiety, fatigue, and physical discomfort can impact and reduce self-efficacy through subjective interpretation (Lee & Lee, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR43)).

Thus, increasing the self-efficacy of teachers is a key strategy for promoting the successful integration of AI in educational settings, and their perception of it as easier to use. Therefore, the following hypothesis is proposed:

* **Hypothesis 6 (H6).** *Higher self-efficacy is positively related to perceived ease of use (PEU).*

### 2.6 External factor—institutional support

Educational institutions, including policymakers such as the Ministry of Education and governing bodies of various schools, play a key role in supporting and facilitating the adoption of AI technologies among teachers. The Ministry of Education influences and shapes the educational environment through its multifaceted role in policymaking, strategic planning, investment, partnerships, professional development programs, technical support, and collaboration initiatives (Kemp et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR34); Sánchez-Prieto et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR60)). With respect to AI integration in schools, the ministry has established standards and policies for responsible AI technology use, along with awarding professional development credits to incentivize teacher engagement in AI training, respecting teacher autonomy, and providing schools with the necessary infrastructure and tools for effective AI implementation (Al Darayseh, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR5); Chiu et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR16); OECD, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR54); Popenici & Kerr, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR57); Zimand-Sheiner et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR77)).

At the school level, support for AI adoption comes from both the private and public sectors, including municipal and ownership-based institutions. Schools rely on the Ministry for guidance, resources, and training opportunities, which contribute to creating an environment that is supportive of AI adoption. As a result, the collaboration between schools and the Ministry is required to ensure that teachers have access to infrastructure and technology and to encourage them to participate in professional training courses, in addition to encouraging collaborative work between teachers and planning lessons with AI resources (Gabby et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR27); Kemp et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR34); Popenici & Kerr, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR57)).

Additionally, teacher autonomy, defined as the perception that teachers have regarding control over themselves and their work environment, plays an essential role in making decisions about using technologies; for example, curriculum autonomy is related to job stress, motivation, job satisfaction, professionalism, and empowerment (Pearson & Moomaw, [2005](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR56)). Autonomy allows teachers to experiment with new technology and improve their teaching methods, contributing to successfully integrating AI-enhanced resources.

Thus, the Ministry of Education and schools play complementary roles in AI adoption among teachers, while environmental factors significantly contribute to technology acceptance (Granić & Marangunić, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR31)). A collaborative, flexible, and adaptive environment that addresses school and student diversity while ensuring accessibility to tools and resources is essential for promoting AI use among teachers and raising awareness of its usefulness and ease of use (Chiu et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR16)). Therefore, the following hypotheses are proposed:

* **Hypothesis 7a (H7a).** *Institutional support is positively related to perceived usefulness (PU).*
* **Hypothesis 7b (H7b).** *Institutional support is positively related to perceived ease of use (PEU).*

3 Research design
-----------------

### 3.1 Data collection and sample

Data was collected using a self-administered questionnaire to test the hypotheses empirically. The university’s ethics committee confirmed that the study meets the conditions set out in the procedure for approving a study that is not a clinical trial in humans. Participants were recruited with the help of the Israeli Teachers' Bureau, which distributed the questionnaire by email to a group of its members. Additionally, reminders were sent through teachers' Facebook and WhatsApp groups. All participants were assured of confidentiality, and consent to participate was obtained before the survey began. Teachers who consented to participate were provided with access to the online questionnaire via a digital link. This approach was designed to reach teachers with proficiency in using digital platforms, which is a fundamental aspect of using AI tools. Data collection lasted approximately one month. It took about 15 min for participants to complete the questionnaire.

The study focused on currently employed schoolteachers, excluding retirees. Initially, 432 respondents participated in the survey. After removing those who didn't meet the criteria or provided incomplete responses, the final sample comprised 400 usable responses (12.2% response rate). The sample included teachers from across the country, 79% of them women and 21% of them men. This gender distribution aligns with the national composition of teachers in Israel, as reported in the Comptroller's report on Israel's education system (Knesset, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR37)). Participants ranged in age from 24 to 67 years (M = 45.16, SD = 9.43), with teaching experience ranging from 1 to 41 years (M = 16.87, SD = 10.4). The majority (93.6%) possessed at least an intermediate level of English proficiency, and 72.8% held a Master's degree or higher. This sample represents diverse cultural and educational contexts from several different religions, including Jewish (48%), Muslim (33%), and Christian (19%) teachers. The schools included both elementary (39%) and secondary (61%) levels, ensuring a wide K-12 spectrum of educational stages. The participating teachers represent a broad range of disciplines, categorized into humanities (36%; e.g., history, citizenship, literature, and art) and sciences (31%; e.g., mathematics, biology, chemistry, and computer science), as well as interdisciplinary subjects (33%; e.g., social sciences, business management, technology and special education).

The majority of participants (91.2%) had some familiarity with AI tools in general; 85.7% of them were specifically familiar with Generative AIEd tools (e.g., ChatGPT, BARD, Canva, Tome, Gamma, Bing, etc.). Moreover, 73% reported actively using these tools in practice while 30.75% of the participants have received Generative AI training (see Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#Tab1)).

A confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) procedure was used to estimate construct validity and reliability. The results show an acceptable fit for all the measurements (χ2(1360) = 3399.54, p < 0.05 (χ2/df < 3); comparative fit index (CFI) = 0.942; normed fit index (NFI) = 0.903; root mean square error of approximation (RMSEA) = 0.058); and Standardized Root Mean Square Residual (SRMR) = 0.049; see Table [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#Tab3)). The constructed standardized regression estimates were all above 0.50, indicating an acceptable fit of the measures. Convergent validity and internal consistency were examined and confirmed via average variance extracted (AVE), composite reliability (CR), and Cronbach's alpha (see Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#Tab2)). AVE values exceeded the 0.50 threshold, while CR values surpassed 0.70, meeting recommended standards. The findings confirm that the measurement model exhibits adequate validity and reliability, ensuring the robustness of subsequent analyses.

5 Discussion and conclusions
----------------------------

Generative AI has the power to reshape education, but very little is known about what motivates teachers to adopt these advanced technologies in their classrooms. On the basis of the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) (Davis, [1989](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR19)), this study presents a conceptual framework for explaining teachers' AI adoption behavior. Additionally, the theoretical model is complemented by integrating internal factors, such as intrinsic motivation and self-efficacy, and institutional support as external influencing factors.

First, the findings correspond with previous research (Granić & Marangunić, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR31); Legris et al., [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR44)), emphasizing the importance of TAM's core principles in the adoption of AI technology for education. These results highlight that when teachers perceive AI tools to be beneficial and easy to use, they are more likely to integrate them into their teaching practices, reinforcing the predictive power of the Technology Acceptance Model in educational contexts.

Second, internal factors, including teachers' intrinsic motivation and self-efficacy, are found to have a positive relationship with the perceived usefulness and ease of use of AI technology, respectively. These findings are consistent with previous research in education, which highlights the connection between intrinsic motivation and perceived usefulness (Tabernero & Hernández, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR66); Yang, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR72)), as well as between self-efficacy and perceived ease of use (Bukhari et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR12); Lai et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR42)). Hence, teachers who are interested in technology and believe in their ability to implement it are more open to integrating AI into their teaching practices. These findings suggest that cultivating these internal drivers can facilitate AI adoption. Intrinsic motivation drives teachers to explore AI tools out of curiosity and satisfaction, while self-efficacy ensures they feel confident and capable of effectively integrating these tools. The positive association between intrinsic motivation and perceived usefulness suggests that teachers who are driven by curiosity to explore AI are more likely to appreciate its advantages. Similarly, teachers with higher self-efficacy in using AI tend to find it easier to use, reflecting the connection between self-belief and perceived ease of use.

Third, the dual role of institutional support emerges as a key finding. Contrary to our expectations, the relationship between external factors, particularly institutional support, did not impact AI adoption through perceived usefulness or ease of use. This can be attributed to implementation gaps (Kizilcec, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR36)). While institutions may provide support in terms of policy, infrastructure, or general guidelines, this support often falls short of addressing the specific, actionable needs of teachers who adopt AI tools. This failure to provide practical, actionable guidance tailored to teachers’ specific needs makes AI less relevant to their day-to-day teaching contexts. Furthermore, teachers' autonomy in selecting technologies also plays a significant role, as they prioritize tools that reflect their unique teaching styles, relying on personal experiences or peer recommendations rather than institutional directives (Mazman Akar, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR49)).

Thus, institutional support was found to exhibit both direct and indirect paths to teacher intention to use AI. The direct path suggests that institutional support, such as providing resources, training, and infrastructure, can directly influence teacher intentions to adopt AI. Simultaneously, the indirect path is through the hierarchical mediation of internal factors and traditional technology adoption factors such as ease of use and usefulness. This finding reveals that institutional support positively impacts teachers' intrinsic motivation and self-efficacy, which in turn influence their perceptions of usefulness and ease of use, respectively. These results suggest two types of teachers: some directly adopt AI if it is recommended institutionally, whereas others do so only if internal drivers such as intrinsic motivation and self-efficacy are strengthened. This finding suggests that a supportive environment, a technical foundation, and available technical tools are important, but the psychological factors within a supportive environment, such as autonomy, encouragement, and professional learning opportunities, could play a vital role and be more crucial to encouraging teachers to adopt new technologies in their practice (Legris et al., [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR44)).

Fourth, the findings emphasize the importance of providing teachers with credible AI information to enhance their perception of its usefulness. Teachers who regard AI-related information as trustworthy are more likely to recognize its practical benefits and integrate it into their work. Sharing reliable and authoritative information through institutional channels is crucial for fostering positive views of AI's potential (Zimand-Sheiner et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR77)). Credible sources, such as ministry guidelines, peer recommendations, and traditional mass media, play a vital role in raising awareness and helping teachers understand AI's advantages (Ajala, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR2); Al Darayseh, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13393-z#ref-CR5)).

### 5.1 Theoretical contribution

This study makes several important theoretical contributions to the acceptance and intention to use AI technologies, particularly in the context of teacher adoption.

First, by integrating the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) with additional factors, this study provides a more inclusive theoretical framework for understanding the complex interaction of variables influencing teachers' intentions to use AI. Traditional TAM primarily focuses on perceived usefulness and ease of use as determinants of technology acceptance. This study extends TAM by incorporating intrinsic motivational factors (intrinsic motivation and self-efficacy) and external contextual factors (institutional support and information credibility), creating a more comprehensive model that addresses the specific needs and challenges of educational contexts.

By integrating intrinsic motivation as a predictor of perceived usefulness, the study emphasizes the role of teachers’ internal drive and interest in adopting AI tools. Similarly, self-efficacy is linked to ease of use, highlighting the importance of teachers' confidence in their ability to implement AI successfully. These findings demonstrate that teacher adoption of AI is not merely a function of its functional utility but also depends on fostering personal interest and building confidence. This advancement deepens the understanding of TAM by showing how psychological factors amplify and interact with perceived usefulness and ease of use, offering a more holistic perspective on technology adoption in education.

This study further enhances TAM by incorporating contextual external factors—particularly institutional support and information credibility—that are vital for understanding AI adoption in educational settings.

The study contributes a new perspective on the dual nature of the education system with respect to technology adoption and sheds light on the complex relationship between institutional support and adoption. This study reveals the dual pathways through which institutional support affects teacher intentions to use AI directly and indirectly through its impact on intrinsic motivation and self-efficacy. This dual process view contributes to a better understanding of how organizational factors can influence individual perceptions and motivations, eventually influencing technology adoption.

> Lastly, by including information credibility as an external factor, the study expands TAM to account for the quality and reliability of information teachers receive about AI tools. The credibility of institutional or external information sources impacts teachers’ perceptions of the benefits and feasibility of using AI in their classrooms, underscoring the necessity of trustworthy communication channels to foster adoption.

Overall, this study advances the theoretical discourse on technology acceptance by proposing a comprehensive model that integrates external factors (institutional support and information credibility) with internal factors (intrinsic motivation and self-efficacy) within the established TAM framework. This enriched model provides a stronger foundation for understanding the variables influencing teacher adoption of AI technologies and highlights the interconnectedness of psychological, organizational, and contextual factors. Doing so advances our understanding of the complex relationships among the variables influencing teacher adoption of AI technologies, providing a strong theoretical foundation for future research in this field.

### 5.2 Implications for practitioners and policymakers

This study offers several important implications for practitioners and policymakers seeking to promote and facilitate the adoption of AI technologies in education. A supportive environment for AI integration must be established by school leaders and policymakers, with comprehensive strategies that emphasize the practical benefits of AI tools. These efforts should leverage credible channels such as Ministry of Education guidelines to ensure accurate and trustworthy communication.

Allocating resources is an essential foundation for AI adoption, encompassing investments in infrastructure, tools, and technical support to remove barriers and ensure equitable access across schools. For instance, hands-on workshops, where teachers can interact with AI tools like ChatGPT or Canva, are particularly effective in building confidence and illustrating practical applications such as content creation (e.g., creating lesson plans), administrative tasks (e.g., automating grading) and personalized learning.

Providing targeted information about AI through workshops, online materials, and collaborative learning communities is equally critical. Collaborative cohorts where teachers co-design AI-enhanced lesson plans foster a sense of community and shared learning. Peer-shared success stories and case studies can further demonstrate AI's real-world benefits, inspiring broader adoption by showcasing its impact on tasks like simplifying administration and enhancing student engagement.

Tailored training programs are vital for building teachers' self-efficacy and intrinsic motivation. These programs should focus on practical, real-world applications of AI, transparency in how AI tools function, and addressing privacy concerns to build trust. Exposing teachers to accurate information about AI through trusted channels—such as ministry guidelines, peer case studies, and professional communities—reduces misconceptions and fosters positive attitudes toward the usefulness and ease of use of AI tools.

Policymakers should prioritize the development of comprehensive strategies that allocate resources for infrastructure, technical support, and professional development. Recognition programs or rewards for innovative AI implementation can further nurture a culture of exploration and continuous learning.

Additionally, approaches should be tailored to the diverse needs of teachers. For intrinsically motivated teachers, autonomy and flexibility are essential to encourage innovation and creativity. Strengthening internal motivations through positive evaluations and meaningful incentives can foster a culture of experimentation. For teachers who rely on institutional guidelines, clear and transparent directives from education ministries, coupled with training programs aligned with individual teaching styles, can enhance self-efficacy and motivation, facilitating seamless AI integration.

By addressing these factors, educational leaders and policymakers can create an environment that supports AI adoption, fosters teacher confidence, and drives meaningful innovation in teaching practices.

### 5.3 Limitations and future research

Some limitations should be noted. First, this study focuses on the Israeli education system. Therefore, future research could examine this study's conceptual model in the settings of other countries to strengthen the results. This would consider specific regional and cultural conditions. Moreover, this study examined teachers without delving into potential gender differences in AI adoption. Future research could explore how gender influences the adoption of AI.

Second, since technological development is fast and dramatically changing, especially in the field of AI, it is recommended that additional studies be conducted at different times. This is to detect any changes in public attitudes and behavior.

Third, the study focused on teachers with some proficiency and ability in using digital tools, as data collection was conducted via an online questionnaire. This approach may limit the inclusion of teachers who lack experience or access to digital platforms. Future research could broaden the scope by employing paper-based questionnaires or alternative data collection methods to analyze the behaviors and opinions of a more diverse population of teachers.

Fourth, this study was conducted within the specific context of K-12 education, focusing on the unique challenges and opportunities faced by teachers in this setting. The findings may not fully capture the dynamics of AI adoption in other educational contexts, such as higher education, and informal learning environments. Therefore, future research should explore how the proposed model applies to these diverse contexts. These extensions will help validate and generalize the findings across varied educational ecosystems.

Finally, issues such as teachers’ worries about job stability or ethical concerns regarding data privacy could be areas to investigate. Understanding what factors contribute to teacher resistance in this area could assist researchers in devising strategies for ethically integrating AI tools into educational environments. In addition, researchers could examine how school leadership and administrative policies influence teacher attitudes and behaviors toward AI. This exploration could guide the creation of environments that foster technological innovation in education. In general, there appear to be paths for future research built upon the groundwork laid by this initial study.
