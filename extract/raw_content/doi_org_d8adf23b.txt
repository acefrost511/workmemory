## Abstract
Generative AI can augment diagnostic learning, yet the impact of explanation style on novices’ reasoning remains unclear. We evaluate two styles—technical (evidence-/probability-based) and analogical (case-based)—in a randomized controlled trial with 60 third- and fourth-year medical students who diagnosed twelve de-identified acute abdominal pain cases. After an initial judgment, participants were randomized to one of three conditions (technical, analogical, or control \[no AI advice\]) and received ex post AI advice (or none in control) while thinking aloud; outcomes were diagnostic accuracy, confidence calibration, and cognitive load analyzed via mixed-effects models and protocol analysis. Both AI styles improved accuracy over control; analogical yielded the highest accuracy and the lowest cognitive load, whereas technical produced the best confidence calibration but increased load. Think-aloud data showed that technical explanations triggered deeper evidence search, broader differentials, and more active agreement/disagreement with the AI; when the AI was incorrect in 2 of 12 cases, students in the technical condition were less likely to follow it, though estimates were imprecise. Results were robust to alternative measures and specifications and consistent across typical/atypical cases. Positioning explanation style as a design lever for AI-assisted learning, we show how design choices translate into generalizable knowledge for diagnostic training and decision calibration. Technical explanations are suited to cultivating critical reasoning and calibrated confidence; analogical explanations efficiently support Hypotheses generation when cognitive bandwidth is limited.
## Introduction
The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into healthcare has revolutionized clinical decision-making, especially in diagnostics, where AI systems excel at analyzing complex medical data to deliver precise predictions (Esteva et al., 2019). Generative AI, with its ability to generate human-like explanations, holds particular promise for medical education by scaffolding diagnostic skills among novices (Chew & Achananuparp, 2022). Yet, the efficacy of such AI assistance hinges critically on how outputs are presented, notably the style of explanations provided. This study examines how two generative AI explanation styles—technical (focusing on clinical evidence and probabilistic reasoning) and analogical (drawing on case-based similarities)—influence medical students’ diagnostic decision-making, with emphasis on accuracy, confidence calibration, and cognitive load.
As novice diagnosticians, medical students grapple with processing intricate clinical information amid limited expertise and elevated cognitive demands (Fraser et al., 2015). Cognitive load theory posits that information presentation shapes learning efficiency: overly complex formats inflate extraneous load, impeding performance, while optimized designs foster germane load for schema-building (Sweller, 2020). Complementing this, dual-process theory delineates intuitive (System 1) processing—fast and pattern-driven but bias-prone—and analytical (System 2) processing—deliberate and evidence-based but effort-intensive (Kahneman et al., 2011; Mance, 2022). AI explanations can modulate these cognitive modes: technical explanations may activate System 2, enhancing calibration through critical evaluation yet potentially heightening cognitive load; analogical explanations could leverage System 1 for intuitive pattern recognition, boosting accuracy but potentially encouraging superficial engagement and biases. This dual-process interpretation extrapolates established theory to AI-generated explanations (Kahneman et al., 2011; Mance, 2022) and is conceptually consistent with evidence on cognitive biases in prehospital diagnostic decision-making synthesized by Awanzo and Thompson (2025), even though their review does not explicitly adopt System-1/System-2 terminology.
Empirical evidence on AI in healthcare diagnostics remains equivocal. While some research highlights accuracy gains from human–AI synergy (Li et al., 2022; Tschandl et al., 2020), others report marginal or null benefits, often attributed to opaque advice or user overload (Aoki et al., 2020; Cabitza et al., 2023; Kiani et al., 2020). Explainable AI (XAI) mitigates these issues via interpretable rationales, yet optimal explanation designs for novices—particularly in balancing transparency with usability—are underexplored (Busch et al., 2023; Jussupow et al., 2021). Recent insights into AI advice timing indicate that ex post delivery (post-initial judgment) promotes deeper engagement than ex ante advice (Yin et al., 2025). Extending this foundation, we concentrate on ex post advice to probe how explanation styles affect novices’ performance, filling a void in understanding how design choices facilitate knowledge acquisition and mitigate cognitive pitfalls in medical training.
This study advances the field on several fronts. First, it enriches XAI scholarship by elucidating how explanation styles cognitively impact medical students, complementing timing-focused studies (Cabitza et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2025) and underscoring design as a lever for human–AI knowledge co-creation. Second, it helps resolve inconsistencies in AI's diagnostic utility by spotlighting explanation design's moderating role, yielding actionable insights for educational innovation. Third, it illuminates trade-offs between cognitive load and confidence calibration in novice training, addressing a pressing gap in healthcare knowledge management.
