Abstract
--------

The rapid advancements in modern technologies have opened new possibilities for enhancing educational experiences for students with Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND). This paper conducts a systematic review of 139 studies on the integration of AI, VR, and LLMs in Special Education. Using a deductive thematic analysis framework, it identifies key themes and challenges to synthesize the current state of knowledge and propose future research directions. The findings underscore the transformative potential of AI and Immersive Technologies in fostering personalized learning, improving social engagement, and advancing cognitive development among SEND students. Additionally, current SEN methodologies and practices are defined, teachers'attitudes toward inclusion and technology adoption, and the prevailing technological tools utilized, based on various syndromes and disorders. Challenges such as ethical considerations, accessibility barriers, and resource limitations are also discussed. Moreover, this study explores tailored technological tools specifically designed to meet the unique needs of these students. The paper concludes with limitations, recommendations for cross-sector collaboration and inclusive policies to ensure these technologies are effectively utilized to prioritize and enhance learning experiences for SEND students, as well as future directions.

1 Introduction
--------------

In 1975, the Education for All Handicapped Children Act (EAHCA), renamed the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), stood for the educational rights of all children, regardless of their disability (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR95)). In 1994, in Salamanca, Spain, 92 governments and 25 organizations embraced inclusive education under the motto"Education for All"(The Salamanca Statement and Framework for Action on Special Needs Education, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR221)). Article 24 of the United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UNCRPD) underscores the demands of those with disabilities for quality and tailored education (United Nations, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR231)). The CRPD of 2006 champions SEND individuals' participation and social inclusion, safeguarding against societal misconduct (UNICEF(a), [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR229)). Nevertheless, SEND children and young people are still facing social stigma and prejudice, especially those living in poverty (Servili & Bermejo et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR213)).

In the last 45 years in the USA, there's been a 45% increase in Special Education students—from 3.6 million in 1976–77 to 7.3 million in 2021–22 (Pendharkar et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR180)). About 40% of public schools report a lack of Special Education teachers, while 32% of children benefit from IDEA's learning disability support. Autistic children's representation rose from 1.5% in 2000–01 to 12% in 2021–22 (Schaeffer et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR211)). In 2020–21, 14% of SEND youths aged 14–21 dropped out and 5.4% were home-schooled. According to UNESCO, 15% of German and 59% of Hong Kong parents view SEN students as classroom disturbances; 37% of Queensland's SEN students leave regular schools (UNESCO, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR228)). Globally, 1 billion people have some form of disability, including nearly 240 million children (UNICEF(b), [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR230)). Organizations promote inclusivity, using AT (Assistive Technology) or ICTs (Information and Communication Technologies) in education for personalized learning, communication, and cognition, enhancing SEND students'social, communicational, and problem-solving skills.

Assistive Technologies (AT) is defined by the International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (ICF) as"any technology … improving the functioning of a person with a disability", whereas the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) states AT is"any product"aimed at SEND individuals for protection, advocacy, and enhancing quality (Borg et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR26)). SEN students should achieve social and emotional fulfillment in class, attaining parity in competencies (Black-Hawkins et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR23)). Mapping the difficulties a student may face, whether is a SEN student is not, as they are depicted in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#Fig1), show the challenges that ATs can potentially overcome. Newly emergent or established technologies, such as LLMs and VR or AR in the forms of ATs may ensure inclusion of all students within the classroom. This heterogeneity with the class consists of typically developed children, SEN students (Autism Spectrum Disorder, Down Syndrome and more), student with disabilities (motor or visual impairments, asthma, heart disease and more), immigrants, as indeed all these children are called to function and interact as a unity, ensuring inclusion is established, through the aid of ATs.

As a means of interaction, LLMs, often called the'Big Bang'phenomenon in AI and NLP, have captivated the scientific community (Kalyan, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR104); Liu et al., [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR131)). AI enables systems to perform tasks requiring human intelligence, while NLP allows computers to understand and generate human language. Together, they empower LLMs to revolutionize language-based interactions. Figure [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#Fig2) illustrates the brief historical evolution of LLMs, as they are sophisticated models, a type of neural network composed of vast parameters and trained on a vast amount of unstructured data, like books, websites, texts and more (Mbakwe et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR146)). Their ability to reason, generate multilingual text, summarize, answer questions are their most well-established abilities (Chung et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR42); Li et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR129); Liu et al., [2023b](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR132); Ozkaya, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR169)).

LLMs have found fertile ground for application in many scientific fields (Liu et al., [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR131)). Especially in education, which shapes future generations, learning and teaching strategies are affected by AI and LLMs and thus LLM-based educational systems seem to have promising potential (Darayseh, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR44); Li et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR130)). The extensive amount of knowledge in which LLMs have been trained is an outstanding asset, along with their human-like responses (Cardona et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR31); Li et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR130)). According to Li’s review paper, Mathematics (Luo & Specia, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR139)), Writing (Fan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR59)), Programming (Liventsev et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR133)), Reasoning (Ho et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR89)) and knowledge-based (Arefeen et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR13); Vu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR239)) are the main domains of applying LLMs in the field of Education. Their integration into Special Education is particularly crucial due to the field's complexity, requiring thoughtful and deliberate actions.

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a well-researched disorder for Scientific Community, as being technologically empowered by LLMs, various news studies have emerged. Affordable software enhancing social skills, applications using object detection and speech-to-text technology, LLM-based system in detecting autism, supporting system for autistic children, LLM-based Therapist for ASD are notable of establishing inclusion using ATs (Firsanova et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR64); Jafri, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR98); Mukherjee et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR157); Pai et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR171); Ren et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR195)). Research options seems to be endless in applying LLMs in Special Education, as for individuals with intellectual disabilities multi-modal conversational search system or images for non-verbal communication, are created, or Individualized Educational Support System for children with developmental disorders, or generic images for communication in individuals with disabilities, seems to fuel the interest of Scientific Community (Mack et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR140); Ogoshi, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR164); Roomkham et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR199); Saffar et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR202)). Biases are on the table, as studies are trying to uncover them toward disabled communities in LLMs (Gadiraju et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR70)).

ATs in the form technological means can be found a potential representor in LLMs, yet other technologies should be potential candidates to co-exist. As an established educational environment, VR is also implemented, as an educational tool (Allcoat & von Mühlenen, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR7)), serves the Common Good (Lamoureux et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR121)) and is thoroughly researched in fostering social. Virtual Learning Environments (VLE) and Collaborative Virtual Learning Environment (CVLE) are formed (Moolman et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR152); Naimi-Akbar et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR161)), fully adapted to various disorders and disabilities, such as ASD, adult Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD), intellectual and developmental disabilities, dyslexia and more (De Oliveira Malaquias & Malaquias, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR46); De Vasconcelos et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR47); Eden, [2008](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR52), [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR53); Kotlewska & Nowicka, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR115); Lu & Schmidt, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR135); Michalski et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR148); Passig & Eden, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR178)). Furthermore, virtual games & labs, metaverse, 3 d virtual language learning are viable option among vast alternatives applied VR in Special Education (Abrams et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR2); Gavronskaya et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR73); Kudry et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR118); Kurniawati et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR119); Wasserman et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR241); Xie et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR248)). Indeed, establishing an VLE with the aid of LLMs, as a supplementary or primary means of communication in Special Education may forge new dynamics for Scientific Community.

This review highlights the significant advancements in the application of VR, AR, and LLMs in Special Education. However, while these technologies are being implemented individually, it remains uncertain how they could be effectively integrated in a combined manner for SEND students. To address this uncertainty, this study focuses on the following Research Questions (RQs):

* **(RQ1)**. How is the Scientific Community exploring the application of VR and AR in Special Education?
* **(RQ2)**. How are LLMs being utilized to address the needs of students in Special Education, as reflected in the literature?
* **(RQ3)**. How can the integration of LLMs and VR create new opportunities for Special Education?
* **(RQ4)**. How do existing studies conceptualize the success and challenges of using VR and LLMs in Special Education?
* **(RQ5)**. What SEN methodologies and practices are defined by the Scientific Community to support the implementation of VR, AR, and LLM technologies within them?
* **(RQ6)**. What factors influence teachers'attitudes toward the inclusion of SEN students and their adoption of these technologies in Special Education?
* **(RQ7)**. What are the dominant technological tools, including Immersive technologies and LLMs, that provide benefits for individuals with Special Needs based on their specific syndrome or condition?

Given these pressing challenges and opportunities in the integration of modern technologies in Special Education, this study employs a deductive thematic analysis approach to systematically explore existing literature. By focusing on predefined themes, the methodology aims to uncover patterns and insights aligned with the study’s research questions.

2 Methodology
-------------

This paper employs the deductive thematic analysis framework developed by Thomas and Harden (Thomas & Harden, [2008](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR222)), as a methodological basis for conducting a systematic review on the integration of AI, VR, and LLMs in Special Education. It serves both as a methodological guide for thematic analysis and as a resource for synthesizing research in this field. The study aims to identify, evaluate, and synthesize the research questions outlined (RQ1–RQ7), identifying key themes and challenges to assess the current state of knowledge and propose future research directions. Thematic analysis was chosen for its flexibility and systematic approach in identifying patterns and themes within the selected literature, making it particularly suitable for understanding the complexities and recurring concepts in these domains.

### 2.1 Databases and timeframe

The literature search was conducted using three academic databases: **Arxiv** and **ScienceDirect,** because these databases aggregate articles from a wide variety of scientific journals, offering broad and diverse coverage. Their selection ensures access to multidisciplinary research, enabling a comprehensive review of the topics explored in this study. Future research could include additional databases, such as Web of Science or Lens, to provide broader perspectives and further enrich the findings. The search was carried out in two phases, from **December 1, 2023, to February 29, 2024**, and again from **September 16, 2024, to October 8, 2024**. These timeframes ensured the inclusion of the most up-to-date and relevant studies.

### 2.2 Systematic review process

This study employs a **systematic review methodology** to investigate the integration of AI, VR, and LLMs in Special Education. The process consisted of the following steps:

Initiation Based on Research Questions:

1. 1.
   
   This study explores key research questions (Q1–Q7) that guide the investigation into the applications, opportunities, and challenges of VR, AR, and LLMs in Special Education. It also examines SEN methodologies and practices, the inclusion of SEND individuals, teachers'attitudes toward these technologies, and the role of technological tools—including immersive technologies and LLMs—tailored to specific syndromes and disorders.

Defining Inclusion Criteria:

1. 2.
   
   To ensure the relevance and quality of the studies included in the review, the following criteria were applied:
   
   * **Language**: Only studies published in **English** were included.
   * **Source Credibility**: Studies must be from **established and credible academic sources** with direct relevance to the domains of LLMs, VR, AR and Special Education.
   * **Relevance to Themes**: The studies must focus on the application of VR, AR, and LLMs in educational settings, particularly for students with special educational needs and disabilities (SEND) and contribute meaningfully to identifying key themes.

Data Collection and Reduction:

1. 3.
   
   A structured literature search was conducted using databases such as ArXiv and ScienceDirect, employing targeted search terms. A structured set of search terms was employed to capture a comprehensive range of studies related to VR, AR, and LLMs in Special Education, including their applications, challenges, and their role in SEN methodologies and practices, teacher involvement, and technological support for individuals with specific syndromes and disorders. The search queries included:
   
   1. 1.
      
      [“Virtual Reality”] OR [“Augmented Reality”] AND [“Large Language Model”] IN [“Special Education”]
   2. 2.
      
      [“Virtual Reality”] IN [“Special Education”]
   3. 3.
      
      [“Large Language Models”] IN [“Special Education”]
   4. 4.
      
      [“LLMs agents”] IN [“Special Education”]
   5. 5.
      
      [“Virtual Reality”] OR [“Augmented Reality”] AND [“Large Language Model”] IN [“Autism Spectrum Disorder”]
   6. 6.
      
      [“Virtual Reality”] OR [“Augmented Reality”] AND [“Large Language Model”] IN [“Intellectual Disabilities”]
   7. 7.
      
      [“Virtual Reality”] OR [“Augmented Reality”] AND [“Large Language Model”] IN [“Down Syndrome”]
   8. 8.
      
      [“Virtual Reality”] OR [“Augmented Reality”] AND [“Large Language Model”] IN [“Motor Disabilities”]
   9. 9.
      
      [“Virtual Reality”] OR [“Augmented Reality”] AND [“Large Language Model”] IN [“Visual Disabilities”]
   10. 10.
       
       [“Virtual Reality”] OR [“Augmented Reality”] AND [“Large Language Model”] IN [“Special Needs”]
   11. 11.
       
       [“SEN Methodologies”] OR [“SEN Practices”] IN [“Special Education”]
   12. 12.
       
       ["Inclusion"] AND ["SEN Teachers"] IN ["Special Education"]
   13. 13.
       
       ["Teacher-AI Collaboration"] AND ["Large Language Model (LLM)"] IN ["Special Education"]
   14. 14.
       
       ["Virtual Reality"] OR ["Augmented Reality"] AND ["Teacher Perceptions"] OR["Inclusion"] IN ["Special Education"]

The selection process resulted in a total of 139 studies, as it is depicted in Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#Fig3) (Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#Fig3)). These were systematically screened against the inclusion criteria, resulting in a reduced dataset of studies directly relevant to the research focus. This process ensured that only high-quality, pertinent data was retained for further analysis.

1. 4.
   
   **Deductive Thematic Analysis**: The refined dataset was analyzed using a deductive thematic analysis framework:
   
   * **Line-by-Line Coding**: Data from the selected studies was systematically coded to extract critical insights and align them with the study’s objectives. This process identified recurring patterns and key elements related to predefined themes, including the roles of VR, AR, and LLMs in Special Education, SEN methodologies and practices, teacher engagement, and syndrome-specific technological means.
   * **Development of Descriptive Themes**: The systematically coded data was categorized into broader descriptive themes, capturing recurring patterns and key findings. These themes encompassed the role of VR/AR in skill development, the personalized learning opportunities facilitated by LLMs, the integration of SEN methodologies and practices, teacher engagement in technology adoption, and syndrome-specific technological applications. Additionally, challenges related to accessibility, implementation, and the effectiveness of these technologies in special education were identified.
   * **Articulation of Analytical Themes**: The descriptive themes were further synthesized into higher-order analytical themes, offering in-depth insights into the integration of VR, AR, and LLMs in Special Education. This phase examined the synergy between immersive technologies and AI in fostering inclusive education, assessed their impact on SEN methodologies and teacher engagement, and proposed frameworks for effective implementation. Furthermore, it highlighted existing gaps in accessibility, policy frameworks, and teacher preparedness, emphasizing the need for structured guidelines and professional development to support their successful adoption.

This systematic approach, combining rigorous data selection with thematic analysis, provides a robust foundation for articulating the current state of knowledge. ### 2.3 Minimizing bias

To ensure the reliability of the findings, two independent reviewers participated in the data extraction and coding process. Discrepancies in coding and theme identification were resolved through discussion. This approach helped minimize bias and increase the credibility of the results.

3 Findings
----------

This section presents the findings derived from the **systematic review and deductive thematic analysis**, focusing on **Immersive Technologies** and **LLMs** through relevant studies, addressing their **integration into Special Education** and the **challenges associated with their implementation**. Additionally, it explores **SEN methodologies and practices, as well as teachers’ attitudes toward technology, and the application of technological tools categorized by syndromes and disorders**. This approach provides a comprehensive perspective on the role of these technologies in Special Education, as their integration is of high importance.

### 3.1 VR and AR in special education

VR has emerged as a powerful tool in Special Education, offering personalized learning environments that cater to the unique needs of students with disabilities. By enabling educators to modify teaching strategies, VR allows for the adaptation of instructional content to align with the cognitive, sensory, and motor abilities of individual learners (Chiazzese et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR38); Luna et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR138); Mangina et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR142); Parmaxi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR177)). This adaptability is particularly advantageous for students with ADHD, and other learning disabilities, as immersive VR environments enhance engagement, minimize distractions, and provide structured, interactive experiences that reinforce learning concepts (Chiazzese et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR38); Mangina et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR142)). Furthermore, VR integrates scaffolding and multimodal feedback, allowing students to progress at their own pace while receiving real-time guidance and corrections—an essential factor in fostering self-regulated learning and improving knowledge retention (Parmaxi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR177)). Research also highlights the effectiveness of VR-based interventions in literacy and language learning, particularly for students with ADHD, by creating engaging, gamified learning experiences that sustain attention and motivation (Luna et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR138)). While some studies explore AR applications for students with ADHD and literacy interventions (Chiazzese et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR38); Mangina et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR142)), VR remains a distinct and emerging tool with promising implications for inclusive education.

Additionally, VR significantly contributes to accessibility in Special Education, empowering individuals with hearing and speech impairments. A major challenge faced by these individuals is the lack of a standardized visual representation of Sign Language (SL), which often creates barriers to social inclusion (Cheok et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR37)). Advances in technology, particularly in hand gesture recognition, have led to the development of innovative projects such as ISENSE, which visualizes sign languages to support university students (Bisio et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR22)). Studies focusing on SL integration in VR environments aim to enhance social interaction for individuals with hearing and speech impairments, demonstrating the progress made in this field (Aziz & Othman, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR15); Brega et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR29); Ghoul & Othman, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR77); Kasapakis et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR109); Naglot & Kulkarni, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR159); Quandt, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR182); Raval & Gajjar, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR192); Sandler & Lillo-Martin, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR207); Vaitkevičius et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR234); Velmathi & Goyal, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR237)).

Empowering communication skills, VR has been integrated into arts-based interventions to foster cognitive and social skills in students with special needs. The Open Doors to the Arts project, for instance, creates a 360-degree VR environment designed to promote exploration and social integration among autistic students (Heintzelman & Fogt, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR87)). Research suggests that combining VR with the arts not only enhances critical thinking, concentration, and creativity but also enriches the learning experience by incorporating immersive and engaging activities. Complementing this, technologies like Kinect and 3D cameras facilitate game-based learning, further contributing to the development of motor, cognitive, and language skills (Allcoat & von Mühlenen, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR7); Kusumaningsih et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR120); Sheehan & Katz, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR214)).

The extensive body of research on VR in Special Education underscores its potential to support SEN students in acquiring essential skills, improving social engagement, and developing speech and emotional intelligence (Bisio et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR22); Gerhátová et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR74); Ghoul & Othman, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR77); Ironsi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR97); Luca et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR136); Safi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR203); Wasserman et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR241)). Additionally, VR has proven to be particularly effective in adapting to diverse learning contexts, especially in STEM education, where it facilitates hands-on, interactive learning experiences (Ke et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR111); Moon et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR153); Sokolikj et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR217)). Motion-based games that utilize body tracking technology further enhance engagement by promoting the development of motor coordination and interaction skills, which are crucial for students with disabilities (Kourakli et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR116)).

VR has **firmly established itself as a transformative tool** in Special Education, providing **immersive, adaptable, and personalized learning experiences** that address the diverse needs of students with disabilities. As an **already well-integrated technology**, VR plays a **pivotal role** in shaping inclusive educational environments. Moving forward, its potential could be **further amplified** through **integration with emerging technologies**, such as **Large Language Models (LLMs)**, to enhance **personalized support, real-time communication, and adaptive learning pathways** for SEN students.

### 3.2 AI and LLMs in special education

AI benefits children with impairments across devices (Garg & Sharma, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR72)). Liu's review (2012–2021) identifies AI as a key educational tool, enhancing personalized learning and teaching competencies (Liu et al., [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR131)). Generative AI's uniqueness and sophisticated outcomes have made it a megatrend (Martin et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR144); Tlili et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR224)). Martin highlights ChatGPT's significant research interest and AI in Education (Castelli & Manzoni, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR32); Liu et al., [2023b](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR132)), supporting critical thinking and enriching learning experiences (Darvishi et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR45); Dwivedi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR51); Essel et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR58); Kasneci et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR110); Liu et al., [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR131); Rahman & Watanobe, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR185); Rahman & Islam et. al, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR186); Rospigliosi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR200); Tlili et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR224)).

AI has made significant strides in Special Educational Needs (SEN), offering personalized learning and support. AI adapts to the specific learning needs of SEN students, creating individualized pathways for instruction. Studies by Cardona and Garg highlight AI’s ability to provide tailored resources, helping learners overcome various challenges (Cardona et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR31); Garg & Sharma, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR72)). Furthermore, AI fosters engagement and emotional support, reducing isolation and empowering students. AI tools also enhance the learning process through NLP, improving knowledge acquisition and assessment (Zoana et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR254)).

For visually impaired learners, AI tools enhance interaction and cognition by adapting to user needs. AI systems designed for this purpose function as cognitive extensions, allowing visually impaired students to navigate their environment more independently (Xie et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR248)). However, studies have revealed biases in AI systems, particularly chatbots, which tend to depict people with disabilities as having more limitations compared to non-disabled users (Urbina et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR233)). Addressing these biases is crucial to ensure fair and inclusive AI-driven educational tools.

In autism, AI has proven especially useful for early diagnosis and intervention. AI-driven tools such as NLP and speech analytics provide quantitative insights into speech and language patterns, improving diagnostic accuracy (Trayvick et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR227)). Additionally, AI models analyze behavioral and biological markers, such as eye gaze and MRI, to detect autism earlier and offer targeted interventions (Pandya et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR174)). Speech frameworks like MT-Net leverage AI to screen for mental disabilities, processing speech data to achieve high diagnostic accuracy (Meng et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR147)).

AI also plays a role in behavior recognition for autistic individuals, using eye-tracking data and machine learning to assess behaviors and customize interventions (Jeyarani & Senthilkumar et al, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR100)). Vision-based AI models like the Video Swin Transformer further enhance autism-related behavior recognition (Deng et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR48)). Robotic interventions utilizing LLMs have also been developed to assist children with autism, delivering dynamic, personalized content during therapy sessions (Mishra et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR149)).

In addition to autism, AI and LLMs are being used to improve text accessibility for individuals with cognitive impairments by simplifying complex texts into Easy-to-Read formats (Martínez et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR145)). These advancements in AI support a more inclusive educational environment, ensuring that learners with various disabilities have access to resources tailored to their needs. As AI continues to evolve, addressing biases and ensuring accessibility will be key to its successful integration in special education (Almufareh et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR9)).

The **advancement of AI and Large Language Models (LLMs)** has already shown **remarkable potential** in enhancing **personalized learning, accessibility, and inclusion in Special Education**. However, their true impact can be **amplified through integration with Immersive Technologies**. This convergence **opens new avenues for research**, encouraging the scientific community to explore **synergistic applications that optimize learning outcomes, foster social interaction, and address cognitive and emotional challenges**. By embracing this **holistic approach**, the integration of LLMs, and Immersive Technologies has the potential to **reshape Special Education**, ensuring that learners with diverse needs have access to **equitable, effective, and engaging learning opportunities** tailored to their unique abilities.

### 3.3 VR and LLMs in special education

VR enhances ASD children's social skills through activities like physical education (Mokmin & Ridzuan, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR151)) and storytelling (Ke et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR111)), while AI integrates into STEM and music education (Martin et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR144); Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR240)). Southgate explores AI and VR's impact on Science education engagement and performance (Southgate, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR219)), a direction further investigated by Martin (Martin et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR144)). Affective Computing (AfC) analyzes human emotion for educational integration (Ludlow, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR137)), with Jurado introducing a Virtual Human (VH) mimicking human emotional and cognitive dynamics, featuring a range of expressions and engaging in conversations enhanced by GPT3 (Llanes-Jurado et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR134)). However, applying VR/AR and LLMs in Special Education lacks a clear framework, underscoring the need for inclusion (Llanes-Jurado et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR134)).

BERT accelerates the humor abilities of Virtual Voice Assistants (VA) and VAs, enhancing personalized jokes and feedback but facing limitations (Annamoradnejad & Zoghi, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR12); Mottini & Chowdhury, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR155)). It was also utilized for Intelligence Virtual Assistance, virtual well-being coaching, and interactive language learning, despite speech-to-text errors and limited training data (Savelieva et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR209); Uppoor et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR232); Winata et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR246)).

VAs have evolved with the implementation of LLMs (GPT- 3 and GPT- 4): in Higher Education for personalized and adaptive systems, in mobile phones, as improved learning systems, learning English, in Metaverse for virtual interactions and content creation (Chamola et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR34); Guan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR81); Sajja et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR205); Zhou, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR252))(Chamola et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR34); Guan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR81); Sajja et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR205); Zhou, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR252)). They also attain more advanced and complex structures, like adapting to human communication with T5 and Transformer CopyNet or thinking LLM-Based Conversational VAs for providing enhanced user satisfaction, engagement and guidance (Lee et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR124); Park & Kulkarni, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR176)). LLM-based VAs provide better techniques, the ability to adapt to user needs, and consistency. However, they give biased or false answers and can't be used on mobile devices, according to Guan’s study (Guan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR81)). Autonomous Visualization Agents (AVAs) are suggested for forming visualized systems, yet issues with prompting and in decision-making, where options are many (Liu et al., [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR131)), are stated. Affective AI is ideal for unstructured dialogues, corresponding with various facial expressions. It supports 13 languages; the data is not stored; if any misbehavior occurs, it stops. Adjustments to the VA’s appearance or background, along with feedback, are possible and thus they can be applied in the fields of Education, Language Learning, and more (Abadie et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR1); Hasan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR84)). Adabie, presents ChatGPT as a Virtual Robot and educational tool for teaching and learning interactive scenarios (Abadie et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR1)). Academy Khan creates Khanmigo, a LLM-based VA for personalized learning support and organizing lesson plans while promoting creative thinking. Only English, Spanish and Portuguese are available, subscription is mandatory and it is accessible in limited geographical areas. Ethical considerations are also pointed out, especially for younger learners (Khan Academy, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR3)).

VR worlds with prompts in GPT- 3 are proposed for game development and content creation, yet multimodal interactions have not been achieved (Roberts et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR196)). Motion Prediction on Face Motion with VR and GPT- 3 occurs as facial expressions and speech are adjusted to a given dialogue (Ng et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR162)). LLM-based AR environments for cognitive skill acquisition through Foreign Language Learning are ideal for preschool children (Topsakal & Topsakal, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR226)). An AI-ABA (AI-Augmented Learning and Applied Behavior Analytic) system in an LLM-based Virtual Environment (VE) is proposed by Ghafghazi for children with Developmental Disorders (Ghafghazi et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR75)). AI-based STEM Education with AR for children is studied by Chiu as an interactive educational tool (Chiu & Li, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR39)). Fernyhough suggests LLM-based VEs for IS (Inner Speech) for cognition and memory development, motivation, attention, adaptability to new situations and analysis of complex terms (Fernyhough & Borghi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR63)). However, complexity issues hinder any future advancements, as well as safety issues and misinterpretations in the interaction. Human Digital Twins, with GPT- 3 and VR, form emotional and personalized interactions and social engagement (Iqbal et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR96)). Nevertheless, it provides minimal functionality and other issues.

The partnership between technology powers becomes a notable research trend, with scant evidence on their integration into Special Education. There's an evident absence of specific scientific literature and frameworks in this area. Figure [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#Fig4) presents a theoretical model involving teachers, students, and parents as key stakeholders, positioned in outer circles. VR/AR and LLMs facilitate interactions between students and parents, and between students and teachers. It covers interactions among students, with or without disabilities, directly, through technology, or with teacher/parent support. The model only mentions"interaction"between teachers and parents, indicating uncertainty about the role of these technologies in their relationship.

### 3.4 Challenges in applying LLMs and immersive technologies

Immersive Technologies are in the spotlight of research, as well as LLMs set a prominent technology, yet there is a profound lack of scientific interest in applying them to diverse groups of people (Creed et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR43)). The Scientific Community has to clarify the “hows” and “whats” that these technologies can achieve. All the stakeholders are intrigued in determining any challenges and opportunities, yet some significant issues ought to be addressed:

**The vision of Special Education, the role of AI and LLMs.** Frameworks for conceptualizing the vision of Special education should be formed, fulfilling a learning experience for all students (Cardona et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR31)). LLMs can potentially advocate, correct, assess, and provide any type of information to students or teachers. Othman states that “GPT continues to advance and evolve …… becoming more powerful” (Othman, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR167)). While teacher instability has been noted (Section 3.6), LLMs offer a stable, emotionally safe environment and have the potential to mimic human emotions, as shown in studies on humor (Mottini & Chowdhury, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR155)) and social emotions (Llanes-Jurado et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR134)). Nevertheless, LLMs imitate human intelligence, as their responses rely on statistical calculations. Yet, the structure of the human brain is far more advanced and not yet fully understood (Cardona et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR31)). LLMs lack a “human understanding” of how humans interact (Birhane et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR21)). Furthermore, claims of the superiority of LLMs’s abilities may be exaggerated, provoking false or non-existent information (Birhane et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR21); Chuang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR41); Ozkaya, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR169)).

**Teachers as Key Drivers of Technological Advancement.** Cardona suggests that learning can be enhanced by AI, yet unqualified or biased teachers undermine any attempt to incorporate of these technologies (Cardona et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR31)). It is of high significance to determine whether SEN Teachers can become agents of obsolescence or prosperity in adopting Immersive technologies within the classroom (Haddon [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR83); Rahman & Watanobe et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR185)). Allen proposes the use of VR simulations in which SEN teachers practice teaching basic literacy skills to a virtual student avatar (Allen & Stecker, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR8)).

**LLMs as VAS or Intelligent Tutoring System (ITS) in Special Education (**Frasson et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR68)**).** LLMs support multimodal forms of interaction, like images, audio, text-to-speech, and more (Kamath et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR105)). These types of communication may allow people with disabilities to express themselves and be advocated in the learning process. Furthermore, Feedback is the key element in the identification of the educational demands and achievements of each student (Mousavinasab et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR156)).

**Government’s policies and its role in forming a protection net in applying LLMs and VR.** The government's role as a significant stakeholder should adopt legal, effective policies, that should form a stable framework for applying LLMs based on the well-being of children (Cardona et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR31); Rodrigues et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR197); Rodriguez et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR198)). It’s also the prime net of protection for the participants in VEs, as any leak of information should be avoided, as crucial data is exposed to the public (Cao et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR30)).

**LLMs have no valid framework for application in the field of Special Education.** Education is a field that should be handled with caution in conjunction with LLMs, and Special Education (Liu et al., [2023b](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR132)). Best practices, guidelines and proper frameworks should be formed.

**LLMs, VAs, cyber bulling and cyber security.** LLMs are employed to detect cyber bullying, yet their effectiveness remain underexplored in current research (Hedderich et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR86); Ogunleye & Dharmaraj, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR165)). They are vulnerable to manipulation by hackers and cyberattacks, which can distort their responses, leading to discomfort, chaos, and a loss of trust (Chan et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR35)). Furthermore, VAs raise issues of cyber security (Kalhor & Das, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR103)), by a lack of privacy or protection policies, which should be taken into account by Scientific Community. Zero Trust, a novel approach in cyber security with the motto “never trust, always verify” can be a potential barrier to protection (Fernandez & Brazhuk, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR62); Haddon, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR83)).

**LLMs, Metaverse and addiction issues.** It’s a “3D universe” (Bojic, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR25)), as people socialize, play and enjoy various activities. More advanced metaverse forms have emerged, like “Emphatic metaverses” (Pai et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR170)), as well as versatile educational metaverses that can be potentially formed (Ferdousi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR61)). Addiction may occur as it has been observed with various technological means (Bojic, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR25)). When VR becomes the only means of existence and comfort for an individual, addictions can lead to unpredictable and potentially jeopardizing outcomes (Creed et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR43)). Dark Verse is also another renamed version of Metaverse, in which the lack of supervision and protection policies can lead to cyber-attacks, which could potentially cause chaos (Johri et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR102)). Additionally, there is growing concern about the impact of heavy reliance on LLMs, which could either enhance or potentially hinder students'skills, a topic that warrants further investigation (Birhane et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR21)).

**Inclusive, versatile syndrome-based representations of Interaction in LLM-based VEs.** Individuals with divergent needs (geographical barriers, diverse cultures and disabilities) demand diverse approaches to the design and application of these technologies (Wehbe et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR243)). In particular, SEN students struggle with various learning difficulties and personalized learning strategies are advisable (Hawke, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR85)). Furthermore, it requires further exploration whether there is a unified framework in VR that reciprocates to the severity and disorder, as well as to the personality of SEN children (Badilla-Quintana et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR16); Marienko et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR143)). Spending a significant amount of time in a VE, SEN students may experience fatigue (Souchet et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR218)). Promoting “healthy” interactions and establishing easy-to-use equipment is advisable for people with severe disabilities (Creed et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR43)). Furthermore, various studies enhance rehabilitation in a VE as an effective perspective (Blandford et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR24)).

**Emotion-Based, heterogeneous personalized VR Education.** VR should be affective with a palette of emotions (trust, care, warmth and respect), enabling children to flourish mentally and emotionally (Dozio et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR50)). Furthermore, personalized learning strategies may advocate SEN students’ development, yet one-to-one interaction or individualized virtual sessions may not promote social interaction (Saalfeld et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR201)), as well as heterogeneous educational needs are challenging for diverse group of students within the classroom (typically developed children, SEN students, immigrants and more) (Gabaldón-Estevan et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR69)).

**LLMs, VR and cost-effectiveness issues applied for Special Education**. VR is expensive for mass application (Arian et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR14)), while solid design and application frameworks and guidelines have not been established (Farra et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR60); Korkut & Surer, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR114)). Further research should shed light on any dark spots or harmful intentions that can negatively affect SEN students. On the contrary, LLMs can be applied with zero or limited cost to various devices, such as smartphones and desktops (Cardona et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR31)), yet a unified strategy of integration should be formed.

### 3.5 Special education methodologies and practices

SEN students with mild disabilities are enrolled in mainstream classrooms and thus positively influenced by their classmates, as relationships are forged, beneficial for both parties (Gottfried, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR80); Horoi & Ost, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR91); Huang et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR93); Pan & Luo, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR173); Rangvid, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR190); Schwab, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR212)). Nevertheless, SEN children on the verge of an emotional outbreak, disturbing the learning process (Balestra et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR18); Egalite, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR55)). Even among SEN children, there is unequal treatment, as parents with ASD children report dissatisfaction with the school community, highlighting the lack of qualified school staff, in comparison to DS (Down Syndrome) children, who are more likely to attend regular classrooms, while receiving additional support (Herwegen et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR88)). ASD children and people with various disorders experience self-isolation (Bossaert et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR28)), as well as self-destruction and mental issues (O’Hagan et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR163)). ASD people can meet educational goals, especially at upper educational levels (Goodall, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR79)), yet Home education is advocated in many countries, as in the USA 2.5 million children are learning at home (Ray, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR193)). It’s supported by parents of children with ASD, as the mainstream classroom deems any effort to include these people (O’Hagan et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR163)). This general dissatisfaction, particularly among SEN students and their family needs to be properly addressed, highlighting a significant challenge. In response, the integration and further testing of technological solutions could play a pivotal role in addressing these concerns.

Special Education involves various methodologies, as the Scientific Community suggests. Through experimentation, SEN students are actively engaged in the learning process (Gerhátová, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR74)), as well as with sensor-based games (Kourakli et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR116)). Rachmawati proposes DI (Differentiated Instruction) as an educational learning strategy for Special Education (Rachmawati et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR184)). ILP (Individual Learning Plan) is set up to meet the unique educational needs of each student and those struggling with various cognitive, emotional, or mental deficiencies, are in greater need of effective learning interventions. Activities should be inspired by real life. This is an approach to how the theory of Socio-Constructivism occurs during the learning process in special education (Voinea & Purcaru, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR238)). A Pedagogy formed by a game-based learning strategy is also advocated (Johnson-Glenberg et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR101); Karadimitriou & Roussou, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR107)), as well as curriculums and educational plans, which should also be adapted to the distinctive personality of each SEN student (Zirkel & T, [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR253)). Evaluating these methodologies as essential tools can facilitate the effective implementation of VR/AR technologies and LLMs, thereby enhancing and personalizing the experience of learning for SEN students, ultimately enriching their educational journey.

Parental involvement is encouraged in Israel, but gaps remain in improving inclusion policies (Orly, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR166)). In comparison with Palestine, educators have the willingness, yet there is a profound lack of infrastructure, as well as economic and political instability and other issues (MacKenzie et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR141)). Russia is still in the process of forming inclusive educational environments (Valeeva, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR235)). In advanced economies, like Germany and Sweden inclusion seems to be applied with a divergent approach. The federal states in Germany do not have a unified policy and an assessment acknowledges the disorder before any action proceeds. On the contrary, Sweden promotes inclusion for most children, provided they can reciprocate to the basics. Any kid can receive assistance in the learning process, without being characterized as a student with special educational needs (Sansour & Bernhard, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR208)). Inclusion policies vary globally, as there is no unified framework, as highlighted by previous studies. However, VR technologies have the potential to transcend geographical barriers, in which people of all nationalities and religions can connect remotely, utilizing LLMs as a powerful means of communication.

Teachers play a pivotal role in fostering inclusion, yet in special education, they face aggression by students (Levy, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR127)). EDB students stand as a challenge, as their aggression can escalate, which disrupts the learning process (Amstad & Müller, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR11); Lebrun-Harris et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR123); Valenti et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR236)). Emotional disassociation seems to be a perspective for educators to deal in a moment of crisis (Chang, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR36)). It’s an “emotional state” to survive and reciprocate to their professional duties as a SEN educators (Gilmour & Wehby, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR78); Kerr & Brown, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR112)). The mental strain faced by SEN teachers appears to negatively impact their emotional well-being, underscoring the need for alternatives that help maintain emotional stability throughout the learning process. LLMs could serve as a consistent and stable means of communication, supporting emotional continuity and easing this burden.

### 3.6 Attitudes of teachers toward special education and technology

Inclusion is highly valued, as teachers form an environment in which SEN students explore and develop cognitive skills (Moberg et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR150); Savolainen et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR210); Voinea & Purcaru, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR238)). Teachers'Attitudes Towards Inclusion (ATI) indicate the feasibility of integrating SEN students into school communities (Ai et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR6); Moberg et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR150)). Educators classroom management skills are essential, though experienced teachers may exhibit negative ATI and younger teachers may face low self-esteem issues (Bandura, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR19); Gülsün et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR82)). Furthermore, due to SEN students with EBD (Emotional and Behavioral Disabilities) teachers are inclined to abort and change schools (Gilmour & Wehby, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR78)). According to O’Hagan, teachers cope with SEN students unsuccessfully (O’Hagan et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR163)). They demonstrate a deficiency in implementing practical interventions for SEN students (Webster & Blatchford, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR242)). Home education is a common educational practice, yet parents are burned out of adopting various roles in their household, along with the prime education of their children (Daniels et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR183)). SEN Teachers seem to be on the verge of emotional fatigue, in comparison with volunteers, who express possessiveness, while interacting with disabled people (Sagone & Caroli, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR204)).

Teachers in France are thoroughly researched toward ATI. A contradictory definition of “selection function” acts as an assessment tool of the worthiness of each student to achieve learning goals. This term is also in line with the principle of mediocrity, as a general means of discrimination against the abilities of SEN students (Khamzina et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR113)). Educators seem to be the prime agents of instability and uncertainty (Krischler & Cate, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR117); Levy & Khoury-Kassabri, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR128)). Various considerations are to be raised in terms of management and inclusion of SEN students in a moral, warm, safe, and trusting learning environment.

Recent research on Teacher-AI Collaboration (TAC) highlights the potential of LLMs to enhance teachers'capacity in various educational tasks. Teachers perceive LLMs as supportive tools that can assist with grading, student progress tracking, and personalized learning, without replacing their critical instructional roles (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR125)). They appreciate AI's ability to analyze real-time data to offer tailored educational resources but emphasize the need for maintaining human oversight in interpreting data and making instructional decisions (Owoseni et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR168)). Concerns persist around the socio-emotional aspects of teaching, as AI may struggle to handle complex emotional dynamics effectively (Xuefei et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR249)). Furthermore, teachers express a need for professional development to improve AI literacy and various concerns in AI integration (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR125); Owoseni et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR168)). The frameworks proposed for integrating LLMs into teacher training focus on automating content generation for learning objectives and offering personalized feedback, though concerns remain about the oversimplification of feedback (Barno et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR20); Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR125)). Teachers advocate for collaboration with expert educators to refine AI-generated insights (Xuefei et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR249)).

Similarly, while VR, AR, and MR have been highlighted for improving student engagement and motor skills, teachers emphasize the need for more professional development to ensure these tools are integrated successfully into curricula (Pérez-Muñoz et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR181)). Teachers acknowledge the potential of the Metaverse to create inclusive, engaging learning environments, yet there is a critical gap in research focused on how these technologies can serve SEND students effectively (Tlili et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR223)). Additionally, concerns about accessibility within the metaverse, especially regarding learners with disabilities, remain a significant barrier. The need for inclusive learning designs that benefit all students is echoed by educators, who are seeking better tools and resources to ensure equitable access to these immersive technologies (Zhang et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR250)).

### 3.7 Technological means for people with special needs: Customized applications and devices

Designing an educational system for people with special needs using AI and immersive technologies is a significant and sensitive task. Here are several considerations based on various special needs:

1. 1.
   
   Visual Impairments: Considerations for individuals with blindness or severe vision impairments should encompass a range of assistive technologies, including text-to-speech software, smart glasses (Mukhiddinov & Cho, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR158)), screen readers, and Braille displays (Hori & Okada, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR90)). Immersive technologies need to be adaptable, providing audio descriptions for visual content to ensure inclusivity (Afonso-Jaco & Katz, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR5)). Those with low vision can benefit from features like high contrast, large text, and voice commands. AI-powered and GPS-based applications play a vital role in helping individuals recognize objects and people, navigate different environments, and advocate social engagement (Sameer et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR206)). Additionally, haptic devices (Papadopoulos et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR175)) and wearable technology (Leong & Lim, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR126)), offer valuable options for enhancing accessibility and independence for these individuals.
2. 2.
   
   Hearing Impairments: Visual or vibrating alerts can replace auditory signals (Dim et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR49)), while closed captioning and sign language interpretation should be integrated into video content (Suo et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR220)). AI-driven lip-reading technologies could also be a novel addition (Rajab & Hashim, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR188)). Moreover, sound amplifiers, cochlear implants (Essaid et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR57); Eden & Leibovitz-Ganon, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR54)), telecoil systems are established technologies in conjunction with AI that can be of great value asset to them.
3. 3.
   
   Cognitive Disabilities: Individuals with cognitive disabilities might benefit from a simplified interface and content, with clear and concise instructions. Immersive technologies can be used to create predictable and less distracting environments (Wenk et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR244)). AI can be used to adapt learning content to the user’s pace and understanding (Zhao et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR251)). Indeed, the goal for these people is to learn, communicate and perform daily tasks independently through cognitive improvement in their daily life routines.
4. 4.
   
   Motor Disabilities: Technology should provide a diverse range of assistive devices and solutions for people with motor disabilities. For those with limited mobility or fine motor skills, touchless input options like eye-tracking or voice commands can be vital (Borges et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR27); Ramos et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR189)). Adaptive hardware and customizable interfaces that cater to individual mobility needs should be considered (Floreani et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR66); Gannouni et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR71)). AI and immersive technologies could embrace a leading role in accommodating emotional and mental fulfillment, abolishing any physical barrier (Creed et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR43)).
5. 5.
   
   Learning Disabilities: AI can provide personalized learning experiences tailored to the individual's learning style and pace. Techniques like multisensory approaches (Gharaibeh & Dukmak, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR76)), immediate feedback, and step-by-step guidance can be beneficial. Immersive technology can offer practical, hands-on experiences in a controlled, repeatable environment (Flores-Gallegos et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR67)). The learning process should be accomplished in line with social inclusion and mental empowerment, fully adapted to the special needs of these people.
6. 6.
   
   Autism Spectrum Disorders (ASD): ASD is a well-researched disorder in the fields of robotics, VR and AI. Technological means should prioritize concentration, motivation and trust through Empathetic AI (Fletcher-Watson & Bird, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR65); Nagy, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR160)), Educational Apps, Digital therapeutics (Chu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR40); Jeong et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR99); Wu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR247)) and therapeutic activities. For users on the autism spectrum, customizable sensory settings (to reduce or eliminate overwhelming stimuli) and structured, predictable interfaces are important. Immersive technologies can help practice social situations or routines in a safe environment (Mosher et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR154)).
7. 7.
   
   Psychological/Emotional Disorders: For individuals with anxiety, depression, or PTSD, a system should provide a stress-free, comforting environment. AI-powered psychotherapy tools (Raile, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR187)), wearable technology (Kang & Chai, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR106)) and mental health Apps promoting relaxation and reducing emotional overload is vital (Balcombe & Leo, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR17)). Furthermore, Digital journals, VR Therapy (Wiebe et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR245)), AI-based mood tracking, meditation, or analyzing psychometric traits can potentially boost the mentality (Pellert et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR179)). Privacy, security, and an easy way to pause or exit the experience can help users feel safe.
8. 8.
   
   Speech Impairments: For users with speech difficulties, alternative communication methods such as text-based input, the selection of pre-made phrases, or AI-driven predictive text can enhance interaction. AI-based logotherapeutic apps (Raile, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR187)), Voice amplifiers, are potentially innovative approaches in the field of logotherapy. Non-verbal users call for extra provision with wearable communication devices, selecting words or letters with eye or muscle movements (Kasapakis et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR108); Redondo et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR194)).
9. 9.
   
   Intellectual Disabilities: Assistive technologies for children with intellectual disabilities (ID) enhance communication, cognitive skills, and inclusion, especially with the aid of AI (Almufareh et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR10)). Tools like Proloquo2Go offer non-verbal communication aids, while TouchChat HD and Choiceworks use visual aids for routine understanding (Sigafoos et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR216)). Floreo helps develop social skills in a safe environment, and coding tools like Bee-Bot engage children in learning (Acharya & Mohbe, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR4)). Cognitive apps like CogniFit improve mental functions, and devices like smartwatches and text-to-speech systems promote task-tracking and literacy support, fostering independence (Siberski et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR215)).
10. 10.
    
    Accessibility Standards and Regulations: Ensure the system meets international accessibility standards, such as the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) and is compliant with local laws and regulations regarding accessibility.

These findings highlight the transformative potential and the significant challenges of integrating VR, AR, and LLMs in Special Education. The following discussion contextualizes these insights within the broader educational landscape, exploring their implications for practice, policy, and future research.

4 Discussion
------------

Hristovska acknowledges developing practical strategies for SEN children, stating that inclusion is “a continuous, integral, educational process is the way to acceptance, care, respect, for children with special education needs in regular schools, promoting socio-emotional learning where children who have different needs and capabilities will enable a level of learning and tailored to their characteristics whilst using different strategies of learning… School communities build open, cooperative, flexible, socio-emotional environments” (Hristovska & Jovanova-Mitkovska, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR92)).

The Ministry of National Education outlines five inclusion principles: empathy and respect for diverse student groups, individual curriculum adaptation, interactive teaching strategies —which can be greatly enhanced using VR and LLMs—, professional collaboration, and parental involvement in the learning process (Rachmawati et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR184)). However, the implementation of VR and LLMs for Special Education is unmapped. Emotional and educational high demands of SEN students reinforce the establishment of a robust and precise framework (Rapport et al., [2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR191)).

In addressing the research questions posed, this study derives significant conclusions regarding the application of VR, AR, and LLMs in Special Education, as outlined below. The findings reveal that the implementation of technological solutions in this field is inherently multidimensional, encompassing teaching environments, learning strategies, the evolving role of teachers—particularly in terms of their acceptance of these technologies—and the application of tailored technological tools, often designed to address specific syndromes. Highlighting these aspects is of paramount importance, as it empowers the effective implementation of these technologies and enhances their impact on educational outcomes for students with Special Needs.

### Research Question RQ1: VR and AR in education and Special education

The first research query (RQ1) explores how the scientific community investigates the use of VR and AR in this field. Indeed, these tools have been well-established, advocating personalized learning and fostering social and emotional development. Studies confirm that immersive technologies play a vital role for Special Education, such as project Open Doors to the Arts (Heintzelman & Fogt, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR87)), the use of VR to improve Sign Language (Cheok et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR37)), or in STEM and arts education (Ke et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR111); Parmaxi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR177)). However, challenges such as cost, accessibility, and teacher readiness remain significant obstacles to broader adoption (Rahman & Watanobe, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR185); Souchet et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR218)).

### Research Question RQ2: AI and LLMs in education and special education

Similarly, RQ2, which examines how LLMs are utilized to meet the needs of students in Special Education, reveals their ability to create adaptive learning experiences and simplify complex texts for cognitive impairments (Cardona et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR31); Martínez et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR145)). LLMs support behavioral recognition, early diagnosis, and therapy, particularly for autistic students, using tools like eye-tracking and speech analytics, as well as robotic applications (Deng et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR48); Meng et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR147)). However, biases in LLM outputs and the need to ensure equitable access remain critical issues (Almufareh et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR9)). Despite these concerns, the potential for personalized learning and emotional support provided by LLMs positions them as valuable tools in Special Education.

### Research Question RQ3: VR and LLMs in special education

As discussed in RQ3, the integration of VR and LLMs in Special Education holds the potential for transformative opportunities. However, there are no comprehensive studies that clearly integrate VR, AR, and LLMs specifically for Special Education. While several studies explore these technologies individually or in general education contexts (Abadie et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR1); Chamola et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR34)), this study highlights a lack of unified frameworks or specific research addressing their combined application in Special Education for SEND students. Once the scientific community systematically applies these technologies, clearer insights into the opportunities they can create—such as enhanced inclusion, personalized learning, and emotional support—are likely to emerge.

### Research Question RQ4: challenges in applying LLMs and immersive technologies

The research query RQ4 examines how existing studies conceptualize the success and challenges of using VR and LLMs in Special Education. These technologies present unique challenges, including the lack of clear frameworks, accessibility barriers, and ethical concerns and more, as thoroughly outlined above. However, the findings from the article suggest that every challenge or limitation identified has the potential to be transformed into novel opportunities for innovation. The recommendations and future directions aim to spark the interest of the scientific community, encouraging further research into the implementation of these technological advancements in SEN education to address these challenges and harness their transformative potential.

### Research Question RQ5: special education methodologies and practices

Outlining the landscape of Special Education, inclusion in mainstream classrooms is often regarded as the ultimate goal, as it fosters both social and academic development (Pan & Luo et. al, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR173); Rangvid, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR190)). However, this objective is frequently accompanied by significant challenges, such as behavioral management difficulties and instances of unequal treatment among SEND students (Herwegen et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR88)). For some families, homeschooling has emerged as a viable alternative, primarily due to dissatisfaction with the adequacy of mainstream education in meeting their children's needs (Ray, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR193)). Moreover, the mental health strain experienced by educators underscores the pressing need for supportive tools, such as Large Language Models (LLMs), to enhance communication, sustain emotional well-being, and improve the overall educational environment for SEN students (Owoseni et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR168)). This paper aims to provide a comprehensive overview of the field of Special Education by examining methodologies and practices endorsed by the scientific community, offering valuable insights to enhance inclusion and support for students with SEND people. Indeed, it emphasizes the need for these technologies to be adapted to the prevailing circumstances depicted above.

### Research Question RQ6: attitudes of teachers towards special education and technology

The findings highlight several challenges and opportunities related to teachers'negative attitudes toward inclusion (ATI), low self-esteem among younger teachers, and the behavioral challenges posed by SEN students with Emotional and Behavioral Disabilities (EBD) contribute to significant difficulties (Bandura et. al, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR19); Gilmour & Wehby, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR78); Gülsün et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR82)). Additionally, a lack of proper training in implementing inclusive practices and the prevalence of emotional fatigue exacerbate these issues (Sagone & Caroli, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR204); Webster & Blatchford, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR242)).

On the other hand, research on Teacher-AI Collaboration (TAC) highlights the potential of technologies such as Large Language Models (LLMs) and AI to enhance teachers'capabilities (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR125); Owoseni et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR168)). Moreover, teachers recognize the potential of VR, AR, and MR technologies in fostering student engagement, although issues related to accessibility and inclusion for SEN students remain critical barriers (Pérez-Muñoz et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR181); Tlili et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR223)).These findings underscore the great promise that these technologies hold when effectively implemented in Special Education, highlighting the importance of targeted professional development programs and the creation of accessible, inclusive solutions to empower teachers.

### Research Question RQ7: technological means for people with special needs: Customized applications and devices

The prevailing technological means, as thoroughly depicted above, illustrate the diverse tools and approaches already employed to support individuals with Special Needs. A syndrome-based approach emerges as a pivotal strategy, emphasizing the need to adopt and adjust these technologies to the specific requirements of learners. By leveraging the strengths of both LLMs and immersive technologies, Special Education can effectively address a broader spectrum of needs. This combined approach allows these tools to complement each other, creating educational experiences that are both personalized and interactive. Ultimately, such integration fosters a more inclusive and effective framework for Special Education, ensuring equitable access and enhanced outcomes for all learners.

### 4.1 Limitations

The integration of Immersive Technologies and LLMs in Special Education offers a transformative approach, significantly enhancing personalized learning, emotional engagement, and inclusion. Despite their immense potential, these technologies come with inherent limitations. While several aspects, such as teachers’ lack of training, emotional strain, and accessibility barriers, have already been highlighted as challenges, these elements have been further delineated to provide a clearer and more structured perspective. Challenges address overarching systemic issues, whereas limitations focus on specific practical constraints that hinder effective implementation, ensuring a comprehensive understanding of the obstacles in applying these technologies:

1. 1.
   
   **Technical Barriers**: Immersive technologies often demand high-end infrastructure and technical expertise. For instance, **Mehrfard** highlights the prohibitive costs of VR systems, limiting their scalability, particularly in low-resource settings (Arian et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR14)). Additionally, **Korkut** notes the absence of standardized frameworks, which creates inconsistency in applying these tools effectively in educational settings (Korkut & Surer, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR114)).
2. 2.
   
   **Privacy and Security**: Data protection is a significant concern in virtual learning environments. **Kalhor** argues that the absence of robust cybersecurity measures can expose sensitive student data, especially in immersive technologies where real-time interactions are common (Kalhor & Das, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR103)).
3. 3.
   
   **Teacher Training and Readiness**: While VR and LLMs show promise, their effectiveness heavily relies on teacher readiness. **Rahman** emphasizes that many educators lack sufficient training to utilize these technologies effectively, leaving a gap in practical application (Rahman & Watanobe, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR185)).
4. 4.
   
   **Cognitive and Physical Fatigue**: Prolonged exposure to immersive environments can lead to fatigue and disengagement among students. Studies such as **Souchet** underline the need for"healthy interactions"to mitigate the risk of overreliance on VR, which may negatively impact learners'well-being (Souchet et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR218)).
5. 5.
   
   **Accessibility Gaps**: Technologies like VR and AR often overlook the needs of students with severe disabilities. **Creed** highlights the challenges of designing universally accessible interfaces that cater to a diverse range of physical and cognitive abilities (Creed et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR43)).

While the limitations outlined above highlight key barriers to effectively adopting these technologies, they also present opportunities for targeted interventions. The following recommendations propose actionable strategies to overcome these challenges and foster inclusive, technology-enabled educational environments.

### 4.2 Recommendations

To ensure the effective integration of VR, AR, and LLMs in Special Education, a strategic approach is required. By adopting a structured framework, focusing on inclusion, teacher support, and collaboration, these technologies can address diverse educational needs. This ensures decisions are made sensibly and efficiently, fostering inclusive, adaptive, and ethical learning environments. Some notable recommendations on the frameworks are reported below:

1. 1.
   
   *Inclusion and Support for All SEN Students*
   
   * **Best Practices and Guidelines**: Adopt usability and accessibility principles to ensure SEN students receive appropriate assistance.
   * **Fulfillment of Inclusion**: All educational tools and methodologies must prioritize full inclusion for SEN students.
2. 2.
   
   *Teacher Well-being and Emotional Stability*
   
   * **Support for Teachers**: LLM-based tools should prioritize the well-being of teachers, ensuring they are emotionally stable and equipped with the necessary knowledge.
   * **Emotional Advocacy**: Tools must be designed to alleviate emotional crises that impact classroom stability, promoting a supportive environment for both teachers and students.
3. 3.
   
   *LLM Integration in Educational Settings*
   
   * **Roles of LLMs**: LLMs can act as companions, co-learners, digital twins, virtual assistants (VAs), and more.
   * **Character Qualities**: Any virtual character should embody morality, warmth, empathy, and trustworthiness to build reliable relationships with students and teachers.
4. 4.
   
   *Real-World Learning in Virtual Reality (VR)*
   
   * **Development of Pedagogies and Scenarios**: Create flexible, real-world learning activities and pedagogies using VR, with LLMs providing assistance or leading the activities.
   * **Diverse Educational Themes**: Incorporate a variety of educational themes, such as Arts and physical education, catering to the diverse interests of SEN students.
   * **Additional Options**: Offer supplementary choices to accommodate the varied interests and needs of SEN students.
5. 5.
   
   *Data Privacy and Protection*
   
   * **Privacy Protocols**: Ensure the establishment of robust authentication protocols and data protection regulations to safeguard the private information shared by parents, instructors, and students.
6. 6.
   
   *Multilingual and Multimodal Interaction*
   
   * **Improved Interaction**: Implement tools that are multilingual and multimodal to enhance the quality of interaction for diverse learners.
7. 7.
   
   *Global Research and Collaboration*
   
   * **Global Research Platform**: Virtual environments (VEs) can serve as a research platform, enabling global interaction and collaboration among SEN students and educators.
8. 8.
   
   *Real-Time Interaction in Multiuser Environments*
   
   * **Enhancement of LLMs**: Further develop LLMs to facilitate real-time interaction between users in a multiuser setting, ensuring seamless communication.
9. 9.
   
   *Clarification of VR and LLM Capabilities*
   
   * **Educational Support**: Clearly define the extent to which VR and LLMs can assist SEN students in their educational journey and personal development.
10. 10.
    
    *Establishment of a Central Framework*
    
    * **Guided Values**: The main framework should be grounded in social and moral values, government policies, and supported by research from the Scientific Community.
11. 11.
    
    *Cross-Domain Partnerships*
    
    * **Scientific Collaboration**: Foster partnerships across various scientific domains to establish the core principles guiding the design and implementation of educational tools (Castro-Kemp & Samuels, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR33)).

Accessibility and trustworthiness in learning environments are essential, with face-to-face learning, differentiated strategies, and teamwork as inclusive educational tools. Immersive Technologies and LLMs in Special Education raise the demand for cross-sector, transdisciplinary partnerships focusing on children's educational needs (Castro-Kemp & Samuels, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR33); Rapport et al., [2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR191)).

In addition to these immediate recommendations, there is a pressing need for further research to address unresolved challenges and to expand on the opportunities identified in this study. The following section outlines key future directions for advancing this field.

### 4.3 Future directions

The future of integrating Immersive Technologies and LLMs in Special Education is promising but requires focused research and innovation. By addressing existing gaps and building on emerging opportunities, these technologies can transform educational practices, making them more inclusive and adaptive. Below are some key future directions, which can potentially expand the research:

* **Strategies for Unifying Typically Developed and SEND Students**: Future research should investigate strategies to achieve unity by fostering meaningful collaboration and interaction between typically developed children and SEND students in inclusive environments.
* **Integration of Sensory-Friendly AI-Powered VLEs**: Sensory-friendly AI-powered VLEs should be thoroughly researched to determine their effectiveness in genuinely aiding SEND students to fully interact and engage within a learning environment.
* **Development of Daily Life Skills Training Modules**: Future work should explore how these technologies can empower SEN students by enhancing their independence and preparing them for everyday challenges.
* **Implementation of Practical Interventions**: Future studies should prioritize the development of specific, practical interventions that translate theoretical research into actionable solutions, such as tailored software, prompting tools, or immersive applications powered by LLMs, including VR/AR technologies, avatars, or digital twins, to address the diverse needs of SEND students.
* **Quantitative Research and Feedback-Driven Refinements**: Future research should focus on gathering quantitative data to assess the impact of these technologies on teachers, parents, and SEND students, while systematically incorporating feedback from stakeholders to refine and improve educational tools, ensuring they remain effective, ethical, and aligned with real-world needs.

5 Conclusion
------------

VR and LLMs in Special Education as an"effective intervention"should be applied with respect and"warmth"to students’ uniqueness (Humphrey et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR94); Rachmawati et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR184)). Teachers embracing the call and trained in inclusive learning strategies, as qualified educators could foster inclusion (Engsig & Johnstone, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR56); Paju et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR172); Tometten et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR225)). General Pedagogical Knowledge (GPK) creates effective classroom interactions and VR and LLMs could significantly contribute, making engagement from stakeholders, developers, and researchers vital (Lauermann & König, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13550-4#ref-CR122)). This thematic analysis provides a comprehensive understanding of the integration of VR, AR, and LLMs in special education by identifying key themes across the literature. The identified themes highlight both the potential benefits and challenges of using these technologies to support students with special educational needs, as well as the need for a stable framework in integrating these technologies.
