## ABSTRACT
Due to the fast-evolving nature of AI technologies and their demands for rapid multidimensional social and educational transformation, timely reviews of how to integrate AI in K-12 education should be revisited every five years. Therefore, this systematic review study is to understand how to design content, instructions, and assessments for the integration of AI in schools. It presented the definitions of AI literacy and competency in the context of K-12 education and used the definitions to analyze the selected articles. The review highlighted the need to expand the existing definition of AI literacy by including self-reflection and emotional aspects. This review selected 47 publications published throughout the last ten years (2014–2023) using the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses guidelines. The results identified 13 topics to foster the five confidences and abilities of AI competency, five instructional designs, and five assessment approaches. Self-reflective mindsets were included in very few, if any, studies in the literature on AI education. We also discussed and suggested how the instructional designs and assessment approaches relate to the five confidences and abilities, and the students should self-reflect on their AI understanding to improve their competency.

## 1. Introduction
The rapid advancement of AI technology has made it necessary for individuals to gain essential AI skills and knowledge. As UNESCO ([Citation2024](#)) notes, acquiring AI literacy and competency is vital for people to thrive in modern society. Individuals should understand the potential impact of AI, understand its capabilities and limitations, and explore how to develop the technology for social good (UNESCO, [Citation2021](#)). Recognizing this need, there is a global push to integrate AI in K-12 curricula to educate the next generation (Pedró et al., [Citation2019](#)) and cultivate future AI talent (Chiu, [Citation2021](#), [Citation2024a](#), [Citation2025](#); Chiu et al., [Citation2022](#); Pedró et al., [Citation2019](#); Xia et al., [Citation2022](#)). Equipping people with AI skills and ethical awareness has become a priority worldwide. More studies are needed to discuss the definitions of AI literacy and competency. A few studies define what AI literacy and competency for non-computer scientists are. For example, Long and Magerko ([Citation2020](#)) defined AI literacy as a set of competencies that enable people to thoughtfully analyze AI technologies, interact and collaborate effectively with AI, and utilize AI as a tool in various online, personal, and professional contexts. The definition, which includes 17 competencies and 15 design considerations, is quite comprehensive and may be too complex for a K-12 setting. It could be because the definition was developed by two scholars with strong engineering research backgrounds. The definition may not be based on educational research.
In education, literacy is closely linked to skills, is more about knowing, and mostly concerns knowledge (Falloon, [Citation2020](#); also see Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA) and Trends in International Mathematics and Science Study (TIMSS)). Literacy is measured by objective measures, e.g. tests. Competency is the capacity to carry out a task efficiently and effectively. In other words, literacy involves knowledge; competency involves applying that knowledge effectively and beneficially, as well as pertains to confidence and attitude, and evaluates the performance of an AI user. These are supported by the drafted AI competency framework for students that focuses on application (UNESCO, [Citation2024](#)). Moreover, many studies settle on literacy rather than competency since it requires less challenging. Evaluating students’ understanding of AI should extend beyond just their knowledge, skills, and abilities because of its fast-paced, evolving, disruptive, and transformative nature. With the popularity of generative AI tools such as ChatGPT and Sora, AI literacy and competency need to be revisited, particularly for K-12 education (Chiu, [Citation2025](#); Chiu et al., [Citation2024](#)).
Previous studies (e.g. Long & Magerko, [Citation2020](#)) concerning AI literacy and competency did not specifically cover the ability to learn and work with AI, as well as emotional aspects (e.g. confidences) that are very important for lifelong learning. This lifelong learning includes learning emerging AI technologies – how the new AI tools process data and their new impacts on society – and learning with AI – using the AI tools to effectively, ethically, and healthily learn disciplinary knowledge, e.g. mathematics and language. Foreseeably, AI digital technologies are going to stay, advance, and expand their influences in all forms of human knowledge and activities. Individuals will have AI around them (e.g. mobile phones and laptops). Students should see AI as a learning buddy and have the ability to learn, and working with AI has become a key competency to thrive in the AI age (Chiu, [Citation2024a](#), [Citation2024b](#)). For effective learning with AI, students should have a self-reflective mindset to constantly evaluate their understanding of AI and keep abreast of AI developments (Chiu et al., [Citation2024](#)). For example, rules-based chatbots perform differently than large language model chatbots. Students should have an updated understanding of AI and evaluate the AI tools they use. This is aligned with the guidelines suggested by UNESCO ([Citation2024](#)) and Falloon’s ([Citation2020](#)) digital competence framework. UNESCO ([Citation2024](#)) proposed “AI competency” for both school students and teachers and defines AI competency as “the knowledge, skills, and attitudes students should acquire to understand and actively engage with AI in a safe and ethical manner in education and beyond.” Moreover, one of the competencies – professional engagement – in UNESCO's ([Citation2024](#)) framework for teachers is related to working with AI, self-reflective mindsets, and emotional aspects. Although this is included in the framework for teachers, it is not for students. Falloon ([Citation2020](#)) proposed two new sets of competencies – personal-ethics and personal-professional – in the teacher digital competency framework. The two new competencies cover the major elements of teacher AI competencies – AI ethics and professional engagement (e.g. professional continuous learning). In this study, we argue that learning and working with AI and emotional aspects are important to both teachers and students. Students who are interested in AI and have strong self-reflective mindsets will be able to learn and work with it more efficiently.
## 2. This study and method
### 2.1. Research question
In this study, we synthesize the views above to present the modified definitions of AI literacy and competency as the major framework (Chiu et al., [Citation2024](#)). The original definitions were developed in an iterative cycle by an international panel of five scholars in the field of AI and education, as well as a group of 30 experienced AI teacher participants from different major academic backgrounds. The teacher participants all had more than 3 years of experience teaching AI; most of them were responsible for integrating AI in their schools. The panel and the teacher participants reached a consensus on the following definitions: In this paper, we intentionally present both definitions to distinguish the differences between AI literacy and competency.
\* AI literacy is defined as “an individual’s ability to clearly explain how AI technologies work and impact society, as well as to use them in an ethical and responsible manner and to effectively communicate and collaborate with them in any setting. It focuses on knowing (i.e. knowledge and skills).” (Chiu et al., [Citation2024](#)).
\* AI competency is defined as “an individual’s confidence and ability to clearly explain how AI technologies work and impact society, as well as to use them in an ethical and responsible manner and to effectively communicate and collaborate with them in any setting. They should have the confidence and ability to self-reflect on their AI understanding for further learning. It focuses on how well individuals use AI in beneficial ways.” (Chiu et al., [Citation2024](#)).
AI literacy and AI competency are separate but interdependent, see Figure 1. Individuals who have stronger knowledge about how AI processes data (AI literacy) are more likely to investigate the bias of an AI plant recognition application, i.e. they can examine the effectiveness of the application (i.e. productivity in AI competency). On the other hand, individuals can learn with an AI-based language application in a productive way, which does not imply they know if the application is ethical. Therefore, AI competency extends beyond AI literacy by incorporating emotional aspects and learning and working with AI. Emotional aspects (such as confidence and interest) are directly linked to healthy use. For example, students who are interested in AI would be happy to use it. Learning and working with AI are concerned about effectively learning and working with AI. Therefore, students should have a strong self-reflective mindset to constantly learn AI and use the most up-to-date and effective AI technologies for learning and working.

In this review study, we used the following five areas of AI competencies to analyze the selected articles (Chiu et al., [Citation2024](#)): (1) Technology: confidence and ability to clearly explain how AI technologies work; (2) Impact: confidence and ability to clearly explain how AI technologies impact society; (3) Ethics: confidence and ability to use AI technologies in an ethical and responsible manner; (4) Collaboration: confidence and ability to effectively communicate and collaborate with AI technologies in any setting; (5) Self-reflection: confidence and ability to self-reflect on their AI understanding for further learning, including the use of AI applications. Individuals with stronger self-reflective mindsets are more likely to keep reviewing their AI knowledge and identify areas and needs for further learning.
Currently, there have been many review studies conducted to identify the teaching and learning of AI (Rizvi et al., [Citation2023](#)) and machine learning (Sanusi et al., [Citation2023b](#)), pedagogical design of AI (Xia et al., [Citation2022](#)), AI literacy in school (Casal-Otero et al., [Citation2023](#)), assessment in school AI literacy curriculum (Chiu et al., [Citation2022](#)), and the research trends of AI in education (Chiu et al., [Citation2023](#)). However, they did not provide clear connections among the content, activities, and assessment, as well as consider self-reflective mindsets and emotional aspects (i.e. AI competency).
Accordingly, this systematic review presented our definitions of AI literacy and competency and used the five areas of AI competency to analyze articles. The five areas are viewed as learning outcomes that are associated with content, teaching / learning and assessment. These aim to suggest what and how to teach and assess when fostering student AI literacy and competency in school. Three key research questions guided the review.
RQ1: How does design of content foster AI literacy and competency?
RQ2: What are the major instructional approaches for nurturing AI literacy and competency?
RQ3: How is student AI literacy and competency being assessed?
### 2.2. Article identification
This review followed the PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) guidelines (Page et al., [Citation2021](#)) in three stages: (i) selecting articles, (ii) screening and including articles, and (iii) coding, extracting, and analyzing data.
This review specifically selected articles that were relevant to K-12 students’ AI literacy and competency. To identify the appropriate published articles, three authors engaged in collaborative discussions and developed criteria for article selection, as shown in Figure 2. The following search query was utilized to filter articles with these terms in their titles, keywords, or abstracts, published between 1 January 2014, and 31 December 2023: [(“artificial intelligence”) AND (literacy OR education OR competence OR competency) AND (“K-12”) OR (“school?”) OR (“child\\*”) OR (“kid?”) OR (“kindergarten?”) OR (“pre-tertiary”)]. We conducted the search in ProQuest, Scopus, and Web of Science (WOS), with a focus on the educational field. The initial search resulted in a total of 986 articles: 350 from ProQuest, 420 from Scopus, and 216 from WOS. The investigation focused solely on peer-reviewed journal articles and reputable conference papers published in English.

### 2.3. Article screening and inclusion
Articles for the main analysis were selected through screening and inclusion procedures. First, using the duplication detection function of EndNote 20, 113 duplicate articles were removed. Then, two of the authors examined the titles and abstracts of the articles to identify empirical studies published in journals and conference papers, specifically focusing on school education. Review articles, conceptual papers, commentaries, and editorials were excluded from the selection process. When the two authors had a disagreement regarding paper identification, a third author was assigned to assess the paper and make the final decision. Based on these criteria, 647 articles were further excluded. Additionally, 36 duplicates that were initially overlooked by EndNote 20 were identified and removed, resulting in a remaining 188 articles. The full texts of these 188 articles were thoroughly reviewed, leading to the exclusion of 161 articles that did not meet the criteria. The inclusive criteria are that the articles must be empirical studies and related to AI literacy and/or competency in school education. Among these, 41 articles were not empirical studies, 116 did not focus on school students’ AI competency, 3 were inaccessible in terms of full text, and 1 exhibited unreliable data analysis (e.g. small sample sizes, i.e. less than 30; no baseline score for comparisons; no assumptions checking). After this process, 27 articles remained. A snowballing method, i.e. cited article references from the 27 articles, was then used to identify additional research articles written by the authors, resulting in the inclusion of 20 more articles meeting the criteria. In the end, a total of 47 articles were retained, as shown in Figure 2. Five, 18, and 27 of the selected articles focused on kindergarten, primary, and secondary contexts, respectively.
### 2.4. Coding and analysis
To analyze the articles, we used our definition and a curriculum framework of Chiu et al. ([Citation2022](#)) to form a 2 × 2 analysis framework. The definitions serve the five main areas, while the curriculum framework identifies three domains of AI knowledge for school AI education: (i) fundamentals of AI, (ii) various branches of AI, and (iii) impact of AI. Fundamentals of AI concern what AI is and how it works. Various branches of AI include how AI see (computer vision), hear (speech recognition), speak (text-to-speech), read (natural language processing), reason, simulate, as well as think and create (generative adversarial networks). The impact of AI includes ethical use, social good, and the future of work. The framework was the result of a collaborative effort between computer scientists, education professors, and frontline teachers.
Coding categories for this systematic review were established based on the research questions using both inductive and deductive approaches. To address the three research questions, two non-corresponding authors independently coded the selected articles by extracting the design content and activities related to AI competency, as well as the assessment approaches and instructional designs. The coders first tried coding 10 articles, then had a meeting with the corresponding authors to discuss and confirm the coding approaches, domains, and outcomes. After that, the coders continued extracting and synthesizing information, further updating the framework into five areas: technology, collaboration, impact, ethics, self-reflection. Moreover, the corresponding author acts as a moderator and made the final decision when the two coders had disagreement (Table 1).

## 3. Results
### 3.1. RQ1: how does design of content foster AI competency?
To answer this question, this study used the five areas to analyze the selected articles for review. Figure 3 shows the distributions of the reviewed articles in each area.

#### 3.1.1. Technology: explain how AI technologies work
In the area of technology, around 39% of the reviewed articles suggested four topics: (i) definitions, terminology, and concepts; (ii) history and development; (iii) human intelligence vs. computer intelligence; and (iv) learning from data.
Definition, terminology, and concept. The students should acquire a thorough understanding of the terms, fundamental concepts, and essence of AI machines, as well as how this technology could be used to recognize and address a wide range of issues that people face (Chiu, [Citation2021](#); Kim et al., [Citation2021](#); Lu & Fan, [Citation2023](#); Ng et al., [Citation2022](#)).
History and development. The students should learn about the history of AI and its recent advancements, such as the fourth industrial revolution, and how AI fundamentally changes the concept of how machines work (Chiu, [Citation2021](#); Lu & Fan, [Citation2023](#)).
Human intelligence vs. computer intelligence. The students should identify the fundamental similarities and differences between human intelligence and artificial minds (Baldoni et al., [Citation2021](#), [Citation2022](#); Jiang et al., [Citation2022](#); Shamir & Levin, [Citation2021](#); Su & Yang, [Citation2024](#)). They should be able to differentiate between technologies that utilize AI or those that do not, recognize essential characteristics of AI technologies, and cultivate their comprehension of how AI is present in everyday life (Chiu, [Citation2021](#); Ng et al., [Citation2022](#); Sanusi et al., [Citation2023a](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2023](#)). For example, in the study by Jiang et al. ([Citation2022](#)), the students were able to appreciate the distinction between human and machine decision-making processes by comparing their text categorization techniques with those of the computers. These improved student comprehension of human accountability for the advancement of AI technology.
Learning from data. The students should understand the basic knowledge of learning models, including machine learning, deep learning, supervised learning, and unsupervised learning (Akram et al., [Citation2022](#); Jiang et al., [Citation2022](#); Ma et al., [Citation2023](#)). They should have the skills of training and testing a machine learning model, for example, recognizing human emotions (Vartiainen et al., [Citation2020a](#)) and rubbish types for recycling (Martins et al., [Citation2024](#)). The reviewed studies revealed that Google teachable machines were the most popular tools used in student learning. The studies suggested that by participating in model testing and training, students may get practical or create experience, leading to a deeper comprehension of how AI handles data.
Overall, these four topics enable students to describe what AI is and how it works and evolves. However, how to design and teach two arguably important elements of AI – big data and cloud computing – was not well investigated (Wang & Cheng, [Citation2021](#)). It could be due to their abstract nature, and further pedagogical work is needed to make them accessible to students.
#### 3.1.2. Collaboration: confidence and ability to effectively communicate and collaborate with AI technologies
Around 21% of the reviewed articles discussed how to foster students’ confidence and ability to effectively communicate and collaborate with AI technologies; see Figure 4. They identified five topics that span across different branches of AI technologies: (i) see (computer vision), (ii) hear (automatic speech recognition) and speak (text-to-speech), (iii) read and write (natural language processing and generation), (iv) think and create (generative adversarial networks), and (v) reason.
\* See. This topic focuses on the advancement of student AI understanding in the computer vision and recognition domains. Some reviewed studies introduced theoretical knowledge of visual recognition, i.e. fundamental physics knowledge about light as data for image processing, in their curriculum (Kim et al., [Citation2021](#); Tedre et al., [Citation2020](#); Williams et al., [Citation2023](#)). Some other studies used hands-on and creative approaches to teach this topic. For example, to learn about how classifiers work, the students created and evaluated machine learning to detect facial expressions (Sanusi et al., [Citation2023c](#); Toivonen et al., [Citation2022](#)) and poses (Williams et al., [Citation2023](#)). Vachovsky et al. ([Citation2016](#)) reported how students solved a real-life problem in hospital safety that determines whether doctors use a hand sanitizing dispenser before entering a room. The reviewed articles revealed that Google's teachable machine was the most widely used tool. Overall, this topic was taught in three ways: theory, practice, and problems.
\* Hear and speak. The students in this topic should understand how computers recognize speech. Four of the reviewed studies focused on the use of voice recognition. The students trained models to recognize their voices, names, and other simple voices (Kim et al., [Citation2021](#); Sanusi et al., [Citation2023c](#); Tedre et al., [Citation2020](#); Toivonen et al., [Citation2020](#)). The articles mentioned background noise interfering with model training as a challenge while teaching this topic. Not surprisingly, Google’s teachable machine was the most widely used tool. Moreover, how AI speaks seems understudied; only two studies attempted to include text-to-speech generation in teaching and learning (Van Brummelen et al., [Citation2021](#); Zhu & Van Brummelen, [Citation2021](#)). Overall, these studies focused solely on using voice recognition technology without teaching the students theoretical concepts such as the physics of sound (e.g. pitch and frequency). Voice recognition can move beyond human voices.
\* Read and write. The major content of this topic is natural language processing, which has two main categories: natural language understanding (read) and natural language generation (write). It enables the students to understand how computers comprehend, interpret, and manipulate human language. The reviewed articles suggested that the contents include sentence structure and meaning (e.g. Jiang et al., [Citation2022](#); Van Brummelen et al., [Citation2021](#); Zhu & Van Brummelen, [Citation2021](#)). Two of the studies used real-life problems and tools to demonstrate what the natural learning process does. For example, creating machine translation (Kim et al., [Citation2021](#)) and analyzing tweets to understand the needs of people in disaster areas (Vachovsky et al., [Citation2016](#)) Overall, this topic was taught in three ways: theory, practice, and problems. The reviewed articles focused on reading and its applications. The theories involve linguistic knowledge, which may be unfamiliar to teachers and students.
\* Think and create. In the reviewed articles, the students learned about deepfakes, generative adversarial networks’ generators and discriminators, and other generative AI tools (Ali et al., [Citation2021](#), [Citation2023a](#); Lee et al., [Citation2021](#); Williams et al., [Citation2019](#), [Citation2023](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2023](#)). These teach the students how generative AI tools work. The students experienced how AI created output by using generative AI tools such as text-to-images and critically examined the technology’s benefits (e.g. content creation) and risks (e.g. misinformation). In general, the students experienced the applications of how computers think and create, as well as their impact.
\* Reason. The reviewed studies revealed three major areas in this topic: (i) black box and white box; (ii) inductive reasoning and deductive reasoning; and (iii) rule-based systems and knowledge-based systems. These three areas allow the students to understand how an AI system makes decisions. Black boxes and white boxes are non-transparent and transparent in decision-making, respectively (Shamir & Levin, [Citation2022](#)). Inductive reasoning uses observed patterns to form general rules, while deductive reasoning uses rules to draw conclusions (Ma et al., [Citation2023](#); Shamir & Levin, [Citation2021](#); Williams et al., [Citation2019](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2023](#)). Examples such as “Rock-Paper-Scissors” were used to explain the two types of reasoning (Williams et al., [Citation2019](#)). Knowledge-based reasoning allows a system to learn from previous data and connect relevant parameters to improve solutions, while rule-based reasoning requires machines to follow specific instructions and execute stored rules for tasks (Shamir & Levin, [Citation2022](#)). Overall, the reviewed studies revealed that the students were given opportunities to learn the definitions with superficial examples. The reasoning for complex real-life problems was not explained.

#### 3.1.3. Impact: confidence and ability to clearly explain how AI technologies impact society
\* To nurture the area of impact, around 24% of the reviewed studies suggested that the students should understand the three topics: (i) social good, (ii) future of work, and (ii) risk.
\* Social good. The students discussed how AI has the potential to improve our lives by addressing global, societal, and communal problems and challenges. For example, Healthcare: early detecting diabetic retinopathy through photographic images analyzed by AI algorithms; Environmental Conservation: using AI to gather and analyze data from beehives, aiming to understand and support bee populations, which are crucial for global food supply; Sustainable Practices: AI to optimize agricultural practices, aiming to increase crop yields and reduce food waste, which is vital for addressing global food shortages. They need to understand that careful design and management are needed to make AI a socially good product (Chiu et al., [Citation2022](#); Du et al., [Citation2023](#); Vachovsky et al., [Citation2016](#)).
\* Future of work: The students should be aware of how AI may impact on the future workforce. AI could replace some jobs, but also create new jobs or industries. They should be aware of what skills and competencies they need for future jobs (Lee et al., [Citation2021](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2023](#)). Additionally, the students should be exposed to jobs that included AI usage and to role models in the industries, which give them opportunities to explore future careers aligned with their personalities, strengths, and weaknesses (Lee et al., [Citation2021](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2023](#)). However, some findings indicated that the students might not be interested in this topic, as they thought career planning was early at kindergarten context (Ng et al., [Citation2023](#)).
\* Risk: The reviewed articles suggested that the students should investigate potential risks associated with AI by examining how the technologies have caused harms in various contexts (Ali et al., [Citation2023b](#); Ma et al., [Citation2023](#); Williams et al., [Citation2023](#); Yang et al., [Citation2023](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2023](#)). Some of the articles used a role-playing approach in teaching. The students acted as AI creators and founders to investigate the societal impacts of their envisioned AI businesses, and explore strategies to properly design their businesses to prevent undesirable consequences.
#### 3.1.4. Ethics: confidence and ability to use AI technologies in an ethical and responsible manner
Approximately 16% of the reviewed articles suggested that students should understand that ethical design is not a simple binary decision between good and evil. The ethical design and human bias, or AI ethics, are connected. The students need to confront ethical dilemmas and learn more about how decisions are made (Lee et al., [Citation2021](#); Lin et al., [Citation2023](#); Tedre et al., [Citation2020](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2023](#)). For instance, one study utilized augmented reality and driverless cars as a case study to look at ethical issues with AI (Lin et al., [Citation2023](#)). The students decide on the targeted person (e.g. kids, dogs, pregnant women, men, elderly, etc.) that the driverless cars would hit in various augmented reality scenarios. They discussed moral dilemmas from the perspectives of various roles, such as mothers, friends, and car factories. These aimed to establish a contextualized and interactive learning environment where students may explore ethical issues around AI. Additionally, in a few of the articles, the students developed responsible solutions to real-world issues through the use of design thinking. They were requested to assess the reliability of the model training data used in their development and the effects of their solutions on society (Du et al., [Citation2023](#); Polpanumas et al., [Citation2021](#)).
#### 3.1.5. Self-reflection: confidence and ability to self-reflect on their AI understanding for further learning
No reviewed article directly addressed this area. Since AI is an emerging technology, it is important that the students self-reflect or evaluate their understanding of AI regularly to learn about the latest technology. The reviewed studies used affective or emotional engagement to indirectly enhance this area. The students who have a stronger interest in or a more positive attitude toward AI are more likely to check if they need to update their knowledge of AI technologies (Lee et al., [Citation2021](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2023](#)).
### 3.2. RQ2: what are the major instructional approaches for nurturing AI competency?
The reviewed articles suggested five instructional approaches to teach AI. They were (i) learning through experience, (ii) design thinking, (iii) contextualized learning, (iv) controversial discussions, as well as (v) model training and testing (see Figure 5; Table 2).
\* Learning through experience. Around 10% of the reviewed articles suggested that the students understood the basics of machine learning through experience with AI applications (Sanusi et al., [Citation2023c](#); Vartiainen et al., [Citation2020b](#), [Citation2021](#); Williams et al., [Citation2019](#)). This approach has been used to teach young students (in kindergarten and primary schools) some introductory machine learning concepts. The popular platforms used for this instructional design included Google Teachable Machine, AI for Oceans, and Quick Draw.
\* Design thinking. Approximately 21% of the reviewed articles suggested that with this way of thinking, the students were inspired to empathize with individuals’ needs, identify social problems, generate solutions, create prototypes, test them, and make refinements. This approach was implemented through interdisciplinary projects focused on environmental protection, agriculture, healthcare, and community challenges (Du et al., [Citation2023](#); Martins et al., [Citation2024](#); Ng et al., [Citation2023](#); Sakulkueakulsuk et al., [Citation2018](#); Vachovsky et al., [Citation2016](#)). Some of the studies assigned students specific topics, such as creating recycling bins and classifying mango quality (Ng et al., [Citation2023](#); Sakulkueakulsuk et al., [Citation2018](#)), while others allowed the students to set their own problems (Du et al., [Citation2023](#); Polpanumas et al., [Citation2021](#); Sanusi et al., [Citation2023a](#); Toivonen et al., [Citation2020](#); Williams et al., [Citation2023](#)). Relevance of problems, feasibility of prototypes (Vartiainen et al., [Citation2020b](#), [Citation2021](#)), and ethical issues should be considered in using design thinking for teaching (Du et al., [Citation2023](#); Williams et al., [Citation2023](#)).
\* Contextualized learning. Twelve percent of the reviewed articles suggested that students’ comprehension of the societal implications and ethical concerns of AI was enhanced via contextualized learning, which involved immersing them in particular circumstances. For example, card games, augmented reality, news creator simulations, and digital games have been used to guide students in understanding how AI drives misinformation (Ali et al., [Citation2021](#)), how driverless cars make decisions (Lin et al., [Citation2023](#); Martins et al., [Citation2024](#)), how entrepreneurs reduce possible AI-related risks to their businesses (Ali et al., [Citation2023b](#)), and what factors drive the biases of AI models’ outputs (Yang et al., [Citation2023](#)).
\* Controversial discussions. Approximately 18% of the reviewed articles found that controversial discussions, such as debates and role-playing, helped the students engage in more critical discussions and deeper interactions from a variety of perspectives. Debates on AI ethics encouraged students to engage in a more in-depth discussion regarding the ethical implications and societal impact of AI from numerous angles. Examples include regulations on deepfakes (Ali et al., [Citation2021](#)), generative AI tools for schoolwork (Ali et al., [Citation2023a](#)), and human bias for AI applications (Du et al., [Citation2023](#); Williams et al., [Citation2023](#); Yang et al., [Citation2023](#)). Furthermore, role-playing, which is comparable to debating, can help students obtain a deeper knowledge of artificial intelligence. For example, the students took on the role of crucial components (e.g. discriminators and generators) in talks to feel the system's intelligence (Lee et al., [Citation2021](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2023](#)).
\* Model training and testing. The most commonly used instructional design was model training and testing that encouraged the students to collect their data (Lu & Fan, [Citation2023](#); Sakulkueakulsuk et al., [Citation2018](#)), clean and label their data (Jiang et al., [Citation2022](#); Martins et al., [Citation2024](#)), train and test their models (Lu & Fan, [Citation2023](#); Polpanumas et al., [Citation2021](#); Shamir & Levin, [Citation2021](#), [Citation2022](#)), and evaluate their models’ performance (Martins et al., [Citation2024](#); Tedre et al., [Citation2020](#)). The platforms used for this teaching were accessible, and they were Google Teachable Machine, Machine Learning for Kids, and Snap! (Chao et al., [Citation2023](#); Jiang et al., [Citation2022](#); Ng et al., [Citation2023](#); Sanusi et al., [Citation2023a](#), [Citation2023c](#); Tedre et al., [Citation2020](#)). The high accessibility of the platforms makes AI learning more accessible.

### 3.3. RQ3: how is student AI competency being assessed?
The reviewed articles suggested five approaches to assess AI competency. They were (i) AI literacy test, (ii) self-reported survey, (iii) assessment rubrics, (iv) classroom observations, and (v) interviews, see Figure 6 (Table 3).
\* AI literacy test. Different AI literacy tests were used to assess students’ AI knowledge and concepts. The topics tested included machine learning, neural networks, supervised vs. unsupervised learning (Lu & Fan, [Citation2023](#); Martins et al., [Citation2024](#); Ng et al., [Citation2023](#); Shamir & Levin, [Citation2021](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2023](#)), workflow of an AI system (Tedre et al., [Citation2020](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2023](#)), limitations and capabilities of an AI technology (Tedre et al., [Citation2020](#); Williams et al., [Citation2023](#)), and ethical awareness (Lin et al., [Citation2023](#)). The tests were designed in the form of multiple-choice and short-answer questions. Most of the articles used pre- and post-tests to assess student learning (Hsu et al., [Citation2022](#); Jiang et al., [Citation2022](#); Lin et al., [Citation2023](#); Ng et al., [Citation2023](#); Wan et al., [Citation2020](#)). However, the studies did not validate a comprehensive AI literacy test covering all key learning topics.
\* Self-reported survey. In the reviewed studies, the self-reported survey is one of the most common approaches to collecting student perceptions. The perceptions included self-efficacy, confidence, attitudes, interests, and motivation related to AI learning (Chai et al., [Citation2021](#), [Citation2022](#); Kim et al., [Citation2021](#); Sakulkueakulsuk et al., [Citation2018](#); Shamir & Levin, [Citation2021](#), [Citation2022](#); Vachovsky et al., [Citation2016](#); Williams et al., [Citation2023](#)). Other than how the students think and feel, some of the studies assessed students’ self-reported AI learning, such as AI for social good (Chai et al., [Citation2021](#), [Citation2022](#); Du et al., [Citation2023](#); Vachovsky et al., [Citation2016](#)), and AI-related careers (Jiang et al., [Citation2022](#); Vachovsky et al., [Citation2016](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2023](#)).
\* Assessment rubrics. Rubrics were used to assess students’ AI artifacts and projects. The items in the rubrics included technological proficiency, ethical considerations, potential social impact, creativity and innovation, usability, and user friendliness (Du et al., [Citation2023](#); Ng et al., [Citation2023](#); Polpanumas et al., [Citation2021](#); Sanusi et al., [Citation2023a](#); Williams et al., [Citation2023](#)).
\* Classroom observation. Classroom observation assesses student learning processes that the three earlier score-focused assessments cannot do. The learning process can be assessed by analyzing students’ behavior patterns, group discussions, sensemaking, and interactions with teachers and peers (Tedre et al., [Citation2020](#); Toivonen et al., [Citation2020](#); Wan et al., [Citation2020](#); Yang et al., [Citation2023](#)). The data collection included video recordings, system logs, and reflective journals (Akram et al., [Citation2022](#); Lin et al., [Citation2023](#); Yang et al., [Citation2023](#)). However, very few studies explicitly show how to analyze those qualitative data, e.g. no clear coding schemes.
\* Interviews. The reviewed articles suggested that semi-structured interviews, focus groups, and artifact-based interviews were conducted to identify students’ understanding and conceptions of AI (Dai et al., [Citation2024](#); Ottenbreit-Leftwich et al., [Citation2023](#); Shamir & Levin, [Citation2022](#)). The interview questions focused on topics such as the definition and application of AI, the process of AI, the benefits and harms of AI, and the technical skills of model training (Ottenbreit-Leftwich et al., [Citation2023](#); Sanusi et al., [Citation2023a](#); Shamir & Levin, [Citation2022](#); Wan et al., [Citation2020](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2023](#)).

### 4.1. Suggestions on content fostering the five confidences and abilities of AI competency
Integrating AI in school is still new and can be viewed as two areas: learning about AI in a curriculum and using AI to support learning in various subjects. Hereby, what and how to foster AI literacy (learning about AI) and competency (using AI to support learning) still remain unclear. Our findings explicitly suggest 13 topics to foster four of the five abilities in our definition because no reviewed article addressed the last area – self-reflection. The areas and their topics are crucial for research and practices in AI and education.
1. To foster student confidence and ability to clearly explain how AI technologies work, our findings suggest the clear topics, i.e. what to teach (see subsection 3.1.1). They also revealed that machine learning tools are easily available to students and instructors, allowing them to grasp how computers learn from data by creating machine learning. Furthermore, the two topics – big data and cloud computing – were rarely covered in the studied literature (see subsection 3.1.1), yet they are fundamental to explain how AI technologies work.
2. To foster student confidence and ability to effectively communicate and collaborate with AI technologies in any setting, our findings also expressly stated that the students should study the following topics: see, hear, and speak; read and write; think and create; and reason (subsection 3.1.2). See, hear, read, and write were the most well-documented and investigated topics. It might be because their applications are more popular. The students may readily use the programs for their learning, such as see (facial recognition), hear (convert speech to text in instant messaging platforms), read, and write (AI editing software). Apart from the topics see and hear, the students only experienced the applications of AI tools in the topics. They were told how the tools work. The reviewed studies mainly focused on practice, without sufficient integration of theory or problem-solving skills. In this circumstance, the students may be unable to properly interact and work with AI systems that do more than just see and hear. Furthermore, the reviewed articles missed the interdisciplinary nature of AI (Chiu, [Citation2024a](#); Chiu et al., [Citation2022](#)), and always addressed technical issues, i.e. engineering perspectives. However, teaching the topics requires non-technical knowledge. For example, in order to recognize a dolphin, the students must be familiar with its appearances, features, and characteristics. To determine how positive a message posted in a forum is, the students must be proficient in language literacy. The interdisciplinary nature of the topic should be taken into account when designing the content for AI education.
3. The reviewed articles are adequately documented in order to foster the two abilities to clearly explain how AI technology impacts society and use AI technologies ethically and responsibly. Our findings summarized them and explicitly suggested what to teach. The possible explanation is that this ability overlaps with digital literacy (Martin & Grudziecki, [Citation2006](#)), which is often integrated into school education. The teachers and students are not unfamiliar with the confidences and abilities.
4. No reviewed articles suggested what to foster student confidence and ability to self-reflect their AI understanding for further learning. This finding indicates that the current studies focus on AI literacy rather than AI competency. In other words, the current studies on AI education may not be aligned with UNESCO’s guidelines on AI competency. Moreover, to foster a self-reflection mindset, we recommend that students recognize AI as emerging knowledge, as opposed to traditional knowledge in mathematics and sciences (which rarely changes). Teachers should emphasize that AI competency goes beyond knowledge and is a lifelong learning skill; for example, teachers can show the latest applications of AI for daily use.
### 4.2. How does the five identified instructional designs foster the five areas?
Five instructional designs were identified in the reviewed articles: model training and testing, learning through experience, contextualized learning, controversial discussions, and design thinking.
Model training and testing are closely related to AI knowledge development and are suggested to nurture the area of technology. This instructional design is also very important in the area of collaboration. When the students understand how AI perceives the world through see, hear, and speak, read and write, they do understand the bias and limitations of the output generated by AI. This understanding facilitates student-computer collaboration and communications.
Learning through experience is very important for the students to unpack the black box of AI (Gillani et al., [Citation2023](#)). The black box processes are complicated and abstract, e.g. think, create, and reason. It is not easy to use model training and testing to teach the students the topics. Therefore, we suggest this instructional design can help students learn how to unpack the back box after gaining some understanding of think, create, and reason.
Contextualized learning involves immersing the students in particular circumstances, which enables them to feel how AI impacts their lives and society. These instructional designs are more effective in nurturing the two areas of impact and ethics. It is because the areas are more contextual and depend on the situations. Moreover, this learning enables the students to understand whether they have adequate knowledge to deal with AI in their everyday lives, which is more likely to encourage them to keep their AI knowledge updated.
The instructional design of controversial discussions is particularly important in AI learning because AI output is not binary – true or false. AI outputs may depend on cultures; for example, what classroom learning should be in the east may not be the same in the west. This design is more likely to allow the students to dig deeper into the ethical and moral issues AI brings to us and our society through participating in debates and getting different perspectives. It is more effective in fostering the areas of impact, ethics, and self-reflection.
Among the five instructional designs identified in the reviewed articles, design thinking may be the only learning approach that can effectively foster all five confidences and abilities throughout the iterative project cycle (Chiu et al., [Citation2021](#); Long & Magerko, [Citation2020](#)). The students cultivated social-good attitudes during the empathy phase, considered the risks and interests of stakeholders when defining the problem, and designed and prototyped solutions using their AI knowledge (Chiu et al., [Citation2021](#)). After completing the full design cycle, students reflected on their interests and career opportunities in AI and developed the habit of self-reflection to stay current on emerging technologies and their societal impacts. For example, they can discuss what careers they want to do in the future, and what AI competency they need for the careers.
### 4.3. How do the five assessment approaches evaluate the areas
Our analyses identified five assessment approaches: AI literacy test, self-reported survey, assessment rubrics, classroom observations, and interviews.
AI literacy tests were used to assess students’ knowledge and understanding of concepts. These are critical to AI education. Most current studies on measuring AI literacy use self-reported approaches (Laupichler et al., [Citation2023](#); Lin et al., [Citation2021](#)). They may measure AI self-efficacy rather than AI literacy. To the best of our knowledge, there are few or no reliable and valid AI literacy objective measures available for school education. This finding could highlight that objective measures may be a more appropriate approach to measuring the areas of technology and collaboration.
Self-reported surveys identified students’ self-efficacy, confidence, attitudes, interests, and motivations related to AI learning. It is suggested that they measure the area of self-reflection that is more about student perceptions.
Assessment rubrics evaluate the quality of students’ artifacts and projects. During artifact-making and project-based learning, the students are more likely to have different learning activities, such as presenting their ideas, explaining their solutions, and answering questions. While assessing these activities may allow educators to gain a sense of the knowledge students acquired, it is not as precise as test, especially in groupwork. It could be more appropriate to assess the more open areas, such as impact and ethics.
Classroom observations assess the learning process by examining students’ behaviors, group discussions, sensemaking, and interactions. Although classroom observations were used to evaluate student learning in the reviewed article, this approach only allows school teachers to gain an overall impression of how the class reacts to their teaching, i.e. evaluating instructional designs. Therefore, we suggest observation may be ineffective in assessing the five areas. However, observation may be enhanced by AI applications in the future, i.e. AI can analyze classroom activities and evaluate lesson qualities.
Interview is the last approach identified in the review, which provides insights into students’ conceptions and perspectives on learning AI. It is more appropriate for non-binary areas. Therefore, we suggest interviews are more effective in assessing the areas of impact, ethics, and reflection. Similar to classroom observations, AI can be used to analyze the interview data in the future to precisely and accurately assess student AI competency.
Overall, the integration of AI in schools, which is still in its early stages, can be divided into two areas: learning about AI in the curriculum and employing AI to support learning across several subjects. As a result, what and how to promote AI literacy (learning about AI) and competency (using AI to enhance learning) remain unclear. The above discussions and suggestions based on our definition of AI competency serve as the foundation for beginning AI teachers. We also suggest that the instructional designs and assessment approaches can be scaffolded to better nurture AI literacy and competency. For example, teacher educators should provide scaffolding to facilitate teachers’ instructional design and assessment decisions that are aligned to enhancing students AI literacy and competency.
### 4.4. Research gaps and future research directions
We outlined eight research gaps and their future research directions to foster school student AI literacy and competency.
\* \*\*Lack of studies on fostering the confidence and ability to communicate and collaborate with AI technologies\*\*. Our findings revealed many topics, such as think and create, and AI reasoning was not studied. Future research should be conducted to understand how to foster this confidence and ability. For example, studies could develop an interactive learning platform that allows the students to make conversations to understand how AI reasons; studies could also investigate how the students make their own chatbots that create conversations.
\* \*\*Lack of perspectives on ethical and societal effects of generative AI.\*\* The reviewed articles appeared to be well-documented in terms of both ethical and social impact from AI. However, the introduction of generative AI may provide a new dimension to AI education (Chiu, [Citation2024a](#)). Future research should be performed to better understand how generative AI affects these two abilities.
\* \*\*Lack of research on implementing AI projects using design thinking.\*\* Design thinking may have the potential to develop all five confidences and abilities. However, AI projects differ from non-AI projects in that they are more data-focused. More research is needed to understand how to apply design thinking to data, digital, and AI literacy.
\* \*\*Lack of research on the confidence and ability to learn and work with AI.\*\* More future studies are needed to nurture confidence and ability in a healthy and productive way. We suggest researchers can use student wellbeing or positive education approaches to investigate how students learn and work with AI healthily and productively.
\* \*\*Lack of research on the confidence and ability to self-reflect AI understanding for further learning.\*\* More research was needed to determine how to cultivate this confidence and ability that determine whether students can choose the most effective and up-to-date AI for effective and productive learning. Future studies could employ the psychology element to nurture student self-reflective mindsets.
\* \*\*Lack of validated literacy tests and standardized assessment rubrics.\*\* Future research should use rigorous methods to validate objective literacy tests and standardized rubrics, as well as to suggest coding schemes to measure students’ five confidences and abilities (Ali et al., [Citation2023a](#); Casal-Otero et al., [Citation2023](#); Chiu, [Citation2024a](#); Shamir & Levin, [Citation2021](#); Williams et al., [Citation2023](#)).
\* \*\*Lack of interdisciplinary competency framework.\*\* Although some studies (e.g. Chiu, [Citation2024a](#); Martins et al., [Citation2024](#); Sakulkueakulsuk et al., [Citation2018](#)) have indicated the interdisciplinary nature of AI, there is still little research providing interdisciplinary instructional frameworks to cultivate student AI competency.
\* \*\*More research on AI competency.\*\* This study presented a definition of AI competency and suggested that most studies actually studied AI literacy but not AI competency. We concur with Long and Magerko ([Citation2020](#)) that further research should revisit the concepts of AI literacy and competency. Therefore, to confirm the findings of this study, more studies should be conducted to understand what AI competency is and how to foster and assess it.
\* \*\*More research on how to assess the five dimensions.\*\* This study did not specify the ways in which each assessment method measures each aspect of AI competency. Future studies should use a meta-analysis approach to investigate how the five aspects of AI competency – technology, collaboration, impact, ethics, and self-reflection – are measured.
## 5. Conclusion and limitations
Forty-seven articles over the past 10 years in ProQuest, Scopus, and Web of Science were selected for review to provide evidence on how to foster AI literacy and competency in K-12 education. In this systematic review, we first defined the AI literacy and AI competency for K-12 education and used the five confidences and abilities identified in the AI competency to analyze the articles. The findings clearly suggest what and how to teach and what and how to assess when fostering AI literacy and competency. Researchers and educators can use them for designing and implementing AI education research and practice in K-12 education, but two limitations should be noted. First, most of the articles did not cover generative AI. Future studies should be conducted to understand how this emerging technology impacts the five confidences and abilities. Second, most of the reviewed studies focused on computer science and technology subjects. However, teaching AI could be interdisciplinary. Future studies should be conducted to select more articles that teach AI in other disciplines, such as arts and biology.
