# Mapping student-AI interaction dynamics in multi-agent learning environments: Supporting personalized learning and reducing performance gaps

## Abstract
Multi-agent AI systems, which simulate diverse instructional roles such as teachers and peers, offer new possibilities for personalized and interactive learning. Yet, the specific patterns of student-AI interaction and their pedagogical implications remain unclear. This study explores how university students engaged with multiple AI agents during a six-module course, and how these interactions influenced cognitive outcomes (learning gains) and non-cognitive factors (motivation, technology acceptance). Through the analysis of dialogue data, two core engagement patterns were identified: co-construction of knowledge and co-regulation. Students with lower prior knowledge relied more on co-construction of knowledge sequences and showed higher learning gains and post-course motivation. In contrast, students with higher prior knowledge engaged more in co-regulation behaviors but demonstrated limited learning improvement. Technology acceptance increased across all groups. These findings suggest that multi-agent systems can effectively support differentiated engagement and help reduce performance gaps by adapting to students' varying needs. This study makes three innovative contributions to existing research: it is based on a long-term formal curriculum, moving beyond fragmented, short-term interactions; it investigates a multi-agent collaborative mechanism that simulates diverse pedagogical roles (e.g., AI teacher, AI teaching assistance, AI classmates), distinguishing this work from single-agent systems; and it explores differentiated interaction modes based on prior knowledge, providing critical teaching implications for personalized learning.
## Introduction
Online learning has increasingly become a crucial mode of education. The advent of large language models (LLMs) has significantly advanced interactive, real-time conversation-based learning, enabling students to learn with a high degree of autonomy and address the interactive deficiencies and isolated learning challenges that have long hindered traditional online education (Kim et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024). Compared to traditional online learning environments (e.g., MOOCs), LLM-based online learning systems can support highly personalized learning experiences tailored to individual needs (Chen et al., 2024; Razafinirina et al., 2024). Meanwhile, this innovation demonstrates considerable promise in facilitating co-regulated learning processes between students and AI agents, contributing to the more effective attainment of learning goals.
In recent educational practice, a growing number of LLM-based agents have become key learning partners in online learning. These agents can function in diverse pedagogical roles, such as personalized tutors for problem-solving (Albdrani & Al-Shargabi, 2023), teachable agents for programming education and debugging (Liu et al., 2024), generative agents for teacher training (Lu et al., 2024), and assessors for student evaluation (Willert & Würz, 2025). These applications demonstrate that intelligent agents perform well in playing roles, understanding human instructions, facilitating interaction, and making decisions. Building on the promising capabilities of individual LLM-based agents, the concept of LLM-based multi-agent systems has been proposed to leverage the collective intelligence and specialized skills of multiple agents. Compared to single-agent systems, multi-agent systems offer several key advantages. First, they can simulate a complete teaching and learning process, providing a more holistic and structured learning experience. Second, they create a greater sense of presence for the student by engaging in multi-role interactions that mimic a real-world classroom. In addition, they enable differentiated personalization, empowering students to tailor the roles and functions of agents to better suit their individual learning needs (Yu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025).
However, existing educational research mostly approaches the design of AI-supported learning systems from a technological perspective or focuses on evaluating the effectiveness of the use of specific AI tools. A significant gap remains in understanding the nuanced process of student-AI interaction, particularly within a multi-agent learning environment. To address this gap, this study systematically analyzes the dialogic interaction between students and different AI agents. Each interaction is meticulously coded to identify its nature, followed by a transition analysis and lag sequential analysis (LSA) to uncover the underlying interaction dynamics. This study advances the field in three ways. First, it examines a multi-agent collaborative mechanism, moving beyond single agent seetings. Second, it is conducted in a long-term, formal curriculum setting, providing an authentic and ecologically valid context rather than short-term, fragmented interventions. In addition, it uncovers differentiated interaction modes based on prior knowledge, identifying their critical teaching implications for personalized learning.
Specifically, this study addresses the following research questions.
- 1)
What distinctive behavioral patterns of learning engagement emerge when students interact with multiple AI agents?
- 2)
How do interaction patterns differ between students with high prior knowledge and those with low prior knowledge?
- 3)
How does the interaction with AI agents contribute to students' learning gains?

## Conclusion
This study investigated how students interact with multiple AI agents in an LLM-empowered interactive learning environment and how such interactions shape their cognitive and motivational outcomes. By analyzing over 19,365 lines of dialogue data and comparing behavioral engagement, learning gains, and motivational changes across students with different levels of prior knowledge, the study offers new insights into the mechanisms and effects of human–AI collaboration in educational contexts.

- ### [STEM education: Understanding secondary students’ epistemic cognition in the design process with the support of a personalized multi-agent system](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0360131525002726)
2026, Computers and Education
Show abstract
The engineering design process is a vital component of integrated STEM education, where students' design practices both shape and are shaped by their epistemic cognition (EC). Nonetheless, research on secondary students' EC in STEM contexts is limited, and implementing personalized STEM instruction that accommodates diverse students' interests and needs poses significant challenges. Harnessing a Multi-Agent System (MAS) afforded by Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) offers a promising pathway to address this gap. Grounded in the design thinking model of \_E\_ DIPT (Empathize-Define-Ideate-Prototype-Test), this study developed a \_P\_ ersonalized \_MAS\_, namely EPMAS, to support secondary students' STEM project-based design process. Twenty students, grouped into dyads, participated in a five-day STEM winter camp. We sought to explore students' EC within the EPMAS-supported design process and their perceptions of the system. Data were collected through in-depth group interviews, supplemented with student artifacts and interaction log data, and analyzed using theoretical thematic analysis. Findings revealed students' engagement with epistemic aims, ideals, and reliable processes, reflecting their developed understanding of engineering design. Contrary to common concerns about over-reliance on GAI, students demonstrated high epistemic agency, critically employing EPMAS's stepwise guidance as part of their epistemic repertoire to pursue creative solutions. These results suggest that EPMAS-supported authentic STEM learning holds promise for broader school adoption.
