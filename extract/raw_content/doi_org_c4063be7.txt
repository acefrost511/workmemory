Abstract
--------

Making activities, such as creating individual artifacts via 3D printing, offer great potential for adolescents to acquire skills beneficial for their digital empowerment. To develop interventions that may increase adolescents’ making engagement, their making-related beliefs need to be explored and systematized. Therefore, based on the theory of planned behavior (TPB; Ajzen, [1991](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR2)), this interview study elicited beliefs regarding recreational making with *N* = 29 adolescents. The sample included both female adolescents and those from a low socioeconomic status to ensure that beliefs specific to these groups were not overlooked. The key research output is a category system that aligns with the TPB and differentiates salient beliefs that are important for recreational making. The discussion focuses on how this category system may be applied in future studies to create qualitative interview guidelines and quantitative questionnaires regarding making-related beliefs. The study also discusses how this category system can inform the development of tailored interventions and educational programs that resonate with adolescents’ beliefs.

1 Introduction
--------------

Although technology is part of adolescents’ everyday lives, whether they naturally develop the skills required to use it remains uncertain (Blikstein et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR12)). Thus, with advancing technological opportunities, it becomes increasingly important to foster “awareness, motivation, technical access and competence” in adolescents (Mäkinen, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR42), p. 391). In this context, making activities hold considerable potential to empower adolescents to use technology for self-selected purposes (Smolarczyk & Kröner, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR84)). These include using CAD software, 3D printers, or laser cutters to create individual artifacts. For adolescents, these activities provide attractive opportunities to acquire knowledge through active exploratory and creative processes rather than via direct instruction. Such activities can be pursued in makerspaces, such as Fab Labs, which are open workshops with the goal of providing public access to digital manufacturing technologies (Gershenfeld, [2008](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR28)). The making movement is characterized by a “do-it-yourself (or do-it-with-others) mindset”, and aims at bringing “together individuals around a range of activities, including textile craft, robotics, […] electronics, digital fabrication, mechanical repair, or creation” (Peppler & Bender, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR58), p. 22). Making activities have been claimed not only to foster science, technology, engineering, and mathematics competencies (STEM; Mørch et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR50); Papavlasopoulou et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR56)) but also to empower adolescents for the digital transformation of society and foster so-called 21st century skills (Ioannou et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR35)). This has been particularly evident in recent efforts to integrate artificial intelligence into making activities (Ng et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR52); Vartiainen & Tedre, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR79)). While making activities can take place in formal learning settings, such as schools, Fab Labs can also offer recreational activities for adolescents.

Although making activities are frequently associated with positive outcomes, participation remains uneven (Hsu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR33); Saari et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR66)). Students engaging in making activities are predominantly boys (Voigt et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR81)) and less often adolescents from underprivileged backgrounds (Holbert, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR31)). An equity-oriented approach to making activities is still in its early stages of development (Rouse & Rouse, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR65)). One issue addressed by equity-oriented making research concerns the design of makerspaces. According to Fasso and Knight ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR23), p. 279), it is necessary to “first understand the backgrounds and identity of the learners” and to consider this as the key element in the design of makerspaces. Along the same lines, Aschbacher et al. ([2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR9)) claim that to foster STEM activities, individuals should be able to engage as they are, in line with their identity. A practical starting point to make this possible may be to address potential participants’ making-related beliefs. These beliefs have repeatedly proven to be decisive in explaining young people’s intention to engage in (leisure) activities in various areas (e.g., sport, writing or artificial intelligence; Armitage & Conner, [2001](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR8); Birnbaum et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR11); Ivanov et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR36)). Systematically eliciting and categorizing these beliefs are crucial, especially for tailoring making environments that increase the participation of girls and adolescents from low socioeconomic status (SES) backgrounds, both of whom are underrepresented in the making domain. In addressing these beliefs, the theory of planned behavior (TPB; Ajzen, [1991](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR2)) serves as an effective framework, providing detailed procedures that may be used to adapt the theoretical framework to specific purposes and target groups across a wide range of behavioral domains (Francis et al., [2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR25)).

The study by Schüller and Kröner ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR70)) illustrates how the theory of planned behavior can be adapted to elementary school children’s artistic leisure activities. The authors conducted interviews with elementary-aged children regarding their artistic leisure activities. Evaluating the interviews, Schüller and Kröner ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR71)) inductively expanded the TPB, resulting in a differentiated, comprehensive category system. Although artistic and making activities both rely heavily on creativity, the category system developed by Schüller and Kröner ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR71)) cannot be expected to be directly transferred to making activities with their strong STEM component. Thus, based on interviews with adolescents in which we elicited their salient beliefs regarding making activities, we aimed to develop an elaborate category system capturing adolescents’ beliefs about recreational making activities.

Regarding the category system for making-related beliefs, our objective is to provide a basis for future studies that may be aimed at evaluating making interventions in a twofold way. First, a category system can be used as a basis for interview guidelines and the development of questionnaires. Second, it may inform the development of interventions, ensuring that a broad range of salient making-related beliefs are systematically considered.

2 Theory of planned behavior
----------------------------

The TPB postulates that three clusters of beliefs are sufficient to predict the intention to perform a behavior. The first cluster, behavioral beliefs, underlies attitudes toward a behavior. Based on expectancy-value theory (Eccles & Wigfield, [2002](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR21)) and research in the field of recreational writing (Birnbaum et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR11)), we further categorize these behavioral beliefs into three types: intrinsic value (the personal enjoyment experienced while participating in an activity), congruent consequences (benefits such as learning or producing something useful), and incongruent consequences (the sacrifices required to engage in an activity). The second cluster comprises normative beliefs that form the subjective norm. They reflect the expectations of significant others regarding an individual’s performance of an action. The third cluster refers to the control beliefs that underlie the perceived behavioral control (PBC) related to the performance of a behavior. PBC reflects obstacles and barriers versus resources and enablers related to performing a behavior and are either rooted in the person (e.g., self-efficacy) or in the environment (controllability, e.g., not having enough time).

In the following sections, we provide an overview of research on making-related beliefs, organized according to their relevance for behavioral, normative, and control beliefs. Afterward, we examine the role of beliefs in existing intervention studies in the making field. Since both girls and educationally disadvantaged adolescents, specifically those from low-SES families, are underrepresented in making activities (Saari et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR66)), we make sure to include them in our study aimed at eliciting making-related beliefs. Where research on beliefs regarding making is unavailable, we also draw from adjacent fields, such as STEM education. Based on this, we investigate how the TPB-aligned category system from Schüller and Kröner ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR71)) could serve as a deductive basis for a category system regarding adolescents’ beliefs in recreational making and how it has to be inductively adapted.

### 2.1 Behavioral beliefs of adolescents concerning STEM and making-related activities

In the realm of behavioral beliefs, intrinsic value seems to be an important predictor for pursuing activities, especially during leisure time (Birnbaum et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR11)). This is also true in the context of making and STEM; research studies explore aspects such as motivation and interest (Smolarczyk & Kröner, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR84) ; Ramey & Stevens, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR62)).

In addition to intrinsic value, the congruent consequences of making activities play a pivotal role as determinants of recreational making. When adolescents believe that making activities have positive consequences and a certain utility for everyday life, they might be more eager to participate in them. In several studies, girls benefitted from making activities emphasizing empathy (Peppler et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR59)) and creating things for others (Holbert, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR31)). Further studies support the idea that these congruent consequences in the form of utility value play an important role in STEM, especially regarding underrepresented groups (Asher et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR10)).

### 2.2 Normative beliefs of adolescents concerning STEM and making-related activities

The subjective norm refers to the perceived expectations of significant others, such as parents, teachers, or peers, regarding one’s participation in STEM and making-related activities. Parents’ involvement in their children’s education has been shown to affect STEM-related skills (Thomas et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR78)). Teachers may also influence attitudes, feelings of competence, and motivation (Aschbacher et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR9)). Moreover, a same-gender peer group not supporting STEM is associated with low STEM career interest, particularly among girls (Robnett & Leaper, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR64)).

### 2.3 Control beliefs of adolescents concerning STEM and making-related activities

Beyond behavioral and normative beliefs, perceived behavioral control (PBC) is crucial for the intention to perform the behavior in question. PBC can be differentiated into person- and environment-related control beliefs (Kröner, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR37)). The person side of PBC includes self-efficacy beliefs. Self-efficacy refers to a person’s beliefs about their own ability to perform a task. As low self-efficacy negatively correlates with the intention to participate in various activities (Armitage & Conner, [2001](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR8)), this should also hold true for the domain of making. Self-efficacy beliefs have been investigated, for example, with regard to robotics (Kucuk & Sisman, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR38); Nugent et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR55)). Thus far, making-related intervention studies have predominantly aimed at improving broad science self-efficacy beliefs, with mixed results (Schlegel et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR67); Vongkulluksn et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR82)).

On the environmental side of PBC, controllability refers to obstacles and impediments that can prevent a person from performing the behavior in question. In a study related to the determinants of art-related creative recreational activities (Schüller & Kröner, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR71)), adolescents’ control beliefs (especially availability of time and material) turned out to be salient. These control beliefs can likely be transferred to making, with Fab Labs providing the necessary space and materials.

### 2.4 The role of beliefs in intervention studies from the making field

As outlined in the previous sections, the TPB posits that positive beliefs related to a behavior ultimately lead to stronger intentions and corresponding behavior (Ajzen, [1991](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR2)). As out-of-school making behavior is highly valuable from an educational perspective, it is a crucial research question as to how making-related beliefs may be systematized by applying a well-established protocol for qualitative elicitation studies (Francis et al., [2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR25)). The results may later inform studies employing quantitative, qualitative, or mixed methods that scrutinize which beliefs are most relevant to making-related intentions and behavior. Based on their literature search extending to 2019, Smolarczyk and Kröner ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR84)) pointed out a general scarcity of such intervention studies regarding (recreational) making beliefs. To check whether this still holds true as of November 2024, we used an adapted search term[Footnote 1](#Fn1) in Scopus to search for recent intervention studies (Elsevier, [n.d.](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR22)). Within the time span of 2020 to 2024, we again found few intervention studies targeting adolescents’ beliefs (Cheng & Pang, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR15); Hsu et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR32), [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR33); Martin et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR45); Pijls et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR60); Techanamurthy et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR77)). Moreover, in the studies identified, specific facets of beliefs were targeted, including attitudes toward science (Hsu et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR32), [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR33)), utility for future career (Techanamurthy et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR77)), self-efficacy (Higgins et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR30); Martin et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR45)), beliefs regarding collaboration, creation, and mindset (Pijls et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR60)), and acceptance related to making (Cheng & Pang, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR15)).

Thus, the identified intervention studies regarding making activities targeted various facets of beliefs that may well be subsumed under the TPB framework. Attitudes toward science, design, engineering, and technology refer to how someone enjoys doing something (Hsu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR33)). These beliefs may be considered behavioral beliefs. In Pijls et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR60)), it was asked how children evaluated their making activities and experiences in creation, collaboration, and the development of a respective mindset. The concept of a “maker mindset” (p. 164) refers to persistence, confidence, and enjoyment and thus may be considered as encompassing both behavioral and personal control beliefs. Both collaboration and creation refer to how children behave in a workshop and are part of the actual behavior. Utility for a future job represents the congruent consequences of behavioral beliefs, while the different facets of self-efficacy are part of personal control beliefs.

While all these studies included important beliefs regarding making, none systematically included a comprehensive range of beliefs. In particular, normative beliefs have been omitted most of the time. This approach risks lacking relevant information on the determinants of making engagement. A comprehensive category system of making-related beliefs would therefore be helpful for future intervention studies to ensure that the relevant beliefs are assessed whether by questionnaires or interview guidelines.

### 2.5 Beliefs and practices in making for different groups

Developing effective making interventions requires knowledge of potentially relevant beliefs in different groups. For example, female adolescents, in particular, may hold the belief that making is just a highly technical endeavor that is far from their personal interests. An adequate number of female participants need to be part of the sample to examine this in elicitation studies. How the results gained from such studies might translate into intervention development is exemplified by making interventions that are sensitive to gender-specific belief differences by focusing on electronic textiles, thus linking the teaching of computation to creative crafting practices, such as sewing, which are activities that have traditionally attracted more female students. This is not to say that female students should stick to these niches in the making domain. Rather, it may increase the probability of girls finding personally meaningful entry points into highly technical making activities (Buchholz et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR13)).

Another belief that is informative for designing interventions tailored to attract girls to making activities is related to a specific consequence of engaging in making: designing objects for others (Peppler et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR59)). Along the same lines, a changed framing of making activities might render them more attractive to adolescents from low SES backgrounds. They often place a high value on tackling practical problems and creating solutions relevant to their personal circumstances, for example, by making something that the family could not otherwise afford or by addressing safety issues in their sometimes insecure communities (Calabrese Barton et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR14)). Taken together, these examples underscore the importance of including students with diverse backgrounds in elicitation studies on making-related beliefs.

### 2.6 Aim of the study and research question

Our aim is to achieve a comprehensive and detailed understanding of adolescents’ beliefs, including those of female and low-SES students. For this purpose, beyond the broad categories of behavioral, normative, and control beliefs suggested by the TPB, we may draw on the specific category system of beliefs identified in Schüller and Kröner’s ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR71))[Footnote 2](#Fn2) elicitation study of artistic leisure activities. However, despite a certain overlap in the domains of making and artistic leisure activities, the category system from Schüller and Kröner ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR71)) should not be uncritically transferred to the STEM-heavy domain of making. Thus, while using this category system as a deductive starting point, our research question is as follows: How should this category system be inductively revised based on interview data specific to making activities, thereby resulting in a comprehensive overview of adolescents’ beliefs regarding recreational making?[Footnote 3](#Fn3)

When selecting participants for our study, we focused on including girls and low-SES students, as these groups are known to be underrepresented in the field of making and tend to hold less positive making-related attitudes (Voigt et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR81)). While the same is true for adolescents with a migration background, this is probably due to the high proportion of low-SES students, which is known to contribute to negative educational attitudes and outcomes (Lüdemann & Schwerdt, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR41)). Note, however, that while we will also report group differences in the supplementary material of this article, they are not our main focus. Rather, we include female adolescents and those from low-SES families to ensure that making-specific beliefs that might be more salient to specific subsamples are not overlooked.

3 Method
--------

### 3.1 Sample

The participating students attended sixth to ninth grade (except grade 7) at a high school (gymnasium) in a small town in the state of North Rhine-Westphalia, Germany. The school’s catchment area includes the town itself and several nearby villages. The town lies in a former mining region and has about 40,000 inhabitants. Traditionally, this type of German school is associated with a somewhat privileged family background in terms of parental socioeconomic status or formal education. However, this has changed in the past decades, with almost 40% of current North Rhine-Westphalian adolescents obtaining a university entrance certificate (Landesbetrieb Information und Technik Nordrhein-Westfalen, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR39)). Beyond this, the gymnasium in our study allows for a particularly “non-elite” sample: It is the only gymnasium in a town with a low general level of formal education among adults, yet it has a high proportion of current high school graduates, including those from families with low formal education or migration backgrounds. Thus, it was not difficult to sample the 16 students from families with low formal education needed for our sampling plan, based on a total of 90 students who visited the Fab Lab for an introduction to digital making with their class.[Footnote 4](#Fn4) Nevertheless, the implications of sampling from a single school will have to be discussed.

The participating adolescents completed a questionnaire detailing their sociodemographic data, along with items assessing their engagement in science-related activities and the level of parental support they received. Following the questionnaire, the participants were given a guided tour of the local Fab Lab, while we determined the adolescents eligible for the interviews according to their responses to the socioeconomic data questionnaire. We also reviewed the questionnaires to ensure the inclusion of adolescents with lower levels of science engagement and reduced parental support. In the end, *n* = 15 boys and *n* = 14 girls were interviewed (*n* grade 6 = 15; *n* grade 8 = 7; *n* grade 9 = 7; Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#Tab1)).

In the preliminary questionnaire, we asked the adolescents about their mothers’ and fathers’ occupations and job descriptions. Accordingly, the International Socio-Economic Index of Occupational Status (ISEI) for both mother and father was assigned, following Ganzeboom ([2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR26)), with scores ranging from 10 to 89. The Highest International Socio-Economic Index of Occupational Status (HISEI) score corresponds to a higher ISEI when both parents are considered. Adolescents with HISEI scores of at least 60 were designated as high SES, while those with scores below 40 were classified as low SES. Adolescents whose HISEI scores fell within the range of 40–60 were designated as high SES if at least one parent had obtained a German university entrance certificate (“Abitur”) and were classified as low SES if neither parent had attained an Abitur. Consequently, *n* = 16 had a low SES background and *n* = 13 had a high SES background. Table [S3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#MOESM1) in the supplementary material provides information about the adolescents’ SES backgrounds.

### 3.2 Procedures in conducting the interviews

Prior to the interviews, the data protection officer of the university approved our study. We also obtained consent from parents and asked adolescents for their consent. During the interview, we provided drinks and cookies to make the adolescents feel more comfortable. Interviews were recorded with a dictation machine. Before starting, we explained that there were no right or wrong answers and that their answers would help develop recreational making programs for other adolescents. At the beginning of the interview, the adolescents were shown photos displaying making activities and things created with the help of 3D printers or other digital tools. We developed an interview guideline and tested it prior to this study with two adolescents in a cognitive lab setting to ensure overall understanding based on the procedure suggested by Francis et al. ([2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR25)). The resulting interview guideline is available at Open Science Framework.[Footnote 5](#Fn5) Since the interviews were part of a larger project focused on sustainability, the guideline also included some questions about adolescents’ opinions on sustainability, which are not pivotal to the present study. Four researchers, three of whom were female, conducted the interviews.

### 3.3 Preparation of the interview recordings for the analysis and coding process

We transcribed the interviews based on predefined transcription rules. Then, the interviews were content analyzed as proposed by Francis et al. ([2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR25)) and Mayring ([2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR46)). MAXQDA (VERBI Software, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR80)) was used to code the interviews. Within the software, we recreated the category system with the main TPB predictor construct and all subcategories from an elicitation study related to creative leisure activities of elementary school children as a starting point (Schüller & Kröner, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR71)).

While coding, we assigned statements to the categories according to the content. Thus, when a statement was part of an answer to a question aiming at eliciting a certain type of belief (or to one of the additional questions related to sustainability), we nevertheless coded it to the category that fitted best. For example, if we asked “Why would you pursue making activities?,” aiming at behavioral beliefs, and the answer was “if I felt able to use the technology,” we would code this as a self-efficacy belief and not as a behavioral belief. When coding a statement, the first author determined whether it aligned with an existing deductive category. If not, she created a new inductive subcategory. After coding several interviews, the first author reviewed and refined the emerging categories and codes. This iterative process continued until all interviews were coded.

In the meantime, a differentiated guideline with definitions, delineations, and examples was developed. An excerpt from the codebook and a sample coded passage are provided in the supplementary material (Fig. [S1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#MOESM1) and Fig. [S2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#MOESM1)). The project team discussed the categories to ensure comprehensibility after one third of the interviews were coded. Upon completion of the coding process and the development of the guideline, an independent researcher who was not involved in creating the category system conducted a peer debriefing to verify the comprehensibility and practical utility of the developed guideline and category system. Disagreements were resolved through collaborative discussions, after which a second independent researcher, not involved in developing the category system, performed the follow-up rating of all interviews. The inter-rater agreement based on all statements was κ = 0.71, which is considered good (Cicchetti, [1994](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR16)).

5 Discussion
------------

### 5.1 Main results

The primary objective of this study was to develop a detailed and comprehensive category system for adolescents’ making-related beliefs that could be utilized in future qualitative and quantitative studies on making activities. To this end, building on the TPB and the work of Schüller and Kröner ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR71)), we conducted a qualitative elicitation study from which the category system displayed in Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#Tab2) emerged. Despite the evident parallels between the adapted category system and that developed by Schüller and Kröner ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR71)), assessing the adaptability of the TPB to a novel domain is imperative, since beliefs may differ among groups and across domains (Ajzen, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR3)). Relevant beliefs for a specific domain are expected to appear only through new categories identified in an elicitation study. This overall procedure is important to ensure the quality of subsequent scale and intervention development.

### 5.2 Content validity of the category system

Any category system derived from the TPB is supposed to be based on behavioral, normative, and control beliefs at the top level of the category system (Ajzen, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR3)). Since this is also true for our category system, the basic requirements for content validity can be considered fulfilled. In the following paragraphs, we dive further into the subcategories and aspects subsumed under these three main categories.

#### 5.2.1 Behavioral beliefs

Most of the interviewed adolescents were in favor of making activities and stated that they wanted to try them out, since they perceived them as “enjoyable.” General pleasure also emerged as an important category in Schüller et al. ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR70)). This aligns with prior research indicating that intrinsic motivation is an important predictor of sustained and successful activities (Birnbaum et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR11)). The same is true for the utility-value categories. While our category system distinguishes between utility for existing hobbies and general utility, different utility-value facets were found in other studies from the field of STEM education (Gaspard et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR27)). It is noteworthy that the adolescents in the present study did not mention that they pursued activities to spend time with friends or family, in contrast to Schüller et al. ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR70)). At this point, the question concerning the underlying factors remains speculative. It is possible that in our sample, the idea did not come to mind, given the lack of engagement in making activities among peers.

Another difference from the study by Schüller and Kröner ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR71)) refers to the aspect of *phantasy*. In the present study, respective beliefs were classified under the subcategory of “congruent consequences,” whereas in Schüller and Kröner’s ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR71)) study, they were part of the “motivation in action” subcategory. This is due to the different roles of phantasy in painting and making. While in the original study on painting, phantasy was seen as a means of generating ideas that might well have only a loose relation to reality, in the present study, adolescents emphasized the use of phantasy as a tool for developing real products (e.g., “And I also think 3D printers are very cool because you can simply use them to turn your ideas into reality. For example, if you think you have a really cool idea for a new toy for children, then you can just use it to make it, which I think is very cool”; Interview 1B).

Since we included specific questions on behavioral beliefs related to sustainability, these might have been particularly salient in the present study. This applies to the aspect of *beliefs regarding the avoidance of costs*, *purchase*, *or waste*, illustrating how eliciting beliefs can be tailored to specific research needs. As the students mentioned sustainability only after being prompted, sustainability issues may be less spontaneously salient within the adolescents’ belief systems than indicated by this particular aspect derived from this study.

#### 5.2.2 Normative beliefs

As expected, the adolescents also mentioned that family members or friends might approve of their pursuit of making activities. This corresponds to existing research from various domains highlighting significant others as important supporters of activities (Aschbacher et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR9); Robnett & Leaper, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR64); Thomas et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR78)). In some instances, adolescents indicated that their friends or siblings might disapprove of their involvement in making activities, as this could leave them with less time to socialize.

#### 5.2.3 Control beliefs

In accordance with the TPB, control beliefs were categorized into person- and environment-related control beliefs. The first aspect of person-related control beliefs pertains to self-efficacy, a pivotal variable in educational (Marsh et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR44)) and making-related research (Andrews et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR5); Schlegel et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR67)). The second aspect of person-related control beliefs pertains to grit. Related statements indicated that adolescents would most likely discontinue their making activities. However, our data do not clarify whether adolescents expected to give up specifically in making projects or in general, possibly due to lower levels of grit (F. T. C. Schmidt et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR68)) or conscientiousness (Credé et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR18)). Regardless of the name that might be given to the construct, some tendency of not giving up early might be helpful for undertaking more complex making projects.

Environment-related beliefs also emerged. Most importantly, the students stated that they did not know any place for making activities before they came to the Fab Lab. Although they were introduced to such a space, some students still preferred their making activities to be done at school, since this was a familiar place for them. Another environment-related control belief that came up referred to a lack of time for making activities. This result corresponds to findings from research on recreational painting (Schüller & Kröner, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR71)).

### 5.3 Avenues for a practical application of the category system

Eliciting and categorizing salient beliefs are important for two reasons. First, the category system may serve as a research tool that provides a point of departure for the creation of questionnaire scales. Second, the category system may be used as a basis to design interventions that fit adolescents’ beliefs and worldviews. In the following paragraphs, we will focus on avenues for these practical applications of our category system and provide examples of how the category system may inform intervention design.

#### 5.3.1 The category system as a research tool

To demonstrate how the category system from the present study might be used to develop items for making-related belief scales, we provide some examples from the preregistration of an upcoming questionnaire study (Uhing & Kröner, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR86)). For this preregistration, existing belief scales from other science-related domains have been adapted to the making domain (please see Table [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#Tab3) for an overview). As this has not yet been subjected to empirical testing, alterations may be deemed necessary. Furthermore, it is acknowledged that certain single scales or items may not be applicable in the context of quantitative questionnaires. Consequently, researchers should be cautious and refrain from using the scales without first consulting the preregistered questionnaire study (Uhing & Kröner, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR86)). Alternatively, they may conduct their own studies.

#### 5.3.2 The category system as a basis for intervention implications

In addition to guiding the development of questionnaires, the category system provides a framework for tailoring making interventions to adolescents’ preferences. As such interventions may also be informed by the existent literature, our suggestions in the following section will integrate both sources, the category system and the literature.

Among behavioral beliefs, intrinsic value is probably the most important predictor for engaging in making activities (Birnbaum et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR11)). Providing individuals with autonomy has been shown to increase intrinsic value (Niemiec & Ryan, [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR53); Reeve & Jang, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR63)). Autonomy-supportive strategies for fostering intrinsic motivation can be derived from Reeve and Jang ([2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR63)) and applied to making activities. These strategies include, among others, providing free time to work in one’s own way or providing participants with rationales. Additionally, it is critical to avoid monopolizing machines or materials; instead, participants should be encouraged and supported (e.g., by mentors) to initiate their own projects, such as 3D printing designs.

Individuals may also engage in making activities if they perceive such activities as relevant to their (or others’) lives. This can be concluded from the study of Peppler et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR59)), who asked museum visitors to design inventions to assist grandparents in their daily life, or of Dökme and Hancıoğlu ([2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR19)), who had students solve a practical challenge using robot programming. Moreover, research in the field of STEM education has demonstrated the efficacy of utility-value interventions in enhancing students’ persistence in STEM disciplines (Asher et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR10)). For example, students may be instructed to reflect on the personal usefulness (i.e., utility value) of course material by discussing its relevance to their own lives or the lives of significant others. Additionally, they can be provided with concrete examples illustrating the real-world relevance of a specific task (Albrecht & Karabenick, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR4)). It also fits into the behavior change methods displayed in the meta-analysis of Steinmetz et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR76)), in which the provision of information about the advantages of a particular behavior has been found to be effective.

In contrast to the aforementioned behavioral beliefs, which have been demonstrated to be the strongest predictor of engaging in a behavior, normative beliefs have typically been found to have a weak prediction (Armitage & Conner, [2001](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR8); Birnbaum et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR11)). Nevertheless, it may be important to take care of these beliefs as well, especially when working with younger adolescents or children. Normative beliefs may evolve when significant others act as role models, when making becomes a family activity, or when it is shared within peer groups. In this manner, the individual becomes aware that other people engage in activities and that they are likely to be pleased if the individual also engages in said activities. While the studies of Norouzi et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR54)) and Zimmerman et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR83)) did not explicitly aim to foster normative beliefs explicitly, the inclusion of family and peers, as demonstrated in these two studies, may serve as a preliminary step in this direction. A recently published study by Ahmed et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR1)) explicitly sought to cultivate normative beliefs to enhance STEM students’ teamwork. The study proposed that students’ regular meetings with instructors could help establish social norms that encourage consistent team participation. Although recreational activities are typically voluntary and, in some cases, unstructured, the regular organization of social gatherings with other adolescents may contribute to the development of social norms.

Regarding person-related control beliefs, it may be beneficial, especially for beginners, to facilitate activities that promise straightforward success. However, adolescents also need to learn that failure may be seen as an integral part of the learning process inherent in making activities (Flores, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR24); Searle et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR72)). Research indicates that adolescents benefit from instructional support in open-ended making activities (Searle et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR72)), which may help prevent them from giving up easily.

Although changing the environment, for example, by providing access to making activities at school, may not always be feasible, interventions should still aim to minimize environmental barriers and foster adolescents’ confidence in their own abilities. As the participants in our study expressed a preference for engaging in making activities at familiar places, such as school, the notion of familiarity should be considered. Hynes and Hynes’ ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR34)) study pointed to a “less complex looking space” (p. 880) and rooms that look similar to the spaces that students know from places they are familiar with, such as youth centers. These points should be considered when developing a nonformal makerspace, including the involvement of adolescents or the target group in the process. In order to address other environment-related impediments, the use of implementation intentions (Gollwitzer, [1999](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR29)) involving *if-then* sentences in the formulation of a behavioral plan has been demonstrated to be effective in overcoming obstacles (Steinmetz et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR76)). This is a successful strategy, especially if participants are nudged to plan the next time they would like to join open making activities before they leave.

The suggestions outlined above are supported by relevant literature in STEM and making, but further empirical evidence is needed to ascertain the efficacy of these proposed interventions. Moreover, the moderating effects of age, gender, and other participant demographics need to be scrutinized. It is also promising to work with the target group in the intervention development as part of a participatory design process, as demonstrated in Smolarczyk et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR75)). An intervention may be theoretically sound and administered in a consistent and unambiguous manner; however, this is of little consequence if the target group does not accept it. Finally, while our study focuses on out-of-school making activities, the category system may inform interventions based on informal processes in more formal settings. This aligns with the recommendation made by Colley et al. ([2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR17)) to investigate the effects of formality and informality in different learning situations.

Our category system is situated within recreational making activities; however, future intervention studies may draw inspiration from adjacent fields, such as the *integrated interest development for computing education framework* (Michaelis & Weintrop, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR48)). It emphasizes three dimensions: knowledge, value, and belonging. While the dimension value, encompassing intrinsic and utility value, bears conceptual similarity to behavioral beliefs as defined in the TPB, the dimensions of *knowledge* and *belonging* are more appropriately understood as antecedents or contextual factors that influence the formation of TPB beliefs (Ajzen, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR3)). What distinguishes our belief taxonomy is its comprehensive coverage of behavioral, normative, and control beliefs. This level of differentiation is often lacking in existing frameworks, giving less attention to the normative and control dimensions that also shape behavioral intentions in informal STEM contexts.

### 5.4 Differences in beliefs regarding gender and SES

In addition to developing a category system that captured adolescents’ salient making-related beliefs, we ensured it reflected the perspectives of both girls and adolescents from low-SES backgrounds. In this context, it is reassuring that we generally observed high concordance in the subcategories and aspects into which the making-related beliefs of boys and girls and adolescents from high versus low formal education family backgrounds might be coded.[Footnote 7](#Fn7) Thus, the present study did not identify quantitative evidence of disparities in the salience of various making-related beliefs based on gender or SES. However, it is important to note that this does not preclude differences in the strength of beliefs related to various categories according to the TPB (Ajzen, [1991](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR2)). While our study was not designed to identify them, these may well emerge in quantitative studies with sufficiently large sample size conducted with questionnaires based on the categories developed in this study. For example, in their meta-analysis regarding gender effects at the intersection of social class and cultural context for school-related STEM beliefs, Parker et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR57)) found mostly small effect sizes for gender differences in beliefs. These are moderated, among others, by socioeconomic status. Especially in families with high SES, the differences in math-related beliefs depending on gender were large. Thus, future quantitative research should examine not only the main effects of SES and gender on making-related beliefs but also their respective interaction effects.

Moreover, prior research offers clues about possible group-specific tendencies. Studies indicate that adolescents, particularly girls, prefer to create things for others (Peppler et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR59)) and that both girls and low-SES adolescents respond particularly well to utility-value interventions (Asher et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR10)). Beliefs related to utility value were mentioned by the participants in our study, too. This included (1) beliefs based on the perceived general usefulness of the making activities, which included statements referring to pursuing making activities for their usefulness in everyday life or to help others and (2) beliefs based on the usefulness for existing hobbies, which included statements suggesting that the motivation for making activities stems from existing hobbies and that making is thus considered interesting or useful for the adolescents. In the light of utility-value interventions this implies that these interventions need to be carefully designed: While our category system does not provide evidence whether it is better to target general utility or utility related to hobbies, one needs to keep in mind that especially low-SES adolescents may not have the means for all kinds of hobbies.

### 5.5 Limitations and avenues for further research

For the present study, data were collected from students in grades 6, 8 and 9 at a German high school (gymnasium). Such non-US studies on beliefs are important to inform questionnaire development in various countries, as the majority of research in this field has so far been conducted in the US (Smolarczyk & Kröner, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR84)). We selected the school due to its diverse student body, which spans a range of socioeconomic backgrounds, making it unlikely that key aspects of making-related beliefs were overlooked. Even if this were the case, beliefs that are very marginal are usually not the best starting points for interventions, as these beliefs, by definition, will be held only by a small fraction of the participants. Moreover, as recommended by Francis et al. ([2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR25)), we included more than 25 adolescents in our interview study and relied on a sampling plan to ensure that different perspectives arising from differences in gender and SES were represented. We are confident that the identified categories are generalizable to studies involving adolescents from diverse school types and cultural contexts. This assumption is strongly supported by the extensive body of research on the TPB across a wide range of domains and countries (Naskar & Lindahl, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR51)): Empirical applications of the TPB in the educational domain range from recreational reading, writing, and painting (Birnbaum et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR11); Schüller et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR70); Schüller & Kröner, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR71)), to the domain of sports (Downs & Hausenblas, [2005](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR20)). Moreover, there are plenty of TPB studies from the STEM domain, which, however up to now tended to focus on determinants of career and study choices rather than on leisure activities (Moore & Burrus, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR49); see Lipnevich et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR40) for a rare study on mathematics outcomes). Transferring the general structure of the TPB to different domains is the procedure that has been used in most previous studies. Limitations in generalizability of the present study, however, may arise regarding the age of the participants. It is possible that in conceptual replication studies with adolescents aged 16 to 18 or children below 10, somewhat different subcategories and aspects may emerge. For example, elementary school students below 10 may not yet see a utility for future life, given that this may be beyond their perspectives. Therefore, it is essential to conduct further quantitative and qualitative studies with larger samples from various schools with different academic tracks and from multiple countries. These studies may also focus on identifying frequently co-occurring beliefs, thereby informing the development of more customized interventions.

As a second limitation, three of the four interviewers in the present study were female. As interviewers may affect the perceptions of the interview, with the respondents expressing more positive ratings for interviews conducted by women (Schmiedeberg & Schröder, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR69)), it is recommended that future studies ensure that the number of male and female interviewers is equal.

The third limitation of this study is the participants’ limited prior exposure to making activities. Because of this, we began the survey with a guided tour of the Fab Lab. However, this may also be considered an advantage, as integrating adolescents unfamiliar with making into our study may offer a unique perspective on the topic.

Fourth, we split our sample into low and high SES groups using parental education levels and parental job descriptions. In future studies, information on parental income might be included, but this information is difficult to obtain (Sirin, [2005](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR74)). More SES levels could also be used, although this would require larger samples. Additionally, as adolescents with migration backgrounds participated in our study, their beliefs informed the category system. However, we did not use migration background as an explicit sampling dimension. Future studies should therefore compare the beliefs of adolescents with and without migration backgrounds.

Fifth, we did not employ member checking and thus did not disseminate the results to the participants or interviewed them. Including such a procedure might enhance credibility in future studies (McKim, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR47)). Open-ended questions on beliefs might also be added to subsequent questionnaire studies to ensure the comprehensiveness of the category system.

Sixth, while embedded in a mixed-methods research strategy, the present study is preliminary and qualitative in nature. The category system developed in this study can inform future studies on the development of questionnaires that may be administered in quantitative longitudinal or intervention studies (which may, in turn, be accompanied by qualitative in-depth interviews). While we explored quantitative associations between the elicited categories and gender and SES via chi-square tests of independence in the supplementary material, we decided to refrain from such analyses in the present study due to issues of statistical power that would result from our relatively small sample. In studies with larger samples, the potential of mixed methods can be fully harnessed: Quantitative questionnaires may identify group differences, such as gender-based variations in beliefs, while subsequent qualitative interviews can explore the underlying reasons for these differences.

6 Conclusion
------------

The fine-grained category system emerging from this study underscores the significance of personal and environmental beliefs that motivate adolescents to engage in recreational making activities. While our findings differ in some respects from prior research on leisure activities (Kröner, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13969-3#ref-CR37)), they remain broadly consistent with it. The resulting category system offers a foundation for tailoring recreational making activities interventions to diverse adolescent participants and for evaluating their outcomes through qualitative or quantitative measures. Thus, our study represents an important step toward evidence-based design of making interventions for adolescents. As a next step, questionnaire scales might be developed based on our category system. They could be applied in large-scale and longitudinal studies of making activities and making-related beliefs and in intervention studies aimed at fostering making activities in adolescents. Such studies may provide valuable additional information on how the activities of diverse groups of adolescents may be fostered. Integrating mixed methods, such as combining large-scale surveys with in-depth interviews based on our category system, could expand the quantitative insights from large-scale studies and provide a deeper understanding of adolescents’ making processes.
