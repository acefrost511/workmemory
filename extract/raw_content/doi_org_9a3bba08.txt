Abstract
--------

Increasingly ubiquitous access to Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) presents many challenges, but also opportunities. The fundamental capacity of GenAI to mimic and augment human cognitive functioning, sets it aside from the myriad of previous technological ‘cognitive tool’ innovations that have been promoted as supporting human thinking, problem solving and knowledge construction. Indeed, GenAI has the potential to play a far more substantive and interactive role in knowledge building, founded on real-time dialogic discourse between humans and GenAI working in symbiotic knowledge building partnerships. This article draws on Scardamalia and Bereiter’s early work on human knowledge building communities and Krathwohl’s revision of Bloom’s Cognitive Domain, reconceptualising these to theorise how humans and GenAI might partner in processes of collaborative, joint knowledge construction. It presents a unique model identifying three flexible ‘Zones’, representing different but overlapping components of knowledge building, aligned with Bloom’s cognitive dimensions. It identifies a possible ‘division of labour’ within and across Zones, but argues the primacy of innately human capabilities operating in the Judgement Zone, as crucial to reasoned decision making and accurate knowledge building. The model and its discussion provide new insights into how human-GenAI knowledge building partnerships might be established and sustained.

Introduction
------------

Artificial Intelligence, or AI—computer-based systems that mimic human cognitive functions, are playing an increasingly significant role in many aspects of our lives. Much of this can be attributed to the development of Large Language Models (LLMs), a particular type of AI that is predominantly text-based, using programmed parameters to “learn the nuances and patterns of human languages” (Byrd, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR7), p. 135). LLM AI engines are trained on vast corpuses of data, which are accessed through extensive neural-type architectures that now approach the number of neural connections in the human brain (Rudolph et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR31)). These facilitate ‘learning’ that is shaped both by the data to which LLMs have access (which is usually internet-based), and also through *prompt engineering* – that is, the queries and conditions through which humans interact with LLMs. Setting it apart from previous technological advancements, AI is a generative tool that can simulate aspects of human intelligence, such as the capacity to learn, problem solve, and perform linguistic interactions (Hmoud & Shaqour, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR14)). It achieves this through a “conversational interface” (Malik et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR23), p. 9) that removes the need for complex programming knowledge, making GenAI more accessible to the general population. GenAI can perform complex cognitive tasks that are traditionally attributed to human thinking, prompting reconsideration of how knowledge building occurs in human-technology partnerships.

We suggest the model presented here responds in part to growing calls for improved understanding of what it means to be ‘AI literate’, moving beyond the current emphasis on technical knowledge and skills to use AI in specific work or educational tasks (e.g., Ng et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR24); Pinski & Benlian, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR29)), towards deeper understanding of how AI and humans might collaborate in purposeful and productive knowledge building tasks. According to Taylor and Krishna ([2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR40)), “AI, when actively integrated into team processes in specific ways can help support more innovative collective outcomes in knowledge work, while also increasing productivity” (p. 5). However, they also highlight shortcomings in current research that tends to focus on the function of AI systems in isolation from human operators, rather than exploring how they might collaborate in creative processes of problem solving and joint knowledge construction. They point to the need for more work into what they term *human-AI hybrid systems*, that conceptualise AI and human synergies and capabilities “to promote societal value creation” (p. 3). They claim that such models must prioritise how AI can complement human cognition rather than replace it, and identify how it can be used in ways that extend human creativity and reasoned judgement, in addition to improving work productivity and performance.

Given the relatively recent advent of GenAI into mainstream work and education, the dearth of empirical research exploring *human-AI hybrid systems* and the almost complete absence of conceptual models to guide such studies, the model introduced here represents theorisation from the small number of available articles and commentaries speculating what form such partnerships might take. As a starting point, the model draws on Scardamalia and Bereiter’s ([2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR35)) early research, which investigated the nature of interactions occurring between human members of technology-mediated knowledge building communities. It extends this by theorising the contribution non-human agents such as GenAI might make to such communities, based on emerging literature and commentary in this area.

Literature review
-----------------

### Cognitive tools and GenAI

Back in 1995, David Jonassen, in the *Journal of Computing in Higher Education,* highlighted the emergence of a type of software into mainstream education that he termed *Cognitive Tools.* He described these as:

> Applications such as databases, spreadsheets, semantic networks, expert systems, multimedia/hypermedia construction [that] can function as computer-based cognitive tools that function as intellectual partners with learners to expand and even amplify their thinking, thereby changing the role of learners to knowledge constructors rather than information reproducers. (Jonassen, [1995](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR17), p. 40).

Jonassen’s conceptualisation of what were essentially productivity tools used in business as educationally valuable *cognitive tools,* was based on constructivist learning theories. Influential were Vygotskian notions suggesting “cognitive tools are omnipresent and an inevitable means by which thinking is shaped by culture [and where] the tools empower but simultaneously impose their own constraints on the mental functioning of the learner” (Pakdaman-Savoji et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR27), p. 2). Central to Jonassen’s arguments was the potential of these technologies to operate in cognitive partnerships with learners, enabling qualitatively superior outcomes through “the joint system of learner plus technology” (p. 44). He describes this as learning *with* technology, where tasks are assigned according to the type of processing learners and technology are best at. In describing the contribution of technology cognitive tools to these partnerships, Salomon et al. ([1991](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR32)) identified their potential to lighten the cognitive load of users, through assuming tasks such as computation and data analysis and organisational functions. Pakdaman-Savoji et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR27)) consider this a form of *distributed cognition,* where “key aspects of the task are outsourced to the tool” (p. 10) according to user judgements made about factors such as improved efficiency or productivity, capacity to assist with structuring or organising data, or the extent to which the tool can lessen the user’s cognitive load.

Jonassen ([1995](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR17)) suggested *expert systems* (an early form of artificial intelligence) could also act as powerful cognitive tools to augment human problem solving, through accessing previously stored expert knowledge, “processing the information, arriv(ing) at a decision, and report(ing) the decision to the user” (p. 56)—in much the same way as contemporary AI systems. He further argued the process of building *expert systems* demands deep conceptual and content domain knowledge and an array of advanced thinking skills such as analytical and metacognitive reasoning. He commented that both using and building *expert systems* as and for knowledge representation/s, “engage learners in reflective, critical thinking about the ideas they are studying” (p. 66), enhancing knowledge building and equipping learners with transferable metacognitive skills. However, according to Partridge ([1987](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR28)), early *expert systems* were inherently unreliable and required specialised expertise to operate, making them unsuitable for most day-to-day applications.

While Jonassen and colleagues’ work was undertaken almost 30 years ago, discussions about what role the latest *expert system –* large language model (LLM) Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) can play in learning and knowledge building, are of significant interest. Reflecting on Jonassen and others’ early work, it is easy to see parallels between their conceptualisation of technology cognitive tools, and current discourse about the learning potentials of natural language GenAI systems such as ChatGPT (e.g., Cress & Kimmerle, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR10); Volante et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR42)). Similar to the earlier researchers’ theorisation of computer–human ‘intellectual partnerships’ working to solve knowledge problems, contemporary AI researchers also highlight the “contextually bound relationship between the person and the technology” (Bearman & Ajjawi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR6), p. 4)—rather than the technology itself, as fundamental to effective and appropriate use of GenAI. By this they refer to the importance of learners needing to go “through effortful metacognitive processes of configuring the human-technology partnership” (Bearman & Ajjawi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR6), p. 3) to understand how GenAI can affect their activities, and how it might best be incorporated into their learning. However, although conceptual similarities exist, the nature, functions and capabilities of the technology tools serving as ‘cognitive partners’, has changed considerably. The highly interactive and conversational nature of the user interfaces of LLM platforms such as ChatGPT, and their capacity to draw on massive volumes of online data, position them as fundamentally different and far more powerful than the early ‘productivity’ applications such as databases, spreadsheets, and semantic mapping and multimedia authoring tools, described by Jonassen and colleagues. Their earlier conceptualisation viewed technology tools as an adjunct to human cognition—that is, as aids “used by learners to represent *their* knowledge” (Jonassen, [1995](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR17), p. 66). Working in this way, Jonassen argued these tools could reduce the cognitive load on learners, freeing up resources for them to engage more critically and reflectively on the concepts they were studying, leading to deeper and more robust understandings. In short, these early tools were an aid to human cognition, not a substitute for it.

Although GenAI can certainly aid learners in similar ways to earlier productivity and expert system cognitive tools, their more powerful, interactive and formative nature enables them to play a far more substantive role in knowledge building than their earlier counterparts. As Chen et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR8)) put it, “advances in GenAI are forcing us to negotiate boundaries between human and machine intelligences, especially in the creative domain… [and consider] a renewed vision of education for knowledge creation, drawing on knowledge building’s emphasis on engaging students in continual improvement of ideas” (Chen, et al., p. 10). GenAI’s natural language interface, capacity to draw on immense volumes of training data, and engage far more advanced and accurate inference engines, offers enhanced potential to function in *cognitive partnerships* with learners in a wide range of creative problem solving and knowledge building work.

In this respect, Ouyang and Jiao ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR26)) identify three possible paradigms that might define human-GenAI cognitive partnerships. The first they describe as *AI- directed, learner-as-recipient*, which they associate with behaviourist learning theories where “AI represents the domain knowledge and directs the learning processes, while the learner acts as recipient of the AI service” (Ouyang & Jiao, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR26), p. 2). They align this paradigm with early technology-supported *programmed instruction*, where GenAI introduces learners to concepts in a logical and sequential manner and provides immediate feedback, often supplemented by positive reinforcement. In this paradigm learners are passive recipients of GenAI’s service, “react(ing) to pre-specified sequences of knowledge, follow(ing) learning procedures and pathways, and execut(ing) learning activities set by AI to achieve predefined goals” (ibid, p. 2). The second paradigm they identify as *AI-supported, learner-as-collaborator*. This approach is grounded in socio-cultural learning theories such as constructivism, which emphasise the importance of socially situated interaction with people, tools and information, for knowledge building. Ouyang and Jiao suggest this approach represents a more student-focused use of GenAI, where systems iteratively and adaptively monitor students’ inputs and provide formative feedback “in order to achieve better or more efficient learning” (Ouyang & Jiao, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR26), p. 3). They view interaction in this paradigm as a two-way process, where learners can communicate with the AI system to better understand and improve its decision-making processes, and refine prompts and queries to enhance its outputs.

Ouyang and Jiao’s ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR26)) third paradigm is described as *AI-empowered, learner-as-leader,* and draws upon complexity learning theory that recognises education as a complex, dynamic and adaptive system involving learners, instructors, information and technology—with the goal in this case being “[fully] AI-augmented human intelligence, capability and potential” (p. 4). They argue this to be the most advanced and personalised use of GenAI for learning, where agency is vested in learners and continuous data is made available to them to help refine and enhance their thinking and learning. Furthermore, in this paradigm, Ouyang and Jiao ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR26)) suggest that instructors can benefit from access to “understandable, interpretable and personalised supports” (p. 4) that can assist them in better tailoring learning design and content to meet learners’ specific needs, before and during learning. Central to the efficacy of this synergistic process, however, is the free and multidirectional flow of information between instructors, learners and technology systems, which Ouyang and Jiao indicate is the foundation of effective human–machine cognition.

### Technology in knowledge building

Significant work spanning many years has been undertaken researching the role of technology in knowledge building (e.g., Kirschner & Erkens, [2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR18); Scardamalia & Bereiter, [1994](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR34); Tan et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR38)). Possibly most notable is Scardamalia and Bereiter’s exploration of knowledge building as a technology-supported, community endeavour. According to Scardamalia and Bereiter, knowledge building is a key component of learning. It refers to the creation of epistemic or conceptual artifacts, which requires intentional, social, and iterative processes of *idea improvement* (Scardamalia & Bereiter, [2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR35)). Idea improvement traditionally occurs through discourse – that is, as an “outcome of interactions within or across activity systems” (Tan & Tan, [2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR39), p. 15). Scardamalia and Bereiter argue the importance of discourse generated in technology-supported *Knowledge Building* communities to advancing knowledge, improving ideas, collaboratively solving problems, using and contributing to the base of authoritative information, and creating deeper knowledge *in* (in contrast to knowledge *about*) subject matter (Scardamalia & Bereiter, [2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR35)). Their early research using the collaborative online environment *CSILE* (Computer Supported Intentional Learning Environment) and its more recent iteration, *Knowledge Forum*, suggests that “knowledge is generated in knowledge-creating communities through sustained collective inquiry, theory-building, and evolution of thought” (Tan et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR38), p. 2247), and is supported by close alignment between technology use and knowledge-building goals. In this respect, they signal the value of technology for enhancing collaboration, facilitating visualisation and analytics enabling continuous knowledge improvement, supporting collaborative inquiry, and democratising processes of knowledge building by engaging students from all backgrounds and abilities.

According to Scardamalia and Bereiter ([2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR33)), the advent of smart technologies has stimulated a rethink of the nature of knowledge building partnerships, that recognises the possible role non-human agents have to play in knowledge building. They point to technology’s capacity not only “to support people interacting productively with other people, but also interacting productively with ideas and feedback systems promoting engagement between people and ideas” (Scardamalia & Bereiter, [2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR33), p. 1). They further comment that critical to the effective functioning of technology in knowledge building is the development of self-organising systems, where humans and machines are able to contribute specific capabilities to solve knowledge problems. Central to this they suggest is the role of feedback, which needs to be iterative and adaptive, and “able to evolve to deal with emerging problems and opportunities” (Scardamalia & Bereiter, [2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR33), p. 9). While at the time of Scardamalia and Bereiter’s publication in 2014, mainstream smart technologies had not evolved sufficiently to facilitate partnerships of this nature, almost prophetically, they predicted “that by 2025, [computers] will have automated a number of human activities, including mental ones, that they can do better or at least more economically than we can” (p. 9). However, they comment that crucial to unlocking the inherent potential of human–machine knowledge building partnerships is the establishment of *idea-centred discourse*, where humans, machines, and ideas interact in “self-organised [systems] around the production of understanding” (Scardamalia & Bereiter, [2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR33), p. 11). The model detailed in this article illustrates how such systems comprising humans and GenAI agents might function in knowledge building partnerships.

### AI and knowledge building

Scardamalia and Bereiter ([2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR35)) argue that knowledge building takes place within communities of learners, and is facilitated by processes and technologies whereby collective ideas and discourse about a topic, leads to new or refined knowledge for community members. With the advent of GenAI, potential exists for the AI platform to become an active member of such a community, contributing to idea generation and refinement, and ultimately knowledge building, in conjunction with other community members. Reflecting on Scardamalia and Bereiter’s concept of *idea-centred discourse* as critical to human–machine partnerships, recent research on the use of GenAI in work and learning also highlights the relevance of productive discourse for optimising the value from GenAI-augmented knowledge building. In fact, some researchers claim that “employers are hiring for communication-with-computers skills… [that is] the ability to ‘converse’ with a computer and make use of its responses” (Atchley et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR4), p. 5). Furthermore, Atchley et al. comment that a crucial capability for future workers will be the capacity to work in teams – not only with human teammates, but also alongside AI systems, and possess understandings about the potentials but also the limitations and risks of such systems. They claim that the role of education is to help prepare students to work effectively in these teams, and further their understanding of appropriate functions and tasks humans and GenAI can perform individually and collaboratively, in knowledge building. To this end, Atchley et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR4)) point to the continuing relevance of Krathwohl’s ([2002](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR20)) revision of Bloom’s Taxonomy (cognitive domain) as “the cornerstone of designing and assessing learning [with GenAI]” (p. 5), and its usefulness for informing when and for what purpose/s GenAI might ‘add value’ to human–machine knowledge building partnerships, and the implications and risks from doing so.

Most fundamentally, Atchley et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR4)) argue GenAI can assist by “sharing the cognitive load by supporting lower-level skills, so students can focus on higher-level skills” (p. 7). They term this *cognitive offloading* which can reduce learners’ information processing demands, ‘freeing up’ cognitive resources for more complex higher order metacognitive thinking and problem solving. Generally, Atchley et al. suggests GenAI *cognitive offloading* could occur most frequently in tasks requiring basic access to, and recall and processing of, information – that is, tasks positioned at the lower levels of the revised Bloom’s Taxonomy. In this capacity, GenAI could assist with computational tasks such as performing calculations, sourcing specific information, creating summaries, defining terms, generating timelines, carrying out data analysis, translating, and so on, freeing humans to concentrate on the more complex cognitive processes needed to productively use the information in knowledge building. Dede et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR11)) refer to these capabilities as AI *reckoning skills,* which include “calculative prediction and formulaic decision making, at which computers and AI excel” (p. 2).

However, while *cognitive offloading* of routine or menial information or computational tasks to GenAI can assist with some of the ‘heavy lifting’ associated with knowledge building, Atchley et al. also offer caution about offloading cognitive tasks to technologies such as GenAI. They suggest that doing this may reduce human information sourcing and evaluation skills, and lead to “overestimating the benefit of using AI systems, [by leading us] to believe we have learned more than we have actually learned” (Atchley et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR4), p. 6). Furthermore, they comment that use of GenAI can be motivated by considerations beyond cognitive ones, such as belief that it will result in better grades or scores in assessments, or reduce the time and effort it takes to complete academic or other tasks. Atchley et al. conclude that ultimately it is an individual decision about how GenAI is used, and that this is significantly influenced by personal values and beliefs. However, they point to the critical importance of users’ awareness of personal metacognitive processes to the effective use of GenAI, that will raise their “awareness to make better decisions about how to use the technology appropriately” (Atchley et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR4), p. 6). Curriculum, pedagogy and assessment need to evolve to models requiring greater levels of metacognition, and learners need to be taught how to work in partnerships or teams with “AI-as-collaborator” (ibid, p. 8).

### AI-as-collaborator in knowledge building partnerships

A substantial volume of literature exists highlighting the urgent importance of developing models for productive and effective human/GenAI partnerships for knowledge building (Atchley et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR4); Jarrahi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR15); Ouyang & Jiao, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR26)). Jarrahi et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR15)) describe such models as needing to be *symbiotic,* where “roles assigned to AI will most likely augment humans rather than replace them, thereby achieving *collaborative intelligence*, in which AI and humans enhance each other’s complementary strengths” (p. 91). Jarrahi et al.’s ([2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR15)) *symbiosis* model identifies distinct roles GenAI and humans can perform in knowledge building, differentiated by what they identify as *specialised* (GenAI) and *general* (human) intelligence. In symbiotic partnerships, Jarrahi et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR15)) suggest GenAI excels in aspects of knowledge building which require “specialised intelligence” (p. 92) within the domain of a learning task. These include functions such as managing knowledge content, codifying information, and optimising processes involved in accessing and synthesising information, connecting people with each other and the information needed to build knowledge, and providing alternative insights on data interpretation. However, they comment that humans must remain central to knowledge production and management, and that human *general intelligence* is quite distinct from artificial *specialised intelligence,* and is the foundation of strategic level thinking and decision-making. General intelligence operationalises human-centred capabilities such as foresight and experience, social-emotional intelligence, reflection, curiosity, creativity and imagination in decision-making, that cannot currently be replicated by artificial agents (Jarrahi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR15)). These are important aspects of reasoned judgement, and are integral to “deciding the best course of action by holistically weighing the social and organisational ramifications of various options” (Jarrahi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR15), p. 92).

Dede et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR11)) adopt a similar view, describing the *reckoning* capabilities of GenAI in much the same way as Jarrahi et al.’s *specialised intelligence,* and highlighting the vital role human judgement plays in GenAI-augmented decision-making that is “unbiased, grounded in ethical commitment, and appropriate to the situation in which it is deployed” (Dede et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR11), p. 2). They describe *reckoning* as “calculative prediction and formulaic decision-making” (p. 2), which GenAI can achieve at speeds, scale, and levels of accuracy unattainable by humans. In what Dede et al. identify as *complementary partnerships,* humans apply reasoned judgement to their use (or not) of the results of GenAI reckoning, but also call on social-emotional, attitudinal, and communication elements, as well as knowledge of contextual and cultural factors, to guide courses of action. Further, Dede et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR11)) comment that GenAI lacks the social sensitivity and creativity of humans, it cannot ‘read’ social cues often needed for accurate contextual interpretation, and do not perform effectively “when processing information from many sources representing distinct customs” (p. 7). Currently, GenAI also lacks *embodied experiential knowledge* that drives human intentional action, *collective cultural knowledge* that allows humans to apply more holistic understandings to knowledge building through interpretation of context, attitude, tone of voice and so on, and *personal performative knowledge* where decision-making is guided by human values and consciousness (Dede et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR11)). Dede et al. suggest that with the advent of GenAI, the teaching of high-level judgement skills should be a priority for education and the workplace, to develop an individual’s *intellectual character* which includes “attention to curiosity, open mindedness and scepticism” (p. 11).

Furthermore, there are calls for the development of GenAI-human knowledge building partnership models that reflect a distribution of agency between humans and machines working both individually and in teams, supported by school and workplace educational programs to improve levels of *AI literacy* and enhance higher order thinking and reflective processes (Chen et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR8)). What follows presents a possible approach to conceptualising partnerships that distribute GenAI-human agency in different ways in processes of knowledge building. It draws on Scardamalia and Bereiter’s ([2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR35)) collaborative Knowledge Building theory, aligning GenAI, human, and joint capabilities, with levels of Krathwohl’s ([2002](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR20)) revision of Bloom’s Taxonomy (cognitive domain), to conceptualise how GenAI-human knowledge building partnerships might function. Historically, knowledge building theory has proven to hold high explanatory value for understanding processes of human collaborative knowledge construction, particularly in online learning communities. Its focus on the dialogic discourse occurring between members of these communities in processes of joint knowledge building – and the role of technologies in facilitating this, provides a valuable theoretical lens through which we might better understand such processes in machine-human partnerships. Additionally, Krathwohl’s revision of Bloom’s Cognitive Domain provides a useful and well-known framework for describing the different complexities and processes involved in knowledge building, and can be readily aligned with combinations of possible human, machine, and human and machine contributions to this.

A conceptual model for AI-human knowledge building partnerships
---------------------------------------------------------------

Figure [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#Fig1) depicts contributions GenAI and humans can make to collaborative knowledge building, both individually, and working in dialogic partnerships. While the popular triangle depiction of Bloom’s taxonomy (cognitive domain) is used in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#Fig1), it is not intended that this implies a cumulative hierarchy, or that linearity exists in AI-human knowledge building partnerships. Within any partnership, human-AI activities will transition across and between zones, rather than being defined within a single zone or zones. The gradient shading in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#Fig1) represents varying possible ‘blends’ of AI-human contribution to knowledge building, which may occur concurrently in partnerships. Moreover, the interaction between Applying and Analysing in the *Collaboration Zone* through processes labelled *dialogic discourse,* reflects the iterative, non-hierarchical relationship existing between these processes in knowledge-building partnerships. Indeed, an essential component of knowledge building is *idea improvement –* the foundation of which is the interrogation and critique of ideas in iterative processes of development and refinement (Scardamalia & Bereiter, [2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR35)). In this zone, humans and AI can collaborate in *idea improvement* through the repeated application, analysis and ‘testing’ of emerging ideas, leading to their refinement and decisions about their possible use in knowledge-building. However, like human knowledge-building communities, in AI-human partnerships, an important capacity is determining the point in the idea improvement process where the refined ideas are of optimal quality and value for use in knowledge-building. This requires continuous human evaluation of outputs from AI-human dialogic discourse, and the application of human intelligence and values in reasoned decision-making about the use of generated ideas and information. This is indicated in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#Fig1) by the circular arrows between the *Collaboration* and *Judgement* zones.

Recognising there is flexibility in the application of cognitive processes between and across zones, acknowledges critiques of Bloom’s original 1956 and Anderson & Krathwohl’s ([2001](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR3)) revised taxonomies. As pointed out by others, in reality, any cognitive activity requires the interaction or ‘blending’ of cognitive processes across taxonomic levels, and that they are not as discrete as the original pyramid representation suggests (e.g., Ormell, [1974](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR25); Seaman, [2011](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR36); Soozandehfar & Adeli, [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR37)). However, as Amer ([2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR2)) comments, the taxonomy does help to distinguish between levels of complexity in cognitive processes, and this may assist teachers in designing learning activities (rather than learning objectives as the original taxonomy intended). Therefore, in interpreting Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#Fig1), it is important to understand the representation is not intended to communicate separate or exclusive ‘roles’ for humans and AI in knowledge-building—nor is it intended to represent a hierarchy, linear process, or demarcation between zones. Instead, as indicated by the circular arrows between zones, each zone and its associated cognitive processes will interact in AI-human knowledge building.

The first zone is titled the *Reckoning Zone,* and builds on Dede et al.’s ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR11)) earlier work. We suggest knowledge building activities in this zone focus on building *knowledge about* the subject – that is, “declarative knowledge you can retrieve [about something]” (Scardamalia & Bereiter, [2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR35), p. 9). In this Zone, GenAI can efficiently execute processes associated with basic knowledge building, such as searching, accessing, and managing data and information. This comprises tasks including (but not limited to) defining terms, summarising and synthesising multi-source information, creating chronologies, generating concise information reviews, and so on. For academic work in particular, GenAI-performed tasks in the *Reckoning Zone* can be particularly beneficial. GenAI can potentially enhance productivity by, for example, lessening the time taken to identify seminal studies in a given field or fields and highlight their significance; providing summaries or clarification of key definitions, concepts or research findings; assisting with data retrieval and management, and executing calculations or other computational tasks. ‘Off-loading’ such tasks to GenAI can potentially save time and lessen the load on researchers and learners, making available more cognitive resources that can be applied to deeper interrogation of the GenAI-generated outputs.

Central to optimising GenAI outputs in the *Reckoning Zone* are the functional and operational capabilities of users. These include well-developed information literacy skills and appropriate dispositions, such as the willingness and ability to critically review and confirm the accuracy and validity of GenAI-generated outputs; and *prompt engineering*, defined as “the process of iterating a generative AI prompt to improve its accuracy and effectiveness” (Coursera, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR9), np). Some suggest *prompt engineering* be considered an essential component of the emerging field of *AI Literacy*, as “higher quality prompt engineering skills predict the quality of LLM outputs, suggesting that prompt engineering is indeed a required skill for the goal-directed use of generative AI tools” (Knoth et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR19), p. 1).

Prompt engineering is also integral to tasks occurring in what we describe as the *Collaboration Zone*, where GenAI-human interaction reflects a symbiotic relationship or collaborative intelligence, as described earlier. We suggest *Collaboration Zone* activities reflect a human-GenAI cognitive partnership, in which “AI and humans enhance each other’s complementary strengths” (Jarrahi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR15), p. 91) through iterative dialogic discourse, in which GenAI outputs are progressively refined and improved, using increasingly specific and accurate prompts. Unlike earlier cognitive tools, the natural language interface and advanced interactive and feedback capabilities of GenAI facilitate real time dialogic discourse or ‘conversations’ with users, during which outputs can be enhanced through continuous improvement and refinement of prompts. The iterative nature of dialogic discourse in the *Collaboration Zone* contributes to knowledge building through Scardamalia and Bereiter’s ([2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR35)) *idea improvement* – that is, “discourse whose aim is progressing the state of knowledge” (p. 12). They comment that “idea improvement is, in principle, endless” (ibid, p. 5) and relies on human judgements about maintaining, changing or ceasing a particular line of inquiry, relative to the extent of progress made towards the knowledge building goal. In the *Collaboration Zone*, examples of this might include humans and machines collaborating to iteratively solve complex data problems or refine mathematical formulae (or create new ones) to simplify or optimise operations by testing them against human-imposed criteria. Equally, in creative work, AI and humans can collaborate synergistically with AI being used initially to generate new visual concepts, patterns, designs or campaigns in response to a human-defined brief or specifications, which can be further adapted and refined by iterative AI-human discourse to optimise alignment with specific needs or contexts.

Although Scardamalia and Bereiter’s knowledge building theory originated several years ago, we argue it holds particular relevance for understanding contemporary GenAI- human knowledge building partnerships. While the original theory was generated from the nature of dialogic discourse occurring between human members of knowledge building communities, given the dialogic capabilities of GenAI, we suggest this conceptualisation should now be expanded to include non-human participants. Potentially, GenAI can act as a *dialogic partner,* working alongside humans in the *Collaboration Zone*, targeting more complex analytical and inferential outputs. These might include, for example, performing and refining advanced statistical calculations, contrasting and comparing data identifying trends and patterns, or predicting outcomes from multiple options or scenarios. However, we propose that critical to the value of human-GenAI dialogic discourse in this zone is human tacit and domain knowledge in the specific area or discipline of inquiry, and how effectively these can be applied to refine and optimise GenAI prompts and resulting outputs.

To this end, in the *Collaboration Zone* we consider Bloom’s Taxonomy’s Analysing, Applying (and testing), and Evaluating will occur iteratively, as GenAI outputs are assessed, evaluated and tested by users and prompts are refined and outputs reevaluated, in successive cycles of *idea improvement*. According to Jin et al. ([2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR16)), GenAI can play a valuable role in knowledge building within professional communities, “acting as an inspirational resource that prompts discussion and reflection, rather than delivering a single truth for all to accept or deny” (p. 2). In their study of AI-human interactions within teacher professional learning communities, Jin et al. concluded that GenAI served as an “active participant, transforming how teachers interact, collaborate and construct knowledge… by offering immediate, diverse inputs and stimulating critical reflection and collaborative knowledge construction” (ibid, p. 3). These characteristics are identified by Scardamalia and Bereiter ([2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR35)) as cornerstones of *idea improvement*, which they argue is the foundation of collaborative knowledge building. However, in such processes robust human knowledge is needed to analyse and evaluate GenAI outputs to determine their relevance and accuracy, and also to iteratively refine the prompts used in its generation. Human knowledge is central to beneficial dialogue occurring in the Collaboration Zone, and this must be sufficient to support ongoing processes of idea improvement, and enable informed judgements about when knowledge is sufficiently developed to be of value.

The third zone we identify as the *Judgement Zone,* in which knowledge building focuses on developing deep *knowledge of* the area or discipline of inquiry. Scardamalia and Bereiter ([2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR35)) describe *knowledge of* as “both procedural knowledge and declarative knowledge that would be drawn on when engaged in an activity… [it is also] implicit or intuitive knowledge that may not manifest directly, but must be inferred” (p. 9). They suggest deeper *knowledge of* is essential to “invention, theorising and the solving of ill-structured problems” (p. 10), drawing on distinctly human attributes such as creativity, critical thinking, wisdom, and experience. Dede et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR11)) highlight the importance of human judgement and reasoned decision making in GenAI-augmented knowledge building, as “machines cannot achieve judgement skills in the creative, socially-sensitive, and personally responsible way humans can” (p. 7). According to Dede et al., machines cannot read social cues or nuances, and currently lack experiential knowledge of culture and social context that are necessary for unbiased and ethically sound and reasoned decision-making.

Similarly, Jarrahi et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR15)) point to the nature of human *general intelligence* as fundamental to strategic thinking and decision making, commenting that this “tends to be more holistic—and builds from the uniquely human prerogatives such as foresight, social and emotional intelligence, self-development, reflection, imagination, and curiosity” (p. 92). They suggest exercising human *general intelligence* is essential to making reasoned judgements, and is something that cannot be replicated by machines. As indicated in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#Fig1), we propose that knowledge-building activities in the *Judgement Zone* prioritise human *general intelligence* to make reasoned judgements about if and how to use outputs generated in the *Collaboration Zone.* As such, *Judgement Zone* decision-making draws on affective and ethical dimensions of thinking, which are uniquely human. In this zone, human reasoned judgement based on sound ethical and moral principles is critical to decision-making about if and how to deploy AI-human outputs in knowledge building, taking into consideration the implications and impacts from doing so on other people, society and environment. Decision-making in the *Judgement Zone* also draws heavily on human wisdom, socio emotional intelligence and creative and critical thinking. Currently these attributes cannot be replicated by machines, but are vitally important to appropriate and productive use of AI-human outputs that take into account purpose, context and audience. Ultimately, when using GenAI outputs for knowledge building, human intelligence must be exercised in making reasoned judgements, and decisions must be grounded in ethical commitment and understanding of culture and context.

### Interpreting the model

The model depicted in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#Fig1) records illustrative tasks we suggest could be undertaken in each overlapping Zone, reflecting different ‘blends’ of GenAI and human contributions. However, we do not see these tasks as definitive, nor do we propose the model be interpreted in a hierarchal or prerequisite manner (i.e., ‘lower order’ functions as inferior or prerequisite to ‘higher order’ functions). The model’s purpose is to conceptualise how GenAI and humans might productively engage in knowledge building through executing separate and collaborative tasks, working towards a common goal. Indeed, as we learn more about how humans and GenAI can partner in knowledge building through empirical trials, and the capabilities of the technology further improve, the array of activities and the distribution across ‘Zones’ will undoubtedly change. However, we suggest the greatest potential for GenAI to enhance human knowledge building exists in the *Collaboration Zone*, where it can act as a cognitive ‘sparring partner’, offering prompts, provocations, alternative perspectives, and refinements which could extend human thinking. The natural language interface of GenAI systems support ‘conversation style’ dialogue with users, where ideas can be improved and refined through iterative dialogic discourse – a process which in the past was confined only to human–human interactions.

As with human–human interactions, crucial to the effectiveness of dialogic discourse in the *Collaboration Zone* are human attributes such as domain and tacit knowledge, critical and creative thinking, social and emotional intelligence and contextual and cultural understandings, that are important for iterative refinement of GenAI prompts (prompt engineering), and for judging the relevance and accuracy of GenAI outputs. This latter point is particularly important, given GenAI’s current tendency towards ‘hallucinations’ (Feldman et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR13)) and its limited (if any) understanding of context – the audience, location, culture, or even purpose in a given situation. These limitations mean its outputs can potentially be harmful (Lee & Soylu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR21)) or include biases towards “western, educated, industrialized, economically affluent, and democratic societies” (Liu, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR22), p. 3); or may be prejudiced towards English-speaking jurisdictions in which current iterations of GenAI are trained. Additionally, AI has no capacity to consider social components of knowledge building where the social and cultural setting to which a person belongs influences how they receive and process new ideas, encounters, and experiences, into beliefs and knowledge. As such, there is potential to perpetuate social and cultural prejudices and inappropriately reinforce hegemonic and homogenous perspectives towards different groups of people (Liu, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR22)). This can constrain knowledge building in narrow, limited, inaccurate or inappropriate ways. In this regard, we argue the centrality of *human intelligence* to accurate and ethical human-AI knowledge building, and the essential role human reasoning skills play in this process. Dede et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR11)) comment that many employers classify these as ‘soft skills’, but claim that human judgement and reasoned decision-making, while leveraging individuals’ so-called soft skills, also involves “interpretation of contextual factors and potentially non-human information, to determine a course of action” (p. 3). To this end, they comment that educational programs need to refocus from teaching primarily reckoning skills to ones designed to advance judgement skills, which they suggest will better prepare individuals for working in AI-augmented environments.

Summary and conclusion
----------------------

In summary, we propose the model presented in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#Fig1) offers a flexible framework useful for guiding GenAI-human knowledge building across different environments, including educational institutions, businesses, service industries, and government and non- government agencies. Reflecting on implications inherent in the model, we strongly endorse Dede and colleagues’ calls for education to increase emphasis on building individuals’ reasoned judgement and decision-making capabilities, moving away from what they see as its current emphasis on teaching functional and operational AI reckoning skills. While functional skills such as prompt engineering will continue to remain relevant for the foreseeable future (Bansal, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR5)), human judgement will become even more crucial in GenAI-human knowledge building, as will the exercise of sound personal values and ethics in decision-making about the deployment of AI-generated information. Furthermore, as Acar ([2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR1)) signals, in such a scenario an equally important and relevant capability will become *problem formulation.* As he correctly points out, currently most education systems prioritise problem *solving* over problem *forming* skills in the curriculum. However, with the rapidly advancing capabilities and accessibility of GenAI tools, he suggests this emphasis must change. Acar ([2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#ref-CR1)) argues that curriculum at all levels of education must focus on teaching students how to recognise and formulate ‘problems’ AI can assist with, the skills involved in creating effective prompts and queries, and developing a critical and creative mindset to evaluate and optimise the value of AI-augmented outputs. We contend that problem (re)formulation is a core component of dialogic discourse, and is closely associated with human reasoning capabilities, such as those aligned with the *Judgement Zone* in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10605-2#Fig1).

Reflecting on the appropriateness of the pyramid structure of our model and its alignment with Krathwohl’s revision of Bloom’s Cognitive Domain, we are not suggesting a hierarchy exists between or within the zones described in the model. We have adopted the well-known structure in an effort to descriptively and graphically represent potential opportunities for AI, AI-human and human contributions to collaborative knowledge building, and the iterative interactions that may occur between these. This interpretation is consistent with Anderson and Krathwohl’s (2001) original position on the model, where they state that while some cognitive processes are more complex than others, the pyramid depiction is intended to represent this complexity, rather than imply any sequential or discrete processes.

Like any representation attempting to theorise how rapidly evolving technologies might augment human activity, the model introduced in this article reflects what is currently known and available with respect to GenAI. As the technology continues to evolve, it is probable that different ‘blends’ of AI-human partnerships will emerge, with greater, lesser or different inputs from AI and humans to knowledge building. While we believe the model is sufficiently flexible to accommodate such developments, the current distribution of the zones and the nature of activities within them may change, in response to technological improvements and new AI-human partnership opportunities being identified. Moreover, as machine capabilities advance, human reasoning capabilities such as those indicated in the *Judgement Zone* will become more important for critically and creatively evaluating increasingly complex and nuanced *Collaboration Zone* outputs. Ultimately, humans must maintain agency and control over if and how AI-human outputs are operationalised.

In conclusion, there is no doubt that the advent of GenAI to mainstream knowledge building that affects virtually every aspect of our lives presents challenges, not the least of which is determining ‘truth’ amidst the avalanche of AI-generated mis and disinformation. However, as with previous at-the-time ‘disruptive’ technologies such as Blockchain, the Internet of Things, and even Cloud Computing, it takes time for appropriate use behaviours and applications to emerge. Ultimately, how GenAI is used will be determined by people working individually and collectively, and this will be governed by individual and collective values and ethics. In this article we have attempted to theorise a way forward, where GenAI and humans can partner in approaches that recognise the complementary strengths and capabilities each brings to the knowledge building table. While this will undoubtedly evolve with time, we hope this offers a starting point for much needed discussions and empirical trials that can optimise the considerable potential of GenAI for augmenting human cognition.
