# Incremental project-based robot programming: Effects on young children's computational thinking, executive function, and learning behavioral patterns

## Abstract
Robot programming is an age-appropriate means to cultivate children's computational thinking (CT) and executive function (EF). Project-based learning (PBL) is commonly applied to teach robot programming. PBL involves complex, large-scale projects consisting of multiple sub-projects. Each sub-project introduces new knowledge but offers few opportunities to connect it with previously learned knowledge, thus misaligning with children's cognitive development. In comparison, incremental PBL (IPBL) is a tailored design of PBL that begins with basic knowledge, with each new sub-project building on all learned knowledge from previous sub-projects and progressively adding new knowledge to achieve complete project learning. Hence, this research integrated IPBL into robot programming to develop an incremental project-based robot programming (I-PBRP) approach, and evaluated its effect on young children's CT, EF, and learning behavioral patterns. Ninety-five children aged 5–6 engaged in 12-week interventions. They were randomly assigned to the I-PBRP group and the conventional project-based robot programming (C-PBRP) group. Results manifested that the I-PBRP group achieved better performance than the C-PBRP group in CT and in the three components of EF (inhibition, working memory, and cognitive flexibility) over time. The progressive behavioral analysis manifested that the I-PBRP approach promoted superior performance in robot programming activities and more positive learning behaviors for children. This research has implications for robot programming teaching approaches for young children's CT and EF development.
## Introduction
Computational thinking (CT), a set of thinking skills involving computer science principles for individuals to solve problems, has become a priority for K-12 Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM) education (Wing, 2006; Zhang et al., 2025). In recent years, CT has been progressively integrated into early childhood education (Su & Yang, 2023). Cultivating young children's CT is significant as it is positively associated with STEM learning, cognitive development, and career pathways (Lin et al., 2023; Tsarava et al., 2022).
Programming, which involves children applying computer science principles (e.g., algorithms, debugging) to solve problems, is an effective approach to fostering CT (Hsu et al., 2018; McCormick & Hall, 2021). In programming activities, children plan, handle information, and change their ways of thinking, which also benefits executive function (EF) (Arfé et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2025). EF involves a series of cognitive skills that self-regulate thoughts and behaviors to solve problems (Diamond, 2012). It is closely linked with academic performance, social bonds, and health (Ahmed et al., 2018; Lohndorf et al., 2021). EF and CT share a similar problem-solving nature. Studies have revealed a correlation between them (Liu, 2024; Pellas, 2024; Robertson et al., 2020).
With advancements in robotics, educational robots have been adopted to support children's cognitive growth (Kalogiannakis, Papadakis, & Dorouka, 2020; Misirli and Komis, 2023). Programmable robots are an age-appropriate tool that offers children playful and hands-on experiences of manipulating tangible objects (Chen et al., 2024; Levinson & Bers, 2024). Empirical studies have demonstrated that robot programming could effectively enhance children's CT and EF (Berson et al., 2023; Zurnacı & Turan, 2024).
The teaching approach for robot programming is crucial for fostering CT and EF (Hsu et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2025). Studies have identified that project-based learning (PBL) is the most commonly adopted teaching approach for robot programming (Ching & Hsu, 2023; Pugnali et al., 2017; Saad & Zainudin, 2024). PBL has been shown to be appropriate and effective for young children (Holm, 2011; Lev et al., 2020). Conventional PBL (CPBL) often involves a complex, large-scale project composed of multiple sub-projects. Each sub-project requires children to learn a large amount of new knowledge and apply it to achieve the final goal (Hao et al., 2016; Huang, 2016). For example, Wang et al. (2023) adopted PBL to teach computer programming and designed five sub-projects. Each sub-project introduced new knowledge: “algorithm,” “selection and bubble sort,” “linear and dichotomous search,” “flowcharting,” and “mind mapping.” However, in CPBL, introducing new knowledge in every sub-project offers few chances for children to connect it with the learned knowledge from previous sub-projects. This approach mismatches the staged nature of children's cognitive development, where higher-level skills build upon foundational ones (Piaget, 1971). It also fails to activate children's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD; i.e., the gap between the current and potential level of development to be achieved with appropriate support; Vygotsky, 1987). Furthermore, CPBL may overwhelm children with limited working memory and increase their cognitive load, thereby hindering CT and EF growth (Hao et al., 2016; Liu, 2024).
Incremental PBL (IPBL), a tailored design of PBL, originated from and is commonly used to teach computer programming. Unlike CPBL, it systematically integrates all learned content in previous sub-projects and new knowledge in each sub-project (Huang, 2016; Vega et al., 2012). For example, Huang (2016) applied IPBL to teach Java programming. He designed nine sub-projects, each combining new knowledge with all knowledge from previous sub-projects (e.g., sub-project 3 integrated new knowledge “classes and objects” with all learned content from sub-projects 1, “language basics,” and 2, “strings and arrays”). IPBL matches children's gradual development nature and supports learning within the ZPD by providing optimal challenges based on prior learning that bridge the gap between their current and potential development (Piaget, 1971; Vygotsky, 1987). IPBL also exerts a lower cognitive load by engaging children in sub-projects featured in progressively increased knowledge and similar problem-solving contexts (Hao et al., 2016). Accordingly, IPBL may address the problems of CPBL and be an appropriate teaching approach for robot programming to support children's CT and EF (Di Lieto et al., 2017; Fallon, 2024). For CT development, IPBL progressively connects new knowledge with what children have learned, which reinforces memory retention and enables children to acquire programming knowledge and apply it to solve increasingly complex tasks easily. For EF development, IPBL builds on existing knowledge with each task, encouraging children to stay focused on goals and manipulate information. As tasks become progressively more challenging, children learn to switch between different requirements, enhancing their cognitive flexibility. Although studies have applied IPBL to teaching older students computer programming (Huang, 2016; Vega et al., 2012), its applicability in robot programming for young children's CT and EF development remains under-explored.
Hence, our purpose was to integrate IPBL into robot programming to propose an incremental project-based robot programming (I-PBRP) approach and compare its effect on young children's CT and EF with the conventional project-based robot programming (C-PBRP) approach. Besides, Bers et al. (2019, 2022) underscored the significance of delving into children's behaviors in robot programming learning. However, previous literature reviews identified that existing studies mainly collected quantitative data to examine children's learning outcomes, rather than the learning process (e.g., behaviors; McCormick & Hall, 2021; Su & Yang, 2023). Since children exposed to different teaching approaches exhibit distinct behaviors, analyzing their behaviors offers qualitative and explainable insights into different robot programming teaching approaches (Hwang et al., 2017; Sun et al., 2021). Hence, children's behaviors under the I-PBRP and C-PBRP approaches were further explored. Three research questions guided this study.
- RQ1: What are the differences in CT between children under the I-PBRP and C-PBRP approaches?
- RQ2: What are the differences in EF between children under the I-PBRP and C-PBRP approaches?
- RQ3: What are the differences in behavioral patterns between children under the I-PBRP and C-PBRP approaches?

## Discussion and conclusions
This research proposed an I-PBRP approach and evaluated its impact on 5-6-year-old children's CT, EF, and learning behavioral patterns. Following 12-week interventions, the I-PBRP group performed better in CT and EF than the C-PBRP group over time. Additionally, progressive behavioral analysis disclosed that the I-PBRP approach facilitated superior performance in robot programming and more positive behaviors for children as time progressed.
