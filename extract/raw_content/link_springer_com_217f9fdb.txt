Abstract
--------

This article alerts us to gaps in preparedness for civic participation in technology-mediated networked society. Drawing on dual-process theories and sociocultural frameworks, we argue that reasoning with data involves a balance between intuitive (System 1) and deliberative (System 2) processes, shaped by a productive tension between authoritative and internally persuasive discourse, and the mastery and appropriation of data practices. We focus on the role of data literacy and trust in science in functional scientific reasoning. Using survey data from 461 participants, we found that higher education graduates demonstrated stronger data interpretation competence and a greater propensity to use data in functional reasoning than K-12 graduates. These differences were found between K-12 graduates focused on exact sciences versus humanities and social sciences but were not observed in higher education. Trust in science mediated reasoning with data across all groups, yet levels of trust did not differ significantly between K-12 and higher education graduates. Our findings reveal disparities in data literacy rooted in disciplinary divides at the K-12 level that are bridged in higher education but persist for non-college-bound learners. We propose making trust in science an explicit educational goal and fostering mastery and appropriation of data practices through experiences that balance authoritative and internally persuasive engagement with data. These approaches can better equip all learners, regardless of educational path, for informed civic participation in a datafied world.

1 Introduction
--------------

How well do different educational paths prepare graduates to consider data when grappling with real-world problems? Are graduates of different educational paths equally prepared to contend with socioscientific issues? Socioscientific issues are real-world problems that intersect with science, such as personal health decisions or public policy issues like taxation on sugary beverages (e.g., Zeidler et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR62)). The ability to contend with such issues in contemporary networked society is inextricably linked to digital and data literacy (Shi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR50)).

Attending to evidence in considering such issues can moderate the facile adoption of misinformation and reduce the denial of well-supported scientific claims, such as anthropogenic climate change (Barzilai & Chinn, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR5); Sinatra & Lombardi, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR52)). However, attending to evidence is not prevalent, and we know more about factors that distract from the weight and full meaning of data (Kahan et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR24); Nurse & Grant, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR38)) than about what affects incorporating data in reasoning. Recent findings concerning reasoning about COVID- 19 suggest that attending to data relates to data interpretation competence and trust in science (Tabak & Dubovi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR58); Herman et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR19); Radinsky & Tabak, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR44)).

Therefore, if the goal is to cultivate deliberative publics and functional scientific literacy—the propensity and ability to interpret and use scientific information and data representations for one's purposes—then we need to understand how data competence and trust in science develop. Moreover, to ensure equitable trajectories for all learners, not just those who are college-bound, K-12 (primary and secondary) education must provide a sufficient basis for such development (Zaff, Youniss, and Gibson [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR61)). In this paper, we examine the differential contribution of different educational paths to incorporating data in reasoning about socioscientific issues. Specifically, we investigate whether higher (and tertiary) education or studies focused on exact sciences provide a stronger foundation for data interpretation competence and trust in science.

We examine this through a secondary analysis of a survey-based study on data literacy and reasoning concerning COVID- 19 (Tabak & Dubovi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR58)). In this study, participants answered a series of data-interpretation questions displaying datasets and graphical representations like those published in news media. These questions provided a direct measure of data interpretation competence. Participants also responded to functional COVID- 19-related questions (e.g., would you travel abroad) that referred to these data representations and completed a trust-in-science questionnaire.

In the present paper, we ask: (1) Are there differences between K-12 and higher education graduates in their propensity to incorporate data in socioscientific reasoning? (2) Are there differences between K-12 and higher education graduates in competence in data interpretation? (3) Are there differences between K-12 and higher education graduates in trust in science? (4) Are there differences in these measures between a focus on exact sciences versus social sciences and the humanities?

2 Literature review
-------------------

### 2.1 Theoretical frameworks

We integrate cognitive and sociocultural theory to understand functional scientific literacy. From a cognitive perspective, we draw on dual-process theories (Evans & Stanovich, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR13); Pennycook, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR42)). These theories explain how people may employ either an immediate, fast, mostly intuitive, mode of reasoning, referred to as System 1, or a slower, deliberative, mode of reasoning, referred to as System 2. As we describe in the following sections, dual-process theories have been used to explain how people contend with online information and why they sometimes ignore evidence or adopt misinformation without careful examination (Bago et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR2); Moravec et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR33)). For example, a person can be said to be operating under System 1 thinking if they have a goal of making dietary decisions and receive information, which was forwarded from a familiar discussion group, that a particular product is healthful, and consequently, directly, choose to adopt the product. In contrast, if they proceed to check the message's origin, evaluate its reliability, and search for information to corroborate or refute the claims, then they are engaging in System 2 thinking.

However, we propose that these theories are insufficient for describing the dynamics at play in functional scientific reasoning because they do not directly elaborate on the roles that knowledge, skills, affect, and social factors play. Therefore, we also draw on two sociocultural frameworks (Tabak & Baumgartner, [2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR57); Tabak, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR56)): Bakhtin’s notion of authoritative and internally persuasive discourse (Bakhtin, [1981](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR3)), and Wertsch’s dimensions of mastery and appropriation (Wertsch, [1998](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR60)).

Authoritative and internally persuasive discourse depicts an ongoing dialogue between a person and the multitude of voices in society, where a person’s own voice is constructed through adoption and adaptation from a selection of these voices. Some voices are perceived as authoritative, which means they must be taken as is, unaltered. Other voices are perceived as internally persuasive, which means they appear penetrable and open to negotiation and adaptation. Neither is inherently good or bad; both are necessary.

For example, consider a person who used ratio calculations, posted nutritional information, and health organization dietary guidelines to figure out portion size but decided to fill protein recommendations based only on vegetarian options. It can be said that established computational procedures and posted nutritional values are perceived as authoritative because they are not questioned and are used without alteration. However, dietary guidelines are perceived as internally persuasive because they are perceived as open to negotiation and alteration, enabling the exclusion of the recommended elements of meat and poultry.

Within the second set of sociocultural ideas, mastery refers to developing competence in practices, and appropriation refers to seeing these practices as relevant and adopting these practices as part of one’s toolkit. Training, practice, and experience can facilitate developing mastery, but mastery does not necessarily imply appropriation Wertsch, [1998](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR60)). Making practices part of one’s toolkit requires negotiating and resolving the tension between authoritative and persuasive aspects of practice. If practices are deemed entirely authoritative, they may not be taken up because they may appear alienating or unattainable, but if they are deemed entirely persuasive, then they may be altered to the extent that they no longer align with established and shared societal approaches. Therefore, a balance between authoritative and internally persuasive discourse is needed to support mastery and appropriation. These, in turn, support employing System 2 processes.

We posit that to employ System 2 as part of functional scientific engagement, a person needs to be *inclined* to use deliberative practices—appropriation and be *able* to do so—mastery. The integrated framework provides a means to consider this inclination and ability as arising from mastery and appropriation, which are developed through a productive tension between seeing data practices and science as normative fixtures to be taken up as is as well as viable tools that can be tailored to serve one’s own goals, values, and circumstances.

### 2.2 Data literacy, dual-processes, mastery and appropriation in functional reasoning

Functional scientific literacy and reasoning about socioscientific issues increasingly rely on data literacy, on the ability to interpret quantitative and visual data representations (Bhargava, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR6); Jiang et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR23)). These skills are essential to adult functioning in society (Engelhardt et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR12)). For example, numerical literacy is positively associated with the ability to judge health risks and benefits (Osborn et al., [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR40)). Data literacy also predicted stronger decision-making and financial outcomes (Sinayev & Peters, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR53)) and has been suggested to relate to the judgment of social relationships and behavioral norms (Skagerlund et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR54)). Therefore, mastery and appropriation of data practices could contribute to productive functional scientific literacy. However, neither mastery nor appropriation of data practices are prevalent.

Much of the public struggles with data interpretation and computation (Shah et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR48); Highlights of the U.S. PIAAC Results, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR20)). These difficulties are attributed, in part, to specific challenges in understanding concepts such as distribution, variation, and probability and recognizing how they appear in different types of representations (Muñiz-Rodríguez, Rodríguez-Muñiz, and Alsina [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR34)). Higher education graduates cope better with these challenges (Bakker et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR4)). There is evidence that in OECD countries, completing secondary education does not necessarily provide adequate preparation for the functional use of digital technologies (McFarlane, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR32)). Thus, while K-12 learners may develop mastery of specific quantitative skills, these may not align with those required for using publicly available quantitative information. This inadequate preparedness relates to how learning goals are conceived concerning digital lives more broadly (McFarlane, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR32)) and concerning data literacy specifically (Gebre, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR16)).

As noted, mastery can be achieved without appropriation, and higher levels of education or stronger numeracy do not necessarily imply appropriating scientific consensus (Drummond & Fischhoff, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR10)). Stronger skills may actually bolster motivated reasoning or identity-protective cognition, that is, the process through which people disregard conflicting evidence or attend to and favor evidence that leads to conclusions that align with the position of their affinity groups (Kahan et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR24)). For example, when confronting politically charged issues, i.e., anthropogenic climate change, participants tended to select a response aligned with the position of their political party rather than a response that was better supported by the presented data. In contrast, they did not favor political affiliation when the issue was politically neutral, i.e., the occurrence of a rash. This motivated reasoning effect was stronger among those with higher numeracy (Nurse & Grant, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR38)).

It is an open debate whether motivated reasoning among those with stronger mastery reflects reliance on System 1 thinking (Bago et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR2)), automatically siding with one’s affinity group, or whether it reflects System 2 thinking, where one uses knowledge and skills to explain and justify the in-group position (Kahan et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR24); Nurse & Grant, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR38)). The research described above supports a motivated System 2 explanation. In contrast, others have found that even in politically-laden contexts, when people resist quick judgment and engage deeply, they may overcome susceptibility to fake news (Pennycook & Rand, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR43)). In such cases, higher numeracy is likely to prove an asset. From both sides of the debate, what seems to underlie empirical results is that mastery of computational skills alone does not explain susceptibility to or resistance to misinformation.

Although it does not resolve the debate concerning the role of System 1 or 2 in motivated reasoning, trust (Hung, [1999](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR22)), and specifically trust in science, is an additional factor in processes of appropriation and the take up of scientific consensus. Trust in science can enhance science learning (Krüger et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR28)), and it co-predicts incorporating data in reasoning about COVID- 19 (Tabak & Dubovi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR58)). Still, another study found that when provided with information supporting the consensus, trust in science contributed to adopting scientific consensus only in one of four cases examined (Diamond et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR9)). People may hold different levels of trust between topics over which there is more or less political strife (Bromme et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR7); Diamond et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR9)) and also between trust in scientific institutions versus in scientific methods, with less disparity between these types of trust among those with higher levels of education (Achterberg et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR1)).

Our concern in this paper is with how different educational paths better prepare the public for data literacy in functional reasoning. Given the role of trust in appropriation and the propensity to engage with data, the question of whether higher levels of education foster trust in science is pertinent, but there are mixed findings concerning this relationship (Zelič, Berič, and Grum [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR63); Bromme et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR7)). Moreover, the conceptualization of trust in science and its role in functional and socioscientific reasoning is complex and not yet well understood. Education was found to predict general attitudes toward science but not toward specific or controversial issues (Roberts et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR47)). Others suggest that trust in science may form and stabilize in primary education (Krüger et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR28)). Nonetheless, cross-sectional changes in public trust in science within short periods have also been found (Bromme et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR7)).

The literature reviewed points to relationships between higher education, data literacy, and trust in science, with educational processes and immersion in science contributing to both mastery and appropriation. These, in turn, seem to foster both the inclination and ability to engage in System 2 thinking, but it is unclear whether System 2 thinking might still discount the weight of evidence due to identity protective motivation. However, not all issues are politically charged, and as noted above, information behavior may differ depending on the weight of the political or social strife around the issue. In cases where people have a vested interest in understanding a topic and political or social affinity is not at stake, there can be an advantage to stronger trust in science and scientific knowledge and skills (Hendriks & Jucks, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR18); Shauli & Baram-Tsabari, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR49)).

It is unclear whether we adequately prepare *all* K-12 learners to engage effectively with socioscientific issues in their everyday lives. Nevertheless, we are obliged to do so. This obligation is becoming imperative as more secondary school graduates are unable or choose not to attend higher education (Edge Research et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR11)). Therefore, we sought to examine whether there were differences between K-12 and Higher Education graduates and between areas of study in their propensity to incorporate data and its previously identified associated factors.

3 Materials and methods
-----------------------

### 3.1 Participants

This study is part of a more extensive study (Tabak & Dubovi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR58)). Using the *iPanel* online polling service, we recruited 461 participants. Prior to recruitment, we conducted a statistical power analysis using G\*Power software (version 3.1.9.6) to estimate the appropriate sample size. The analysis was based on an alpha level of 0.05 and a power (1-β) of 0.80 to detect a small-medium-sized effect of f2 = 0.04 (Cohen, [1988](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR8)) using multiple linear regression. The calculation determined that a sample size of approximately 450 participants was sufficient, and our final sample size of 461 was deemed appropriate for the study.

iPanel distributed the survey in September 2020 during the second wave of the COVID- 19 outbreak in Israel. iPanel is one of the most extensive available polling services, and it adheres to the high-quality research code of the European Society for Opinion and Marketing Research. The polling service conducted a representative sampling of Hebrew-dominant individuals who were graduates of either the state-secular or state-religious school systems and varied in gender, age, and socioeconomic status.

The study was conducted following the approval of the university’s ethics committee (#0002076–1).

### 3.2 Measures

#### 3.2.1 Reasoning with data

To assess participants'inclination to incorporate data in their reasoning, they were presented with relevant data and asked to answer open-ended questions regarding four COVID- 19-related issues: whether schools should reopen, whether they would travel internationally, whether they would recommend visiting Israel to their relatives, and what type of face-covering they should use. Participants were encouraged to use the presented data to justify their responses. Each of the four items was evaluated to determine whether their responses were grounded in data, with the total score calculated as the percentage of data-based responses. The coding was done independently by the authors and a research assistant, with a high level of inter-rater reliability (Cohen's kappa = 0.85) demonstrated in a random selection of 25% of the responses. Any discrepancies were discussed and resolved, and the research assistant then coded the remaining 75% of the responses.

#### 3.2.2 Competency in data interpretation

The measure of Competency in Data Interpretation was developed in a previous study (Tabak & Dubovi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR58)). The measure evaluates a set of competencies that assess the ability to interpret and reason with quantitative and visual representations in the context of the COVID- 19 pandemic. It comprises 16 items categorized into four domains: (1) Reading graphs and tables (nine items), which requires participants to extract and interpret information from graphs and tables, as demonstrated in the study by Friel et al. ([2001](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR15)). For instance, participants were presented with a bar graph and asked to answer specific questions about age groups and symptom frequencies. (2) Ratio and proportion concepts (three items) refer to the ability to reason about rational numbers and compare their ratios, as defined by Noelting ([1980](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR37)). Participants were required to reason with extensive quantities of rates and percentages (Howe et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR21)). (3) Statistical reasoning (four items) involves the ability to reason with concepts such as average and range to select the most effective face mask for preventing the transmission of COVID- 19. As described by (Tabak & Dubovi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR58)), the measure showed good face validity. The current study’s 16 competency items presented a fair Cronbach’s alpha internal consistency score of 0.69.

#### 3.2.3 Trust in science

The 21-item questionnaire was developed by Nadelson et al. ([2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR35)) and adapted to the COVID- 19 context by (Tabak & Dubovi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR58)). The questionnaire asks to rate a subjective level of trust in science and scientists on a five-point scale (1 = strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree). The survey's internal consistency of Cronbach's alpha of 0.85 is similar to previous studies.

#### 3.2.4 Demographic characteristics

Demographic characteristics included gender, age, family income level, education level, academic discipline, and level of religiosity.

### 3.3 Data analysis

Descriptive statistics were used to describe the participants’ demographic characteristics, reasoning with data, competency, and trust in science levels. For academic disciplines, responses were coded as *exact and natural science* for fields such as mathematics, physics, computer sciences, engineering, health sciences, biology, and architecture; *humanities and social science* for fields such as history, literature, philosophy, economics, psychology, sociology, anthropology, political science, and law; and other fields were codded as *others*.

Unpaired t-test analyses were conducted to illustrate the effect of educational level on trust in science, competency level, and the use of data in everyday reasoning. Next, to examine the interaction effect of academic discipline and educational level on trust in science, competency, and data-based reasoning, two-way ANOVA tests were conducted. The appropriateness of the two-way ANOVA and the independence of observations was ensured by the study design. Normality of the dependent variables (trust in science, competency, and data-based reasoning) was assessed using the Shapiro–Wilk test and Q-Q plots, with results indicating no significant deviations. Homogeneity of variances was confirmed via Levene’s test.

Hierarchical multiple regression analysis was conducted to examine the relationship between academic discipline, educational level, sociodemographic characteristics, competency, and trust in science on the tendency to use data. Preliminary analyses confirmed that the key assumptions were met. Linearity between the independent variables (education level, academic discipline, trust in science, and competency) and the dependent variable (data-based reasoning) was verified through scatterplots. Independence of residuals was supported by a Durbin-Watson statistic within the acceptable range, while homoscedasticity was confirmed using residual plots. Normality of residuals was assessed with the Shapiro–Wilk test and Q-Q plots. Variance inflation factors (VIF) were below 5, indicating no issues with multicollinearity among predictors.

4 Results
---------

### 4.1 Participants demographic characteristics

Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#Tab1) shows a summary of the demographic characteristics of the study participants. There were 158 participants who were K-12 graduates and 303 participants who were higher education graduates. These proportions correspond with a recent OECD report showing that 47%—56.9% of the general population in Israel hold an academic degree (OECD, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR39)). There were significant differences between participants with secondary school level education (K-12 education) and participants with higher education. Mainly, participants with higher education were older and had higher income levels, and more participants reported studying social sciences (51% vs. 14%) or exact and natural sciences (46% vs. 21%) than participants with K-12 education. 65% of participants with K-12 education reported taking vocational emphasis courses during their school studies, such as accounting, design, marketing, tourism, and more. There were no significant differences in gender or level of religiosity between K-12 education and higher education.

### 4.2 Differences in trust in science, competency, and data use across educational levels

We conducted unpaired t-tests to assess the effect of educational level (K-12 vs. higher education) on trust in science, competency level, and the use of data in everyday reasoning (Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#Tab2)). Overall, as Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#Tab2) shows, there were no significant differences in trust in science between the two groups of participants, K-12 and higher education) t = − 1.345, *p* = 0.179). However, participants with higher education showed a significantly higher level of competency ()t = 6.273, *p* < 0.001). Further analysis showed a significant difference across all three domains of competency: graph and tables reading (t = − 4.653, *p* < 0.001); the ratio and proportion domain ((t = − 5.555, *p* < 0.001), statistical reasoning (t = − 4.090, *p* < 0.001). In addition, participants with higher education tended to incorporate more data in their reasoning than participants with K-12 education (t = 3.454, *p* < 0.001).

5 Discussion
------------

This study sought to understand whether different educational paths affect the propensity to incorporate data in reasoning about socioscientific issues. Based on prior research, we focused our analyses on the interrelationships between data literacy and trust in science (Tabak & Dubovi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR58); Herman et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR19); Radinsky & Tabak, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR44)). We draw on an integration of dual-process theories (Evans & Stanovich, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR13)) and the theoretical dimensions of authoritative and internally persuasive discourse (Bakhtin, [1981](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR3)) and of mastery and appropriation (Wertsch, [1998](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR60)). This framework informs our discussion below concerning educational implications of this study. Our findings highlight the critical role of K-12 education in cultivating agentic and deliberative publics.

The first three of the four questions that we raised asked whether there were differences between K-12 and higher education graduates in their: (1) propensity to incorporate data in reasoning; (2) level of data interpretation competence; and (3) trust in science. The fourth question considered these same three variables with respect to differences in disciplinary focus between exact sciences and the social sciences and humanities within K-12 and within higher education graduates.

Higher education graduates were more likely to incorporate data and had higher competence in data interpretation, but there were *no* significant differences between K-12 and higher education graduates in their trust in science. Within higher education graduates, there were *no* significant differences between those who majored in exact sciences and in the social sciences and humanities in any of the three variables in question (although those majoring in science had higher trust in science than those categorized as studying ‘other’ fields such as technical vocations). In contrast, *among K-12 graduates, those who focused on studying exact sciences had stronger data interpretation competence and a higher propensity to incorporate data in reasoning than those who focused on the social sciences and humanities*. The present study replicates prior findings that both competence and trust in science explain the variance in incorporating data in reasoning. This was true for all the different educational paths that we examined.

### 5.1 Discipline-related gaps in data literacy preparedness for K-12 graduates

We were surprised that there were differences between those whose studies focused on exact sciences compared to those focused on humanities and social sciences at the K-12 but *not* the higher education levels in data interpretation competence and the propensity to incorporate data in reasoning. One possible explanation is that people who focus on the humanities and social sciences at the higher education level had previously focused on exact sciences at the high school level, which accounts for their stronger data interpretation competence. Another reason may be that at the higher education level, students who focus on humanities and social sciences may take courses in social science research methods, and these courses enhance learners'data literacy and appreciation of evidence, bridging any gaps that might arise from the inherent attention to these topics in the exact sciences.

The gaps between those who focused on exact sciences and those who focused on humanities and social sciences at the K-12 level raise a red flag concerning the ways in which K-12 education provides adequate preparation for civic participation for all learners (Zaff, Youniss, and Gibson [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR61)). Though these gaps seem to be bridged through higher education, the problem persists for those who do not pursue higher education, which is true for increasing numbers of K-12 graduates (Edge Research et al. [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR11)). Those who focus on the humanities and social sciences in K-12 may be at a disadvantage in contending with data-based reasoning.

Our findings that show that such a gap did not exist in higher education suggest that overcoming these gaps in K- 12 is attainable. If our suppositions about methods courses are warranted, then these findings further suggest that modest curricular modifications may produce meaningful outcomes and reduce gaps in data literacy between those who focus on exact sciences and those who focus on humanities and social sciences. Current initiatives incorporating data literacy education in social studies (Shreiner & Guzdial, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR51)), such as learning about historical waves of migration through geo-spatial data visualizations (Kahn & Jiang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR25); Radinsky et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR45)), and arts-focused data literacy education (Matuk et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR31)), are promising directions.

### 5.2 The role of trust in data literacy

A striking finding of the present analysis was that, while, as expected, higher education provides an added value to developing competence in data interpretation, there were no significant differences in trust in science between K-12 graduates and those with higher education. With some exceptions, such as Kruger et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR28)), who suggest that trust in science becomes relatively stable after primary education, several studies report that years of education, and higher education specifically, correlate with greater trust in science (Zelič, Berič, and Grum [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR63); Bromme et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR7)). Therefore, we expected our findings to show a similar pattern. Moreover, our findings show that incorporating data in reasoning hinges on both competence and trust in science, therefore, stagnant or limited trust in science can curtail considering evidence in functional reasoning.

One reason why our findings do not fully align with prior research showing correlations between trust in science and level of education is the multiplicity in how trust in science is conceptualized and measured. For example, differences are found between general attitudes to science and to specific topics (Roberts et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR47)) and between those that were more or less controversial (Bromme et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR7); Diamond et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR9)), even within the same group of participants. Responses to items asking about the level of trust in science/scientists versus methods of science (e.g., whether knowledge can only be obtained through unbiased systematic research) yield differentiated constructs and different levels of trust in these two constructs (Achterberg et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR1)). Although these different conceptualizations and forms of measurement reflect different relationships between trust and education, most studies suggest that trust is an important factor in public engagement with science (Tabak & Dubovi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR58); Bromme et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR7); Krüger et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR28); Roberts et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR47)).

Our findings motivate an explicit and early focus on cultivating trust in science in K-12 education. This may seem at odds with our earlier recommendation to extend and deepen data literacy education in the social sciences and humanities. However, these two goals are commensurate. All learners, not just those whose studies focus on exact and natural sciences will contend with socioscientific issues as part of future civic participation. Therefore, to close any gaps between learners who focus on science and learners who focus on humanities and social sciences, such experiences that cultivate trust in science should not be limited to science classes. Socioscientific issues, by definition, include a multitude of considerations beyond those that can be answered by science, and that are often emphasized in the social sciences and humanities, such as moral, social and economic considerations. This multifaceted nature of socioscientific issues should enable streamlined integration across the curriculum (Lee, Wilkerson, and Lanouette [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR30)). Maintaining a combination of trust in science and competence in data interpretation will better prepare *all* learners for incorporating data in reasoning as part of civic participation.

### 5.3 Implications for data literacy education

In this paper, we introduced a theoretical framework that integrates cognitive and sociocultural theory. From this perspective, we expect incorporating data in functional reasoning to reflect educational experiences that cultivate sufficient competence in data practices (mastery) and portray science and data practices as a balance between dictums that must be taken as is (authoritative) and malleable tools that can be adapted to one’s goals (internally persuasive), so that learners adopt these practices as part of their life toolkits (appropriation). We suggest that such educational experiences make one likely to be *able* and *inclined* to employ System 2 processes in functional reasoning and thus more likely to incorporate data in their considerations.

In this study, we found disparities between different educational paths, and that all paths may not provide sufficient support for these types of educational experiences. We proffer that K-12 graduates focused on humanities and social sciences were less likely to incorporate data in reasoning, because their educational experiences did not cultivate mastery nor appropriation, compared to their counterparts who focused on natural sciences. Across the board, relatively low, nascent, and stagnant trust in science limited appropriation and consequently may have limited the extent to which all could have engaged with the data. In line with this framework and considering our findings concerning the roles of competence and trust and their related disparities, we propose a few directions for data literacy education.

The key implication for data literacy education is that it needs to cultivate both mastery and appropriation, and that this is achieved by fostering a balance between authoritative and internally persuasive voice. We see trust as a pivot to enacting this balance. To achieve these interrelated goals, we suggest that instruction should be anchored in two types of experiences: those that reflect functional scientific literacy and those that reflect professional scientific practices.

Experiences that reflect functional reasoning where learners marshal public data resources to grapple with socioscientific issues are needed to help learners develop competence in data literate skills. Doing so in the context of authentic problems and resources makes transfer from learning to real-life settings more viable due to the similarity between learning and future application settings (National Research Council, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR36)). It can also increase the potential for take up and appropriation because these data practices may appear more relevant, and because learners can contend with the difficulties associated with real-life data that is complex, incomplete and at times contradictory in a guided setting with low cost of failure.

Experience with formal scientific practices is also needed. It forms a foundation for those who choose to pursue further science learning and vocations, but more pertinent to our discussion, a view “from within” can contribute to better functional reasoning. When learners conduct their own scientific investigations according to scientific tenets, they confront first-hand the need to operationalize variables, constrain measurements, and interpret results, which provides insight into the role that researchers and their equivocal decisions play in scientific explanations, but they also grapple with the need to support their claims with evidence, adhere to standards of evidence, and reject or refine their arguments in response to the scrutiny and evaluation of their peers (Tabak, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR55)).

Both forms of experience, formal scientific practices and functional scientific reasoning, should include attention to developing meta-conceptual awareness. Considering the processes that were involved in the experience, the rationale and logic that underlie the actions that were taken, similarities and differences with out-of-school contexts, and their strengths and weaknesses. The intention is that such meta-conceptual awareness would include understanding that publicly available scientific data and information is the product of an enterprise that is subject to influence and interpretation but includes measures to promote reliable and trustworthy results (Kienhues et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR26)). It would also include the recognition that the representations of publicly available data and information are curated (Klein-Avraham & Reich, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR27); Pangrazio & Sefton-Green, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR41)).

This meta-conceptual awareness is expected, in turn, to result in a vigilant but trusting stance (Kienhues et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR26)) that gives credence to data and scientific explanations but does not take claims at face value. In this way, we see trust as a pivot that helps people reconcile authoritative with internally persuasive discourse, so that their vigilance makes them more likely to engage in System 2 processes, analyzing data and information using formal tools they had acquired, but incorporating multiple considerations, so that they tailor information to their own needs and circumstances, rather than follow blindly.

### 5.4 Implications for research

We found that trust played a central role, but also that there are mixed findings in the literature, including the present study, concerning the relationship between education and trust. Therefore, future research needs to deepen our understanding of trust in science. We need finer-grained conceptualizations that would provide a shared language with which to distinguish, identify, and measure different aspects of trust in science, such as those that have surfaced in the different studies reviewed in this paper. A robust understanding is likely to arise from integrating theoretical analyses with grounded studies that uncover nuanced positions anchored in publics’ lived experiences. We also need longitudinal and international studies so that we can articulate patterns of development of trust, consider which facets of trust in science may be stable or malleable, characterize contexts in which trust seems to flourish, and identify any critical junctures in which trust grows or declines, as has been found for interest in science (Vedder-Weiss & Fortus, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR59)).

Similarly, it is essential to delve into the complexities of fully conceptualizing data literacy. Our findings suggest that apt data practices may hinge on other forms of knowledge, skills and dispositions, such as trust in science, which may not be included in current educational goals, and thus constrain future data literate practices. Therefore, future research on data literacy education needs to investigate and uncover such related factors.

### 5.5 Limitations

Grounding our study in a single country, we were able to investigate patterns in data reasoning among participants who represent sociodemographic diversity yet share socio-cultural-political contexts and are exposed to common media data representations. Despite the study's geographical limitation, the broader relevance of our results is backed by research indicating similarities in science education between countries (Freeman, Marginson, and Tytler [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR14)). Similarities include common curricular elements, such as incorporating scientific inquiry practices (Heinz et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR17)) based on NGSS (Next Generation Science Standards) guidelines. However, we recommend that future research should consider extending this examination to other countries.

6 Conclusion
------------

In this paper, we pointed to gaps in preparedness to incorporate data in reasoning about issues that arise in people’s non-professional everyday lives. We found that higher education graduates were better prepared than K-12 graduates, and that among K-12 graduates those who focused on exact sciences rather than humanities and social sciences were better prepared. Critical historical analyses of educational imaginaries—the ways of envisioning how education can serve to adjust publics to the effects of innovation—reveal that such educational imaginaries sometimes lead to policies that have unintended consequences of social stratification or exclusion (Rahm, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR46)). Our findings caution that while an emphasis on cultivating data literacy in K-12 education is a necessary and productive goal in datafied networked society, historical associations between data, quantification, and the study of math and science may lead to focusing these educational initiatives in those areas of study. Education has also tended to emphasize cognitive skill development, mastery, and the authority of formal academic knowledge, over dispositions and other ways of knowing (Lampert, [1990](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR29); Tabak, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13537-1#ref-CR56)). Such foci may run the risk of underserved learners.
