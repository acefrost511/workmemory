Abstract
--------

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) technologies can promote significant changes in formal education. This study examines the perspectives and practices of secondary-school teachers who naturally and independently initiated the integration of GenAI into their teaching, focusing on changes in pedagogical design and teaching–learning processes. Teachers’ perspectives were explored through 17 semi-structured interviews, which were analyzed using inductive thematic analysis. In addition, 91 GenAI-enhanced teaching–learning activities designed and implemented by these teachers were analyzed using the SAMR framework (Puentedura, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR47)), enabling the classification of pedagogical practices across four levels: Substitution, Augmentation, Modification, and Redefinition. Two central themes emerged from teachers’ perspectives: adaptation to new technology and significant pedagogical change. The findings indicate that GenAI is perceived as transforming learning processes and pedagogical practices, potentially reshaping the teacher’s role toward guidance and facilitation. Despite current uncertainties, teachers view these tools as essential for preparing students for the future and emphasize the need for mediation. This pioneer study examines how GenAI tools can enable changes in pedagogical design and in teaching-learning processes according to the SAMR framework and introduces an integrative model that links SAMR with GenAI affordances for pedagogical analysis. Regardless of educators’ GenAI adoption stage, instructional uses primarily reflected augmentation of teachers’ capabilities, whereas student learning activities spanned all levels of the SAMR framework, including its higher levels, an uncommon pattern in early technology adoption. Overall, the findings suggest that GenAI has potential to enhance teaching–learning processes when integrated and accompanied by attention to ethical use.

1 Introduction
--------------

In the last decade, AI technologies have advanced dramatically (Rudolph et al. [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR54)). The integration of new technologies with immense computing power and big data analytics has led to the emergence of sophisticated AI algorithms that can learn and improve autonomously (Tan, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR68)). Today, AI tools are capable of learning, reasoning, problem-solving, and enhancing performance in ways similar to human cognition. Additionally, they can perform tasks that typically require human intelligence, such as language understanding, image recognition, and decision-making (O’Connor, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR44)). Therefore, the field is garnering increasing attention daily (Krstić et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR32)) with the prevailing assumption that AI is one of the technologies with the potential to revolutionize our lives (Rudolph et al. [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR54)).

As previously noted, AI tools are exceedingly powerful and possess the potential to induce significant changes across various societal domains. Consequently, AI has the capacity to profoundly impact the ways in which we learn and teach (Chen et al. [2020a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR14), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR15); Rudolph et al. [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR54)). Over the past four decades, scholars have deliberated on the advantages of this technology with the aim of broadening understanding and serving educational program designers (Rudolph et al. [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR54)). Recently, new AI models known as “Generative AI” (GenAI) have emerged. These models are capable of creating innovative content rather than merely analyzing and acting upon existing data (Gozalo-Brizuela & Garrido-Merchan, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR21)). Unlike previous predictive models, generative models are now integrated with Large Language Models (LLMs), which encompass vast amounts of data and together transform existing information to produce novel outputs not present in the raw data (Gozalo-Brizuela & Garrido-Merchan, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR21)). These models underpin chat-based tools, enabling them to engage in natural, open-ended conversations on a wide range of topics (Marzano, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR39); Oates & Johnson, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR45)), formulate coherent, partially accurate, and informative documents (Mahapatra, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR37); Oates & Johnson, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR45)), and respond to various types of queries. The new capabilities of GenAI tools have sparked a global discussion on how to best integrate them to enhance the quality of education and create interactive tasks that align with students’ abilities (Chen et al. [2020a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR14), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR15); Levy-Nadav et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR35); O’Connor [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR44)). Numerous researchers (Baidoo-Anu and Owusu Ansah [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR6); Chen et al. [2020a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR14), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR15); Krstić et al. [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR32); Lameras and Arnab [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR33); Mhlanga [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR40); Tlili et al. [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR70); Warr et al. [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR73)) believe that the use of AI tools may significantly transform the landscape of education, with some even describing it as a revolution (Henriksen et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR23)). Recent studies have already demonstrated that these tools can support teachers in the preparation and design of curricula (Choi et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR16)) and various learning materials (Wu et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR75)), and constructing practice questions and quizzes (Chan et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR13)). Furthermore, recent research indicates that these tools have the potential to improve both the efficiency and quality of teachers’ work (Bura & Myakala, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR10)) and are even perceived by educators as a means to reduce their workload (Kaushik et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR30); Sain et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR57); Shamir-Inbal et al., [2025b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR64); Zhang, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR79)), enhance engagement and motivation for learning (Bura & Myakala, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR10); Jauhiainen & Garagorry Guerra, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR27)), and foster learners’ curiosity (Sain et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR57)).

On the other hand, the use of these tools in educational settings is accompanied by significant challenges (Rodrig et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR51)). Recent studies indicate that LLMs frequently reproduce cultural and linguistic biases, privileging Western, English-dominant norms, perpetuating stereotypes, and generating inaccurate or distorted educational content (Ahmed et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR1); Francis et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR20); Tao et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR69)). Accordingly, teachers and learners who fail to adopt these tools wisely, while exercising critical thinking and the necessary caution, may inadvertently undermine the learning process.

At this juncture, steps are being considered on how to appropriately manage GenAI tools in education, use them wisely, and understand their advantages and disadvantages (Rudolph et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR56)). It is assumed that educators will need to significantly revise the learning tasks they present to students to foster unique skills such as creativity and critical thinking, skills that currently surpass the capabilities of AI tools (Levy-Nadav et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR36); Zhai, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR77)). Therefore, to strengthen the research-based understanding of GenAI integration in education, this study will empirically examine how teachers perceive the use of GenAI tools. Additionally, it will analyze the teaching and learning activities implemented by teachers to assess whether they indicate significant pedagogical changes resulting from the use of these tools.

### 1.1 Literature review

As detailed previously, it appears that diverse learning activities are likely to be altered or redefined due to the use of GenAI tools. One well-known framework that examines learning activities in terms of pedagogical changes resulting from technological integration is the SAMR framework of Substitution, Augmentation, Modification, and Redefinition (Puentedura, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR47)). Developed in 2006, this framework aims to encourage teachers and instructional designers to enhance the quality of education by providing a framework for creating optimal learning experiences through the integration of technological means (Romrell et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR53)). The SAMR framework is based on Bloom’s taxonomy, categorizing levels of technology integration in education from basic to advanced (Paulauskaite-Taraseviciene et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR46)). The framework presents four levels of integration in the teaching-learning process, ranging from traditional learning methods - where technology merely serves as a tool without altering the learning activity, to the redefinition of learning - where technology enables new capabilities that were previously impossible (Hamilton et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR22); Paulauskaite-Taraseviciene et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR46)). In the Substitution level technology acts as a direct substitute for existing learning activities without functional change; in the Augmentation level technology is a direct substitute with functional improvements; in the Modification level technology allows for significant pedagogical redesign; and in the Redefinition level technology enables designing learning activities that could not be conducted without it (Puentedura, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR49)).

Learning activities at the initial levels, Substitution and Augmentation, aim to enhance learning, whereas those at the higher levels, Modification and Redefinition, aim to significantly transform learning processes (Puentedura, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR48)). Puentedura further argues that the framework encourages teachers to “move up” from lower to higher levels of teaching with technology, which can significantly enhance teaching-learning processes (Hamilton et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR22)(. He notes that integrating technological means in education brings pedagogical and managerial challenges, and thus, at the lower levels of the framework, technology integration may not be worth the learning gains. However, at the higher levels of the framework, technology becomes an integral part of the learning activity design and allows for significant and fundamental innovation in educational work, making its integration worthwhile despite the accompanying challenges (Romrell et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR53)).

Numerous studies have utilized the SAMR framework to analyze teaching and learning activities across different age groups (Bicalho et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR7); Blau et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR8); Crompton & Burke, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR17); Drugova et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR18); Kelsch & Wang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR31); Nair & Chuan, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR42), Schwartz & Blau, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR59)). Some of these studies identified learning activities that aligned with all levels of the framework with a particular emphasis on the middle levels - Augmentation and Modification (Shamir-Inbal & Blau, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR61)). Crompton and Burke ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR17)) conducted a systematic review of research analyzing student learning activities in primary and secondary schools using the SAMR framework and found that activities were identified across all levels of the framework. Augmentation was the most prominent level, followed by Modification and Redefinition, while Substitution was observed in the fewest number of activities. Other studies found that most of the activities examined were at the Augmentation level, with only a few reaching the levels of Modification and Redefinition (Avdiel & Blau, [2025b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR4); Hilton, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR24); Miles, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR41); Paulauskaite-Taraseviciene et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR46), Shamir-Inbal et al., [2025a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR63)).

Currently, there is a gap in the research literature due to the lack of empirical examination of actual teaching-learning activities that integrate GenAI tools, despite the evident advanced capabilities of these tools, which make them potentially impactful in both teaching and learning contexts. In instructional settings, they can assist teachers in lesson planning, creating teaching materials and learning tasks, and adapting content differentiated to students’ levels (Ausat et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR2); Tlili et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR70); Trust et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR71)). In classroom learning contexts, these tools can support independent student use, facilitate engaging and relevant learning activities, and enhance active learning within the classroom (Ifelebuegu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR26); Kasneci et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR29); Mhlanga, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR40); Sok & Heng, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR65)). Therefore, it is reasonable to assume that the use of AI tools can constitute significant innovation and transform teaching and learning methods (Su et al. [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR66); Ng et al. [2021b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR43)), thus making their integration meaningful.

Despite the widespread use of the SAMR framework, its critics argue that the framework offers insufficient details on how to implement it when analyzing teaching-learning activities. This leads to an inconsistent classification of such activities (Blundell et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR9); Nair & Chuan, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR42)), a lack of consideration for contextual factors, such as pedagogical goals, institutional support, and digital literacy, along with an emphasis on teachers’ practices over learning (Hamilton et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR22)). Despite the criticism, a comprehensive review of studies using this theoretical framework indicates that its application in research frequently focuses on teaching-learning activities (Avdiel & Blau, [2025a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR3); Blundell et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR9)). Furthermore, the framework has proven highly useful for illustrating the extent and level of technology use in achieving educational goals (Hilton, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR24); Shamir-Inbal et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR62)).

The present study seeks to address this gap in the existing literature by examining teaching-learning activities that teachers undertake with GenAI tools through the lens of the SAMR framework, assessing whether and how these activities are characterized by minor changes (Substitution, Augmentation) or substantial transformations (Modification, Redefinition). This analysis aims to yield a deeper understanding of how this technology can be leveraged and whether it facilitates meaningful pedagogical change. Such insights will assist policymakers and educators in making informed decisions regarding the effective implementation of GenAI, while also contributing to and enriching the existing research literature on the subject.

2 Research objectives and questions
-----------------------------------

The study aims to examine the perspectives of teachers who naturally and independently initiate the use of GenAI tools in their teaching, as well as the role of these tools in promoting changes in pedagogical design and the implementation of teaching–learning processes in secondary schools.

The research questions are:

1. 1.
   
   What are the **teachers’ perspectives** regarding the pedagogical uses of **GenAI tools** for lesson planning and in classroom instruction?
2. 2.
   
   To what extent, in terms of the SAMR framework’s levels of technology integration, does teachers’ actual **integration of GenAI tools in pedagogical design of teaching-learning processes** in secondary schools realize the added value of these technologies?

### 2.1 Methodology

The research employed a qualitative methodology focused on understanding educational phenomena through the perspectives and practices of those who have directly experienced them. Central to this approach were semi-structured interviews with key participants engaged in professional teaching activities, enabling the study to capture the nuanced dimensions of pedagogical practice by amplifying practitioners’ voices. Accordingly, the study adopted a phenomenological qualitative research design with a multi-source approach, aimed at capturing teachers’ lived experiences and meanings associated with the integration of GenAI into teaching–learning contexts. The design triangulates inductive thematic analysis of semi-structured interview data with framework-guided qualitative analysis of teaching–learning activities. Specifically, the analytical process involved multi-stage coding, initial open codes were developed into overarching themes, while the SAMR framework served as a conceptual lens to categorize and evaluate the levels of pedagogical shift. This integrated, multi-source approach enabled an in-depth and holistic examination of pedagogical practices and processes as experienced and enacted by teachers within their authentic educational contexts (Marshall & Rossman, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR38)).

### 2.2 Participants

The study involved 17 secondary-school teachers who had naturally and independently initiated the use of GenAI tools in their teaching-learning practices prior to data collection. These teachers from across Israel taught a variety of subjects and were recruited through open invitations posted in large-scale professional social media groups related to integration of AI in education (e.g., Facebook and WhatsApp). Participant recruitment for the study took place in the summer of 2023, approximately seven months after the release of ChatGPT 3.5. Consistent with Rogers’ ([2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR52)) diffusion of innovations theory, which conceptualizes adoption as a staged and time-dependent process rather than a fixed timeframe, the selected period was intended to allow users to progress beyond initial exposure toward early stages of implementation. Accordingly, this timeframe provided potential users with sufficient opportunity to familiarize themselves with the tool and to explore its capabilities, as well as those of similar GenAI applications. The purpose of selecting this sample was to reach the early adopters of GenAI technology within the broader and diverse community of teachers. The participants who responded to the invitation represented an online sample. Subsequently, they were asked to refer additional teachers who use AI in their classroom, resulting in a “snowball” sampling approach. The goal of this approach was to broaden the sample and reach a larger and more diverse group of teachers who are not active on social media but align with the study’s criteria. Among the participants, ten were English (as a second language) teachers, and seven were social science teachers (civics, history, psychology, etc.). Six of the teachers were ICT coordinators at their schools or district/county-wide ICT trainers. The majority of the participating teachers (14 out of 17) adopted GenAI technology immediately upon its release. Nearly all participants (*N* = 16) identified themselves as having prior experience integrating technological tools into their teaching, and most (*N* = 12) reported that they learned about GenAI independently, without any formal training by the Ministry of Education. Almost all of them (*N* = 16) participated in informal AI training or were members of various social media groups on the subject. All participants indicated that their schools had the technological equipment necessary for working with GenAI tools. Over half of the teachers (*N* = 9) taught in schools where the students’ socioeconomic status was medium to high. Furthermore, teachers were asked about the level of support from school administration for integrating GenAI tools. A significant number (*N* = 12) stated that the administration encouraged the use of these tools, while a minority (*N* = 5) felt that the school administration did not address the issue.

### 2.3 Research tools

To gather information about teachers’ perspectives regarding the use of GenAI tools in the pedagogical design of teaching-learning processes, 17 semi-structured interviews were conducted approximately seven months after the beginning of the GenAI spread. The interview questions focused on several aspects, including teachers’ experiences integrating technological tools in teaching (Example question: “Based on your experience, explain how you think the teacher’s role may change as a result of using GenAI.“) and their perceptions on practices of the GenAI integration in their teaching (“What do you see as the main opportunities and challenges of using GenAI tools in teaching?“), learning (How significant do you think mediation is when using GenAI tools with students?), and assessment processes (“To what extent, given students’ access to GenAI tools, does assigning homework remain relevant?”). The full interview protocol is included in Appendix 1.

Teachers also presented lesson plans they had designed, detailed assessment tasks created with GenAI tools, and described learning activities conducted with students in the classroom using various tools. These activities (*N* = 79), which provided concrete examples of implementation, were analyzed top-down and categorized according to the SAMR framework.

In addition to the activities reported by teachers during the interviews, they were asked to send written samples of GenAI-enhanced teaching-learning activities they designed and conducted with students in the classroom. The purpose of this request was to conduct triangulation to validate and strengthen the findings from the interviews. Through this process, 12 teaching-learning activities were collected, analyzed, and categorized according to the SAMR framework. In total, 91 teaching-learning activities were analyzed.

### 2.4 Research procedure and analysis

The participants’ interviews lasted between 30 and 60 min and were conducted online via Zoom videoconferencing. Following the interviews, a thematic analysis was conducted using a bottom-up qualitative categorization process that included main categories and subcategories, into which teachers’ perspectives regarding the use of GenAI tools were classified. The pedagogical design of teaching-learning activities was analyzed top-down according to the SAMR framework (Puentedura, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR47)) discussed in the literature review. It is noteworthy that the SAMR framework is widely accepted for evaluating teaching-learning activities in the field of education and has been used extensively in various studies examining different technologies across different populations (Bicalho et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR7); Crompton & Burke, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR17); Drugova et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR18); Kelsch & Wang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR31); Nair & Chuan, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR42)). Nevertheless, as mentioned in the literature review, there is criticism regarding the lack of clarity and consistency in the classification of the different framework levels. Therefore, we used a classification similar to recent studies that conducted analyses based on the SAMR framework in an educational context (Paulauskaite-Taraseviciene et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR46)) and two studies that reported high inter-rater reliability of such coding (Schwartz et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR60); Shamir-Inbal & Blau, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR61)). In accordance with the recommendations of Blundell et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR9)), we present here the criteria used to determine the different levels of the SAMR framework. In this study, the pedagogical design of teaching-learning activities was classified as following: (1) *Substitution* – activities where technology provides a substitute for existing learning activities without functional change; for example, creating a table of key concepts for a lesson using ChatGPT instead of Word processor. There is no added value obtained from integrating the technological tool; (2) *Augmentation* – activities where technology provides a positive substitute for other learning activities with functional improvements; for example, using a GenAI chatbot to retrieve information instead of relying on websites such as Google or Wikipedia. The technology enhances or facilitates the instructional processes; (3) *Modification* – activities where technology allows for significant redesign of teaching-learning methods. Activities are performed differently and significantly improved due to the use of technology; for example, teachers who engage in brainstorming with a GenAI chatbot to design new and diverse lessons, assessment tasks, and rubrics; and (4) *Redefinition* – where technology enables the creation of entirely new tasks that could not have been done without the technology integration; for example, students independently conversing with a specialized chatbot on a specific academic subject, using it to learn and deepen their understanding of the material.

The unit of analysis used for coding was a statement (not a participant). The analysis was non-exclusive, meaning that the same statement could be coded into different subcategories. For example, the statement “Two of my students use ChatGPT to personally enhance their writing skills. The chat provides them with focused and quick feedback.” (Teacher 8) was coded as both *personalized and differentiated learning* and *active learning*, since it was an example of an activity that students performed individually without teacher assistance, as well as an activity where students could be active in class.

To assess the inter-rater reliability of coding, 25% of the statements were re-analyzed by a second rater familiar with the research topic and qualitative methods and trained by the first rater. In summary, a very high level of agreement was achieved between the two raters - Cohen’s Kappa = 0.91.

The examples for each of the four levels of the SAMR framework include tasks in which teachers used GenAI as a tool for the pedagogical design of *teaching* activities either in lesson preparations or classroom instruction, as well as pedagogical design of *learning* activities in which students themselves were required to use these tools. 4 Discussion
------------

### 4.1 Teachers’ perspectives of GenAI in education

The first research question aimed to examine teachers’ perspectives regarding the pedagogical uses of GenAI tools for lesson planning and in classroom instruction. As presented above, the participants in the study adopted GenAI technology quite early and used it naturally and independently without clear guidelines from the Ministry of Education. This initial use by the teachers seems to have led them to reflect on how they need to adapt themselves and their work in light of the new technology. The teachers note **that this is a phase characterized by great “uncertainty”** and they are unable to predict where the field will move in the future. This assertion is also presented by Warr et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR73)), who explains that there is no predetermined future as to how AI will play out in our world. Alongside this state of uncertainty, teachers mention that **many educators are apprehensive about the new situation** and, as a result, appear reluctant to use GenAI tools in their work. Given this, policymakers should develop a structured teacher training program in this field to equip educators with the confidence to navigate the new reality, provide opportunities to experiment with the tools and understand their capabilities, and thereby encourage more teachers to integrate these tools into their practice confidently and responsibly rather than avoid their use. Nevertheless, teachers emphasize their belief that this field should be incorporated into the curriculum, as it **prepares students for the future job market**. As suggested in previous studies (Ayanwale et al. [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR5); Lee et al. [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR34); Ng et al. [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR43)), it appears that knowledge and proficiency in this field, which could become highly significant in all our lives, will help students integrate successfully and quickly into society.

Despite the fact that using GenAI tools is new for teachers, they believe that these tools will **significantly change the field of education**. In the research literature, there are numerous references to the potential of GenAI tools to bring about significant changes in the field of education (Baidoo-Anu and Owusu Ansah [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR6); Chen et al. [2020a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR14), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR15); Henriksen et al. [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR23); Krstić et al. [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR32); Lameras and Arnab [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR33)). These references are mostly based on researchers’ forecasts rather than empirical studies. The teachers in this study explain that the introduction of GenAI tools into education represents a substantial change and might even be perceived as a revolution in education, influencing many aspects related to teachers’ work and teaching methods in general. In terms of impact on teaching issues, teachers note that they do not believe GenAI tools will replace teachers, but rather, **the role of the teacher will evolve** into that of a guide, leader, and facilitator rather than merely a transmitter of knowledge. This belief is also shared by other researchers who argue that the tools should be used to enhance teaching-learning, not as a substitute for teachers (Ausat et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR2); Lameras & Arnab, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR33); Zhai, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR78)). In light of the changing role of the teacher and the technological advancements in the field, the teachers explain that there is a need to integrate this subject into professional teacher training. It seems that the future of teaching will include a combination of human abilities inherent to teachers, along with AI tools that enhance their various capabilities, thus resulting in a more effective and beneficial education system for both teachers and students (Chan & Tsi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR12); Ifelebuegu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR26); Tajik & Tajik, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR67)).

Another significant change that may occur due to the introduction of GenAI tools in education is related to **pedagogy and teaching practices**. The participating teachers reported that GenAI tools could help them generate lesson ideas, prepare background materials, enrich their content, and build lessons and assessment tasks more quickly and easily than before. Zhai ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR78)) explains that teachers can use the results obtained from the tools as a starting point for their work, integrating their own knowledge with the provided information to make the results relevant for themselves and their students. Furthermore, as suggested by other researchers (Tajik & Tajik, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR67); Trust et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR71)) and found in this study, with the help of generative tools teachers can develop new curricula or improve existing ones, as well as prepare diverse lesson plans and learning activities. Consistent with the claim of Tlili et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR70)), it appears that the use of GenAI tools could revolutionize lesson planning and assist teachers in preparing for classes in innovative ways. Despite the advanced capabilities of these tools, teachers explain that they still need to **review all outputs**, edit the content, and tailor it to their specific needs. The results obtained from generative tools should serve as a starting point for teachers, upon which they add their knowledge and adapt the content to their needs (Zhai, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR78)).

Regarding learning issues, according to the teachers the primary use of these tools in classrooms is **preferable with advanced students**. It appears that due to the initial stage of use and the required technical means, teachers choose to conduct the first trial of these tools within such an audience. Therefore, it is important to ensure that the use of GenAI tools is not limited to an elite group of students but is accessible to the entire student population to prevent widening the *digital divide* among students (Ifelebuegu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR26)). The empowering capabilities of GenAI tools may aggravate the digital divide between students with access to technological resources and those without, potentially leaving the latter behind (Ifelebuegu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR26)). A report on AI and the future of work, published by the International Monetary Fund in January 2024 (Cazzaniga et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR11)), explains that AI could profoundly transform the global economy, potentially representing a new type of industrial revolution. The report indicates that AI might exacerbate income differences and inequality within countries, as some workers will be able to leverage it to increase their productivity and income, while others who fail to do so will fall behind, thus widening the digital divide among workers. All the teachers who participated in the study reported having technological means at their schools that allow them to use AI tools with their students. In more than half of the cases innovative teachers participating in this study worked with students from medium to high socioeconomic backgrounds. Additionally, 70% of the teachers reported that the school administration supports and encourages the use of innovative technological tools, such as AI tools. These factors illustrate the potential creation of a digital divide between students who lack access to technological resources and appropriate guidance for their use and students from schools that promote innovation and provide a supportive social environment. To bridge the digital divide, AI education should be systematically integrated into national teacher training programs as previously noted (Sayag et al., [2026](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR58); Rodrig et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR51)). The Ministry of Education must also ensure that all schools have the infrastructure required for effective GenAI adoption. Moreover, a comprehensive national strategy should be developed to implement AI education for all students and teachers, with particular emphasis on those from underprivileged backgrounds. Education authorities bear a paramount social responsibility to leverage this powerful technology to reduce, rather than exacerbate, existing inequalities.

When teachers introduce GenAI tools to their classrooms, they emphasize the utmost importance of guiding students on how to use these tools and the specific ways they expect students to utilize them. It appears that the initial use of these tools requires teacher **guidance and mediation**. The teachers in the study believe they must present the tools to students and ensure they use them correctly and appropriately [“It is important that students know how to work with the tools in a controlled manner, while a teacher supervises them to ensure they are using them correctly.” (Teacher 2)]. The findings of this study, along with previous research (Rudolph et al. [2023b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR55); Tajik and Tajik [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR67)), suggest that students can greatly benefit from using GenAI tools, but they need guidance on how to work with them. An example of this can be found in the teachers’ belief that there is **no more room for traditional homework assignments**. Similar to claims made in previous research (Henriksen et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR23)), now that GenAI tools are accessible to all students, the requirement to complete homework independently is no longer practical. When assignments are done at home rather than in the classroom, the teacher has no control over the use of GenAI tools, making it impossible to distinguish between the students’ work and that of the GenAI. Therefore, it is recommended that teachers consider implementing formative rather than summative assessment by designing activities that require students to engage in group discussions, conduct presentations, and work collaboratively as a team (Shamir-Inbal et al., [2025a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR63); Sok & Heng, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR65)).

Another reason these tools require teacher mediation is that they pose ethical issues and sometimes present incorrect information, making teacher guidance and leadership essential in the process at this early stage. The teachers in this study emphasized the importance of discussing the responsible and ethical use of the tools with students in classroom settings. In educational contexts, these ethical challenges are compounded by the societal biases embedded in LLM outputs, often reflecting English-dominant training data that may marginalize non-Western perspectives, perpetuate stereotypes, and reinforce existing inequalities (Kalhor et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR28); Qu & Wang, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR50); Yusuf et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR76)). Teachers therefore play a critical role in guiding students to critically evaluate GenAI-generated content, recognize and question embedded biases, verify information through multiple sources, and apply responsible use practices. Importantly, this mediating role often entails additional instructional responsibilities for teachers; however, such work is essential for ensuring meaningful, ethical, and pedagogically sound integration of GenAI tools in educational contexts. Such efforts not only safeguard learners from potential harm, but also foster digital literacy and ethical awareness, which are competencies essential for navigating an AI-integrated future (Lee et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR34)). Thus, only a few teachers **encourage their students** to use these tools independently. It seems that teachers who have not undergone formal training in using these tools and themselves feel that this is an initial stage of uncertainty, as mentioned above, have not yet formed clear guidelines on how students should use the tools independently.

Overall, the findings indicate that teachers view GenAI as a valuable instructional resource that supports students’ readiness for future learning and work contexts, while also emphasizing the need for cautious and mediated use due to initial uncertainty regarding reliability and bias. These perspectives point to an evolving instructional role in which teachers increasingly act as guides, and they provide an important context for the subsequent analysis of implemented practices through the SAMR framework.

### 4.2 Integrating GenAI into teaching-learning activities

The second research question aimed to investigate to what extent, in terms of the SAMR framework’s levels of technology integration, does teachers’ actual integration of GenAI tools in pedagogical design of teaching-learning processes realize the added value of these technologies. The study’s findings regarding the teacher’s pedagogical design of GenAI teaching-learning activities indicate that they make extensive use of these tools in their work. Data analysis shows that most activities were mapped to the middle levels of the SAMR framework: Augmentation (43) and Modification (21), with a smaller portion of activities at the extreme levels: Redefinition (14) and Substitution (13). Previous studies examining the integration of new technologies in education have shown that, unlike this bell-shaped distribution, most teaching-learning activities examined shortly after the introduction of new technology align with the lower levels of the framework, with only a few reaching the higher levels of Modification and Redefinition (Hilton, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR24); Paulauskaite-Taraseviciene et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR46)). The goal of the SAMR framework is to encourage teachers to “move up the pedagogical ladder” and implement higher levels of technology integration in teaching, which can make a more significant contribution to enhancing teaching-learning processes (Hamilton et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR22)(. Surprisingly, despite the relatively recent integration of GenAI tools in education, only about seven months after the spread of this technology a considerable number of activities were found to align with the framework’s higher levels (Modification and Redefinition). These activities indicate the beginning of a significant change in the nature of the teachers’ work or the nature of teaching and learning activities, even allowing for their redefinition; for example, performing learning activities that were previously impossible, such as receiving multiple perspectives from “characters” with different backgrounds, as shown in Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#Tab2). The substantial execution of activities at the higher levels of the SAMR framework corresponds with the fact that teachers perceive the use of these tools as significant and potentially revolutionary for the education system.

An in-depth mapping of pedagogical design of teaching-learning activities (see Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#Fig2)) reveals that the learning activities teachers conduct with students in the classroom are almost equally distributed across all levels of the SAMR framework. In contrast, most teaching activities are classified under the Augmentation level, with only a smaller portion categorized at other levels. Reported pedagogical design of teaching activities, such as lesson preparation and assessment tasks form, were primarily mapped to the Augmentation level, where GenAI tools enhance and improve teachers’ work without dramatically changing traditional methods. As previous studies have suggested (Bura & Myakala, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR10); Kaushik et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR30); Sain et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR57)), teachers explain that using GenAI tools allows them to enhance their capabilities, reduce the time spent on tasks, and prepare assessment tasks more easily and efficiently than before. Teachers seem to use these tools as “teaching assistants” that help and streamline their work. This approach aligns with teachers’ perception that their role will evolve to include a deep familiarity with GenAI applications and the range of possibilities they offer. Therefore, as Zhai ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR78)) suggests, the Ministry of Education should empower teachers to develop their expertise and advance professionally by providing specialized courses and training programs. This initiative would help alleviate their workload while enhancing their teaching practices and workflow efficiency.

While this use of tools is highly substantial for their work and may enable teachers to be more efficient and significantly reduce their sense of burnout (Huang et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR25); Krstić et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR32)), it does not predominantly reach the higher levels of the SAMR framework. However, when teachers integrate GenAI tools into classroom lessons, they perform activities that align with all levels of the framework. The various activities, particularly those classified at the higher levels of Modification and Redefinition, include innovative tasks significantly different from those done before using GenAI tools. The high number of learning activities at the higher levels of the framework suggests that integrating GenAI tools in classroom learning activities offers substantial added value for students. In light of these findings, it is imperative that more teachers integrate these tools into learning, enabling students to engage in enriching tasks that were not feasible before the adoption of GenAI tools. This finding, that the pedagogical design of teaching activities is mainly classified at the lower levels of the framework, whereas classroom activities span all levels, corresponds with previous research findings (Wahyuni et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR72); Wijaya et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR74)). For example, a study examining the integration of various technological tools, such as collaborative Google tools, presentations, and “Kahoot” in language teaching (Wahyuni et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR72)), found no pedagogical design teaching activities that matched the Modification and Redefinition levels, but many activities that corresponded to the lower levels. In contrast, learning activities using various technological tools were classified across all SAMR framework levels. Another study, investigating the integration of technological tools in teaching during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, found that the vast majority of pedagogical design teaching activities were categorized under Augmentation, with only a few at higher levels, while learning activities spanned all levels of the framework (Wijaya et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR74)).

All participants reported integrating technological tools into their teaching even prior to the advent of GenAI in education, and many adopted GenAI tools immediately upon their availability, suggesting that they can be characterized as early adopters of innovative technologies. This pattern indicates that the participating teachers possessed relatively high levels of digital competence, aligning with key dimensions outlined in the Digital Competence of Educators (DigCompEdu, European Commission, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR19)) framework, such as professional engagement, pedagogical use of digital technologies, and experimentation with innovative tools. Such competence, combined with the advanced affordances of GenAI tools, appears to have supported teachers’ ability to design learning activities at the higher levels of the SAMR framework, where technology enables pedagogical redesign and transformation of teaching–learning processes (Sayag et al., [2026](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR58)). Moreover, the study’s findings suggest that integrating GenAI tools into education has enabled teachers, even in the early stages, to “leap” to the higher levels of the SAMR framework, a phenomenon not typically observed in studies examining the integration of new technologies in education. This finding should be leveraged by education policymakers to reinforce the importance of integrating AI education into the school system and to emphasize the significance of professional development for teachers in this field, alongside the strengthening of their digital competencies (Sayag et al., [2026](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR58); Rodrig et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR51)).

Despite the focus on the higher levels of the SAMR framework, it is important to acknowledge the significance of using innovative technological tools at the lower levels as well. For instance, Hilton ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13925-1#ref-CR24)) argues that integrating technological tools into teaching, even at the lower levels of the framework, enhances student engagement and holds their attention longer compared to traditional teaching methods. Therefore, the value of teaching activities that incorporate GenAI tools, even at the lower levels of SAMR framework, should not be underestimated.

5 Conclusions, implications, limitations, and future directions
---------------------------------------------------------------

This study examined teachers’ perspectives on the integration of GenAI tools that was naturally and independently initiated by teachers, as well as the learning activities they designed using GenAI and the added value of these tools for pedagogical design. This research provides a unique insight into the pedagogical integration of GenAI tools, offering concrete examples from real-world classroom settings, and is pioneering in applying the SAMR framework to the analysis of teaching-learning activities that integrate GenAI technologies. Our findings indicate that teachers perceive the introduction of GenAI tools in education as a significant change that impacts learning processes, pedagogical practices, and potentially transforms the teacher’s role into that of a guide and facilitator. Despite the current uncertainties, teachers believe that using these tools is essential for preparing students for the future and that teachers must mediate their use. Thus, policymakers should support teachers in transforming their instructional practices by providing the necessary technological infrastructure for integrating GenAI tools and offering professional development programs that enhance teachers’ digital competence. Teacher training should provide educators with practical knowledge of GenAI tools, enabling them to mediate their use effectively in the classroom. Such programs should integrate technology-enhanced pedagogical strategies that support innovative and curriculum-aligned instruction. Additionally, they must address the ethical implications of AI, including bias, privacy, and responsible use, to ensure that teachers can guide students in applying these tools thoughtfully and safely. In terms of tool usage, it appears that at this stage most teachers use them with advanced students. This situation could widen the *digital divide* between students with access to technological resources and those without, potentially leaving the latter behind. Therefore, we recommend that education policymakers ensure equitable access to GenAI tools by allocating resources to underserved schools and offering tailored professional development for their teachers. They should also establish clear guidelines and design inclusive curricula to introduce all students to this emerging field.

Analysis of the teaching-learning activities conducted by teachers using GenAI tools shows that, unlike previous studies on the integration of new technologies, a relatively high number of activities align with the higher levels of the SAMR framework (Modification and Redefinition). These activities indicate the beginning of a significant transformation in the nature of teaching-learning activities, even allowing for their redefinition. Therefore, GenAI is likely to significantly enhance teaching-learning processes and even drive substantial pedagogical change. Unlike learning activities, which seem to be distributed across all levels of the framework, most teaching activities were classified under *Augmentation*, as teachers primarily used the tools as teaching assistants. It seems that these tools can greatly improve teachers’ work, yet at this stage they do not serve as indicators of pedagogical transformation. Hence, it is essential to provide all teachers with training on effectively using GenAI tools as teaching aids to improve and streamline their work and to enable them to develop the expertise required to progress toward the higher levels of GenAI integration. Teachers should also familiarize themselves with the study’s findings and accordingly adopt GenAI tools with an understanding of their advanced capabilities and the pedagogical opportunities they offer.

The study participants were early adopters of the technology; therefore, they may not fully represent the broader teacher population in terms of their abilities, interest in the field, and willingness to integrate the tools. Future studies should examine a diverse group of teachers with different skill sets and knowledge levels. Moreover, the findings were collected in one time point. Future study may employ a longitudinal research design to allow for a thorough examination of whether teachers’ perspectives and the variety of teaching-learning activities observed are related to the initial enthusiasm of the new tools. Finally, the data collection was based on the qualitative research paradigm. As integration of GenAI in formal education progresses, we recommend crosschecking qualitative data with field experiments in order to gain a broader and more accurate understanding of the tasks conducted in classrooms.

### Appendix

1. 1.
   
   Tell me about yourself—what age groups do you teach, and in what type of educational setting?
2. 2.
   
   Describe your overall teaching experience (number of years) and your experience integrating technological tools in your teaching.
3. 3.
   
   Please provide a few examples of how you have used technological tools in your instruction.
4. 4.
   
   Have you participated in any training or professional development related to the integration of GenAI tools in education? If so, what was the scope and content of the training? If not— could you share why?
   
   * ○ (For school ICT coordinators: Have you led training sessions for others?)
   * ○ Are you part of any social media groups or forums related to GenAI in education? If so, which ones?
5. 5.
   
   In your opinion, why is it important to integrate GenAI tools into teaching? Please provide examples.
6. 6.
   
   When and how did you start integrating GenAI tools in your teaching practice?
7. 7.
   
   Are other teachers in your school or network also using GenAI tools? If so, do you share best practices and experiences with them?
8. 8.
   
   Does your school’s technological infrastructure support the effective use of GenAI tools? Please provide an example.
9. 9.
   
   Is there administrative support for the use of GenAI tools? If yes, how is that support expressed? If not, how is your use of the tools perceived by colleagues?
10. 10.
    
    Integration of GenAI tools in practice.
    
    1. a
       
       In your planning and preparation:
       
       * How do you use GenAI tools in your routine teaching work. Please provide examples.
       * How do you use GenAI for lesson planning?
       * Do you feel that GenAI tools assist you in lesson preparation? Why or why not?
       * How do you use GenAI tools for assessing student work?
       * Do you revise or modify the suggestions produced by GenAI tools, or do you use them as is in your teaching? Why?
       * What do you see as the main opportunities and challenges of using GenAI tools in teaching? What should teachers be especially mindful of when using these tools in the classroom?
       * Based on your experience, explain how you think the teacher’s role may change as a result of using GenAI.
    2. b
       
       In classroom use with students:
       
       * How do you integrate GenAI tools into classroom activities? (Please provide examples.)
       * Do students work in groups? If so, how are groups formed: by skill level, topic, interest, or student choice?
       * How do students actually use GenAI tools - via smartphones, computers, tablets?
       * To what extent, given students’ access to GenAI tools, does assigning homework remain relevant?
    3. c
       
       Instructions and expectations:
       
       * What instructions did you give to students for completing the various tasks?
       * How significant do you think mediation is when using GenAI tools with students?
       * During students’ use of GenAI tools, which competencies did you aim for students to apply and which did they actually use?
       * Do you think using GenAI tools requires unfamiliar competencies for students? If so, which ones?
       * If new competencies were required, how did you support students in developing them?
       * Did students enjoy or show curiosity in using GenAI tools?
       * To what extent did students find the tasks difficult or easy? Please explain.
11. 11.
    
    How do students respond to learning activities involving GenAI? What feedback do you receive from them? What challenges do they report?
12. 12.
    
    What risks or concerns do you associate with using GenAI tools in the classroom?
13. 13.
    
    In what ways does using GenAI tools enable different forms of teaching from what you are used to? Can you share examples you have implemented or heard from colleagues?
14. 14.
    
    Would you recommend that other teachers use GenAI tools in their work? Why or why not?
15. 15.
    
    Have you encountered ethical issues such as bias or privacy concerns in educational use of GenAI tools? If so, what were they and how did you address them?
