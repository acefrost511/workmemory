Abstract
--------

Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools are becoming increasingly popular in education, providing teachers with new opportunities to enhance student learning experience and build upon existing teaching practices. This study employs a co-design approach to develop AI-integrated learning materials and explore their implementation in elementary classrooms. In collaboration with researchers, twenty-five in-service teachers co-designed engaging, age-appropriate lesson plans aligned with the national elementary curriculum and adaptable to diverse classroom needs. Qualitative analysis of teachers’ focus groups (n = 25) highlighted the co-design approach's role in empowering teachers, fostering a sense of ownership, encouraging knowledge-sharing, and promoting collaboration and enthusiasm. A pilot study conducted in four elementary classrooms with 62 students evaluated the AI-integrated lesson plans and materials through quantitative survey data (n = 62). Findings indicate that students found the AI-supported learning experience enjoyable, engaging, and meaningful, particularly in deepening their understanding of literacy concepts. This paper presents the co-designed materials and provides insights for practitioners and researchers on the future of AI-powered education, offering potential directions for further research.

Introduction
------------

Artificial intelligence (AI) refers to the field of study that combines machine learning, algorithm production, and natural language processing to perform tasks similar to human learning and decision-making (Akgun & Greenhow, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR1); Chiu, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR5)). Recent developments in AI offer numerous educational opportunities by creating innovative and engaging learning experiences for teachers and students. According to the latest research by the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR28)), many countries have developed national policies prioritising the inclusion of AI in school education (Schiff, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR35)). AI technology is becoming increasingly common in education, improving teaching and learning experiences in many ways. Still, additional research is needed on how AI could be integrated into the school curriculum, allowing students to experience and interact with many different AI resources and applications (Kaspersen et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR18)).

The ongoing evolution of AI requires a deeper understanding of the AI’s potential for teachers and students to bridge skill gaps and create new opportunities for the labour market (Casal-Otero et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR3)). In that sense, AI literacy has a significant role in education in preventing the challenges raised in the new, transforming AI-driven digital environment (Cope & Kalantzis, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR6); Kasinidou, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR17)). Various AI applications could contribute to developing many students’ skills, such as problem-solving, critical thinking, and writing skills, significantly impacting students’ growth (Irfan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR16)). Therefore, nowadays, promoting AI literacy is crucial for teachers and students to be knowledgeable and skilled in how AI can be utilised effectively in their future personal and professional lives (Velander et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR41)).

Research studies, mainly in the last decade, pointed out the multiple benefits of integrating AI into educational settings. They emphasised the potential of AI in personalising learning experiences, student motivation and engagement, and teacher support (Akgun & Greenhow, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR1); Chiu, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR5)). Advocates of AI highlight the added value of AI-driven applications for learning and suggest that those applications could create innovative learning experiences for students, enriching the learning environment in general (Seldon & Abidoye, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR36)). A variety of AI tools can leverage the existing teaching practices and methods to address different students’ learning needs and improve their overall learning performance (Holmes et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR14); Zawacki-Richter et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR46)).

In this new AI-driven reality, teachers’ roles must be reconsidered, considering the recent technological advancements and ongoing societal changes and evolutions. This new environment creates challenges and concerns for educators since they need to think about their new role in the classroom and align their professional responsibilities with the AI concept (Fenwick, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR9)). Also, teachers are concerned about the proper use of AI in the school environment and its inclusion in the curriculum in a way that could benefit students (Zhai et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR47)).

While there are many advantages to introducing AI into education, we must consider that this research area is still in the experimental stage and that additional research is required (Dai et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR7)). There is also a need to create a concrete international plan on how AI can be included in curriculum development to enrich teaching practices and learning experiences (Wong et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR43)). Our study uses the co-design approach (Muller & Kuhn, [1993](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR27)), involving teachers and researchers in developing various educational materials that integrate AI and are tailored for elementary students. In this “co-design” process, teachers, in collaboration with researchers, are involved in the design of learning material to be implemented in the classroom. The process enabled participants to exchange practices, discuss the challenges and create innovative resources aligned with elementary education standards.

The study aimed to evaluate the co-design method as a practice for developing AI-integrated lesson plans in elementary education and, secondly, to receive insights from teachers and students regarding their experience with the co-design procedure and how they encountered the course. These insights aim to inform future curricula strategies for integrating AI into national curricula in elementary education. Specifically, the research aimed to explore the potential of using AI technology in language learning concerning mother language acquisition. Research claims that AI applications could be utilised to enhance and improve the educational process of language skills in many ways (Zulkarnain & Yunus, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR48)). Thus, the AI lesson plans created using the co-design method focused on enhancing the students’ learning experience by understanding texts and increasing interest in language courses.

The two-fold research questions were:

RQ1: How do teachers perceive their involvement in the co-design process of AI-integrated educational materials, particularly regarding challenges faced, strategies employed, and future improvement plans?

RQ2: To what extent do students perceive Artificial Intelligence (AI) as enhancing their learning experience in Greek language classes, particularly in terms of understanding texts, increasing interest, ease of creating images, and perceived knowledge acquisition?

The terms “AI-integrated” and “AI-powered” are used synonymously and interchangeably in the context of this article.

Literature review
-----------------

### Generative AI tools and benefits in education

Generative AI, with its ability to produce seemingly original text and multimedia content from existing data sources, has become a transformative force in today’s world (Roumeliotis & Tselikas, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR32)). As Feuerriegel et al., ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR10), p.111) define it, generative AI is the “computational techniques capable of generating seemingly new, meaningful content such as text, images, or audio from training data”. Among the most prominent interactive generative AI models in natural language processing (NLP) is ChatGPT (Wu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR44)), which can process, understand and generate text that resembles human writing based on user input. Tools like ChatGPT and other related advanced AI systems could contribute to the development of curricula and the design of various education courses (Mogavi et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR26)).

Over the past decade, AI advocates claimed that these technologies could have the potential to transform how knowledge is gained (Akgun & Greenhow, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR1)). Also, AI can potentially change how feedback is given, and assessments are assigned to students since the AI tools can provide personalised and automated feedback, changing how the overall students’ performance is assessed (Lameras & Arnab, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR22)). However, we are still at the beginning of this change. The appearance of AI in school environments impacted the way that instruction is performed by students, introducing new teaching methods based on students’ needs and experiences. The use of AI in the classroom can motivate students, increase their engagement in the learning process and offer innovative interactive experiences that make the learning experience fun and enjoyable (Du Boulay, [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR8)).

Furthermore, AI-powered tools could enable students to develop their critical thinking and digital literacy skills, both significant in today’s digital world. Therefore, their usage for learning purposes is essential (Su et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR39)). According to Spector and Ma ([2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR38)), although AI is still being developed, these tools could develop critical thinking since they enable students to perform different tasks, pass through various stages during learning, and practice innovative tools. Another significant advantage is the personalised instruction that AI can provide for each student based on specific needs and interests. AI can offer teachers data for their students, empowering them to create tailored lesson plans and adopt teaching strategies aligned with student strengths or weaknesses (Su et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR39)). In addition, many AI tools provide personalised feedback highlighting the learning needs of the students in specific areas to help teachers find alternatives and motivate their students (Chen et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR4)). Finally, research conducted on AI (Kazakoff et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR19)) showed that AI can greatly influence elementary students’ reading comprehension and mathematics abilities.

### Impact of AI on skill development

Recent literature claims that using AI can potentially enhance the development of critical thinking and digital skills and improve the student’s learning achievements. A recent large-scale global study (Ravšelj et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR30)) found that students primarily used ChatGPT for brainstorming, summarizing, and research support, appreciating its role in AI literacy and digital skills, but noting its limitations in professional writing, interpersonal communication, and critical thinking. At the same time, AI can increase productivity since it can automate tasks that require time and effort (Zulkarnain & Yunus, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR48)). As Misthou and Paliouras ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR25)) argued, AI in elementary classrooms could boost students’ critical thinking by offering them opportunities for reflection, problem-solving practices and engagement with real-world examples. Implementing AI practices at the school requires moving from conventional practices to an innovative approach, including machine learning concepts, ethical considerations of the use of AI, and privacy issues (Ali et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR2)). In this direction, many European projects, through various activities, promoted AI literacy among educators, young people and school students (Voulgari et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR42)).

Along the same lines, using AI in the early stages of schooling, like in elementary schools, is crucial and requires continuous support, tailored training programs and other professional development opportunities for teachers and practitioners (Peng et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR29)). In addition, research studies in foreign language acquisition highlighted the impact of AI on elementary students’ motivation and engagement (Zulkarnain & Yunus, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR48)). It is now urgent to prepare students for the new AI-driven life and equip them with some necessary skills to address future challenges regarding the use of AI. Thus, the inclusion of AI literacy into the K12 curriculum is now more critical than ever (Misthou & Paliouras, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR25)). Lastly, AI technology has been utilised to assist children with autism in learning STEM skills and improving social-emotional and communication skills, showcasing the potential of AI in supporting diverse learning needs in elementary education (Hughes et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR15)).

### Considerations for AI integration

However, integrating AI into elementary education classrooms raises challenges and ethical considerations. It is essential to be aware of all the challenges that AI brings in case of integration into our educational environments. These challenges or considerations exhibit a degree of similarity across all academic disciplines. Privacy and security issues are crucial, especially when handling children’s data. Ethical solid guidelines and regulations are necessary to protect student data and ensure the proper use of AI tools (Holmes et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR14)). Additionally, over-reliance on AI and technology can impact human interaction and dehumanise education. Balancing the exploitation of AI’s potential with preserving the essential human aspects of teaching and learning is critical (Luckin et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR24)).

Nevertheless, AI tools offer students personalised learning experiences and individualised feedback, potentially transforming education. However, addressing ethical and practical challenges is essential to enhance students’ understanding of the world rather than diminish it. Ongoing research by educators, researchers, and policymakers is necessary to develop efficient and ethical AI systems for elementary schools. There is still a need for more research on making AI education accessible to all learners (Lin & Van Brummelen, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR23)).

### Co-design approach for the development of learning material

The co-design method in educational settings and environments provides a valuable framework for teachers and researchers to promote the sharing of knowledge, fruitful collaboration, and foster innovation. This method emphasises the cooperation and teamwork between teachers with a common goal, in this case, to develop educational material and intervention in the classroom considering the complexities and practical issues of a school environment (Georgiou & Ioannou, [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR12); Lin & Van Brummelen, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR23)). The approach relies on participatory design principles, which ensures that the ultimate co-designed deliverable, due to educators’ collaboration, is both innovative and tailored to the specific needs and strengths of the students (Muller & Kuhn, [1993](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR27)).

During this collaboration, teachers can freely express their opinions and concerns and engage in a co-creation process that will be valuable for their professional careers as educators (Roschelle & Penuel, [2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR31)). Teachers could bring the students’ side to light, while researchers or experts could contribute to applying teachers’ ideas meaningfully and effectively (Sanders & Stappers, [2008](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR34)).

Moreover, teachers can provide researchers with the areas in which AI tools could benefit students, such as providing tailored and instant feedback, analysing student performance data, etc. (Holmes et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR14)). Several studies in this area pointed out the significant role of teachers in this co-design process, as they are experts on the students’ needs and classroom climate and knowledgeable about the curriculum and its objectives (Kosmas & Zaphiris, [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR20), [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR21); Tzortzoglou, Kosmas, & Avraamidou, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR40)). Teachers can choose and propose the most appropriate AI applications or tools for each school subject based on curriculum standards, teaching practices, and students’ specific characteristics and learning needs (Dai et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR7)). The method has been used to develop new curriculum material in science and to prepare various educational materials and resources (Gazulla et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR11); Severance et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR37)).

In a nutshell, the co-design method is valuable for learning as it creates opportunities for collaboration between professional teachers, the sharing of good practices, and the development of educational materials tailored to students. This process also allows teachers to further develop their teaching careers by acquiring new skills and knowledge that will help them. This paper employed the co-design approach as a good practice or method to design an intervention for classrooms that utilises teachers’ valuable insights.

Methods and materials
---------------------

This study employs a co-design approach to develop an AI-powered educational intervention for elementary-level literacy courses (grades 1–6). We conducted a co-design workshop with 25 in-service elementary language teachers. This collaborative process aimed to create learning materials that seamlessly integrate AI tools into the elementary curriculum. We employed a combination of data collection strategies to evaluate the effectiveness of the co-designed lesson plans incorporating AI and teachers’ perspectives on the co-design approach. These included co-designed lesson plans, focus groups, and open-ended student questionnaires.

### Study participants

Twenty-five teachers participated in the co-design workshop and were recruited from an open call to social media. The inclusion criteria were a) they teach literacy courses (in our case, the Greek language and literature) in elementary classrooms (1st grade to sixth), b) they can attend the face-to-face workshop and implement the intervention at a later stage, c) they have basic knowledge of the use of AI tools such as Gemini AI, ChatGPT, etc. Twenty-two participants were female, and three participants were male. Their age ranged from 29 to 53 (M = 39, SD = 7.02). Three researchers were involved in the process. Furthermore, the learning material was piloted with 62 students in four elementary classrooms (1st, 2d, 4th, and 5th grade). 26 were boys, and 36 were girls who attended mainstream elementary classrooms (Mean Age = 9, SD = 1,50).

All participants were provided with detailed information about the study. Participation was anonymous and voluntary, with students having the option to withdraw at any time without any consequences. To ensure data protection, the online survey was only available to students enrolled in elementary education, with appropriate consent obtained from parents or guardians. Ethical approval was granted by the Centre of Educational Research and Evaluation in Cyprus (CERE/133045).

### Procedures

#### The co-design approach

The co-design approach was implemented in a six-hour workshop, during which participating teachers and researchers worked collaboratively to co-create the educational material, focusing on integrating the different AI tools in the elementary classroom. The primary objective was to integrate AI effectively into the elementary education curriculum, enhancing teaching and learning experiences. The researchers organised a multi-session workshop that included presentations, group discussions, and hands-on activities on using specific AI tools to provide all the teacher participants with a foundational understanding of AI. Teachers had the opportunity to engage with and practice some AI tools and applications (see Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#Fig1)).

The co-design process with teachers and researchers

The instructions given to participating teachers in the co-design workshop are to integrate at least three different AI tools. The researchers made this decision considering the hours/duration of intervention, the age and level of the students, the ease of such implementation for students, and lastly, the study’s research questions. Additionally, this phase involves piloting to determine if lesson plans can be incorporated into the classroom effectively and to receive feedback. Most of the time, the workshop was dedicated to teachers’ collaboration. The researchers divided participants into groups of 4–5 based on the grade level they were teaching that specific school year. The objective was to collaborate and design lesson plans that incorporate AI tools (at least three tools they have previously practiced) into a mother-language curriculum. Researchers oversaw and facilitated the process by offering guidelines, sample lesson plans, and practical information on utilizing specific AI tools in the classroom (see Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#Fig2)).

**Fig. 2**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z/figures/2)

The co-design workshop with teachers

The discussions among teachers concentrated on student evaluation and engagement, logistics, and teamwork. Initially, teachers crafted the lesson plans by integrating a minimum of three AI tools. At first, teachers designed the lesson plans by integrating at least three AI tools. Subsequently, the teachers presented the lesson plans to fellow participants to obtain feedback and make necessary revisions. In the end, teachers co-created three integrated AI lesson plans meant for implementation in their classrooms. Then, the teachers collaboratively co-created three lesson plans: one aimed at grades 1–2, another for grades 3–4, and the last for grades 5–6. Each lesson plan was designed in a way that allows other teachers interested in applying them in the classroom to adjust them as required. Following discussions among teachers, the three most appropriate AI resources, considering the curriculum and the student’s age/level, were the tools listed in the three categories outlined in Table [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#Tab1). The primary objectives of the lesson are to improve students’ comprehension of the text and to familiarise them with the concept of AI. Each of the three categories of tools serves a distinct purpose within the lesson.

* Tools like ChatGPT are used to create, edit, and summarise text so that students can compare it with their “humanised” texts and identify language errors.
* Tools for images allow students to put their ideas/imagination in the picture and create images based on their texts.
* Tools for video creation allow students to collaborate and develop a video based on the text, share their video with others and discuss.

All these tools in the three categories enabled students to practice all the essential skills for language learning: writing, reading, and speaking.

#### Piloting the educational materials in the classroom

The co-design approach involved a dynamic piloting process with 62 students across various elementary grades (1st, 2d grade, 4th, and 5th grade) in four Cypriot schools in Limassol and Nicosia. The researchers initiated the process with rigorously designed and targeted instructional units, aiding teachers in mastering the key content elements of each lesson plan. Teachers were initially provided with pre-designed lesson plans incorporating AI tools. Nevertheless, this acted as a launchpad for further investigation. Teachers had the opportunity to try out the proposed tools, identify other AI resources that could be beneficial for their teaching objectives, and review the lesson plans. They were able to propose modifications or alternative AI tools tailored to their students’ grade levels. This collaborative approach ensured the intervention materials were adaptable and addressed diverse learning needs. Following the implementation of the revised lesson plans, teachers reflected on the results and their instructional experiences. The students contributed to the evaluation process by filling out a survey, providing valuable insights about their involvement and achieving learning outcomes. This cyclical method, involving teacher experimentation, modifications, and reflection, sought to enhance the AI-powered lesson plans to advance the educational intervention strategy. ### Data collection

At the end of the co-design workshop, focus groups were conducted with a small group of participating teachers to collect their feedback and perspectives. The focus groups aimed to discuss with participating teachers a) their viewpoints on the co-design approach, b) the challenges encountered throughout the co-design procedure, and c) their upcoming plans and recommendations. Participating teachers were divided into small groups of 8 people and attended a 2-h online focus group. Two researchers served as facilitators for every focus group to ensure that the process ran seamlessly and that all participants had the opportunity to share their thoughts, ideas, and feelings. Specific subjects and questions addressed during the focus groups include, among others, the teachers’ perceived experiences from the co-design workshop, the challenges encountered, their involvement in the process, recommendations for further improvements of the process, and plans for the intervention. Some indicative questions of the focus group are:

* How did you find the experience of working collaboratively with researchers?
* Was the collaboration effective or ineffective? Can you provide any specific examples?
* How did the co-design process influence your sense of ownership over the lesson plans?
* What are the main benefits of this approach when developing learning materials?
* In what ways could students benefit from the co-created materials?
* What challenges, if any, did you encounter during the co-design process?
* Did you receive sufficient support from researchers? What additional support, if any, would have been helpful?
* How do you plan to implement the AI-integrated lesson plans in your classroom?
* Do you feel confident using AI tools with your students? What additional professional development or training would help you feel more prepared?

After the piloting phase in classrooms, a questionnaire was administered to all participating students. It assessed their learning experience with the co-created learning materials developed during the co-design workshop. These materials were tested with 62 elementary school students across four classrooms. The questionnaire included five Likert-scale questions, rated on a 5-point scale from "Not at all" to "Very much," using happy face emojis instead of numerical values to enhance user-friendliness and age-appropriateness. The multiple-choice questions were:

* "How much did Artificial Intelligence (AI) help you understand the text better?"
* "Is the Greek language class more interesting when using AI?"
* "How easy was it to create images with AI?"
* "How much do you feel you now know about using AI?"

Additionally, students were asked to select what they enjoyed most about the AI-powered lessons from the following options:

* "Creating images with AI assistance."
* "Generating text with AI assistance."
* "Creating videos with AI assistance."

Finally, an open-ended question invited students to share any difficulties they encountered during the learning intervention. As stated earlier, this paper aims to investigate the effectiveness of the co-design approach for teachers within this context. Assessing language abilities before and after the implementation of AI is beyond this study’s scope. Nevertheless, we intend to initiate a subsequent research phase to evaluate the lessons with a broader group of elementary school students.

AI-Integrated educational materials
-----------------------------------

In the co-design workshop, teachers collaboratively developed comprehensive lesson plans integrating various AI tools. The thematic topic, "The Summer," was selected from the national elementary education curriculum to ensure consistency across all three grade groups (1–2, 3–4, and 5–6) and align with the school year’s relevance. Each plan, co-developed with teachers and researchers, follows a standardized structure adaptable to educators’ needs (see Fig. [4](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#Fig4)).

**Fig. 4**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z/figures/4)

Key components of the AI-powered lesson plan flowchart

Variations include text selection and activity difficulty, tailored to suit the developmental levels of each grade. These lesson plans incorporate diverse, hands-on activities designed to introduce AI interactively, fostering curiosity, critical thinking, and digital literacy. By engaging with AI tools, students gain practical experience while developing ethical awareness and foundational digital skills. While this section presents the overall structure and content of the lesson plans, they remain flexible for teachers to adapt based on their specific classroom needs. The AI-integrated lesson plan follows a structured approach to embed AI tools into elementary education effectively. It begins with clear learning objectives, prior knowledge assessment, and classroom setup to prepare students for AI-enhanced activities. Brainstorming sessions activate curiosity, leading into hands-on AI-powered tasks tailored to different grade levels, with alternative options for differentiation. Assessment and feedback power AI for real-time evaluation, while the lesson concludes with reflections on AI’s role and ethical considerations. An annexe with supporting resources is also provided for further exploration and deeper engagement.

### The lesson plans

The co-created lesson plans follow a structured approach that aligns with the overall aim of the curriculum unit "Summer" while considering the classroom grade, students’ prior knowledge of AI, and their digital skills. Each lesson plan provides clear instructions for every activity and includes alternative options to address potential challenges students or teachers face. The following lesson plan outlines the overall structure, allowing teachers to adapt it based on their students’ specific needs, skill levels, interests, and age. In many cases, alternative approaches are suggested to offer flexibility for different classroom contexts. The lesson begins with an overview of essential prerequisites, including what students should know before engaging with the activities, teacher preparation requirements, estimated time for completion, classroom management guidelines, learning objectives, necessary equipment, and assessment techniques for evaluating student progress. The educational program’s learning objectives align with the primary education syllabus, particularly concerning the Greek language class.

These objectives are:

* Break a text into episodes and transform it into a multimodal format by generating images with AI tools.
* Compare their own texts with AI-generated texts, analysing content and linguistic differences, such as changes in verb tenses.
* Create a multimedia video using words from the text.
* Produce a new text by transforming one textual genre into another with the help of AI.

The lesson begins with a collaborative discussion that aims to familiarise students with the topic. Using the materials from the various texts being examined, students are invited to raise questions and concerns such as: "Is summer the best season?”, "Why do people enjoy summer so much?" "Is summer everyone’s favorite season? Yes, or No? Explain why". The reflections are displayed on the classroom board following the students’ contributions. The students are invited to express their opinions freely by studying various texts as "evidence" and, with the help of relevant vocabulary, to answer the questions they raise. The main aim is to help students understand the value of being in contact with loved ones (family, friends) during holiday periods, being in touch with nature and being able to travel more freely. Specifically, students can explore various texts from diverse types and genres beyond the textbook and compare them to produce their own multimodal and multimedia texts using AI (i.e. ChatGPT or Gemini AI).

The unit can begin with the painting "The Straw Hat" by N. Lytras, prompting students to share their initial thoughts about the person depicted, her emotions, and the setting. They can brainstorm words that come to mind or record their responses using Mentimeter. Next, students can compare how a child experienced summer 50 years ago versus today. Using the AI tool Muse.ai or Copilot, a ready-made image can be transformed to reflect a different era. This allows students to identify similarities and differences between the girl in the painting as she might have appeared 100 years ago and a child today, as well as to explore changes in habits and daily life over time.

Additionally, students’ written predictions about the text they will be taught can serve as a stimulus for the lesson. These predictions can be generated using the "Story Chain" language prediction tool. Students will be given words, phrases, sentences, and some images related to the text. Based on these, they will write a short paragraph predicting the story, individually or in groups. Next, the same words and phrases will be used as a prompt for the Gemini AI tool to generate its own prediction. This process allows students to extract initial information from their predictions. As more images, the title, and the author/illustrator of the text are revealed, they can refine their ideas. The teacher records keywords on the board through a brainstorming session based on students’ responses, enriching their predictions and guiding them toward initial conclusions. The teacher then reads the text aloud while students follow along. They are encouraged to use a comprehension note code (e.g., question marks, underlining, stars) to mark key points. At the same time, the teacher asks them to identify the main characters as they listen. After reading, students compare their predictions with the actual story, focusing on narrative elements such as setting, characters, and the problem (pleasant or unpleasant) and its resolution. They also compare their predictions with those of Gemini to see which is closer to the actual story. Following this, the teacher poses processing questions about the text, and students are asked to write their own continuation of the story based on the central question: "Why do we like summer so much?". The same question is then given to Gemini. Finally, students compare their texts with Gemini’s, analysing both content and linguistic elements, which are recorded for further discussion.

The lesson plans continue with additional activities that incorporate various AI tools. Students first discuss the role of images in understanding a text. They are then asked to use Muse.ai, Ideogram, or Copilot to generate an image that best represents their text interpretation. The next activity is collaborative. Students are divided into four groups, each responsible for co-creating different story episodes. They then use VideoGen, Flikki, or InVideo to produce a video based on their story. The generated images and videos are uploaded to the classroom folder for feedback and further discussion (see Fig. [5](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#Fig5) for sample screenshots from the co-designed lesson plans). Finally, student evaluation occurs continuously through their participation in all activities, as well as through exercises in the worksheet provided at the end of the unit. The main keywords used in the word cloud were “summer,” “hat,” “lady,” “island,” and “shy.”

Findings
--------

The findings section presents the experiences of both teachers and students in the study. By analysing teachers’ co-design process (qualitatively), we uncover their perspectives, challenges, and suggestions for future AI integration. Student experiences, captured through questionnaires, explore quantitative and qualitative aspects, including perceptions of AI’s impact on learning and any challenges encountered.

### Teachers’ co-design experiences

The qualitative data from focus groups was transcribed and coded as described in Saldaña ([2009](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR33)). The data analysis started with open coding based on the focus group’s three main categories/topics: a) Teachers’ perspective for the co-design process, b) Challenges, and c) plans and recommendations. The next step was interpreting each category using the emerging themes and codes to formalise the categorisation.

#### Teachers’ perspective on the co-design approach

Analysing the data from the focus group, four essential elements have emerged which highlight the benefits of such a co-design process: a) the interaction with other colleagues and experts, b) the sense of ownership, c) the professional development, and d) the quality of the outcome.

The teachers’ perspective confirmed that the co-design approach to developing AI-integrated educational material was significant, especially regarding interaction with colleagues and researchers in the field (84% of teacher participants, a total of 21). As they said, “[…] *the co-creation process helped us to understand how AI could be integrated into the classroom, taking into account our specific context*” (P3).

During this process, the teachers had the opportunity to discuss with their colleagues the potential benefits and challenges of the intervention, the possible strengths of the intervention, and the challenges that might be faced during such intervention. Per one teacher, the knowledge gained from their interaction with colleagues helped them “*anticipate problems and identify solutions that could benefit their students in the classroom*” (P16). Along the same lines, the participating teachers (72%, 18 out of 25) agreed that this process was valuable for them, and they learnt a lot regarding the potential of the use of AI in their classrooms. As two teachers said, “*Collaboration with the researchers was valuable since I had the opportunity to learn from them how my lesson could be more engaging and meaningful for my students*” (P1), and “*my interaction with the experts in the field helped me realised the power of AI for education and the impact that those tools could have on my students’ learning experience*” (P22).

Moreover, the teachers expressed their opinions in depth on this co-design process (60%, 15 out of 25), pointing out that this approach was significant for them as it created “*a sense of ownership since I was involved dynamically in the co-creation of an innovative material that would leverage my teaching practice*” (P18). The fact that teachers are involved in creating such material from scratch, from the beginning to the end, investing time to design something new for them in collaboration with other colleagues, creates a “*sense of belonging and responsibility*” (P24). Teachers’ engagement with the creation of the learning material was significant since they had a “*voice on what needs to be integrated, what not, and how*” (P2) based on the students’ needs, the classroom’s reality and complexities and based on the goals’ curriculum for this specific course.

Teachers (88%, 22 out of a total of 25) also claimed that involvement in this co-design process “*was an amazing opportunity to develop their skills and enrich their knowledge on the use of AI for learning purposes” (P11).* Indeed, 88% of the teacher participants argued that this process was a *“great*” (P2) and “*unique opportunity*” (P16) to understand more profound AI concepts and how students could benefit from AI tools in the classroom. This opportunity, as one teacher mentioned, “*helped me understand the philosophy of AI and practise some AI tools to be prepared to use them with my students*” (P4). In contrast, another teacher argued, “*I had never had the chance to be involved in such co-design opportunity, and this was an excellent opportunity to develop further my professional career as an educator*” (P13).

Last, participating teachers (72%, 18 from a total of 25) pointed out that “*the final material is of high quality since we had the chance to discuss the activities, test them, and receive expert feedback*” (P12). The structure of the co-design workshop allowed teachers enough time to discuss the implementation, find alternatives, “*interact with the material and make changes*” (P25), and co-create lesson plans that “*could be adjusted easily by any teacher who might be interested in applying those tools in the classroom with students*” (P25).

#### Challenges

Apart from the highlighted benefits of the co-design approach, some teachers (64%, 16 out of a total of 25) have pointed out some challenges regarding the co-design workshop, such as the preparation needed before the workshop, the logistics, and the unique characteristics of each classroom.

Some teachers (40%, 10 from a total of 25) claimed that preparing for this workshop is crucial to allow all participants to engage in the activities meaningfully. One teacher said, “*I should have been more prepared for this workshop regarding my knowledge about the use of AI. I had previous experience with AI, but some tools were new for me*” (P1). The lack of engagement with AI was reported by one teacher as well, who said, “*Previously, I used only chatbots in my classroom. Therefore, I needed more time to understand how the other AI tools could be used to benefit my students*” (P23). One of our inclusion criteria in selecting the participating teachers was previous knowledge and engagement with AI tools. However, some teachers (24%, 6 from a total of 25) seemed to need more time to practice, understand and test the AI tools.

Along the same lines, participating teachers (40%, 10 out of 25) pointed out some logistics that could be improved, such as the time available during the workshop for further discussion, exchange of ideas and sharing knowledge and feedback. One participant teacher (P15) argued that “*the time available for the discussion was not enough. I would love more time to receive feedback from other colleagues and hear their suggestions for further improvements. We had this chance, but in my opinion, we needed more time*”.

Furthermore, most teachers (76%, 19 out of a total of 25) discussed that the biggest challenge in this kind of workshop is to find a way to co-create a lesson that accommodates all students’ needs and interests. What is mentioned by participating teachers is that “[…] *the students and the classroom climate are so different from classroom to classroom that make the creation of a lesson plans so challenging*” (P4). With that opinion, other teachers agree:

“[…] *we needed to design many alternatives in each activity to be sure that all students could benefit from this lesson*” (P7),

“[…] *it was so difficult to come up with the final decision in some activities since our students, even they have the same age, are so different*” (P12),

“[…] “*My classroom is different in terms of setting and available equipment, so I had to think of some other alternatives to implement based on my context*” (P22).

#### Teachers’ recommendations

Almost all participating teachers (84%, 21 out of 25) agree that this co-design process allowed them to create appropriate, aligned, and practical lesson plans for elementary teaching and learning. Most of them (92%, 23 out of 25) clearly stated that “*this intervention will benefit students a lot, allowing them to engage in an innovative and interactive learning experience*” (P17) and “*start understanding the potential of the AI in their school performance*” (P20). Many teachers (64%, 16 out of 25) reported their commitment to implementing these lesson plans in their classrooms and continue working on using AI with their students.

Some teachers (35%) stated some suggestions regarding the co-design approach:

“[…] *this co-design process should be part of our curriculum since we can learn a lot from other colleagues and experts*” (P3),

“[…] *the AI must be introduced to all the teachers as part of their professional development*” (P24),

“[…] *these lesson plans, which is a result of our collaboration, needs to be further improved based on the students’ feedback and comments*” (P9).

### Students’ learning journey experiences

In exploring Students’ Learning Journey Experiences, we quantitatively assess how AI influences students’ learning in Greek language classes. We look at how AI helps them understand texts better, increases their interest in the subject, facilitates creating images, and enhances their understanding of AI. Also, through open-ended questions, we aim to understand if students faced any challenges during the intervention. This approach allows us to capture the measurable impacts of AI and the human insights into how it shapes students’ educational journeys in elementary education.

Figure [6](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#Fig6) depicts student enjoyment of the AI-integrated lesson using a 5-point Likert scale (data from 62 students). The results reveal a positive trend, with over 42% (26 students) finding the "Very Enjoyable" lesson indicating high engagement. An additional 29% (18 students) rated it "Somewhat Enjoyable," suggesting they enjoyed it with some reservations. However, some students (14, or approximately 23%) were "Undecided," implying mixed feelings. A smaller group (4 students, or about 7%) rated the experience as "Not Enjoyable," indicating a minority found it unengaging or unsatisfactory. This distribution of enjoyment levels offers valuable insights for educators and researchers. While it highlights the potential of AI to create engaging learning experiences, it also suggests room for improvement. Analysing these results alongside learning outcomes and student feedback can help identify opportunities to refine teaching methods, content selection, or the specific AI tools used. This will ultimately lead to a more engaging and effective learning experience for all students.

Discussion
----------

In this initial exploration, the study investigated how the co-design approach efficiently created lesson plans that employ AI features in elementary education. The study collected insights from teachers and students experimenting with the materials to answer the three major research questions. In summary, taking into account the results from this research, we claim that the essential conclusions are a) the introduction and familiarisation of students with AI need to be done as early as possible, starting with some easy tools with a specific purpose within the learning procedure in the classroom, b) the co-design approach could be a suitable process for teachers when designing an educational intervention with the technology. In the next section, a comparison of the findings with the literature review is provided.

### Teacher perception of the co-design approach (RQ1)

Learning materials were collaboratively co-designed by teachers, which turned out to be very successful. The results showed that when teachers collaborate with researchers, they can make materials that suit the student’s age, grade level, curriculum and interests. This corresponds to a study by Dai et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR7)) that pointed out how K12 educators have limited opportunities for creating knowledge. Our research also backs the idea that working together enriches teachers’ skills and teaching methods (Hamilton et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR13)). Some teachers faced challenges like needing more AI tool training and adapting materials to various student needs, but they used feedback and teamwork to solve these issues. Future efforts could include incorporating AI training into teacher programs and refining materials based on student input to enhance education.

### Student perception of AI-enhanced learning (RQ2)

During the AI-driven educational strategies pilot with 62 students from different grade levels in four schools in Cyprus (situated in Limassol and Nicosia), important insights were gathered for future improvements. Initially, teachers received pre-made lesson plans integrated with AI technologies, inspiring innovation and a sense of curiosity. The participating teaching group had the chance to test the proposed tools, search for alternative AI resources that could support their teaching practices and provide added value to the designed lesson plans. In addition, they reflect on the existing tools, propose changes, or suggest alternative AI tools that are equivalent to the students’ grade levels and meet the age restrictions. This collaborative work confirmed that the co-created learning resources could be a starting point for introducing students to the AI concept. In this phase, we aim to familiarise students with the use of AI from the early stages of schooling. Our intention for the next phase is to explore the impact of these AI tools on students’ learning results and academic performance.

Students ‘ collective feedback demonstrated a positive perception of the impact of AI tools on the learning experience. Specifically, students defined the tools as enjoyable, engaging, useful, and innovative, mentioning only a few obstacles during their usage in the classroom. This underlines the importance of preparing students for the ethical and appropriate use of AI for educational purposes (Lameras & Arnab, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR22)). Although students appreciate the use of AI, teachers remain concerned about effectively incorporating these tools into classroom settings (Lameras & Arnab, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR22)). The research revealed that students generally believed that AI-enhanced their learning process. Data analysis demonstrated high levels of enjoyment and increased interest in lessons that integrated AI technology, especially those involving text understanding and image creation. However, some students faced technical issues, such as connectivity problems that impacted their interaction with AI tools and proficiency levels. Sometimes, these challenges hindered students’ interest and understanding during the learning and teaching activities. However, students were able to share ideas that highlighted significant barriers, including the need for clear policy direction, access to practical experiences and technical support.

### Implications for AI integration in the classroom

The results provide essential perspectives for teachers (practitioners) and researchers. We claim that AI is different from other technologies because it is an entirely new concept for education, and the research on its effectiveness and impact on students’ learning is still unclear and limited. Also, the use of AI includes many ethical issues that need to be considered in every such implementation in the classroom with children. This part underscores crucial aspects to remember when incorporating AI into primary school settings.

#### Focusing on research and teacher training

If researchers are to integrate AI into education effectively, they need to modify this method to identify which AI resources are suitable for different courses and student age groups in elementary school classrooms. Identifying the best practices for teacher preparation and course design is crucial to making the most of AI in education. Therefore, teachers must put their professional growth at the forefront for AI’s effective and ethical use. These training programs will vaccinate teachers with the required skills to apply the AI principles for authentic and personalised learning based on each student’s need for a smooth introduction of AI in the school curricula (Peng et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR29)). A work by Chen et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR4)) stated that only a few studies have looked at how AI tools are used in real classroom settings compared to those focusing on web-based or online learning environments.

#### Preparing the future workforce

Notably, the labour market of tomorrow is crucial as AI technology continues to influence individuals’ roles and responsibilities in various industries. The future workforce will require individuals to hold technical skills and knowledge related to AI to secure suitable employment opportunities (Yang et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR45)). Research efforts should explore the broader impact of AI education in elementary schools, particularly regarding how it prepares students for the future workforce. Investigating how AI education fosters STEM competencies within K-12 education is a critical area of focus (Misthou & Paliouras, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR25)). Educators can begin strategically integrating AI education by incorporating age-appropriate concepts into the curriculum that will prepare students with the necessary skills to navigate the evolving AI-driven world they will enter.

#### Enhancing gender-balanced and inclusive classrooms

Other researchers are using robotics and AI to enhance STEM and social skills in primary school students, especially those with autism spectrum disorder (ASD). Hughes et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-025-10492-z#ref-CR15)) state that further research should examine AI’s impact on broader student learning outcomes, especially for those with diverse learning needs. This kind of research should explore how AI can support social and emotional development and the growth of critical thinking skills for all students. AI tools in classrooms can help teachers improve STEM skills, social-emotional development, and communication for all students, regardless of their background or learning preferences. Educators and researchers must collaborate to unfold AI’s potential for creating inclusive and effective learning environments. AI-based initiatives can, therefore, form equal, diverse, and gender-balanced learning opportunities in a digitally transforming world.

### Final remarks and future research

The co-design process offered valuable insights into how collaboration between teachers with the involvement of experts or researchers could enhance the development of new curriculum courses or innovative learning materials, creating significant resources to be implemented with students. Some challenges mentioned by participating teachers include the number of participants in each group, the time allocation for tasks and the preparation needed before the co-design.

This research could act as the basis for more exploration and investigation of the use of AI in elementary education. We intend to test our findings in an extended audience and various learning contexts to see whether the different classroom or students’ characteristics affect the AI’s impact on students’ performance. The results from this study demonstrate that AI in the school could motivate elementary students to be more engaged in the learning process and interact with the learning material.

We intend to expand our research further, including more classrooms, school subjects, and students from all levels of education (i.e., pre-elementary and secondary students). In the future, we aim to involve more teachers in creating materials aligned with Cyprus’s national curriculum. Additionally, we plan to investigate how AI long-term impacts students’ critical thinking and digital literacy.

Finally, our results are encouraging regarding the future of AI in education. What is needed is the investigation of the integration of AI in educational settings, working closely with teachers to improve the methods, approaches, and efforts of introducing AI tools in elementary classrooms.
