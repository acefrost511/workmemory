Abstract
--------

This study examines the evolving relationship between AI chatbots and academic integrity and students’ AI chatbot usage in high schools one and a half years after the release of ChatGPT. Through a comprehensive survey of students across six schools (N = 4,354) in the United States, we investigated students' self-reported cheating behaviors, patterns of AI use across different school-related tasks, and student perspectives on appropriate AI use in academic settings. Our findings revealed that overall cheating rates remain stable at 72.06%, consistent with historical baselines and prior studies, suggesting that AI availability has not changed overall cheating prevalence in high school. Additionally, more students reported using AI chatbots for support tasks like concept explanation and idea generation. Regarding students' reported preferences for allowing AI chatbots for school-related tasks, at this point, they still strongly supported using AI for conceptual understanding and brainstorming, and they maintained clear boundaries against using it for completing entire assignments. These findings suggest that while AI’s prevalence has not altered the patterns of academic integrity at schools, students' evolving perspectives on appropriate AI use provide valuable insights for schools and administrators integrating AI into traditional school settings.

Introduction
------------

Increasingly powerful large language models like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini have made AI tools more capable of generating human-like text, providing logical reasoning, and completing multi-stage academic tasks. While they have been objects of fascination for industry and the general public, this rapid advancement has intensified concerns about academic integrity in schools. A recent lawsuit, where parents challenged their child's school's policies on AI use in assignments, has brought these concerns to the forefront of public attention (Tenbarge, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR41)). Centered on when and whether to allow students to use AI in school, this case revealed one of the challenges schools face in defining appropriate AI use. As schools grapple to balance helping students develop digital literacy while maintaining academic integrity, educators and parents have limited empirical evidence to guide their decisions.

A recent study by Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)) provided initial empirical insights into this issue in the same academic year that ChatGPT was released. Specifically, overall cheating rates remained the same as reported rates prior to the release of ChatGPT, although students did report using AI at times for school-related activities. This prior research was completed in collaboration with a non-profit organization that has collected comprehensive survey data on students’ school experiences and academic integrity for over a decade. Leveraging the longitudinal survey data, the researchers were able to compare students’ anonymous self-reported cheating behaviors, similar to established instrumentation for academic integrity research from McCabe ([2001](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR24)), from the six months before and after ChatGPT’s release from a subset of schools. Their findings revealed patterns that challenged widespread assumptions about AI's immediate impact on academic integrity, namely that the emergence of AI would increase students' cheating rates at schools (Benito, 2023). Among the three different types of schools included in their sample – private, public, and charter – the overall cheating rates remained comparable from 2019 to 2023 following ChatGPT's introduction, as approximately 60–70% of students reported engaging in at least one form of academic dishonesty in the month both before and after ChatGPT was released. These rates were comparable with historical baselines established by McCabe ([2001](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR24)) and the Josephson Institute ([2012](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR19)).[Footnote 1](#Fn1)

The Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)) study noted three potential limitations of these initial findings. First, since the data was collected during the first six months of ChatGPT's availability, students may not have had sufficient time to explore and understand the tool's capabilities. Also, most schools had not yet developed formal policies regarding the use of AI, leaving students to navigate these new technologies without institutional guidance. Additionally, the number of schools in the original study was small because only those schools had provided data at the time points that were compared. However, Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)) still offered a baseline for understanding how students’ cheating behaviors might evolve as AI tools become more sophisticated and widely adopted.

Here, we present a follow-up study that increases the number of schools and considers the possibility that more time was needed to see the impact of AI tools on student cheating behaviors. Existing theories of technology and innovation adoption suggest that with more time, drivers of technology adoption and use can increase. From the perspective of diffusion of innovations theory (Rogers, [1962](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR34)), generative AI would most likely be used by early adopters. The vast majority of users would begin using it later. Similarly, the technology acceptance model (TAM) emphasizes increased recognition of the utility of a new technology, leading to increased use (Davis, [1989](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR10), [1993](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR11)). For students, it may simply have been that the first months of chatbot availability, despite tremendous hype and interest, were too short a time for students to accept and adopt the technology and use it in ways that constituted academic integrity violations.

Another consideration, beyond needing more time for awareness and adoption of the technology to spread among students, is that it may have been difficult for students to determine whether or not AI use violated academic integrity policies. Few teachers had established AI policies in place at the end of the 2022–2023 academic year (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)). By 2024, more schools and teachers had begun developing classroom policies on AI use and integrating AI literacy into their curricula, providing students with more guidance for their technology use decisions and making it more visible when a student was in violation of academic integrity expectations.

This paper builds on the prior research by examining how patterns in students' cheating behaviors have changed, aiming to provide additional insights based on a survey conducted from February to May 2024. Similar to the prior research, this study was completed in collaboration with a non-profit organization collecting comprehensive survey data on students’ school experiences and academic integrity for over a decade. This round of data collection, which occurred more than a year after ChatGPT's release, enabled us to consider increased adoption over time as would be suggested by TAM and the diffusion of innovations model.

This paper provides a focused follow-up analysis examining how student cheating behaviors and AI use have evolved after a full academic year of AI use experience. By comparing the survey responses with historical baselines and how they relate to the prior study, our analysis examines three key dimensions: changes in students' self-reported cheating behaviors, patterns of AI use across different school-related tasks, and evolving student perspectives on appropriate AI use in academic settings.

Background
----------

### Recent advancements in generative AI

Since late 2022, generative AI has progressed through rapid development and widespread adoption across domains, particularly in education. AI chatbots powered by large language models (LLMs) demonstrate increasingly powerful capabilities in natural language understanding, reasoning, and generation (Naveed et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR27); Hadi et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR17)). OpenAI’s GPT-4o model introduces enhanced logical reasoning and incorporates multimodal interactions, while Claude from Anthropic and Google’s Gemini also push boundaries in coding assistance, mathematical reasoning, and task completion (OpenAI, 2023). The fierce competition among major AI companies has accelerated development in specialized chatbot capabilities, including improved instruction-following, reduced hallucination, and enhanced safety measures (Stanford HAI, 2024).

The integration of AI chatbots and generative AI tools into K-12 education from 2023 to 2024 has presented both opportunities and challenges for various institutions. Due to the increasing prevalence of AI technologies in both academic and professional settings, many institutions recognize that teaching students how to effectively and ethically utilize AI tools has become as crucial as traditional digital literacy (Walter, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR45)). Institutions have started to develop AI literacy curricula instructing students on the rationale behind AI algorithms and appropriate AI use in learning environments (Almatrafi et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR2); Xie et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR48); Zhang, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR50)).

Beyond institutional challenges, both educators and students have concerns regarding AI adoption. Recent research with over 1,400 teachers reveals that educators express varying concerns, such as misinformation and reduced social interaction, about using AI chatbots in their daily tasks at school (Chen et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR8)). From the students' perspective, while they have recognized AI as a revolutionary technology that can assist their learning, they expressed concerns regarding the ethical considerations associated with its use (Farhi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR15)).

Despite these concerns, scholars have shown that when properly implemented, AI tools can enhance student engagement (Ezeanya et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR14)), support students' writing (Mohamed et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR26)), and provide personalized feedback (Xu et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR49)). To design new educational technologies that can empower students and enhance their learning processes, we need to understand how AI and AI-powered technologies are being used and perceived in educational settings.

### Academic integrity and “cheating”

Academic dishonesty, or "cheating," is a concern for educators who believe that certain behaviors and decisions made by students undermine educators’ ability to evaluate student learning effectively. Before the general public became aware of generative AI technology and its potential student applications, academic cheating had long been documented as a widespread phenomenon among U.S. secondary school students. According to the Josephson Institute’s, [2012](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR19) study of over 23,000 high school students in the U.S., 52% of respondents reported cheating on an exam in the previous year, and 75% admitted to copying someone else’s homework (Josephson Institute, [2012](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR19)). Other research also demonstrated the significant prevalence of cheating behaviors. In a study by Wangaard and Stephens ([2011](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR46)), 95% of 3,600 students from six economically and racially diverse high schools in the Northeastern United States acknowledged engaging in academic dishonesty during the previous academic year.

The impact of AI on academic dishonesty has become a topic of increasing public and scholarly discussion (Denkin, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR12)). When AI first became prevalent at the end of 2022, Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)) found no discernible increase in academic dishonesty reports using existing measures, suggesting that such tools had not substantially altered overall patterns of student cheating. Recently, researchers have become increasingly interested in AI's effects on students' cheating behavior. However, due to challenges with accessing student misconduct records or even knowing that those are an accurate measure (e.g., some teachers will tell the student to redo an assignment without AI and not report a violation to the central office), most studies now focus on assessing the perceptions of teachers, parents, and students regarding AI's potential impact on cheating behaviors. For instance, the Walton Family Foundation conducted the first national survey of teacher and student attitudes toward ChatGPT. Their results revealed that while teachers, K-12 students, and parents view AI chatbots as legitimate educational support tools, undergraduate college students indicated a tendency to use them for cheating purposes (Walton Family Foundation, 2024). Although the survey provides valuable insights, it lacks detailed information about which specific academic tasks students might use AI chatbots to cheat on and how they might employ these tools for cheating. Hence, based on the limited literature, after AI gained its prevalence in the public for two years, two key questions regarding student cheating behavior in the AI era remain understudied. First, whether cheating rates have actually increased with AI chatbots becoming prevalent in educational environments. Second, which types of academic tasks are students more likely to use AI chatbots for, and whether these uses constitute cheating or not?

To advance our understanding of these two facets of AI in education, the survey study conducted by Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)) provides a foundation for our current study. We adopted the same student survey instrument with minor modifications to incorporate questions that emerged from previous students’ short responses. We recognize that students’ self-report surveys may face two main challenges: overcounting, where students may over-report incidents due to heightened sensitivity to the behaviors being studied, and undercounting, where students may under report due to the negative connotations of cheating (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)). While these limitations exist, research shows that self-report surveys remain reliable instruments when used consistently over time, as they can effectively detect changes in behavior patterns even if absolute numbers are not perfectly accurate (Samuelstuen & Bråten, [2007](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR37)).

### Generative AI adoption

Based on empirical work, many factors, such as ethical concerns, social norms, and psychological factors, could contribute to individuals' technology adoption (Alsharhan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR3); Borenstein & Howard, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR7)). Here, we focus on factors that more directly support our understanding of students’ AI adoption patterns in educational contexts. The diffusion of innovations theory (Rogers, [1962](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR34)) is one framework. According to Rogers ([1962](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR34)), the adoption of technology follows predictable patterns, beginning with early adopters and progressively expanding to a larger population as visibility rises.

When ChatGPT was first released in late 2022, many school districts reacted negatively to the tool, implementing bans (Elsen-Rooney, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR13)). However, students from less restrictive contexts began exploring the use of generative AI in educational settings, with more than half finding it acceptable for researching new topics (Sidoti & Gottfried, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR39)). These students are the early adopters who are typically more willing to experiment with new tools in a variety of tasks, whereas later adopters rely more heavily on peer visibility, shared norms, and reduced uncertainty before adopting (Sahin, [2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR36); Thayer, [2013](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR42)).

As early adopters shared experiences through social media, and companies increased marketing efforts, more students were exposed to AI-related content, trends, and resources, enabling more positive evaluations of AI and a deeper understanding of its capabilities (Li & Zheng, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR22)). Research grounded in the technology acceptance model (TAM) further indicates that increased exposure and hands-on experience strengthen perceptions of usefulness and ease of use, thereby increasing adoption in educational contexts (Davis, [1989](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR10); Granić & Marangunić, [2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR16)). Studies focusing on secondary school students similarly show that familiarity and perceived utility can predict students’ willingness to adopt emerging learning technologies (Amores-Valencia et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR4); Papakostas et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR29)).

Collectively, these theoretical perspectives provide a foundation for examining whether observed changes in students’ AI usage patterns and perceptions of appropriate AI use align with predicted trajectories of technology diffusion in educational contexts. They also help situate students’ current AI chatbot use along an adoption curve, suggesting that adoption of generative AI in educational contexts is likely to increase over time as exposure, peer and school norms, and perceived usefulness continue to develop.

Research questions
------------------

Considering that more time has elapsed for students to become familiar with generative AI and more schools have been defining and enforcing AI use policies, we are exploring whether there have been changes in students' usage patterns – specifically those that some consider violations of academic integrity. In particular, we pose the following research questions with the 2023–2024 academic year in mind:

• Research question 1: How do patterns of high school students’ academic dishonest behaviors in the second year of AI chatbot availability relate to pre-AI baselines and findings from the initial post-ChatGPT period?

• Research question 2: How frequently do high school students report using chatbot AI technology for school-related tasks in the 2023–2024 academic year?

• Research question 3: To what extent and for which activities do high school students believe at this time point that chatbot technology should be permitted for academically-related student use?

Method
------

### Data collection/survey

Using the same instruments as Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)), this study focuses on a survey administered from February to May 2024 that took students 20 to 42 min to complete. The survey comprises 150 items and focuses on students' perceptions of their school experience, including course load, academic behaviors, and school climate. To ensure ethical data collection and protect student privacy, our survey protocol consists of the following procedures.

Before the survey, students and parents/guardians provided assent after receiving explicit information about the study’s background and purpose. Parents or guardians may opt their children out. Then, students are typically informed that their responses are anonymous and will not be linked to them individually. Moreover, they are informed that their responses to closed-response survey questions would not be accessible to any school personnel, and the survey administrators do not collect students’ names in any of the items. Students may complete this survey in Spanish or English and were reminded that they could skip any questions.

After the survey, if the survey administrator found that students responded to one of the few open-ended questions with information that identified themselves, the survey administrators removed this identifiable information before sharing it with the researchers. However, if students indicate an intention to harm themselves or others in one of these open ended questions, researchers will provide school administrators with information such as IP address, date and time the survey data were collected, AP and Honors course taking, gender and racial identity, and grade level to help them identify the student, which is sometimes possible and sometimes not possible given the nature of the information and the size of the schools.

There are three sets of items relevant to our research interests (see Table [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab1) for sample questions from each set).

The first set relates to cheating behaviors (adapted from McCabe, [2001](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR24)). These items are presented with the prompt, "In the past month, how often have you engaged in any of the following actions?" There are 12 items in this section, with four response options: "Never," "Once," "Two to Three times," and "Four or More times." The items cover behaviors ranging from plagiarism (such as copying material without citation) to unauthorized peer collaboration (such as sharing or obtaining assessment answers from others).

Given AI's increasing prevalence, the survey includes two items closely related to AI chatbots. Firstly, item C39 ("Paraphrasing or copying a few sentences of material from a written source without referencing/citing it in a paper or on a project") can include plagiarism behaviors that encompass both traditional plagiarism from human-authored sources and plagiarism involving AI-generated content. Secondly, item C41 addresses another emerging form of academic dishonesty through technology ("Using an Artificial Intelligence or digital device (e.g., ChatGPT, smartphone) as an unauthorized aid during an assessment, school assignment, or homework").

Additionally, we construct our survey items in specific ways to elicit information in a minimally judgmental way. In particular, we follow McCabe’s ([2001](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR24)) approach of asking about specific behaviors rather than asking students to identify themselves as ‘cheaters’ or as having ‘violated the honor code,’ as research shows this behavioral approach yields more responses (Curtis, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR9); Rettinger & Gallant, 2022). To further encourage honest responses to these items, the survey provided multiple notices to students and their caregivers that their responses are de-identified and confidential. Moreover, these questions are embedded among 150 total questions that cover a range of topics—the survey is not presented as one that is about cheating but about a whole host of matters from the student perspective and experience.

We note that there are several cheating behaviors in the survey that do not necessarily implicate AI or plagiarism. Beyond being consistent with prior research studies that examine “cheating” (which encompasses many different behaviors), this serves two purposes. One is to consider whether there is a way in which AI affects various forms of undesirable behavior. For example, AI making some school tasks simpler might make students disengage with school entirely and engage in other cheating behaviors that do not require AI. Or AI might be more readily used rather than copying off a classmate because AI is more accessible, and thus copying from a friend would decrease. Another is rhetorical – to re-articulate that when “cheating” is mentioned, there are many different behaviors to consider and that addressing cheating in schools can be its own concern of which AI is involved but not itself driving. As other studies have shown, cheating had already been happening before the release of AI. If cheating is the concern, the most appropriate response to addressing cheating may not be one that is strictly about AI.

The second set of relevant items asks students to self-report their use of AI chatbots for coursework-related tasks. These items are presented in a single section with the prompt: "In the past month, have you used an AI chatbot (such as ChatGPT) for any school-related tasks?" The section contains nine items addressing chatbot usage: seven multiple-choice items about specific coursework tasks, with the same response options as the cheating items, and two additional items. One of the final items asks students whether they use chatbots for "Other" tasks not listed in the previous seven items; if they answer yes, they may provide brief responses describing these tasks.

In Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)), this set of questions was only asked of students who reported some prior experience with AI chatbots such as ChatGPT. In the current study, this was asked of all respondents regardless of their stated prior use of AI. The current set also has an additional use of AI that was not included in Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)): the use of AI to summarize readings or other texts. These questions are all specifically about AI chatbot use, although in the larger set of cheating behavior items, there are items in which AI could be one possible tool for the behavior (see Table [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab1) for illustration).

The third set of relevant items assesses students' views on whether AI chatbots should be permitted for coursework-related tasks. These items follow the prompt: "To what extent do you think an AI chatbot (such as ChatGPT) should be allowed for the following school-related tasks?" The section contains nine items: seven multiple-choice items with four response options ("Allowed sometimes or under some circumstances," "Allowed all of the time," "Not allowed," and "I am not sure"), and two items about "Other" coursework tasks that students believe should be permitted for chatbot use.

We asked these “should” questions to account for the possibility that students may view certain behaviors as acceptable, even when they are not engaging in them. There could be interesting situations where students do not think certain uses of AI should be acceptable but are using it that way regardless, or if they are not using AI in a certain way but think that doing so should be acceptable. Additionally, if future studies of teachers' beliefs about what should or should not be acceptable regarding AI reveal differences from students' beliefs, these findings may inform how to help teachers and students reach agreement on AI policies.

### Data

The survey in this study consists of student responses from six schools: two public schools, two private schools, and two charter schools.[Footnote 2](#Fn2) This represents double the number of schools studied by Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)), while also maintaining three distinct school types that may differ based on the student populations drawn to each. These six schools were selected based on student availability and to ensure demographic variety across the sample. They are from four states across multiple time zones in the continental United States. The schools’ sizes, the corresponding response rates for each school type, and their demographics that can be reported with consideration for formal confidentiality agreements are described in Tables [2](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab2), [3](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab3), [4](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab4). Specifically, the sample included 4,354 high school students (82.71% response rate) with 20.72% being seniors, 35.94% being juniors, 28.30% being sophomores, and 15.04% being freshmen. As shown in Tables [2](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab2), [3](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab3), [4](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab4), our sample has balanced gender representation and diverse ethnic backgrounds that allows us to capture perspectives from students with varied backgrounds.

### Analytical methods

#### Preprocessing survey responses

To fully understand student behavior patterns, we pre-processed the responses in three steps. First, we only included students who responded to all standard cheating behavior items. Second, we recoded the cheating and chatbot usage items to indicate "No" for students who had never engaged in the described behavior and "Yes" for those who had, regardless of whether they had done so once or more than four times. This binary re-coding helps distinguish between students who have and have not engaged in these behaviors, as the frequency within a one-month period may not accurately reflect long-term habits. Third, for AI chatbot allowability items, we treated "I am not sure" as non-responses, excluding less than 10% of responses from each school for items A1 to A4 and A8, and less than 16% for items A5 and A6. Because A5 and A6 are about using chatbots to generate code and images, we attributed the higher percentage of "unknown" responses to the fact that not all high school students enroll in courses that require coding or image creation activities, and that these are capabilities that may not have been as visible in chatbots at the time.

#### Reliability test

With the preprocessed data, we conducted a reliability test for the three sets of items. We adopted Cronbach’s alpha. The results show that the cheating scale is internally consistent, with a Cronbach's alpha of 0.878; the AI chatbot use items have a reliability of 0.645, and the AI allowability items have a reliability of 0.821. These results indicate that our items demonstrated reliability.

#### Analysis

We analyze survey results using two analytical strategies. First, we examine students’ self-reported academic dishonest behaviors and AI usage patterns. In particular, we compare this academic year’s findings with those reported by Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)) to discuss differences in overall patterns relative to survey responses from the 2022–2023 academic year.

While we would prefer to use inferential tests to evaluate change over time statistically, we concluded that methodological constraints preclude valid longitudinal or quasi-longitudinal inference in our case. The surveys were administered to different sets of schools across the two research periods, making key assumptions underlying standard inferential tests, such as sample independence and population comparability, difficult to satisfy for tests such as t-tests or chi-square tests (Rao & Bannerman-Thompson, [2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR30); Sauro & Lewis, [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR38)). These constraints stem from practical challenges inherent in these school-based survey administrations. In particular, resampling the same schools and students across academic years is complicated by student mobility, cohort turnover due to grade progression and graduation, changes in school participation or administrative approval, and variation in school-level policies regarding research access and consent (Blazar & Kraft, [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR6); Rasberry et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR31); Waechter et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR44)). More specifically, the schools entered into structured agreements with Challenge Success for sampling, as the data are used by the schools as well. As a result, some schools prefer to be sampled only at a certain cadence, and matching previously sampled students is not feasible under the established privacy agreements. Together, these factors make it infeasible to construct the stable panel of schools or students over time that would meet the assumptions we consider necessary for valid inferential tests of change. Nevertheless, descriptive comparisons remain valuable for situating the current findings within the emerging literature on student AI use and academic integrity. That is, we believe it is of more value to the field to provide descriptive comparisons now, given the many questions that are emerging. Prior methodological work emphasizes that descriptive analysis can play a critical role in documenting patterns, contextual variation, and emerging trends when causal or longitudinal inference is not warranted (Loeb et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR23)). To further enhance interpretability, we disaggregate results by school type, making visible similarities and differences in AI-related behaviors and perceptions across public, private, and charter school contexts.

Second, for students' short-answer responses to chatbot allowability (N = 83, 2.44% of the total sample) and usage items (N = 131, 3.84% of the total sample), we performed qualitative coding to identify representative themes across their answers. Specifically, we adopted an inductive approach, extracting themes directly from students' responses. The team reviewed all responses together and discussed to determine themes that appeared consistent and representative across all responses.

Findings
--------

### Students’ cheating patterns remain consistent in comparison to historical baselines and prior study

Across the six schools surveyed in the 2023–24 academic year, 72.06% of students reported engaging in at least one academically dishonest behavior in the prior month. When examining individual school types, the dishonesty rates were highest in charter schools (78.99%), followed by public schools (74.69%), while private schools showed a notably lower rate (61.22%) (See Table [5](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab5) for full results). Compared with the survey results from Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)), which reported cheating rates of 59.9% and 69.5%, the prevalence of academically dishonest behaviors in the current sample appears broadly comparable.

### Students’ AI chatbot usage on academic tasks

#### Students’ AI chatbot exposure

Respondents who reported prior use of an AI chatbot were asked, as part of the detailed AI chatbot usage scale, how they had used such tools during the preceding month. The item was worded: “In the past month, have you used an AI chatbot (such as ChatGPT)?” Response options included: “I have used one,” “I know what it is, but I haven’t used one,” and “I don’t know what an AI chatbot is and/or I don’t know how to use it.” Survey responses across different school types are summarized in Table [6](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab6). Overall, at the time of the survey for the 2023–2024 academic year, more than 90% of respondents reported either knowing about or having used an AI chatbot.

In the prior study by Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)), students from the private high school reported lower levels of AI chatbot use: 35.6% indicated they had used an AI chatbot, 57.7% reported they had never used one, and 6.7% reported that they did not know what an AI chatbot was. In contrast, students from the public high school reported higher rates of prior use: 54.0% reported prior use, 36.8% reported no prior use, and 9.2% reported no knowledge of AI chatbots. Overall, a larger proportion of public high school students reported prior use of AI chatbots than students at private high schools.

Compared to the prior year’s data, the current findings indicate a substantial increase in students’ exposure to and use of generative AI chatbots, with the majority of students now reporting at least some prior experience using these tools.

#### Students’ reported AI chatbot usage on specific academic tasks

The most common use of AI chatbots is to explain concepts, problems, or topics (81.63%), followed by idea generation (68.65%) and text summarization (59.21%) (see Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Fig2)).

#### Students’ other AI chatbot usage

In addition to these items, the survey included an open-ended item in which students could specify any other school-related AI chatbot usage (N = 131). The most common use was for general exploration, where students used AI chatbots to generate helpful resources (e.g., "Generate Google Sheets formulas" and "I once asked ChatPGT to generate a schedule for me to follow because I had problems sticking to one in the past.") and explore topics they were interested in (e.g., "finding good sources," "Messing around," "understand history"). Some students also identified language-related tasks, using AI chatbots for editing and writing assistance (e.g., "writing emails" and "find passive voice and grammar errors in my paper, but not modify it"). Students also reported AI chatbots being really helpful in explaining mathematical concepts (e.g., "Mainly to describe math better," "To give a deeper understanding in math problems," and "To get a main overview over courses or lessons we will be learning in the future. (Mainly math)"). Other students mentioned using AI chatbots for entertainment purposes (e.g., "for fun," and "ask stupid questions in class when I'm bored").

We have also identified scenarios where students reported incidences where teachers incorporated AI as part of their curriculum to introduce these tools to students. This is reflected through students' comments like "To finish a school project that required the use of ChatGPT," "For a Spanish project we had AI pretend to be a celebrity as we interviewed the AI," and "For a class assignment centered around the use of AI."

Most specified uses were school-related, but we also found students reporting potential misuse of AI chatbots for academically dishonest behavior. For instance, instead of using AI chatbots to study math, like the examples above, some students reported using AI chatbots to "solve math problems" and "do math." One student reported using AI chatbots to "cheat on a test."

### Students’ perceptions on the allowability of AI chatbot usage

#### Students’ perceptions of AI chatbot allowability for academic tasks

The survey responses indicated varying levels of preference for the allowability of AI chatbot use across academic tasks (see Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Fig3)). More than half of the students believed AI chatbots should be allowed to support conceptual understanding and explanations, with 59.87% saying it should "always" be allowed and only 8.06% saying it should "never" be allowed. For idea generation (56.95%) and text summarization (51.51%), more than half of the students believed AI chatbots should “sometimes” be allowed for these two purposes. However, students showed the strongest reservations about using AI for completing entire assignments or writing all papers, with 82.73% saying it should “never” be allowed.

#### Students’ perceptions of AI chatbot allowability for other academic tasks

While students may also indicate “other” tasks for which they wish teachers to allow AI use, the identified tasks are similar to those mentioned in the items themselves. For instance, the most common responses concerned idea exploration, concept explanations, and writing edits. For the first identified usage, students claim that they have used an AI chatbot for “more recreational or outside of school use like learning about your topics and interests” and “to create titles and names.” For the concept explanations, students claim that they use AI chatbots “to explain the content in a more helpful way” and “to help explain math problems.” For writing edits, students specify using AI chatbots to “use a simpler AI for adjustment and grammar” or “to receive suggestions for revision of an essay.”

Discussion
----------

This study aims to describe how students report using AI in the 2023–2024 academic year, taking into account that there has been more time for diffusion of innovation and technology acceptance, and involving a larger sample than an earlier study (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)). Centering on students' voices and experiences, we investigated self-reported cheating behaviors, chatbot usage patterns for school tasks, and students' perceptions of the allowability of chatbots for academic purposes to explore the current relationship between students and AI in educational contexts. While we observed differences across school types (as reported in Tables [5](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab5), [6](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab6), [7](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab7), [8](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#Tab8)), the overall patterns remain consistent across different school contexts.

### Stability in students’ self-reported academically dishonest behaviors

Based on McCabe ([2001](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR24)) and the Josephson Institute's ([2012](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR19)) established baseline cheating rates, with approximately 52% of students reporting exam cheating and 75% admitting to homework copying, the cheating rates we identified (72.06% on average) also fell within this range. As Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)) suggest a 60%-70% cheating rate among high school students, our findings align with their study. Thus, given that AI chatbots have been accessible to students for more than 1.5 years, our findings suggest that students’ overall self-reported cheating rates have remained comparable to historical baselines and to our prior study. As Denkin ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR12)) argues, while new technologies may have altered the methods students use to cheat, the availability of AI tools does not necessarily increase overall cheating rates. Our findings also suggest that cheating rates remain high and comparable to historical records.

### Increase in students’ AI chatbot exposure and usage on academic tasks

For students who reported using AI chatbots for school-related tasks like idea generation and concept explanation, the pattern remains similar with the study done by Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)), where among students who had used AI chatbots, the most common reported uses were generating ideas for papers (Private High: 39.13%, Public High: 50.00%) and explaining new concepts (Private High: 42.65%, Public High: 39.29%). These tasks are more related to supporting activities in which they use AI chatbots as assistants to complete other tasks. Meanwhile, regarding the item inquiring about students’ unauthorized technology use, we also observe an increase in students’ self-reported use of AI to complete the entire assignment using an AI chatbot. These patterns in using AI for supporting tasks as well as for unauthorized use cases support the need for what Walter ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR45)) describes as the emergence of AI literacy, in which students need guidance on how to use AI tools as aids for learning rather than shortcuts for coursework.

The qualitative responses from the students add to our findings on the items and offer additional insights into AI’s integration in educational settings. We have identified that the specified use of the AI chatbot aligns with their responses to the items, with “idea generation” and “concept explanation” identified as two of the most important benefits they have gained from AI. Meanwhile, based on students’ responses, we observe that some teachers have introduced the technology by making it a mandatory component of in-class assignments or projects. This reflects the growing attention to teaching students to use AI tools effectively and ethically in modern education (Almatrafi et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR2); Walter, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR45)).

### Students remained opposed to AI use for completing entire assignments

Regarding students' perceptions of the "allowabilities" of using AI chatbots for school-related tasks, students reported varying levels of acceptance across task types: about half of the students support AI use in brainstorming and text summarization, whereas strong opposition was reported to using AI for complete assignment writing. Across all six schools, students strongly believe that using AI chatbots to "write the entire paper" for them should "never" be permitted, demonstrating their firm boundary in understanding that this behavior may constitute academic dishonesty and undermine their learning and skill development. The Pew survey (Sidoti & Gottfried, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR39)) reported similar findings, with nearly 75% of respondents selecting "not acceptable" when asked whether the use of ChatGPT was acceptable or unacceptable for writing essays. On the other hand, Pew found (similar to our findings) that the vast majority who had an opinion on the matter (over 80%) considered ChatGPT acceptable for researching new topics. Such widespread acceptance of AI for research and brainstorming purposes underscores the critical need for students’ digital literacy development. Given the risk of misinformation and biases within the AI chatbot responses, teachers should consider teaching students how to use AI-generated information well, just as we should teach students to evaluate search results on a search engine.

### Lack of AI literacy and guidance on AI use in academic tasks

Overall, our findings reveal stable rates of self-reported academic dishonesty alongside emerging and increasing AI use, highlighting the essential dynamics in the early stages of generative AI adoption among high school students. Rather than indicating a shift in students’ cheating rates, these results are consistent with a long-standing coevolutionary pattern in education, in which students continually explore new ways to gain academic advantage while educators respond by developing new norms, policies, and instructional strategies to maintain academic integrity (McCabe et al., [2001](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR25); Whitley, [1998](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR47)). Although the specific technologies involved change over time, this reciprocal process between student behavior and institutional response has characterized academic integrity challenges for decades (Stephens & Wangaard, [2013](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR40)). Through this lens, generative AI may not bring new patterns of academic dishonesty but a new iteration of an ongoing negotiation between students’ practices and educators’ expectations. Students’ reported boundaries around AI use, particularly their strong opposition to using AI chatbots to complete entire assignments, suggest that norms regarding appropriate use are already beginning to form, even as experimentation continues.

Accordingly, our findings aim to support future instructional design and learning outcomes in two ways. First, our research results provide researchers with a fundamental understanding of which academic tasks students consider suitable for AI chatbots and offer preliminary insights into how students may use AI chatbots to facilitate their daily tasks. This empirical evidence establishes a benchmark for future longitudinal studies that track how students' perceptions and use evolve as AI technology advances. These usage patterns also reflect the development of boundaries among students regarding technology use in educational settings. Second, our findings inform school administrators and educators about the tasks for which students tend to use AI chatbots and what academic tasks students have accepted AI as an appropriate tool. We hope this information will encourage meaningful discussions in professional development and policy committees, shifting the focus of conversations about speculation toward evidence-based decision-making regarding the proper integration of AI into practice.

### Limitations

While our study provides insights into the impact of AI chatbots on academic integrity and students’ daily practices in schools, we recognize four limitations that warrant the audience’s consideration when contextualizing and generalizing our findings.

First, although we make targeted comparisons between the current study and prior work (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)), the use of different samples precludes applying key inferential tests of change over time. Because the surveys were administered to different sets of schools and students, we concluded that the assumptions required for longitudinal statistical inference were not satisfied. As a result, cross-year comparisons in this study are descriptive rather than inferential. However, this is still information that we believe is of value and interest to the field.

Second, this study's use of self-reported survey data may introduce previously noted potential biases. Students may underreport due to the negative connotations associated with academic dishonesty. Because of this, the cheating phenomenon in schools may be larger than reported here. Accordingly, our study aims to help people understand the relevant changes, if any, in students' behavior as new technologies are introduced to education settings. Future studies could incorporate more complementary methods, reporting qualitative data analysis to validate the quantitative findings. While admittedly imperfect, we have followed prior research protocols (McCabe, [2001](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR24)) to obtain the most accurate research results to inform future researchers and educational technology designers.

Third, the study's sample includes six schools—two each from private, public, and charter school types—with varied demographics across four states. With double the schools and more regions included than the 2024 study done by Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10587-1#ref-CR21)), we are reporting consistent results with different schools and student populations. While this diversity enhances the robustness of findings within the sampled contexts, it still has limits with respect to generalizability to the broader U.S. high school population. Different schools may exhibit distinct patterns of AI adoption and academic dishonesty. Several contextual factors beyond school type may also impact students’ behaviors in technology adaptation. School-specific policies on AI use, varying levels of technological infrastructure, and differences in academic support systems could all affect students' engagement with AI tools. Future studies should aim for more geographically and demographically diverse samples to ensure findings reflect a broader range of educational settings.

Fourth, our study, based on data collected in 2024, captures a crucial snapshot of how students interact with AI chatbots like ChatGPT, and it represents their engagement with these tools at a specific moment in technological development. As these AI systems evolve at an unprecedented pace, students' behaviors and perceptions will likely evolve in parallel. This temporal limitation underscores the importance of longitudinal research to track how emerging AI capabilities influence academic practices and ethical decision-making in educational settings.

Conclusion
----------

This study provides a still-developing understanding of AI chatbots’ impact on academic integrity and students’ perceptions and adaptations to such technology. We reveal how academic communities are navigating the integration of AI chatbots, balancing traditional educational values with technological advancement. Evidence of teachers incorporating AI literacy into their curricula and our findings showing students' widespread usage of AI for idea generation and concept explanations both indicate that AI tools are increasingly integrated into traditional educational spaces.

Accordingly, our results aim to inform educational technology designers and researchers regarding students’ current patterns and preferred usage of AI in educational contexts, providing empirical evidence to support and inspire future technological development. As AI technology advances rapidly, ongoing research is continuously needed to examine factors driving student behaviors and decision-making in this evolving educational landscape.
