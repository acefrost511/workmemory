## Abstract
Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) is rapidly expanding within L2 education and is increasingly positioned as a core resource for lesson planning. This study explores how 22 pre-service teachers mobilized multimodal GenAI tools, including DALL-E for image generation and ChatGPT for instructional support, to plan Grades 3–4 Chinese-as-a-second-language (L2) vocabulary lessons in Hong Kong primary schools. Using the TPACK framework, we examined how their pedagogical, content, and technological knowledge interact during this process. Lesson artefacts, reflective forms, and semi-structured interview responses were analyzed. Results showed that participants actively employed a range of multimodal GenAI functions across instructional stages, with image-generation tools being the most frequently used (34 instances). They were aware of the Chinese character features and pupils’ cognitive and cultural characteristics to produce culturally attuned teaching materials. Rather than static overlap, content, pedagogical, and technological knowledge continuously shaped one another through iterative negotiation. Theoretically, the study operationalizes the TPACK framework into a GenAI-supported, multilingual primary setting and illustrates how the three knowledge domains co-evolve through recursive negotiation. Practically, it underscores the value of collaborative design, systematic cultural vetting, and the deliberate integration of interactive chatbots to enhance L2 vocabulary instruction.

## 1. Introduction
Teaching Chinese vocabulary to second language (L2) learners presents considerable challenges due to the distinctive features of the Chinese language. Unlike alphabetic languages, Chinese characters are non-alphabetic and differ significantly in phonology, orthography, and morphology, posing difficulties for L2 learners (Shen & Xu, [Citation2015](#)). Furthermore, the vast number of characters and words required for proficiency can be overwhelming (Zhang, 2016). These challenges underscore the necessity of incorporating advanced technologies to facilitate Chinese vocabulary instruction (Lan et al., [Citation2015](#)). Multimodal generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) emerges as a promising solution to address this issue. Multimodal GenAI tools are capable of processing and generating content across multiple modes of communication, including text, audio, and visual data, to produce cohesive and contextually relevant outputs[Footnote1](#) (Bewersdorff et al., [Citation2025](#); Wu et al., [Citation2024](#)). A pioneering study has reported that multimodal GenAI could significantly enhance L2 English learning by providing students with enjoyable, personalized learning experiences (Liu et al., [Citation2024](#)). Despite its potential, research on the application of multimodal GenAI in L2 education remains limited, particularly in the context of L2 Chinese. Therefore, further empirical studies are needed to explore how this innovative technology can be effectively integrated into various aspects of L2 teaching, such as lesson planning.
Described as the process of designing learning activities for groups of students, lesson planning is an essential competency for teachers globally (Enow & Goodwyn, [Citation2018](#)). Researchers have investigated the incorporation of technology into lesson planning to enhance this instructional activity. For instance, Guo et al. ([Citation2025](#)) employed chatbots to support teachers’ lesson planning in L2 English writing courses. The employment of technology not only facilitates the alignment of instructional strategies with curriculum objectives but also fosters interactive learning activities, which are vital for deeper student engagement and knowledge construction (Kurt et al., [Citation2014](#)). Given the emerging significance of multimodal GenAI in L2 instruction, it is anticipated that this technology will increasingly assist teachers in lesson planning (Kohnke & Zou, [Citation2025](#)). Empirical research is warranted in this field, particularly grounded in theory.
The Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge (TPACK) framework (Mishra & Koehler, [Citation2006](#)) offers a theoretical perspective for examining the multifaceted knowledge that teachers require to effectively integrate technology into their instructional practices. It is built upon the premise that effective teaching with technology requires a deep understanding of the complex interplay between three core components: content knowledge (CK), pedagogical knowledge (PK), and technological knowledge (TK) (Koehler et al., [Citation2013](#)). The framework highlights the importance of not only mastering each of these domains individually but also understanding their intersections: pedagogical content knowledge (PCK), technological content knowledge (TCK), technological pedagogical knowledge (TPK), and ultimately, technological pedagogical content knowledge (TPACK) (Mishra & Koehler, [Citation2006](#)). These intersections represent the nuanced ways in which teachers can blend their expertise from different domains to develop effective teaching practices.
Given the critical role that teachers play in the application of GenAI for language instruction (Jeon & Lee, [Citation2023](#); Yao et al., [Citation2025b](#)), it is imperative to investigate the content, pedagogical, and technological knowledge, as well as their intersections of L2 teachers in this endeavor. Therefore, in the context of L2 Chinese education in Hong Kong, this research aims to examine teachers’ TPACK in the application of multimodal GenAI tools in vocabulary lesson planning. This study contributes to the literature by revealing how teachers orchestrate various GenAI tools in the under-researched context of L2 Chinese, with particular attention to the dynamic interplay among knowledge domains during lesson design.
## 2. Literature review
### 2.1. Technology-supported lesson planning in L2 instruction
Lesson planning is a fundamental component of effective L2 instruction (Pang, [Citation2016](#)), which ‘exposes teachers’ beliefs, understandings, and orientations’ concerning the curriculum, the subject matter, the students, and pedagogical approaches (Baecher et al., [Citation2014](#), p. 120). A well-constructed lesson plan acts as a guide that ensures successful classroom instruction by aligning pedagogical decisions with core educational objectives (John, [Citation2006](#)). With the advancement of computer technology, teachers have increasingly utilized various technological tools to support their development of lesson plans. Accordingly, Harris and Hofer ([Citation2011](#)) argued that effective technology-supported lesson planning requires teachers to carefully consider the alignment between content goals, learning activities, and available technological resources. Specifically, teachers must select learning activities that are not only engaging but also strategically matched to the curriculum content and the time available for instruction (Harris et al., [Citation2010](#)). Contextual factors, such as the availability of resources and students’ access to technology, also play a crucial role in shaping the planning process (Harris & Hofer, [Citation2011](#); John, [Citation2006](#)). By prioritizing student-centered approaches and ensuring that technology serves to deepen intellectual engagement, teachers can create effective and dynamic learning environments (Harris & Hofer, [Citation2011](#)). Following the guidelines outlined in the aforementioned literature, a number of recent empirical studies have investigated the utilization of computer technology by L2 teachers to enhance their lesson planning (Crosthwaite et al., [Citation2023](#); Guo et al., [Citation2025](#)).
For instance, Guo et al. ([Citation2025](#)) explored how 10 English-as-a-foreign-language (EFL) teachers in China incorporated chatbots into their writing teaching. The participating teachers were able to align the chatbot’s functionalities with pedagogical goals and design diverse teaching activities. They also demonstrated professional knowledge by supplementing the chatbot with additional instructional strategies to address the limitations of the technology. These studies provide valuable insights into the integration of technology in lesson planning within the context of L2 instruction (Crosthwaite et al., [Citation2023](#); Guo et al., [Citation2025](#)). Besides, they validate the guidelines proposed by Harris and Hofer ([Citation2011](#)), which emphasize the alignment of content goals, learning activities, and technological resources. However, it is important to note that the technologies employed in these studies do not encompass GenAI. While Guo et al. ([Citation2025](#)) utilized a chatbot, which exhibits some level of intelligence, it still differs significantly from the capabilities of GenAI. This distinction highlights a gap in the literature, underscoring the need for further investigation into how GenAI can support lesson planning in L2 instruction, as illustrated in the following section.
### 2.2. The potential of GenAI-supported lesson planning in L2 instruction
The advent of GenAI has revolutionized the landscape of L2 education. GenAI tools can simulate authentic conversations, provide real-time feedback, and generate a variety of text genres, thereby enhancing learners’ linguistic skills and confidence (Kohnke et al., [Citation2023](#);  Lu et al., [Citation2024](#)). Over the past few years, numerous empirical studies have explored the application of GenAI in various aspects of L2 instruction. These studies examine both students’ psychological and behavioral responses to GenAI-supported L2 learning (e.g. Derakhshan, [Citation2025](#); Shafiee Rad, [Citation2025](#); Yao et al., [Citation2025a](#)) and teachers’ utilization of GenAI to enhance their instructional practices (e.g. Derakhshan & Ghiasvand, [Citation2024](#); Xu et al., [Citation2025](#); Yao et al., [Citation2025b](#)). The findings provide empirical evidence regarding the benefits and limitations of GenAI in L2 education, as well as insights into how students and teachers effectively leverage this innovative tool for their respective purposes. Given the significance of GenAI, it has also been employed for lesson planning. For instance, empirical studies within the domains of physics and mathematics education have demonstrated the viability of employing GenAI in lesson planning, as well as its efficacy in enhancing student learning outcomes (Karaman & Göksu, [Citation2024](#); Yeadon & Hardy, [Citation2024](#)). However, in the field of L2 instruction, pertinent research remains predominantly theoretical in nature (e.g. Hong, [Citation2023](#)). There is a pressing need for empirical research to explore the practical applications of GenAI in this area.
A recent study provides a preview of this issue. Dornburg and Davin ([Citation2025](#)) investigated how ChatGPT can facilitate the creation of L2 instruction lesson plans, focusing on prompt specificity, output variability, and historical biases. The findings reveal that increased prompt specificity does not necessarily correlate with higher quality outputs; however, prompts that included a checklist of criteria yielded the highest quality lesson plans. The study also highlights significant variability in output quality, suggesting the necessity for iterative prompt refinement. Most importantly, historical biases may exist in the lesson plans generated by ChatGPT, such as lack of attention to culture in L2 learning. The authors thus indicated that ‘the teaching of culture is an area within which current AI falls short … and that this particular aspect of lesson planning requires human attention when using generative AI’ (Dornburg & Davin, [Citation2025](#), p. 12).
### 2.3. Lesson planning as a means to measure TPACK
The TPACK framework is an extension of Shulman’s ([Citation1986](#)) Pedagogical Content Knowledge (PCK) model, which emphasizes the interplay between CK, PK, and the synthesis of these two domains, that is, PCK. CK refers to the understanding of subject matter, PK involves the methods and processes of teaching, and PCK represents the integration of these elements to effectively convey content (Shulman, [Citation1987](#)). Recognizing the growing importance of technology in education, Mishra and Koehler ([Citation2006](#)) expanded this framework by incorporating TK, which pertains to the ability to use and understand technology. This integration led to the development of additional constructs: TCK, which explores the relationship between technology and subject matter; TPK, which examines the intersection of technology and teaching methods; and the comprehensive TPACK framework, which encapsulates the dynamic interaction among technology, pedagogy, and content knowledge, providing a holistic approach to technology integration in educational settings (Koehler et al., [Citation2013](#)).
Recently, with the wide application of GenAI technologies in the field of education, scholars have proposed the AI-TPACK framework, which adds an AI-Ethics dimension to the original TPACK; the new dimension encompasses four elements: transparency, fairness, accountability, and inclusiveness (Karataş & Ataç, [Citation2025](#)). Despite the innovation of the new AI-TPACK framework, the present study employs the traditional TPACK for methodologically grounded reasons. The application of multimodal GenAI in L2 Chinese vocabulary instruction represents an emergent pedagogical context where the fundamental mechanisms of technology integration remain under-described. In such novel domains, employing a mature, empirically validated framework like TPACK ensures construct operationalization is anchored in decades of cross-contextual validation.
Regarding the evaluation of teachers’ TPACK, self-report approaches, such as questionnaires, open-ended questions, and interviews, have been widely utilized in the existing studies in the field of general education (Koehler et al., [Citation2011](#)) and L2 instruction (Tseng et al., [Citation2022](#)). However, such methods have their inherent limitations concerning the reliability and accuracy of the instruments. Abbitt ([Citation2011](#)) indicated that, ‘As with any self-reporting measure, the ability of the instrument to accurately represent knowledge in the TPACK domains is limited by the ability of the respondents to assess their knowledge and respond appropriately to the survey items’ (p. 291). Therefore, researchers have increasingly employed lesson planning as an objective method to assess teachers’ TPACK in L2 education (e.g. Crosthwaite et al., [Citation2023](#); Guo et al., [Citation2025](#)). For instance, in Guo et al. ([Citation2025](#)) study on chatbot-supported L2 writing instruction, the participating teachers’ lesson plans were analyzed to identify how they incorporated the chatbot into their teaching strategies, which were further coded within the TPACK framework. These studies have demonstrated that the analysis of lesson plans provides an objective and reliable assessment of teachers’ technological, pedagogical, and content knowledge. To enhance the reliability of the findings, the present study adopts a multi-source data collection approach. While analyzing participants’ lesson plan materials as a core data source, it is supplemented by reflection forms and semi-structured interviews, thereby ensuring more robust analytical results through cross-validation of multiple data sources.
### 2.4. Overview of the literature and the present study
The review of existing literature reveals two critical gaps. Firstly, while there is a burgeoning interest in the application of GenAI in L2 education, current research on technology-supported lesson planning has seldom explored the use of GenAI, especially multimodal GenAI. Given the great potential of GenAI to enhance lesson planning (Dornburg & Davin, [Citation2025](#)), it is imperative to investigate how multimodal GenAI can be effectively utilized in lesson planning for L2 instruction.
Secondly, the existing body of research on TPACK and technology-supported lesson planning predominantly focuses on L2 English education, with limited attention given to the context of L2 Chinese education. This gap is particularly critical because L2 Chinese’s logographic writing system, with its character morphology and tonal features (Shen & Xu, [Citation2015](#)), poses distinct challenges for vocabulary acquisition that differ fundamentally from alphabetic L2 English, necessitating specialized technological and pedagogical mediation. Although Xu et al. ([Citation2025](#)) examined the integration of technology in L2 Chinese instruction through the TPACK framework, their study did not specifically address lesson planning. Koehler et al. ([Citation2013](#)) argued that ‘technology, pedagogy, and content do not exist in a vacuum, but rather, are instantiated in specific learning and teaching contexts’ (p. 16). Therefore, the present study on lesson planning within the L2 Chinese education context can broaden the current research by providing empirical evidence from diverse contexts.
In light of these identified gaps, the present study aims to address these issues by examining the role of multimodal GenAI in lesson planning for L2 Chinese vocabulary instruction in Hong Kong from the perspective of the TPACK framework. The following two research questions (RQ) guide the whole study.
1. How did the teachers employ multimodal GenAI in lesson planning for L2 Chinese vocabulary instruction?
2. What constituted teachers’ content, pedagogical, and technological knowledge, as well as their integration, in this pedagogical activity?
## 3. Research method
### 3.1. Context and participants
This study was conducted within a 13-week graduate-level teaching methodology course (‘Curriculum and Instruction in Chinese Language Education’) at a public research university in Hong Kong during the spring semester of 2025. In alignment with the university’s 2024–2025 AI literacy mandate, GenAI applications were explicitly embedded in the curriculum, including the instruction and assessment of the course. The 35 enrolled students attended one 3-h session weekly and collaborated in groups of four or five to develop lesson plans for Grade 3–4 L2 Chinese vocabulary using multimodal GenAI tools.
From the eight groups, 22 participants (Mean age = 25.95, SD = 5.52) from five groups consented to participate, yielding five syllabi and five slide sets for analysis. While all participants were graduate students, we deliberately sought diversity through convenient sampling that captured Hong Kong’s unique professional development landscape. As Table 1 shows, the cohort comprised both novices (zero teaching experience) and experienced practitioners (some exceeding five years). This composition reflects Hong Kong’s professional development landscape, where teachers often pursue master’s degrees to transition to more advantageous positions.

### 3.2. Study procedure
At the beginning of the semester, we spent half an hour to introduce the TPACK framework and its related components to the graduate students in class. This introduction was intended to provide a comprehensive understanding of the integration of technological, pedagogical, and content knowledge to enhance teaching practices. Following this, we outlined the requirements for the students’ assignments. Specifically, the students were required to collaborate in groups of four to five to develop teaching plans for the course ‘I Like Being Me,’ which targets Grade 3 or 4 L2 Chinese learners in Hong Kong. This group-based approach was adopted because Chinese language teachers typically engage in collaborative lesson planning within authentic teaching environments. The primary content of the course included various Chinese vocabulary related to emotions, such as happiness and sadness. The assignments required students to develop a teaching syllabus and create teaching slides. The students were required to complete the assignments throughout the semester and submit them at its conclusion.
Students were given autonomy to select and utilize any available multimodal GenAI tools for their lesson planning tasks. This deliberate autonomy was pedagogically intentional. The current GenAI landscape is crowded with rapidly evolving applications, and practicing teachers must routinely decide which specific tool best fits their lesson objectives, student profile, and institutional constraints. Therefore, we sought to elicit data that reflect the authentic integration processes teachers enact outside the research context. The tools they employed spanned several categories: image-generation platforms (e.g. DALL-E, Canva, Copilot), interactive game tools (e.g. Kahoot, Quizlet Live, Deepseek), voice and audio generators (e.g. Speechling, Talkpal AI, AIVOOV), video-creation utilities (e.g. Animaker, Elai.io AI), and conversational chatbots (e.g. ChatGPT, POE). This open-ended approach ensured that participants’ choices reflected authentic teacher decision-making rather than prescribed tool use. Ethical approval was obtained from the institutional review board prior to the study.
### 3.3. Data collection
We collected three types of data for analysis. The first type comprised the teaching materials, specifically the syllabi and teaching slides, developed by the participants and submitted at the end of the semester. The second type consisted of a reflection form (see Appendix A) completed by the students and submitted alongside the teaching materials. In this form, students were required to reflect on their experiences with applying various multimodal GenAI tools for lesson planning, the challenges they encountered, and the solutions they devised. One week after the semester concluded, the students participated in semi-structured interviews which were conducted with each group and lasted approximately 30 min (see Appendix B for the interview protocol). The interviews were conducted in Chinese, and the entire process was audio-recorded and transcribed verbatim. Only the content relevant to the analysis was translated into English.
### 3.4. Data analysis
Two research assistants with postgraduate degrees in language education analyzed the data. During the coding process, we segmented the lesson content into four key instructional stages: lead-in, teaching, practice, and feedback. This segmentation is grounded in the typical structure of primary school Chinese lessons in Hong Kong. Specifically, the lead-in stage activates prior knowledge through context-setting activities; the teaching stage introduces target vocabulary \*via\* explicit instruction and multimodal explanation; the practice stage provides controlled and communicative opportunities for application; and the feedback stage assesses comprehension through formative evaluation and immediate feedback.
To address RQ1, we relied on the existing literature on multimedia and multimodal learning (e.g. Mayer, [Citation2005](#); Moreno, [Citation2005](#); New London Group, [Citation1996](#)) to code how the participants employed multimodal GenAI tools for lesson planning from three analytical perspectives, namely, cognitive, affective, and sociocultural. For example, within the cognitive perspective, we examined attention guidance (Mayer, [Citation2005](#)), where GenAI-produced animations or visuals were used to direct learners’ focal attention to newly introduced lexical items. The affective perspective was exemplified by sparking interest (Moreno, [Citation2005](#)), as when teachers deployed GenAI-generated songs or vivid character images to arouse students’ motivational engagement during learning. Under the sociocultural perspective we examined contextualization (New London Group, [Citation1996](#)), illustrated by GenAI-generated scenes that situated emotion vocabulary in authentic Hong Kong settings.
Content analyses were conducted to examine the submitted syllabi and teaching slides. Initially, the two research assistants thoroughly reviewed the data to identify instances where multimodal GenAI tools were explicitly mentioned or implied. We focused on how these tools were integrated into the lesson planning process, paying particular attention to the modes of communication they supported (e.g. text, audio, visual, etc.) and the specific functions they served. Next, we employed an open coding process to categorize the data. This involved generating initial codes that captured the categories related to the use of GenAI in lesson planning. The identified codes (Table 2) were iteratively refined and grouped into the three perspectives (i.e. cognitive, affective, and sociocultural). For instance, we identified the code ‘AI-generated images for vocabulary presentation’, which was first categorized into the category of ‘information enhancement’ and then grouped into the ‘cognitive perspective’.

## 4. Results
### 4.1. How participants employed multimodal GenAI in lesson planning
The five groups actively employed various available multimodal GenAI tools, including interactive game tools, image-generation tools, video-generation tools, voice tools, audio-generation tools, and GenAI-guided chatbots. As shown in Table 4, these tools were summoned in varying frequencies to fulfil a range of pedagogical functions. Overall, image-generation tools dominated every instructional phase, with their concentration on the practice stage (14 instances), followed by substantial use during lead-in and feedback. Interactive game tools also peaked during practice activities (12 instances), whereas voice, as well as video- and audio-generation tools were distributed more evenly but remained less frequent, and chatbots were largely confined to the teaching segment. These divergent usage frequencies indicate that the various tools were employed to serve distinct pedagogical functions during lesson planning. The three sub-sections below accordingly report these functions through cognitive, affective, and sociocultural lenses. The detailed breakdown of the functions these tools fulfilled at each instructional stage is summarized in Appendix C.

#### 4.1.1. Cognitive perspective
As shown in Table 2, the participants predominantly employed GenAI for information enhancement (56 instances), where GenAI-generated visuals, audio, or animations were used to co-construct meaning through multiple modes. The next most frequently used cognitive functions were memory support (15 instances) and attention guidance (10 instances). Memory support referred to linking verbal or visual cues, such as GenAI-produced picture-word pairs, to strengthen retention and retrieval. Attention guidance, on the other hand, involved the use of color, gesture-like animations, or other dynamic cues to focalize learners’ attention on target lexical items. For instance, in the teaching slides, Group 1 generated an image, featuring a vivid illustration of emotional expressions of ‘哈哈笑’ (Hahaha laughter) and ‘蹦蹦跳’ (Bouncing and jumping) (Figure 1). The vivid emotional image enhanced the lexical input by providing visual context (information enhancement), the bright colors and animated characters drew learners’ focus to the target emotion words (attention guidance), and the pictorial representation of each expression created an associative cue likely to aid later recall (memory support).

#### 4.1.2. Affective perspective
The five groups primarily employed various GenAI tools to spark students’ interest in Chinese vocabulary learning (28 instances). According to the description in Table 2, sparking interest refers to evoking situational interest and boosting motivational engagement by means of video, music, or vivid visuals. In the present study, GenAI-generated audios, games, and highly saturated emoji-style visuals were frequently utilized. For instance, Group 4 embedded a GenAI-supported pronunciation game in the practice stage. Their tool invoked an intelligent speech-recognition engine that first modelled the standard pronunciation of the target words ‘快乐’ (happiness) and ‘哈哈笑’ (Hahaha laughter). Students then repeated the items aloud and received instantaneous, color-coded feedback (‘发音非常标准 Pronunciation is excellent!’ or ‘注意声调和节奏哦Pay attention to tone and rhythm!’) on accuracy of segments, tone, and rhythm memory. This created an engaging learning environment in which every correct pronunciation was rewarded with immediate visual praise and every error triggered supportive hints, thereby sustaining students’ situational interest and reinforcing the core vocabulary in the same affective move.
#### 4.1.3. Sociocultural perspective
Contextualization was the most frequently invoked sociocultural function, accounting for 10 instances recorded (Table 2). It refers to introducing contextual settings \*via\* authentic-like images or videos to enhance situational authenticity. Group 2 exemplified this strategy in their lead-in stage. The participants generated three photographs to display distinct emotional states; students were then invited to talk about their feelings when viewing each image. These photos, used in conjunction with the design of classroom discussion, created an immediate contextual setting for the target emotion-related vocabulary.
There were two instances of cross-cultural understanding which refers to offering multilingual and intercultural mediation to help students notice culture-specific emotion expressions. This code captures instances where GenAI resources were used to highlight cultural nuances of emotional display that differ from the learners’ own cultural norms. Mindful of the sizeable Indian-descent pupil population among young L2 Chinese learners in Hong Kong, Group 3 generated paired pictures depicting both Chinese and Indian children (Figure 5), thereby creating an intercultural reference point for emotion-talk activities.
### 4.2. The contents and interactions of TPACK
As reported in Table 3, the dataset revealed a coherent topology of the three core domains, PK, CK, and TK, each articulated through a set of empirically grounded themes and sub-themes. Triangulated with the findings obtained in [section 4.1](#S0004-S2001), we first examined the substance of these three knowledge components. We then analyzed the interactions among PK, CK and TK, tracing how the participants’ lesson planning instantiated the integrative domain of TPACK.
#### 4.2.1. Pedagogical knowledge
Within the PK dimension, the participants demonstrated a nuanced understanding of learner characteristics, instructional methods, and teacher roles. Under the theme of learner characteristics, three sub-themes were identified: attention span, cognitive development, and cultural differences (Table 3). First, the participants consistently emphasized primary school students’ limited attention span, noting that instructional materials must be concise and visually engaging to maintain focus. For instance, G5S2 remarked in the interview, ‘video introductions must be concise and paired with guiding questions; otherwise, students’ attention easily drifts.’ Second, the participants acknowledged the characteristics of primary school students’ cognitive development, that is, their way of thinking remains concrete and image-based, particularly when dealing with abstract emotion vocabulary. G3S4 explained in the interview, ‘emotions are abstract, and GenAI-generated images can render them into tangible forms for instruction.’ This point was evidenced by the frequently employed image-generation function during lesson planning (Table 4). Third, cultural differences in emotional expression were highlighted as a key consideration. For instance, G3S2 documented in the reflective form that ‘primary school students in Hong Kong come from different cultures; instruction must account for cultural differences in emotional expression and interpretation’, as reflected by the inclusion of culture elements in the teaching slides (Figure 5).
The knowledge of instructional methods was exemplified by contextualized, gamified, and visual instruction, as well as multimodal integration. Contextualization was widely used to embed vocabulary within meaningful narratives. G2S4, for example, reported in the interview that ‘we should create the context for students to learn and use the vocabulary. This will facilitate their understanding.’ This is reflected by their group’s creation of emotion-themed pictures and subsequent student discussions about feelings evoked by these images (Figure 4). Gamification was another important aspect of instructional methods. G1S5 noted in the interview that ‘multimodal GenAI can gamify vocabulary learning, deepening understanding and memory.’ Similar approach was also adopted by other groups, such as the pronunciation game developed by Group 4 (Figure 3). Visual instruction was emphasized as a means to support character recognition. G4S2 reported in the reflective form that ‘in character recognition, pictographic origins were taught using images to facilitate identification.’ Additionally, multimodal integration, that is, combining audio, visual, and kinesthetic inputs, was frequently mentioned as a strategy to reinforce memory, as illustrated by G5S2’s remark in the interview that ‘playing pronunciation and stroke-order animations engages multiple senses in learning Chinese characters.’

Concerning teacher role, despite the extensive use of GenAI tools, the participants consistently affirmed the central role of teacher in instructional design and delivery. They emphasized a teacher-led, AI-supported model, where technology serves as a supplement rather than a substitute (e.g. G1S5: ‘Excessive use of GenAI may distract students and undermine the teacher’s central role.’ Interview). This critical stance was echoed by others who advocated for the critical screening and adaptation of GenAI-generated materials. G5S1 stated in the interview, ‘GenAI-generated materials may not align with objectives; we revised them based on textbook standards.’ Moreover, some participants viewed the integration of GenAI as an opportunity for professional growth. For instance, technology can act as a catalyst for reflective practice and instructional innovation when used judiciously (e.g. G3S3: ‘GenAI can inspire creativity and support teachers in developing novel pedagogical approaches.’ Interview).
#### 4.2.2. Content knowledge
The knowledge of CK dimension embodied two overarching themes, language knowledge and cultural awareness, each instantiated through specific sub-themes (Table 3). Concerning language knowledge, the first sub-theme addressed Chinese character morphology. The participants consistently mentioned the pictographic and compounding logics of the Chinese character. G4S2, for instance, stated in the interview that ‘Chinese is a pictographic writing system that combines form and meaning.’ The second sub-theme concerned phonetic-semantic properties of the lexicon, with particular attention to polyphonic and polysemous items that frequently confuse young learners in Hong Kong. G4S1 noted in the reflective form that the mismatches between pronunciation and meaning ‘can easily lead to lexical confusion and impede vocabulary retention.’
Regarding cultural awareness, the participants exhibited both intercultural and local sensitivity in Chinese language teaching. Interculturally, G3S4 indicated in the interview that ‘it is essential to consider students’ diverse cultural backgrounds,’ a stance reflected in paired illustrations that juxtaposed Chinese and Indian children displaying identical emotions (Figure 5). Locally, Group 2 illustrated the importance of local Chinese culture in their teaching syllabus, ‘When non-Chinese-speaking students learn Chinese culture, they can gain a better understanding of China, as well as the social environment and folk culture of Hong Kong where they live, so as to integrate better into community life’ (Figure 6)
#### 4.2.3. Technological knowledge
The knowledge of TK dimension encompassed two overarching themes: digital resource management and generation and assessment and feedback (Table 3). Under digital resource management and generation, the first sub-theme concerned instructional material generation. The participants leveraged GenAI to produce multimodal artefacts (e.g. G4S3: ‘In vocabulary instruction, we used GenAI to generate pictures to help students understand the words visually.’ Interview). This practice was repeatedly echoed across groups, such as the examples in Figure 1 by Group 1. The second sub-theme, instructional material revision, captured the iterative refinement cycle that participants deemed essential. As G4S2 explained in the interview, the first prompt rarely yielded classroom-ready output, necessitating two or three rounds of prompt refinement, ‘adjusting the contents and requesting a Hong Kong setting’ to ensure accuracy. The third sub-theme, resource integration, referred to the embedding of various GenAI-generated contents into the teaching platform. G5S1 described a typical workflow in the reflective form, ‘We first created vocabulary cards, and then tested them on computer to ensure usability in instruction.’ Similarly, Group 4 created a GenAI-powered pronunciation game and integrated it into their teaching slides (Figure 3).
The second major theme, assessment and feedback, was enacted through formative assessment and automated feedback. For formative assessment, the participants praised GenAI’s capacity to produce low-stakes, game-based quizzes that provide instant learning analytics. G5S3 reflected that ‘Kahoot was used to design post-class quizzes for online review. Its instant ranking provided formative data that highlighted which tones still needed remediation.’ Automated feedback, the second sub-theme, centered on pronunciation evaluation. G4S1 indicated in the interview that ‘GenAI pronunciation assessment gave instant feedback on correctness, though occasional inaccuracies occur.’
#### 4.2.4. TPACK in action: PK–CK–TK interplay
Based on the results reported in the previous three sections, we observed that CK, PK and TK did not operate as discrete silos; rather, they recursively interact and influence each other during multimodal GenAI-supported lesson-planning. First, the characteristics of GenAI itself tightened the nexus between TK and PK. On one hand, the participants regarded the technology as a catalyst for teacher professional development (PK). This perceived expansion of instructional repertoires encouraged the use of GenAI-driven assessment and feedback (TK). On the other hand, they were aware of GenAI’s limitations, such as unreliable output, thereby proposing a complementary, teacher-led while GenAI support mode (PK). This stance was related to the systematic revision and integration of GenAI-generated contents in lesson planning (TK). Together, these complementary two aspects constituted the lived enactment of TPK within the planning process.
Furthermore, CK and PK co-constrained each other. Knowledge of Chinese characters (i.e. the CK of pictographic roots, polyphonic and polysemous pitfalls) was filtered through PK insights into learners’ limited attention span and concrete-operational cognition. This intersection determined the choice of contextualized, gamified, and visually saturated instructional methods that could render abstract lexis tangible. Besides, the CK’s emphasis on cultural awareness was related to PK considerations of cultural inclusivity. Collectively, these intersecting demands propelled the key TK move: instructional material generation, wherein the participants orchestrated various GenAI tools to produce culturally tuned and graphically rich artefacts (e.g. pictures, audios, games), thereby reflecting their TPACK in GenAI-assisted lesson planning in L2 Chinese vocabulary teaching.
## 5. Discussion
### 5.1. RQ1: the employment of multimodal GenAI in L2 Chinese vocabulary lesson planning
This study examines how pre-service teachers used multimodal GenAI for lesson planning in primary-level L2 Chinese instruction in Hong Kong, filling the empirical gap in the literature on GenAI-supported L2 lesson planning (Guo et al., [Citation2025](#); Hong, [Citation2023](#)). By adopting multimodal GenAI that integrates images, audio, video and games rather than single-mode text generation, the study not only meets the diverse needs of primary classrooms but also provides a reference for future studies at other educational levels. Specifically, we examined tool-use frequency and functional categories during four stages of teaching. Image-generation tools were used most frequently, especially during the practice phase, interactive game tools occupied a moderate position, while video-, voice- and audio-generation utilities were less deployed and scattered across lead-in, teaching, and feedback stages. These analyses offer empirical evidence for GenAI-assisted L2 lesson planning. Compared to traditional non-GenAI lesson planning, which relies on static resource curation (Harris & Hofer, [Citation2011](#)), GenAI-assisted planning introduced iterative revision cycles (e.g. 2–3 prompts per output) and critical vetting demands unprecedented in pre-AI contexts, representing a fundamental shift from selection to co-creation.
The literature consistently highlights that technology-enhanced lesson planning is only effective when the chosen tools are tightly aligned with content objectives and learning activities (Harris & Hofer, [Citation2011](#)). Our analysis of the participants’ teaching materials shows that they operationalized this principle across the four instructional stages. For example, they matched image-generation utilities to pictorial illustration in lead-in (Figure 4) and employed interactive game tools for vocabulary practices (Figure 3). Such stage-specific employment indicates a deliberate effort to synchronize GenAI affordances with pedagogical intents. Furthermore, while Crosthwaite et al. ([Citation2023](#)) report that practitioners often struggle to weave technological outputs into coherent strategies, the vast majority of our participants displayed a robust capacity to re-design, adapt, and integrate GenAI-generated materials. This success may be attributed to two course-design features. First, every group was required to complete a template which documented the difficulties they encountered, as well as their solutions and reflections (Appendix A). Second, planning was performed collaboratively, allowing peer questioning and discussion. Therefore, the cyclical interplay of experimentation, reflection, and discussion may support the participants to overcome the strategic-integration dilemma.

Notably, our findings respond directly to the concern raised by Dornburg and Davin ([Citation2025](#)) that GenAI-generated lesson plans frequently carry hidden cultural biases. This endeavor neutralized the cultural bias embedded in the initial GenAI outputs and modeled for pupils how linguistic accuracy and cultural relevance coexist in Chinese literacy development.
### 5.2. RQ2: Constituents and integration of TPACK in GenAI-supported lesson planning
Viewed through the TPACK framework, the lesson planning episodes illuminate how the participants negotiated content accuracy, pedagogical appropriateness, and technological feasibility when embedding GenAI into their lesson designs (Koehler et al., [Citation2013](#); Mishra & Koehler, [Citation2006](#)). By triangulating data from multiple sources, the present study provides a more reliable and comprehensive account of TPACK.
Specifically, pedagogical knowledge was manifested in the participants’ understanding of primary pupils’ limited attention spans, concrete-imagistic thinking, and multicultural emotional repertoires. Content knowledge focused on the distinct characteristics of Chinese vocabulary learning, including the pictographic logic of characters, the polyphonic/polysemous traps created by Cantonese-Putonghua exposure, and the need to weave in both Chinese cultures and learners’ home cultures. Technological knowledge was reflected in two ways: generating and refining relevant teaching materials, and providing immediate assessment and feedback with the assistance of GenAI. Notably, although our study employed traditional TPACK, participants’ cultural vetting practices align with AI-TPACK’s ethical dimension. As Celik ([Citation2023](#)) cautions, GenAI can widen inequality gaps \*via\* biased training data, necessitating teacher competencies in assessing fairness and individual differences. Our participants’ deliberate inclusion of diverse cultural references in GenAI outputs represents nascent ethical reasoning, suggesting that foundational TPACK enactments may naturally develop the sensitivities later formalized in AI-TPACK.
These findings complement the existing literature that predominantly focuses on the investigations of TAPCK in English language course lesson planning (Crosthwaite et al., [Citation2023](#); Guo et al., [Citation2025](#)), where alphabetic systems reduce visual processing demands. Chinese vocabulary instruction requires pictographic decomposition and tonal-semantic mapping, affordances uniquely enabled by GenAI’s multimodal capabilities, as evidenced by participants’ frequent image-generation for character morphology. Koehler et al. ([Citation2013](#)) remind us that the content, pedagogical, and technological knowledge is always shaped by the particulars of a given educational setting. Our findings extend the framework into a GenAI-supported, primary-level L2 Chinese learning context in Hong Kong, providing a reference for future pertinent research in other educational and cultural settings.
Importantly, as shown in Figure 7, the findings explicate how GenAI’s affordances and flaws recursively co-constrain the three knowledge bases (i.e. TK, PK, and CK). Rather than functioning separately, they continually reshaped one another. The technology’s capacity to catalyze professional growth (PK) motivated adoption of GenAI-driven assessment and feedback (TK), while its inconsistent outputs demanded sustained professional judgment in revising and integrating materials, thereby instantiating the TPK nexus. Simultaneously, Chinese-character specifics and cultural inclusivity (CK), filtered through primary pupils’ limited attention and concrete cognition (PK), necessitated visual-contextual instructional approaches. This, in turn, prompted strategic orchestration of GenAI tools to craft culturally attuned, multi-sensory artefacts that embody the resulting TPACK fusion. These findings align with Mishra and Koehler ([Citation2006](#)) and Koehler et al. ([Citation2013](#)) dynamic TPACK formulation, and also operationalize the TPACK framework to the context of GenAI-assisted L2 Chinese vocabulary lesson planning.

### 5.3. Practical implications
Based on the findings, we propose the following practical implications for lesson planning that incorporates multimodal GenAI in L2 instruction. Firstly, teachers may collaborate as a team when employing various GenAI tools for lesson planning. As reported in the present study, a collaborative format may pool pedagogical insights that solitary planning often misses. Peer discussion continuously cross-checks GenAI suggestions against students’ cognitive levels, cultural references, and curriculum goals. Institutionalizing such co-design circles therefore may turn GenAI from an individual productivity tool into a communally vetted resource, enhancing both instructional quality and teacher confidence. These collaborative cycles are equally vital for in-service teachers, whose robust pedagogical content knowledge may further enhance GenAI integration, as reflected by the informed judgment of experienced practitioners like G4S1 and G4S2 who vigilantly addressed polyphonic/polysemous pitfalls and iteratively refined GenAI outputs for character morphology. Professional development should therefore scaffold these collaborative cycles through structured training, enabling teachers to systematically audit GenAI outputs against curriculum goals.
Secondly, teachers must deliberately audit and localize the cultural content embedded in GenAI-generated materials while addressing broader ethical and bias considerations. Model training data disproportionately reflect dominant discourses; therefore, outputs can omit or misrepresent the cultural practices that L2 learners actually encounter (Dornburg & Davin, [Citation2025](#)). Teachers may consistently encounter such algorithmic biases, including culturally mismatched imagery and pronunciation errors, requiring systematic human vetting. Since language acquisition is inseparable from cultural meaning-making, teachers should build a systematic cultural checkpoint into their planning routine, comparing GenAI-produced images, audio, and texts against the local context and inserting community-specific references. Teacher education programs should develop critical AI literacy protocols that include auditing training data provenance, evaluating output fairness across learner demographics, and establishing transparent documentation of GenAI-assisted design decisions. Such targeted revision can ensure that multimodal GenAI resources support culturally responsive and ethically sound teaching.
Thirdly, teachers should deliberately expand the interactive dimension of multimodal GenAI. The lesson plans reported in the present study reveal a predominant reliance on image-generation tools, whereas conversational chatbots which are capable of sustaining real-time, learner-specific output were rarely exploited. Given that human–GenAI interaction is a critical aspect in L2 acquisition (Rong et al., [Citation2025](#)), teachers are advised to embed chatbots within their instructional sequences. By scripting chatbots that elicit vocabulary in culturally relevant scenarios and provide instant formative feedback, teachers can transform GenAI from a visual aid into an interactive speaking or writing partner, thereby multiplying opportunities for learners to practice and consolidate new lexical items.
However, these recommendations must be tempered by practical constraints. Beyond cultural bias, participants in the present study reported reliability issues with GenAI pronunciation feedback and occasional image-generation errors, necessitating teacher verification. Cost and accessibility also emerged as critical barriers, as premium GenAI tools may be unavailable in under-resourced schools, potentially widening digital equity gaps. Such disparities echo longstanding concerns in the literature over the digital divide and infrastructural readiness that shape actual technology uptake (Warschauer, [Citation2004](#)), particularly in the field of computer assisted language learning (Rafiee & Abbasian-Naghneh, [Citation2021](#)). These limitations highlight the need for institutional infrastructure, sustainable funding models, and critical AI literacy training to ensure equitable implementation across diverse educational contexts. Teacher education programs should therefore prepare educators to navigate not only pedagogical integration but also these systemic barriers.
## 6. Conclusion
This study investigated how pre-service teachers employed multimodal GenAI in lesson planning for primary-level L2 Chinese vocabulary instruction and how their pedagogical, content, and technological knowledge interacted within the TPACK framework. The findings revealed that the participants strategically aligned GenAI tools with instructional goals, with image-generation being the most frequently used. More importantly, the integration of CK, PK, and TK was shaped by learner characteristics, linguistic features of Chinese, and the affordances and limitations of GenAI. The participants actively filtered, revised, and localized GenAI-generated content to ensure pedagogical relevance and cultural appropriateness, demonstrating a dynamic and context-sensitive enactment of TPACK. Theoretically, this study extends the TPACK framework into a GenAI-supported, multilingual primary context, offering empirical evidence of how technology, pedagogy, and content knowledge co-evolve during lesson planning in this new environment. Practically, it highlights the importance of collaborative design, cultural vetting, and the expansion of interactive GenAI tools in teacher education and professional development.
Nevertheless, this study is not without limitations. Firstly, the participants were pre-service teachers within a single university context, which may limit the generalizability of the findings. Future research could expand to in-service teachers across diverse educational contexts. Comparative studies across different languages and proficiency levels would further enrich our understanding of how TPACK manifests in AI-supported L2 education.
Secondly, due to space constraints, this paper did not investigate the impact of teachers’ GenAI-supported lesson planning on student learning. While studies in other subjects, such as mathematics and physical education, have found that such an approach can improve student performance (Karaman & Göksu, [Citation2024](#); Yeadon & Hardy, [Citation2024](#)), future research could examine whether similar benefits occur in L2 contexts. Thirdly, while our TPACK-based analysis effectively captured foundational technology integration processes, AI-TPACK offers additional constructs, such as AI-specific literacy, human-AI co-orchestration, and systematic bias evaluation, which future research could employ for pertinent research. Finally, this study deliberately focused on teachers’ TPACK negotiation processes rather than the pedagogical effectiveness of GenAI-generated materials. We did not systematically evaluate whether these outputs achieved intended learning outcomes. For instance, Figure 1’s static image for ‘Bouncing and Jumping’ inadequately depicts motion, potentially limiting its utility for differentiation. Future research should therefore move beyond planning processes to examine the effectiveness of teaching materials and subsequent student learning gains through classroom implementation studies.
