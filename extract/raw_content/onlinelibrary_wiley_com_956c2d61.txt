## Abstract
The purpose of this study is to explore the role of prompt engineering in utilizing GenAI tools like EduAid, Magic School, and ChatGPT to enhance lesson planning efficiency and content relevance. Additionally, it highlights the need for AI literacy and continuous professional development to align AI outputs with diverse student needs. A qualitative case study was used to collect data from 17 high school teachers from private and governmental schools. Document analysis, written reflections, and focus group talks were used as data sources to get detailed information about how the teachers used and thought about GenAI tools. The results show that effective prompts helped teachers use GenAI tools better, which makes lesson planning and material more relevant. Teachers emphasized that AI-generated material needed to be constantly improved to better meet the needs of all of their students. This study highlights the importance of developing AI literacy through continuous professional training to effectively enable teachers to integrate AI tools into their teaching practices.
## 1. Introduction
Education is one of the many industries dramatically impacted by the quick development of technology [[1](#bib-0001)]. Among these technological advancements, prompt engineering in GenAI tools has emerged as a significant innovation [[2](#bib-0002), [3](#bib-0003)]. GenAI tools, including EduAid, Magic School, and ChatGPT, employ artificial intelligence (AI) to produce content derived from user input [[1](#bib-0001), [4](#bib-0004)]. The effectiveness of these tools heavily depends on the prompts entered by users, highlighting the importance of prompt engineering [[5](#bib-0005)]. Prompt engineering involves crafting precise and effective input to elicit AI systems’ most valuable and relevant output [[6](#bib-0006)]. This study examines the utilization of prompt engineering by teachers in Generative AI tools for lesson planning, as well as their experiences and perceptions regarding this practice.
Prompt engineering enhances the quality and relevance of AI-generated content, establishing it as a crucial skill for educators [[7](#bib-0007)]. Mastering prompt engineering enables educators to effectively utilize AI tools, thereby optimizing time management, generating varied educational content, and customizing lesson plans to address the unique requirements of their students [[8](#bib-0008)]. For example, teachers used different student-centered strategies to plan their lessons, such as the 5Es strategy, problem-based learning, project-based learning, or engineering design process [[9](#bib-0009)]. Among these, the 5E model, commonly used by teachers, is widely recognized for its structured approach to lesson planning, focusing on the engage, explore, explain, elaborate, and evaluate stages [[10](#bib-0010), [11](#bib-0011)]. It guides students from the curiosity of asking questions and being engaged in their learning process to reflection and evaluation.
Research indicates the considerable potential of AI tools in education, especially in the automation and enhancement of lesson planning [[9](#bib-0009)–[12](#bib-0012)]. The effectiveness of these tools is significantly influenced by the quality of the prompts provided by users [[13](#bib-0013)]. Prompt engineering is an essential competency that allows educators to derive the most pertinent and beneficial content from AI systems [[2](#bib-0002), [7](#bib-0007), [13](#bib-0013)]. Although the potential is acknowledged, research specifically examining the role of prompt engineering in educational contexts remains limited. This study investigates the practical applications of prompt engineering within educational contexts, contributing to the development of essential AI literacy skills for the future. Educators must develop AI literacy skills to effectively integrate AI tools into their teaching practices [[1](#bib-0001), [14](#bib-0014)]. AI literacy encompasses comprehension of AI system functionality, effective interaction with these systems, and critical evaluation of AI-generated outputs [[15](#bib-0015)]. Prior research highlights the significance of AI literacy in equipping educators and students for a technology-driven future [[11](#bib-0011), [14](#bib-0014)–[16](#bib-0016)].
Regardless of the growing interest in AI in education, significant gaps remain in the literature regarding the practical applications of prompt engineering with GenAI tools for lesson planning [[1](#bib-0001)]. For example, some studies have specifically examined how teachers interact with GenAI tools, the effectiveness of the prompts generated, and the overall impact on lesson-planning processes [[2](#bib-0002), [13](#bib-0013), [17](#bib-0017)]. This study addresses these gaps by providing empirical evidence on using generative AI tools in educational settings and offering recommendations for their effective integration.
The current literature offers a broad view of AI’s potential in education but often lacks specific insights into the practical challenges and strategies for effective prompt engineering. For instance, studies by Heston [[2](#bib-0002)] and Nazary et al. [[13](#bib-0013)] emphasize the importance of prompt quality but do not explore teachers’ experiences crafting these prompts. Similarly, research by Chiu et al. [[7](#bib-0007)] highlights the advantages of AI-generated content, yet it does not adequately address the iterative process educators engage in to refine prompts. This study seeks to address existing gaps by thoroughly examining teachers’ experiences with GenAI tools, emphasizing the iterative process of prompt engineering and its influence on lesson planning.
Mastering prompt engineering is essential for AI literacy, allowing teachers to utilize GenAI tools in lesson planning. This study enhances existing literature by examining the development and application of AI literacy skills among teachers via prompt engineering. This study is essential due to the increasing incorporation of AI technologies in education and the imperative to assist teachers in enhancing their AI and digital literacy skills [[18](#bib-0018)]. In the United Arab Emirates (UAE), there is a strong emphasis on incorporating digital tools to enhance educational outcomes, which aligns with the national agenda of UAE Vision 2021 [[19](#bib-0019)]. The national agenda is aimed at establishing a first-rate education system using advanced technologies to improve teaching and learning outcomes. Furthermore, the study supports Sustainable Development Goal 4: Quality Education, which emphasizes the importance of inclusive and equitable education for all [[20](#bib-0020), [21](#bib-0021)].
Reflective practice is a critical component of teacher professional development. Schön’s concepts of reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action provide a robust foundation for understanding teacher-reflective practices. Reflection-in-action refers to the process by which educators assess their experiences and implement immediate modifications to enhance teaching methods and results [[22](#bib-0022)]. This prompt reflection enables educators to adjust their methods in real-time, thereby improving the learning experience as it progresses. Conversely, reflection-on-action necessitates that educators analyze their experiences postevent, facilitating comprehensive analysis and fostering long-term enhancements. This type of reflection facilitates a more thorough evaluation of teaching practices and outcomes, promoting continuous professional development.
Complementing Schön’s theories, Kolb’s experiential learning theory emphasizes learning through experience and reflection. According to Kolb [[23](#bib-0023)], effective learning occurs through a cyclical process involving concrete experience, reflective observation, abstract conceptualization, and active experimentation. This cyclical process provides a structured framework for capturing teachers’ reflective practices as they interact with AI tools for lesson planning. Kolb’s model provides a comprehensive understanding of how teachers learn from their experiences and apply new knowledge to their practice.
Kolb’s experiential learning model further delineates the process of experiential learning by incorporating stages of reflection and conceptualization [[23](#bib-0023)]. This study builds on this literature by examining how teachers reflect on their use of AI tools both during the lesson planning process (reflection-in-action) and after completing their lesson plans (reflection-on-action). By integrating these theories, the study is aimed at providing a deeper understanding of teachers’ experiences and insights, thereby informing the development of practical AI tools for educational purposes.
This study is aimed at investigating the effectiveness of teachers in employing prompt engineering when utilizing GenAI tools for lesson planning. This study evaluates the effectiveness of prompts created and utilized by teachers, their experiences with prompt engineering, and their perceptions of various GenAI tools in lesson planning. The following research questions guide the study:
\* 1.
How effective are the prompts entered by teachers in generating lesson plans using generative AI tools? How effective are the resulting outputs in meeting lesson planning objectives?
\* 2.
What are the teachers’ experiences with using prompt engineering in generative AI tools to plan their lessons?
\* 3.
What are the teachers’ perceptions of using prompt engineering in planning their lessons with different generative AI tools?
## 2. Methodology
This study employed a qualitative case study design utilizing triangulation to explore the effectiveness of prompt engineering in GenAI tools for lesson planning. The qualitative approach is well-suited to gain in-depth insights into teachers’ experiences and perceptions, particularly in educational research where context and participant perspectives are crucial [[24](#bib-0024)].
This design is appropriate when the boundaries between the phenomenon and the context are unclear [[25](#bib-0025)]. This study examines the effectiveness of prompt engineering within the context of teachers’ plans utilizing GenAI. The case study method facilitates an in-depth analysis of this interaction in its natural context. This study utilized methodological triangulation to improve the validity and reliability of the findings, incorporating multiple data sources to examine the phenomenon [[26](#bib-0026)]. Triangulation facilitates the cross-verification of data, yielding a more comprehensive and balanced understanding of the research problem [[27](#bib-0027)].
### 2.1. Participants
The participants in this study were 17 high school teachers purposefully selected from private (\*n\*1 = 7, 41.18%) and governmental (\*n\*2 = 10, 58.82%) schools. The primary criterion for selection was the teachers’ willingness to engage in the study and complete the tasks as outlined. This approach ensured that the participants had a genuine interest in exploring the potential of AI in their teaching practice.
The sample consisted of a greater proportion of female teachers, with 12 (70.59%) females and 5 (29.41%) males. The teachers instructed a variety of grade levels. Specifically, one (5.88%) taught Grades 8–9, one (5.88%) taught Grades 8–11, two (11.76%) taught Grades 8–10, and one (5.88%) taught both Grades 8 and 10. Other teachers indicated sole grade level teaching: 3 (17.65%) taught Grade 8, 2 (11.76%) each for Grade 11 and Grade 12, 2 (11.76%) taught Grade 10, and 1 teacher (5.88%) taught Grades 11–12. This indicates that the surveyed teachers cover a wide range of grade levels.
Science emerged as the most commonly taught subject, with nine educators (52.94%) instructing in biology, chemistry, physics, environmental science, and general science. Mathematics accounted for five teachers, representing 29.41% of the total. Each subject, including English, Computer Science, and Geography, was represented by a single teacher, accounting for 5.88%. This demonstrates a robust representation of STEM disciplines. Most teachers, 10 (58.82%), indicated 11–15 years of teaching experience. Of the participants, 7 (41.18%) possessed 6–10 years of teaching experience, suggesting that all individuals were relatively experienced in the field of education. The ages of the teachers exhibited considerable variation, categorized as follows: 3 (17.65%) aged 30–35 years, 7 (41.18%) aged 36–40 years, 6 (35.29%) aged 40–45 years, and 1 (5.88%) aged 46–50 years.
### 2.2. Instruments
#### 2.2.1. Teachers’ Task (Document Analysis)
The task assigned to the teachers aimed to examine the utilization of GenAI tools by educators in lesson planning, thereby addressing the initial research question of the study. The assignment required developing a 20–30 min lesson plan utilizing backward design principles, focusing on defining end goals first and then planning in reverse to align objectives, activities, and assessments [[28](#bib-0028)]. The aim is to document the prompts submitted by educators to AI tools and the corresponding outputs produced by these tools.
Teachers were instructed to create a lesson plan utilizing an AI tool with which they are familiar, including options such as EduAid.AI, Magic School, and ChatGPT. The document analysis primarily concentrated on the prompts submitted by the teachers and the resultant outputs produced by the AI tools in the sections outlined in Appendix [A](#sec-0056). In this task, teachers anticipated that AI tools would produce detailed, sequential lesson plans. However, several AI-generated outputs were overly broad, requiring additional modifications to fulfill specific classroom requirements.
#### 2.2.2. Teachers’ Written Reflection
The teachers’ written reflections were an integral part of the study, providing valuable insights into their experiences with GenAI tools for lesson planning, which addresses the second research question of the study. Using Kolb’s [[23](#bib-0023)] experiential learning model, the teachers reflected on their activities, observations, challenges, and strategies and considered how their practices might evolve (see Appendix [B](#sec-0057) for the reflective questions).
#### 2.2.3. Focus Group Discussion
The focus group discussion is aimed at addressing the third research question of the study. The objective is to gain a deeper understanding of teachers’ experiences and to collect qualitative insights that cannot be obtained solely through document analysis and written reflections (reflection-on-action).
The focus groups were conducted online using the Zoom conference platform, lasting 45–50 min. Ten teachers participated in the discussion, distributed equally into two groups, structured around semistructured, open-ended, reflective questions adapted from Kolb’s experiential learning model. The questions were sent to two educational experts to determine their face validity and clarity. The experts compared the questions to the purpose of the study and provided feedback. They commended the questions for their alignment with the study’s objectives and their ability to elicit detailed and meaningful responses from the participants (see Appendix [C](#sec-0058)).
### 2.3. Procedure
A qualitative case study approach was employed utilizing methodological triangulation, incorporating data collection through document analysis, written reflections, and focus group discussions.
First, an initial meeting was conducted with the participating teachers to ensure clarity and consistency in task completion. The meeting outlined the study’s purpose and presented detailed guidelines. Educators received an overview of the task specifications and the application of backward design in lesson planning. The study discussed the selected GenAI tools and the documentation procedures for input and output. After the meeting, teachers received an online link where they could copy the prompts entered into the AI tools and a copy of the outputs generated by the AI tools.
Then, the participants completed a reflective questionnaire based on Kolb’s experiential learning model [[23](#bib-0023)], detailing their experiences and insights from using the GenAI tools (reflection-in-action). These reflections included descriptions of their activities, observations of the AI tools’ effectiveness, conceptualizations for improvement, and plans for future use of AI tools in lesson planning. The reflections helped identify challenges faced in writing prompts for GenAI tools and how teachers modified their prompts to get better outcomes. The reflections were submitted through an online platform.
The focus group discussion was conducted online via the Zoom conference platform to examine teachers’ perceptions regarding using GenAI tools (reflection-on-action). The data obtained from document analysis, written reflections, and focus group discussions were analyzed thematically to identify principal themes and subthemes [[29](#bib-0029)]. This method facilitated the incorporation of various viewpoints and a thorough assessment of the efficacy of prompt engineering in GenAI tools. Integrating data from various sources facilitated a comprehensive and rigorous analysis, mitigating bias associated with relying on a single data type and aiding in validating the findings.
#### 2.3.1. Ethical Considerations
The research ethical committee of Zayed University approved the study and assigned a Research Incentive Grant (RIF) no. 23071, with Ethics Application Number ZU23\\_068\\_F. Participants received consent forms at the beginning of the study. They were informed that their participation was voluntary, anonymous, and confidential and that they could withdraw from the study at anytime. Accordingly, informed consent was obtained from all participants, ensuring their voluntary participation and confidentiality.
## 3. Results

One hour was the duration selected by six teachers, representing 35.29% of the total, thus making it the most prevalent choice. Four teachers accounted for 23.53% of the sample, taking 1.5 h, while three teachers represented 17.65%, taking 2 h. Two teachers (11.76%) completed the task in 30 min, while one teacher each (5.88%) took 10, 20, and 45 min. This variation suggests differing levels of familiarity with GenAI tools.

#### 3.1.1. Objectives
The Objectives section lists the goals that educators hope to accomplish with their lesson plans. The objectives produced by AI tools following multiple prompts from educators promote clarity and coherence in instructional goals. For example,
Prompt entered by T1: “Rewrite exponential functions to identify rates. Interpret the parameters of an exponential function within the context of compound interest problems. Construct exponential models given two points or by using regression.”
Objective generated: “Students will be able to rewrite exponential functions to identify rates, interpret the parameters of an exponential function within the context of compound interest problems, and construct exponential models given two points or by using regression.”
T3 entered: “Students will be able to demonstrate an understanding of osmosis in plant and animal cells, including its effects on cell structure and function.”
Objective generated: “Students will understand the process of osmosis in plant and animal cells and how it impacts cell structure and function.”
Similar prompts across different subjects highlighted the adaptability of the tools.
T9 entered: “Create objectives for a lesson on the principles of chemical bonding.”
Objective generated: “Students will explain the types of chemical bonds and their properties.”
#### 3.1.2. Introduction
The introduction sets the stage for the lesson, capturing students’ attention and providing context. The generated introductions were contextually relevant, often enhancing the teachers’ initial prompts. For example,
T2 entered: “Begin by showing students a simple electrolytic cell setup. Ask students to brainstorm any applications they know of that use electrolytic cells.”
Introduction generated: “Students will explore various applications that use electrolytic cells and understand the underlying principles of electrolysis.”
T3 entered: “Show students the pictures of the potato and strawberry soaked in sugar solution. Ask students to make observations about the changes they see.”
Introduction generated: “Students will engage in a hands-on exploration using a picture of a strawberry soaked in a sugar solution to observe and analyze the changes that occur over a period of time.”
Other introductions included the following:
T9 entered: “How can I introduce the concept of chemical bonding to high school students?”
Introduction generated: “Start with a short video explaining the different types of chemical bonds followed by a class discussion.”
#### 3.1.3. Main Activity
The main activity is the core part of the lesson, where students engage in hands-on learning. The generated activities were interactive and diverse, ensuring student engagement and practical understanding. Examples include the following:
T1 entered: “Explore (8–10 minutes): Provide students with simple exponential functions and compound interest problems to solve in pairs.”
Activity generated: “Think-Pair-Share Activity: Exponential Functions and Compound Interest. Step 1: Think. Step 2: Pair. Step 3: Share.”
Similar prompts resulted in similarly effective responses.
T8 entered: “Design a hands-on activity for teaching Newton’s laws of motion.”
Activity generated: “Students will perform experiments to demonstrate each of Newton’s laws using a cart and track.”
T16 entered: “Develop a hands-on experiment for a lesson on acid-base reactions.”
Activity generated: “Students will perform titrations to determine the concentration of an unknown acid or base.”
#### 3.1.4. Technology Integration and Differentiation
This section involves incorporating technology and addressing diverse learning needs. The generated suggestions were practical and tailored to enhance learning experiences. For example,
T2 entered: “Engage: Begin the lesson by showing students a real-life example of an electrolytic cell in action, such as the electrolysis of water to produce hydrogen and oxygen.”
Output: “Integration of Technology: Utilize simulation software or virtual labs to allow students to explore electrolytic cells in a digital format.”
T3 entered: “Provide a virtual lab platform where students can perform experiments.”
Output: “Add links to high-quality animations and diagrams on osmosis.”
T11 entered: “Develop a plan for using technology in a lesson on climate change impacts.”
Output: “Use climate modeling software to predict future climate change impacts and scenarios.”
#### 3.1.5. Formative Assessment Planning
Formative assessments help gauge student understanding throughout the lesson. The generated assessments were diverse and aimed at continuous evaluation. Examples include the following:
T1 entered: “Evaluate (5–7 min): Provide students with a set of problems that require them to rewrite exponential functions, interpret parameters, and construct exponential models.”
Output generated: “Students will be able to rewrite exponential functions to identify rates, interpret the parameters of an exponential function within the context of compound interest problems, and construct exponential models given two points or by using regression.”
Similar prompts were addressed with tailored formative assessments.
T8 entered: “Generate formative assessment ideas for a physics lesson on Newton’s laws of motion.”
Output generated: “Use quick quizzes and concept maps to assess understanding of Newton’s laws.”
T16 entered: “How can I assess students’ understanding in a chemistry lesson on acid-base reactions?”
Output generated: “Utilize lab reports and interactive quizzes to assess understanding of acid-base reactions.”
### 3.2. Teachers’ Written Reflections
Teachers’ evaluations of GenAI tools for lesson planning indicate a beneficial effect on efficiency and organization. Challenges concerning specificity, alignment with educational standards, and the necessity for refinement were identified. The reflections suggest significant potential for AI tools to improve educational practices, contingent upon their ongoing development to address the specific needs of educators and diverse student populations. ## 4. Discussion
### 4.1. RQ1: How Effective Are the Prompts Entered by Teachers in Generating Lesson Plans Using Generative AI Tools? How Effective Are the Resulting Outputs in Meeting Lesson Planning Objectives?
The analysis of teachers’ tasks revealed notable variations in the duration taken to complete the AI-assisted lesson planning, with the most common duration being 1 h. This variation likely reflects differing levels of familiarity and proficiency with GenAI tools, a factor noted in previous research by Nazary et al. [[13](#bib-0013)].
The variation in time taken reflects the iterative nature of prompt engineering, as described by Chiu et al. [[7](#bib-0007)]. Teachers who required more time likely engaged in additional iterations to enhance their prompts, demonstrating the significance of iterative processes in obtaining high-quality AI-generated results. This finding highlights the necessity for thorough training in prompt engineering to improve the utilization of GenAI tools in lesson planning.
Most teachers assessed their experience with AI tools as moderate to high, indicating a general comfort with the technology. The presence of certain teachers needing additional support highlights the necessity for continuous professional development in AI literacy. This is consistent with existing literature [[1](#bib-0001), [14](#bib-0014)], which supports the need for educators to acquire skills essential for the effective integration of AI into teaching practices.
The evaluation of specific prompts and generated outputs revealed a high degree of coherence and alignment, especially in formulating instructional objectives. For example, when a teacher provided a prompt regarding exponential functions and compound interest, the AI’s output aligned closely with the specified educational objectives. This indicates that well-designed prompts can effectively direct AI tools to generate pertinent and precise content, supporting the conclusions of Heston [[2](#bib-0002)]. The adaptability of GenAI tools across various subjects was also apparent. Prompts in physics, chemistry, biology, and mathematics produced precise and contextually relevant learning objectives. A prompt for a high school physics lesson on Newton’s laws produced an objective that explicitly outlined the application of these laws. The ability to generate subject-specific content aligns with the findings of Druga et al. [[11](#bib-0011)], who highlighted the extensive applicability of AI in creating educational materials.
Moreover, the AI-generated introductions and main activities were particularly effective in enhancing lesson engagement and ensuring practical understanding. For instance, an engaging introduction for a lesson on Newton’s laws of motion using a cart and track demonstration was directly aligned with the teacher’s prompt. Other examples, such as hands-on activities for teaching osmosis and titrations in chemistry, demonstrated the AI’s capability to generate varied and practical learning tasks. These findings suggest that AI tools can significantly enhance lesson introductions, making them more engaging and contextually relevant, which is critical for effective teaching [[7](#bib-0007), [15](#bib-0015)].
The AI-generated suggestions for technology integration and differentiation were practical and specifically designed to improve learning experiences. Incorporating virtual lab simulations for a lesson on cellular respiration and offering interactive online tutorials for a mixed-ability class on chemical bonding were effective strategies that catered to diverse learning needs. The findings are consistent with the work of Steinbauer et al. [[16](#bib-0016)], which examined the role of AI in facilitating personalized learning environments.
Planning for formative assessment was enhanced by AI-generated outputs, which provided a range of continuous evaluation methods. Exit tickets and peer evaluations for assessing understanding of the osmosis supplied educators with practical tools to measure student comprehension during the lesson. This aligns with the literature highlighting the role of AI in enhancing formative assessment practices [[10](#bib-0010)].
### 4.2. RQ2: What Are the Teachers’ Experiences With Using Prompt Engineering in Generative AI Tools to Plan Their Lessons?
#### 4.2.1. Concrete Experience (Feel)
Teachers reported a positive impact of GenAI tools such as EduAid.AI, Magic School AI, and ChatGPT on lesson planning, indicating that these tools enhanced efficiency in the initial stages of the process. This is consistent with the research conducted by Long and Magerko [[14](#bib-0014)] and Varghese and Chapiro [[1](#bib-0001)], which emphasizes the capacity of AI tools to improve educational practices by promoting efficiency and organization. EduAid.AI demonstrated effectiveness in generating detailed lesson components, facilitating teachers’ access to differentiated activities, thereby supporting Varghese & Chapiro [[1](#bib-0001)]. Magic School AI was recognized for its efficiency, particularly in generating ideas for assessment and differentiation. The findings indicate that GenAI tools can substantially decrease the time and effort needed for lesson planning, enabling teachers to concentrate more on instructional strategies and student engagement, as noted by the Author (2023).
Although the tools were user-friendly and beneficial, additional refinement was frequently required to ensure outputs matched particular classroom requirements. This underscores the iterative aspect of prompt engineering, as described by Chiu et al. [[7](#bib-0007)], highlighting the necessity of refining prompts to attain high-quality AI-generated outputs.
#### 4.2.2. Reflective Observation (Watch)
Observations revealed that AI tools enhanced lesson planning through improved structure and efficiency. Teachers utilizing ChatGPT observed that it facilitated the prompt generation process efficiently, although slight modifications were occasionally required to ensure alignment with educational standards and particular classroom settings. Several educators emphasized the effectiveness of EduAid.AI in content curation, recognizing its significant role in enhancing classroom activities. Challenges arose when AI-generated content required manual adjustments to align with specific classroom contexts, highlighting the significance of relevance and alignment with educational objectives, as noted by Nazary et al. [[13](#bib-0013)].
Despite these advantages, certain limitations were observed. Educators noted that AI tools occasionally generated excessively complex outputs or generalized recommendations that failed to sufficiently meet the varied learning needs and abilities within particular classrooms. This underscores the need for more specific and customized AI-generated content to address the particular requirements of educators, aligning with the conclusions of Druga et al. [[11](#bib-0011)] regarding the continuous evolution of AI tools to accommodate varied educational demands.

Teachers encountered several challenges with AI tools, particularly regarding the specificity and relevance of AI-generated content. Issues such as needing multiple iterations to refine prompts and difficulties aligning AI outputs with curriculum standards were common. Another challenge is the AI’s ability to fully understand context-specific needs. This finding aligns with the literature on AI literacy, emphasizing the need for educators to develop robust AI literacy skills to effectively integrate AI into their teaching practices [[1](#bib-0001), [14](#bib-0014)].

This emphasizes the complementary function of AI tools alongside the essential role of teacher expertise in education, consistent with the findings of Varghese and Chapiro [[1](#bib-0001)].
#### 4.3.7. Active Experimentation (Do)
Teachers reflected on potential modifications to their utilization of AI tools moving forward, demonstrating a proactive stance towards enhancing and tailoring their application of AI in educational contexts, as indicated by Steinbauer et al. [[16](#bib-0016)].
Several teachers indicated a desire to investigate AI tools with specialized functionalities, highlighting the necessity of continuously adapting AI technologies to address the changing demands of education. This aligns with the World Bank’s [[18](#bib-0018)] findings regarding the essential role of continuous professional development in AI literacy.
#### 4.3.8. Overall Impact and Future Use
Teachers evaluated the influence of AI tools on their understanding of AI in educational contexts and their feasibility for consistent integration in lesson planning. The findings highlight the necessity of harmonizing AI integration with professional expertise to facilitate effective and innovative lesson planning, as supported by Varghese and Chapiro [[1](#bib-0001)]. The necessity for guidance and training for new teachers was highlighted, emphasizing the importance of continuous professional development in facilitating the effective integration of AI technologies into teaching practices, as noted by the UAE National Committee on SDGs [[20](#bib-0020)].
## 5. Implications
The implications of this study are that, even with rich sources of lesson planning in GenAI tools, effective use entails a combination of continuous professional development, AI literacy, and an iterative model for prompt engineering. The results confirm that effective engineering of prompts helps teachers make a more direct link between lesson content and educational objectives. Besides, AI tools can reduce cognitive overload during lesson planning, and teachers can then devote their full attention to instruction and student activity. Teachers learned that AI output, even when useful, sometimes required refinement and retuning in an attempt to perfectly tailor it to specific individual classroom requirements. This underlines not only utilizing AI tools but also developing competencies for effective communication with such tools through effective prompt engineering. Iterative refinement of prompts is an important part of getting high-quality, relevant output, according to the study.
From a pragmatic perspective, the study promotes additional training programs for teachers in AI literacy, particularly in terms of prompt engineering. Teachers with proper training in developing effective prompts experienced easier incorporation of AI tools in lesson planning. However, tools must become even more specific and tailor-made in an effort to cater to the variegated and ever-changing demands of a classroom environment. Teachers have expressed a desire for more flexible AI capabilities that can respond to changing educational standards, grade levels, and subject-specific requirements. Additionally, this work highlights the necessity for a balanced model in which AI tools work to amplify, not supplant, teacher expertise. AI can, for example, work to make parts of lesson planning easier, but teachers will remain at the center of curating information in a way that makes it relevant, correct, and sensitive to students’ needs. As AI technology continues to mature, teachers’ expertise in tailoring AI output for use in their individual educational environments will become ever more critical.
## 6. Limitations
This study has several identified limitations. The study included teachers who volunteered to participate, which may introduce self-selection bias. The presence of teachers with prior interest or proficiency in AI tools may bias the results, leading to more favorable experiences. The study examines particular generative AI tools, such as EduAid, Magic School, and ChatGPT, which may not encompass the functionalities of other existing tools. This limitation may restrict the insights obtained concerning AI’s role in lesson planning. The study’s emphasis on high school teachers and particular grade levels may restrict its relevance to educators in other educational contexts, including elementary or tertiary education. The sample size of 17 high school teachers restricts the generalizability of the results. Further study is required to use a larger and more heterogeneous sample to validate the findings across other educational contexts. The study depends on self-reported data from teachers, which may be influenced by personal biases or inaccuracies in memory regarding their experiences.
## 7. Recommendations
This study recommends educator training that emphasizes key areas to improve the effectiveness of generative AI tools in lesson planning. Comprehensive AI literacy programs must be implemented to ensure that teachers grasp the fundamentals of AI and demonstrate proficiency in prompt engineering, especially in the iterative refinement process. Training should emphasize the role of AI tools in facilitating personalized learning through customized strategies that address the varied needs of students. The training should assist teachers in tailoring AI outputs to fit particular classroom contexts, thereby ensuring that AI enhances their professional expertise. Educators should utilize AI tools to develop formative assessments, including peer evaluations and exit tickets, to facilitate ongoing student evaluation. The training should prioritize the role of AI in optimizing lesson planning, thereby minimizing time allocated to administrative duties, enabling educators to concentrate on instructional methods, and enhancing student engagement.
## 8. Conclusion
This study provides critical insights into the significant role of prompt engineering with GenAI tools in lesson planning, emphasizing its potential to improve the educational process substantially. The findings demonstrate that practical and iterative prompt engineering allows teachers to maximize the potential of GenAI tools, including EduAid, Magic School, and ChatGPT, to develop more impactful and pertinent lesson content. Effective prompt engineering facilitates the development of customized lesson plans that cater to varied student needs and enhance efficiency in the planning process, as evidenced by the research of Chiu et al. [[7](#bib-0007)] and Nazary et al. [[13](#bib-0013)].
The findings indicate that although GenAI tools offer teachers organized and efficient solutions, manual refinement of AI outputs is frequently required to align with particular classroom needs. This highlights the significance of AI literacy in education, especially the iterative refinement process essential for enhancing AI-generated content [[2](#bib-0002), [7](#bib-0007)]. Teachers reported favorable experiences with these tools, indicating that prompt engineering enhanced the quality and relevance of lesson outputs [[1](#bib-0001)]. Nonetheless, specific challenges, including the AI’s restricted capacity to adequately address the diverse abilities of students, were also observed [[11](#bib-0011)].
This study emphasizes the necessity of ongoing professional development in AI literacy, corresponding with the essential requirement for educators to effectively incorporate AI tools into their instructional methods [[1](#bib-0001), [14](#bib-0014)]. The evolution of AI necessitates that educators maintain proficiency in developing prompts and refining AI outputs to align with educational standards. The World Bank [[18](#bib-0018)] emphasized that fostering AI literacy skills is essential for educators to promote inclusive and equitable learning environments.
This study confirms that prompt engineering is essential for educators to optimize using GenAI tools. Although these tools provide significant advantages in time efficiency and content creation, the expertise of teachers is critical for refining AI outputs to align with curriculum standards and address specific student needs. As GenAI shapes the future of education, it is necessary to equip educators with prompt engineering skills and AI literacy to promote high-quality, personalized, and inclusive education [[13](#bib-0013), [21](#bib-0021)].

Objective(s): Teachers began by defining clear, specific learning objectives for their lesson. These objectives were entered into the AI tools to generate relevant content and strategies.
\* 2.

\* 5.
