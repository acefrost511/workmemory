Abstract
--------

This study aimed to develop and validate a digital skills self-assessment tool for Serbian youth (DSATy), based on the Digital Competence Framework (DigComp). Following scale development procedures, an initial set of 54 constructed items was refined to 45 after piloting and cognitive interviews with students. In a cross-sectional survey, the questionnaire was completed by 914 secondary school and undergraduate students. Subsequently, the sample was divided into two balanced subsamples by gender and educational level: an index sample (*n* = 452) for exploratory factor analysis and a reserve sample (*n* = 462) for confirmatory factor analysis. The analyses revealed a valid and reliable seven-factor structure with 33 items, integrating four practical/technical skills (Managing Data and Collaboration, Content Creation, Device Protection, Problem Solving) and three cognitive digital skills (Evaluating Information and Content, Critical Knowledge for Online Communication, Health and Environmental Protection). This sophisticated structure of practical and cognitive digital skills was confirmed through a second-order model and measurement invariance testing across gender and educational level. Validated and reliable, DSATy fills the existing gap in Serbia for the target population by providing policymakers and educational institutions with a tool for comprehensive assessment, monitoring, and promotion of digital skills to enhance learning outcomes and prepare students for active, safe, and responsible participation in the digital society. Grounded in the European DigComp framework, DSATy also offers opportunities for future cross-cultural validation.

1 Introduction
--------------

The digital revolution has profoundly transformed Europe’s economic and social landscape, integrating information and communication technology (ICT) into nearly every aspect of daily life. In the expansive realm of digital media and the rapid advancement of ICT tools and devices, digital literacy and skills have become essential for active participation in knowledge-based societies. Recognizing this, the Council of Europe identified digital competence as one of the key skills for personal well-being, employability, and active citizenship (European Parliament and Council of the European Union, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR72); Council of the European Union, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR15)). Aligned with quality education and Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 4.4.1 (United Nations [UN], [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR78)), which track progress in relevant ICT skills for employment and entrepreneurship, educational systems worldwide promote these skills to improve learning outcomes and prepare students for the digital society (OECD, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR59), [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR58); Timotheou et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR73)).

A large number of theoretical approaches have been developed to address the challenges posed by technology-based societies. Gilster ([1997](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR26)) was the first to introduce the term “digital literacy,” followed by concepts such as digital competence, media literacy, and ICT literacy. Most of these overlapping concepts (Iordache et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR41)) identify more or less the same digital skills, which can be grouped into two categories: functional/technical and cognitive (e.g., Haddon et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR31); van Laar et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR81)). Cognitive skills are especially important for children and youth, as they protect them from the negative consequences of ICT use (Livingstone et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR49); Smahel et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR68)). Issues such as excessive internet use (Gui & Büchi, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR30); Lopez-Fernandez & Kuss, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR50)), cyberbullying, and exposure to harmful content (Bedrosova et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR5); Machackova et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR52); Tercova & Smahel, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR70)) seriously threaten the socio-emotional development of students (Kasturiratna et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR43); Zewude et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR88)). Addressing these challenges requires educational systems to develop not only ICT skills but also students’ cognitive awareness, ethical behaviour, and resilience.

An essential prerequisite for developing digital skills is the availability of validated tools to assess students’ competencies and monitor their progress. Most existing instruments, as highlighted in systematic reviews (Godaert et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR27); Saltos-Rivas et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR64); Siddiq et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR66); Sillat et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR67)), primarily target younger students or the general population. Only a few scales are designed for upper secondary and undergraduate students who are preparing to enter the workforce and actively contribute to today’s digital democratic society.

The aim of this study is to develop and validate an instrument for assessing digital skills among students in Serbia aged 14 to 25, based on the contemporary international Digital Competence Framework for Citizens (DigComp). DigComp has been empirically validated among primary school students in Serbia (Kuzmanović, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR46)), but no comparable tool exists for older students, which is essential for identifying digital skill gaps and empowering them through education to safely, confidently, and responsibly use ICT. This targeted age range aligns with the classification of Sustainable Development Goal 4.4.1 for monitoring digital skills (UN Statistics Division, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR77)) within the youth population transitioning from secondary to higher education. One of the specific aims of this study is to explore whether the instrument consistently measures digital skills within this group, taking into account potential differences in developmental characteristics and contextual experiences of adolescents and young adults.

2 Theoretical framework
-----------------------

### 2.1 European digital competence framework

The European Commission’s Joint Research Centre established DigComp based on an analysis of existing theoretical concepts and models as a key step toward standardizing the understanding and assessment of digital competences (Ferrari, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR21)). This framework introduced as version 1.0 (Ferrari, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR22)) has been updated several times. DigComp 2.0 (Vuorikari et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR82)) revised and expanded the original framework to address a wide range of devices and adopted inclusive vocabulary, such as “digital technologies” and “digital environment”. Inspired by the European Qualifications Framework (European Commission, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR19)), DigComp 2.1 (Carretero et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR11)), focused on practical and cognitive competence development and expanded the proficiency levels from the initial three (foundation, intermediate, and advanced) to eight levels. Furthermore, these new levels are presented in relation to task complexity, autonomy, and cognitive processes. The most recent version, DigComp 2.2 (Vuorikari et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR83)), addresses emerging trends such as artificial intelligence, data ethics, and sustainability, with new examples of the knowledge, skills, and attitudes, making the framework increasingly comprehensive and adaptable to real-world applications. By integrating various relevant perspectives, DigComp represents one of the most robust models for defining and measuring digital skills and competences.

To date, the DigComp framework has been widely implemented across many European countries (e.g. Domingo- Coscollola et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR18); Kluzer & Pujol Priego, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR45); Godhe, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR28); Zakrajšek et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR87)) and beyond (e.g. Cabero-Almenara et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR9)) for a variety of purposes, most commonly within formal education systems. In the Serbian context, it has been implemented as a framework in national strategic documents and as a basis for strategies aimed at enhancing teachers’ digital competences (Ministry of Education, Science and Technological Development, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR71)). Given that teachers play a crucial role in fostering students’ digital competences and integrating ICT into classroom practice, the Digital Competence Framework for Educators (DigCompEdu) (Redecker, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR61)) and DigComp (Ferrari, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR22)), as complementary frameworks, have strongly influenced national educational policies and research aimed at developing instruments for assessing digital competences (e.g., Basilotta-Gómez-Pablos et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR4); Mattar et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR53); Muammar et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR56)). For example, the Spanish Ministry of Education, Culture and Sport adapted the European framework (Carretero et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR11); Redecker, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR61)) and developed the national Common Framework for Digital Competence for Teachers (INTEF – Instituto Nacional de Tecnologías Educativas y Formación del Profesorado, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR40)), which promotes the development of digital competences at all educational levels. This framework, adapted to the Spanish context, also supports the development and empirical validation of instruments for assessing both students’ and teachers’ competences based on the DigComp framework (e.g., Cabero-Almenara et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR8); García-Valcárcel et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR25); González-Mujico, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR29); Rubio-Gragera et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR63); Tourón et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR74)).

In other words, DigComp has become a widely accepted tool for defining and measuring digital competences.

### 2.2 Cognitive and practical digital skills

DigComp provides a definition of digital competences, emphasizing three elements—knowledge, skills, and attitudes—that enable the critical, confident, and creative use of ICT to achieve goals across various domains (Ferrari, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR22)).

Knowledge refers to factual information and insights acquired through learning, while attitudes represent the motivators for performance. Digital skills encompass the abilities to apply knowledge and use know-how to complete tasks and solve problems in digital environments (Vuorikari et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR82)). In other words, digital skills manifest as the practical performance of both knowledge and attitudes. For this reason, although all previous studies have been based on the broader concept of competences (e.g., Lucas et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR51); Vuorikari et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR84)), in this study we focused exclusively on digital skills, as they inherently encompass knowledge and attitudes. Focusing on digital skills as a distinct construct allows for precise operationalization and measurement, avoiding the blurring of results that occurs when attempting to measure knowledge, skills, and attitudes simultaneously. Moreover, one of the first large cross-cultural research projects, DigCompSAT (Clifford et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR14)), which includes 82 self-assessment questions to evaluate all 21 DigComp competences, confirmed that digital skills are a key factor within the broader construct of digital competences.

Since there is relative consensus on the central role of digital skills, one important factor contributing to the complexity of their measurement is that digital skills encompass both practical and cognitive components. Early assessments of digital skills, grounded in the digital or computer literacy approach, primarily focused on technical abilities such as software installation and managing data for online collaboration and task completion (Ferrari, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR22)). However, focusing exclusively on practical/technical skills can lead to passive engagement and fails to fully address active, reflective, ethical, and responsible participation in digital society (Helsper et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR34)). With technological advancement and growing awareness of potential risks associated with ICT use, cognitive digital skills—such as critical thinking, safety, and ethical communication—have become increasingly important in measuring digital competencies (e.g., Haddon et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR31); Fraillon & Rožman, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR24); Livingstone et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR49)). A recent international project (Helsper et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR34)), based on the definition that links positive outcomes and harm prevention in ICT use with digital skills, emphasizes the role of cognitive skills. Building on this approach, the authors developed a measurement model that encompasses ethical communication, content creation, information navigation, and technical/operational skills (van Deursen et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR80)).

Although the DigComp, as a multidimensional structure, does not explicitly separate digital skills into practical and cognitive, they are implicitly included (Carretero et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR11)). Originally established, the framework’s structure has been retained in the latest update (Vuorikari et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR83)), and it encompasses 21 competencies grouped into five main areas, as shown in Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#Tab1). Each core dimension is actually composed of cognitive or practical competencies (sub-dimensions). Instruments based on DigComp successfully cover its key dimensions (e.g., Lucas et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR51); Tzafilkou et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR75); Vuorikari et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR84)) but there is still no tool that clearly distinguishes technical and cognitive sub-dimensions. One reason for this might be that cognitive skills are often considered less directly connected to the digital context and are harder to measure compared to technical skills (Haddon et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR31); Siddiq et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR66)). Additionally, the transversal nature and interconnectedness of practical and cognitive digital skills contribute to overlaps among core dimensions and sub-dimensions, further complicating the development of a sophisticated tool for measuring and holistically addressing the development of digital skills (Ferrari, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR22); Vuorikari et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR82)).

### 2.3 Instruments for measuring students’ digital skills

Based on the analysis of systematic reviews across different educational levels, a great diversity is observed in the structure and characteristics of existing instruments based on the DigComp framework. We summarized those key conceptual and methodological challenges.

#### 2.3.1 Conceptual challenges

The existence of numerous overlapping theoretical frameworks with DigComp, such as digital literacy, media literacy or ICT literacy, allows flexibility in instrument design; however, the origins and meanings of these concepts are not the same (Iordache et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR41)). Therefore, it is emphasized that when constructing an instrument, it is necessary to specify and operationalize the key dimensions in accordance with the theoretical concept (Mirazchiyski, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR55)). Otherwise, such instruments fail to confirm a theory-based structure, thereby limiting their broader applicability, validation potential, and comparability. Accordingly, Siddiq et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR66)) noted that most instruments for primary and secondary students assess only a limited range of competences, primarily focusing on operational and technical skills, while areas such as the safe and responsible use of ICT and problem-solving are seldom represented. This imbalance is further confirmed by a meta-analysis of 73 instruments in higher education (Saltos-Rivas et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR64)), which highlights the uneven representation of certain dimensions compared to others—a finding also supported by other meta-studies (Godaert et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR27); Sillat et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR67)).

Given that the DigComp construct includes attitudes, knowledge, and skills, certain conceptual challenges also arise. From an analytical perspective, it is desirable to distinguish these components; however, as Aesaert and van Braak ([2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR1)) and Siddiq et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR66)) emphasize, developing a single tool that encompasses all components and dimensions is neither feasible nor practical. Theoretically, these three components are not only parts of a broader construct but also distinct entities that require operationalization according to their specific definitions. Digital skills, as a complex and performance-based component, encompass knowledge and attitudes as their internal elements. Therefore, further operationalization and inclusion of knowledge and attitudes as separate constructs may undermine methodological precision by creating instruments that do not clearly distinguish these components, leading to difficulties in the interpretation and practical application of results. Although digital skills are a key component of digital competencies (Clifford et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR14); Helsper et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR34)), most previous studies have been based on the construct of digital competencies (78%), while only 3% focused solely on digital skills (Saltos-Rivas et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR64)). This highlights the need for instruments that focus specifically on individual constructs within digital competence, such as digital skills, rather than attempting to measure knowledge, skills, and attitudes simultaneously. Focusing on a single construct allows for more precise operationalization, clearer interpretation of results, and improved validity, addressing a gap in the existing DigComp-based tools.

#### 2.3.2 Methodological challenges

Systematic reviews revealed that tools based on the DigComp often exhibit incomplete reporting of reliability and validity, with a significant number failing to address these aspects at all (Siddiq et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR66); Sillat et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR67); Mattar et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR53)). The observed studies often include internal consistency, face validity, content validity, and exploratory factor analysis (EFA). However, EFA and confirmatory factor analysis (CFA), which are key methods for evaluating construct validity, are relatively rarely used together. Saltos-Rivas et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR64)) found that only 25% of reviewed instruments use CFA to confirm the structure of the instrument. In addition, analysis of recent instruments based on DigComp indicates that they often suffer from excessive simplification of the complex theoretical framework (Lucas et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR51); Mieg et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR54)), insufficient sample sizes (Tzafilkou et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR75)), overly broad target populations (Schwarz et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR65); Vuorikari et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR84)), or excessive instrument length (Clifford et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR14)).

In line with the conclusions of other reviews, the predominant measurement design across all educational levels relies on self-assessment, based on participants’ subjective perceptions of their digital competence, while a smaller number of studies incorporate objective assessments (González-Mujico, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR29); Helsper et al., [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR34); Saltos-Rivas et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR64); Siddiq et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR66)). Performance assessments allow for the direct testing of abilities through tasks that simulate real-life situations, providing an objective insight into the actual level of digital skills (Aesaert & van Braak, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR2)). However, these assessments are more suitable for evaluating technical and operational skills and are less appropriate for assessing more complex, context-dependent aspects such as communication skills, collaboration, or digital content creation (Helsper et al., [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR34)). Furthermore, this approach requires significant resources, including the involvement of human evaluators and high financial costs, making it impractical for large populations (van Deursen & van Dijk, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR79)).

Self-assessment is a more efficient method as it allows for a large number of questions to be asked in a short period of time, features straightforward scoring, and imposes minimal demands on financial and human resources. However, this method has limitations, such as respondent bias, which often fails to reflect actual performance (Hatlevik et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR33)). Nonetheless, when properly designed and incorporating techniques such as cognitive interviews, self-assessment can be a suitable option for assessing skills in large samples (Aesaert & van Braak, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR2)). Best practices recommend using factual questions such as “know how to…,” or “able to…,” to avoid socially desirable responses and obtain valid data (Helsper et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR34); van Deursen et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR80)). These types of questions better distinguish digital skills from digital self-efficacy (e.g., “How good are you at…?“) or activity types (e.g., “Do you use…?“), which, as indirect measures, were often used in early studies (Haddon et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR31)).

Although a combination of objective and subjective assessments is the best approach, most studies show that the differences between them are not significant (e.g., Aesaert et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR3); Nguyen & Habók, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR57)). When validly designed, self-assessments, despite their limitations, may serve as a useful tool for measuring actual skill levels.

### 2.4 The current study

This study aims to develop and validate a self-assessment tool for digital skills among Serbian youth, grounded in the DigComp conceptual framework. The research questions (RQ) are as follows:

### RQ1

What is the latent structure of the developed digital skills instrument, and to what extent do the factors identified through EFA align with the key dimensions of digital skills outlined in the DigComp?

### RQ2

Does the factor structure obtained through EFA demonstrate an adequate model fit when tested using CFA?

### RQ3

What are the reliability and validity of the developed digital skills instrument?

### RQ4

Is there evidence of a second-order factor representing digital skills, and how do the relationships among the identified first-order factors contribute to this higher-order construct?

### RQ5

Is the developed instrument invariant across gender and educational levels?

Given the varying number and structure of factors in previous studies, we did not set specific hypotheses but generally assumed that the instrument’s latent structure in the Serbian sample would reflect the main dimensions of DigComp (Vuorikari et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR83)). Additionally, we assume that the resulting multidimensional structure will encompass both practical and cognitive aspects of digital skills and that all identified factors will contribute to a common higher-order construct (Carretero et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR11); Ferrari, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR22)).

Including participants from different educational levels and genders shaped the study design and allowed for testing measurement invariance, to examine whether the instrument functions comparably across educational and gender groups, given potential developmental and contextual differences between adolescents and young adults. Age groups correspond to educational levels, with secondary school students aged 14–18 and undergraduate students aged 19–25. This age range was selected to capture youth across upper secondary and early higher education, a developmental period characterized by intensive use of digital technologies in both learning and everyday contexts.

3 Research design and method
----------------------------

This study used a quantitative descriptive cross-sectional survey design to develop the Digital Skills Assessment Tool for Youth (DSATy). Following established scale development procedures, the process included defining the conceptual framework, item generation, selecting an appropriate response format, content validation with experts, pilot testing with students/cognitive interviews, item analysis and refinement, and construct validation through statistical methods and finalizing the instrument with clear documentation (Carpenter, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR10); DeVellis, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR17)). These steps were applied in the development of DSATy through three sequential phases:

* Phase I: conceptual definition, item generation, and content validation through expert review and student feedback;
* Phase II: EFA on a representative sample to identify the latent structure; and.
* Phase III: CFA on an independent sample to verify the factor structure and test measurement invariance.

### 3.1 First phase: Generation of items and content validation

Based on the DigComp (Carretero et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR11); Clifford et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR14); Vuorikari et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR83)), an initial draft of 54 items was created, covering 21 competencies across five dimensions:

> (1) **Information and data literacy** (e.g. *I know how to find and follow pages or groups that are of interest to me*; *I know how to copy data to multiple locations*),
> 
> (2) **Communication and collaboration** (e.g. *I know how to share data and content on the cloud*; *I am able to assess which content is inappropriate to post on social media*),
> 
> (3) **Digital content creation** *(e.g. I know how to create and maintain a blog*; *I know how to use at least one option to check the origin of copyright*),
> 
> (4) **Safety** (e.g. *I know how to check if my devices are infected with malware*; *I know how to protect my physical health while using digital devices*),
> 
> (5) **Problem-solving** (e.g. *I know how to find the cause of the problem when my phone freezes*; *I am able to assess which programs are the best choice to complete a specific task*).

Each sub-dimension included at least two items to ensure coverage of digital skills within the DigComp framework. The initial items were written in Serbian and then back-translated into English. To ensure relevance over time, statements were framed in a general manner, with specific examples included in brackets to enhance clarity and comprehension. While inspired by tools like DigCompSAT (Clifford et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR14)) and item examples from DigComp 2.2 (Vuorikari et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR83)), DSATy was tailored specifically for Serbian youth (see Supplementary Appendix A on OSF).

Aligned with the DigComp framework (Vuorikari et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR83)) and best practices for self-assessment instruments (e.g., Clifford et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR14); Helsper et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR34)), each item began with phrases such as “I know how to…” or “I am able to…” to minimize subjectivity and ensure the measurement focused on digital skills rather than related constructs. The response format used a five-point Likert scale (1 to 5), reflecting levels of autonomy aligned with the framework, ranging from foundation “I don’t know what it is” to the advanced level “I can easily do it and guide others if needed.” Given the purpose of the DSATy, a highly specialized level was not included. In addition to skill-related items, the questionnaire included demographic information (e.g., gender, age, type of school).

#### 3.1.1 Content validation: formulation and item selection

Content validation involved expert review to ensure the relevance, comprehensiveness, and alignment of items with the definitions of digital skills. Experts were selected based on their experience in digital competence research and educational assessment, and they independently reviewed each item for conceptual accuracy, linguistic clarity, and coverage of all DigComp dimensions. Their feedback led to several minor wording adjustments prior to the cognitive testing phase.

Additionally, cognitive interviews were conducted with secondary school students (*n* = 15) and undergraduate students (*n* = 15) to assess the clarity and interpretation of the items. Cognitive interviews are a qualitative method used to explore how respondents understand and mentally process survey questions, helping researchers identify ambiguities, misinterpretations, or unintended meanings (Willis, [2005](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR85)). During these interviews, participants were asked to explain in their own words what each question meant to them, provide examples, and indicate any missing response options or difficulties in answering. This step aimed to confirm that the underlying concepts of the DigComp framework made sense for Serbian youth.

The item selection process was guided by predetermined exclusion criteria: items were removed if they had unclear wording, conceptual overlap with other items, low relevance to DigComp definitions, poor interpretability during interviews, or were found to be too easy or too difficult, indicating a limited ability to differentiate among respondents’ skill levels. Out of 54 initial draft items, nine were excluded based on these criteria.

In addition to item exclusion, based on the feedback from interviews, several clarifications were made, such as replacing the term “tools” with “applications” (e.g., Item 1.3.17: *I know how to use applications and software for organizing*…), adjusting “community” to “group” (Item 1.1.2: *I know how to find and follow pages or groups that are of interest to me*), and adopting the anglicized term “editovati.”

This iterative, theory-driven process, which incorporated feedback from both experts and students, resulted in a refined set of 45 content-valid items (see Supplementary Appendix A on OSF). These 45 items then proceeded to Phase II (EFA) and Phase III (CFA), where the instrument was further refined and finalized. Details on item reduction and final instrument composition are reported in Sect. 4 Results.

### 3.2 Participants and procedure

The study was conducted between May and November 2024 with 914 participants (48.7% male) aged 14–25 years (*M* = 18.16; *SD* = 2.79), including 55.1% secondary school students from Belgrade (vocational or academic tracks) and 44.9% undergraduate students from the University of Belgrade (humanities or technical sciences). To ensure proportional representation by age and gender, secondary school students were recruited from all four grades (ages 14–18), while undergraduate students were selected from all years of study (ages 19–25).

Parental education levels varied: 35.7% of mothers and 41.5% of fathers had completed secondary education; 45.5% of mothers and 38.7% of fathers held university degrees, while 13.9% of mothers and 12.7% of fathers had doctoral degrees. A small percentage of parents had only completed elementary education (1.3% of mothers and 2.2% of fathers).

All participants provided informed consent, including parental consent for minors. Incentives included gift cards (4 Euros) or course credit. The online survey was administered via Google during class hours, supervised by the study authors. Ethical approval was granted by the Faculty of Special Education and Rehabilitation, University of Belgrade.

### 3.3 Data analysis

The study employed a cross-sectional design, with participants completing the questionnaire once. The sample was then divided into two subsamples: an index sample for EFA (*n* = 452) and a reserve sample for CFA (*n* = 462). Both subsamples were balanced by gender and education to reduce bias and allow for testing invariance with equal sample sizes across groups. The sample sizes met the recommended guidelines of at least 10 times the number of items for EFA and a minimum of 200 cases for CFA (Li, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR48)). Examining skewness and kurtosis revealed non-normality; therefore, methods suitable for ordinal data and non-normal distributions were used (Kyriazos & Poga-Kyriazou, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR47)). Analyses were conducted using IBM SPSS 21 and the lavaan package in JASP 0.19.

#### 3.3.1 Second phase: applying EFA

The EFA was performed on a sample of 452 participants to identify the initial factor structure (Henson & Roberts, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR36)). The Principal Axis Factoring (PAF) extraction method was chosen due to the ordinal nature of the data, its robustness to non-normality, and the aim to identify underlying latent constructs. Data suitability was confirmed by the Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin (KMO) test (> 0.70) and Bartlett’s test of sphericity (Kaiser, [1974](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR42)). Factor retention was based on: (a) eigenvalues ≥ 1 (Kaiser, [1974](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR42)); (b) scree plot (Cattell, [1966](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR12)); (c) parallel analysis (Horn, [1965](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR38)); and (d) retaining factors explaining 50–60% of the cumulative variance (Carpenter, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR10)). Promax rotation was used initially, and varimax was applied in cases of low correlations (*r* < 0.20) (Fabrigar & Wegener, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR20)). Item selection criteria included: (a) at least three items per factor with loadings ≥ 0.40; (b) removal of items with cross-loadings (> 0.30 or difference ≤ 0.10); (c) a simple and interpretable structure; (d) alignment with the theoretical framework; and (e) internal consistency of factors with Cronbach’s α ≥ 0.70.

#### 3.3.2 Third phase: applying CFA

The CFA was conducted on an independent sample (*n* = 462) to test the baseline model, the second-order model, and measurement invariance across gender and age groups (Rogers, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR62)). The Unweighted Least Squares (ULS) method was chosen due to the ordinal nature of the data and its robustness to non-normality (Crede & Harms, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR16); Xia & Yang, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR86)). Covariance-Based Structural Equation Modeling (CB-SEM) was employed, as it is the preferred approach when the goal is theory testing and confirmation of the measurement model (Hair et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR32)). Model fit was evaluated using standard indices: CFI and TLI > 0.95 for good fit and > 0.90 for adequate fit (Bentler, [1990](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR6)), Root Mean Square Error of Approximation (RMSEA) < 0.06 for good fit and < 0.08 for reasonable fit (Browne & Cudeck, [1993](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR7); Steiger, [1990](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR69)); and Standardized Root Mean Square Residual (SRMR) < 0.05 for good fit and < 0.08 for adequate fit (Hu & Bentler, [1999](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR39)). For non-invariance criteria (Chen, [2007](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR13)), invariance was indicated by ΔCFI ≤ 0.010 with ΔRMSEA ≤ 0.015 or ΔSRMR ≤ 0.030 for loadings, and ΔSRMR ≤ 0.010 for intercepts or residuals.

Convergent validity was assessed using average variance extracted (AVE ≥ 0.50) and composite reliability (CR ≥ 0.70) for each factor to ensure that the items adequately represent their respective factor (Hair et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR32)). To assess discriminant validity, the heterotrait-monotrait ratio (HTMT) was used, along with Fornell and Larcker ([1981](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR23)) analysis by comparing the square root of AVE to inter-factor correlations. The HTMT values below the threshold of 0.85 (Henseler et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR35)) and the square root of the AVE greater than the correlations with other latent factors confirm that factors represent distinct dimensions of the DSATy and do not overlap conceptually. Internal consistency was assessed using Cronbach’s α and McDonald’s ω, with acceptable values ≥ 0.70 to ensure that the items within each factor consistently measure the same underlying dimension. McDonald’s ω was included as it provides a more robust estimate than α, as it does not assume unidimensionality and is less influenced by the number of items in the scale. 4 Results
---------

Before conducting the EFA, the suitability of the data was assessed. The KMO measure was excellent at 0.923, and Bartlett’s Test of Sphericity was significant (χ²(990) = 9208.813, *p* < 0.001). The absence of multicollinearity was confirmed, with inter-item correlations below 0.68, indicating that the data were suitable for factor analysis (see Supplementary Appendix B on OSF). Additionally, multivariate normality was evaluated using Mardia’s test, which indicated significant multivariate non-normality (χ²(16215) = 52176.18, *p* < 0.001; Z = 143.40, *p* < 0.001), thus supporting the use of the PAF extraction method.

### 4.1 RQ1: What is the latent structure of the developed DSATy, and to what extent do the factors identified through EFA align with the key dimensions of digital skills outlined in the DigComp?

The EFA with the PAF estimator was performed on a sample of 452 participants to determine the correct number of latent factors. Different criteria for factor retention produced conflicting recommendations. Eigenvalues exceeding 1.0 and Cattell’s scree test supported retaining seven factors, whereas parallel analysis suggested a five-factor solution. Both solutions were evaluated using promax rotations (Fabrigar & Wegener, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR20)).

Although the five-factor solution aligned with the five main DigComp dimensions, the seven-factor model provided a better overall fit and more effectively captured both practical and cognitive aspects of the retained factors. The item structure was reviewed using multiple criteria, resulting in the elimination of 12 of the initial 45 items: four items did not load on any factor (< 0.30), two items displayed double loadings, three items had borderline loadings (< 0.40), and three items lacked conceptual alignment with their respective factors (see Supplementary Appendix C on OSF). At the same time, two items (12, 16), distributed across a factor to which they do not originally belong, were retained due to their high loadings and theoretical justification in relation to the criterion of cognitive-practical factors.

Through content analysis of the obtained seven factors and DigComp dimensions, with minor modifications relative to DigComp, the factors were named as shown in Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#Tab2), including the statistical parameters for each factor.

Out of the 45 content-validated items entered into the EFA, 33 items were retained and grouped into seven distinct factors in the sample of Serbian youth. These factors are consistent with the DigComp framework, but also clearly distinguish between practical (F1, F2, F4, F6) and cognitive factors (F3, F5, F7) within the seven-factor structure. The internal consistency of each factor was satisfactory, and inter-factor correlations ranged from 0.17 to 0.60, indicating related but distinct constructs within digital skills (see Supplementary Appendix D on OSF). Together, these seven factors account for approximately 50% of the total variance in digital skills, indicating that the way digital skills are grouped in the Serbian sample aligns with the main areas defined in the DigComp framework. Although some factors contribute to a lesser extent, all are conceptually important and provide a solid foundation for further CFA.

### 4.2 RQ2: Does the factor structure obtained through EFA demonstrate an adequate model fit when tested using CFA?

CFA was conducted on an independent sample of 462 participants to test the seven-factor measurement model with 33 items identified in the EFA. Prior to analysis, multivariate normality was assessed using Mardia’s test of multivariate normality. The skewness statistic (χ²(6545) = 22,468.74, *p* < 0.001) and kurtosis (z = 98.71, *p* < 0.001) indicated significant deviation from normality, meaning the assumption of multivariate normality was violated. To ensure the suitability of the dataset for CFA, multicollinearity was also evaluated. Item correlations were moderate, with the highest value being 0.67, indicating sufficient distinctiveness and absence of redundancy (see Supplementary Appendix E on OSF).

In line with the subsequent research questions, we first tested the first-order model and then examined its reliability and validity. Given the ordinal Likert scale and significant skewness and kurtosis, two estimation methods were examined: ULS and Diagonally Weighted Least Squares (DWLS) (Li, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR48)). Additionally, the Satorra-Bentler correction to the χ² test was applied, which is recommended in cases of significant non-normality to ensure reliable model fit indicators (Crede & Harms, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR16); Holgado-Tello et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR37)).

Although DWLS estimation can be advantageous in some cases, it produced overly optimistic fit indices in our study, consistent with literature warnings about DWLS inflation, especially in complex models (Xia & Yang, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR86)). Conversely, ULS yielded more balanced and realistic fit indices, better reflecting model-data fit. Hoelter’s critical N suggested DWLS requires a sample size over 500, supporting ULS use with our 462 participants. Given the seven-factor structure, higher-order factor, and moderate sample, ULS was more appropriate. Technically, ULS minimizes residual variances without using the full weight matrix, making χ² less reliable; thus, χ²/df is preferred, with values < 3 indicating good fit (Kline, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR44)).

In accordance with this, CFA with Satorra-Bentler correction showed good fit according to all key indices: χ²=1051.097, df=474, χ²/df=2.22; CFI=0.989, TLI=0.987, RMSEA=0.031, SRMR=0.050. These results confirm that the seven-factor structure with 33 items retained from the EFA adequately represents the structure of digital skills grounded in the DigComp theoretical framework. The model captures both technical and cognitive dimensions.

All factors demonstrated good reliability (≥ 0.70), except for Factor F7, which was slightly lower but still acceptable given it includes only three items (Hair et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR32)). A smaller number of items can naturally lower the reliability statistic, but the value still indicates that the items of this factor behave sufficiently well as a set of indicators for this dimension.

The standardized factor loadings exceeded the recommended threshold, indicating good internal consistency and convergent validity of the factors within the DSATy (Hair et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR32)). Furthermore, CR values for all constructs confirmed good convergent validity, although three factors had AVE values below the recommended threshold of 0.50. Overall, the results confirm the robustness of the DSATy instrument’s latent structure, with no issues regarding reliability or validity across the seven latent factors. The findings indicate that all factors consistently measure their intended constructs. This evidence, alongside satisfactory EFA and model fit, supports our hypothesis that the DSATy’s factor structure aligns with the dimensions outlined in the DigComp framework.

Given the complexity of the second-order model, the results suggest it fits the data well, with good internal consistency for the second-order digital skills factor (ω = 0.80).

### 4.5 RQ5: Is the developed instrument invariant across gender and educational levels?

5 Discussion and implications
-----------------------------

The main aim of this study was to develop and validate a tool to assess digital skills among Serbian youth, based on the DigComp framework. This framework defines digital skills as the ability to apply knowledge and use know-how to solve problems in a digital environment and includes five dimensions and 21 sub-dimensions (Carretero et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR11); Ferrari, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR22); Vuorikari et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR83)).

Based on this framework, our first research objective was to explore the latent structure of the developed DSATy instrument. Of the 45 content-validated items, 33 were retained through EFA, grouped into seven factors aligning with the DigComp model. This seven-factor structure, further confirmed through CFA, clearly distinguishes four factors commonly classified as practical/technical and three as cognitive factors. Practical/technical digital skills involve the ability to apply ICT knowledge and use know-how to create digital content, solve ICT-related problems, protect digital devices, manage data, and collaborate online. Cognitive digital skills encompass the ability to apply know-how from non-ICT domains (critical thinking, ethical behavior, health, and environmental protection) in digital environments, to evaluate information, safeguard health and the environment, and engage in ethical online communication.

DigComp framework does not explicitly distinguish between practical and cognitive digital skills. By empirically confirming and differentiating these two types of skills across seven identified factors, the DSATy instrument extends and refines the DigComp framework by mapping which sub-dimensions correspond to practical and which to cognitive skills. In doing so, through a more detailed operationalization of the framework, the DSATy structure reveals both similarities and differences in relation to the existing DigComp sub-dimensions. We discuss them by main dimension names and sub-dimension codes, aligned with Tables [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#Tab1) and [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#Tab2).

Similarities include replication of *Content Creation* (5 items, codes 3.1; 3.2) and *Problem-Solving* (6 items, codes 5.1; 5.2), both involving ICT knowledge applied to digital content creation and problem solving. Cognitive factor *Evaluating Information and Content* (three items) reflects critical thinking, but applied in digital environments, matching DigComp sub-dimension 1.2.

The two subsequent factors diverge from the original Communication and Collaboration dimension, reflecting differences between its cognitive (2.1; 2.5; 2.6) and practical/technical sub-dimensions (2.2; 2.4). Items from the technical sub-dimensions grouped with Data Management (1.3) from the first DigComp dimension, forming the *Data Management and Collaboration* factor (five items). This cross-dimension grouping of practical/technical digital skills aligns with Ferrari’s ([2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR22)) view of digital literacy as practical/technical skills enabling tasks and collaboration. At the same time, these results suggest that, in the Serbian sample, digital collaboration is perceived as more closely related to data management skills than to online communication skills. In line with this, items from the cognitive sub-dimensions (2.1; 2.5; 2.6) formed a separate *Critical Knowledge for Online Communication* factor (six items), representing ethical knowledge and behavioural rules for online communication. Most previous studies using the DigComp framework have replicated Communication and Collaboration as a single factor (e.g., Mieg et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR54); Tzafilkou et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR75)), but our findings align with theories and instruments that distinguish practical digital skills from those requiring critical thinking (e.g., Helsper et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR34); van Deursen et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR80); Peart et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR60)).

The Safety dimension differentiated according to the same principle in our sample. The *Device Protection* factor (three items) replicated the practical Safety sub-dimension (4.1), reflecting the ability to apply ICT knowledge and use know-how to protect digital devices. The two other cognitive Safety sub-dimensions—Protecting Health (4.3) and Protecting Environment (4.4)—merged into one factor, named *Know-How to Protect Health and Environment* (5 items). This factor requires domain-specific knowledge on health and the environment protection and the ability to apply it in digital context. It is interesting that the Safety dimension, which in DigComp includes four sub-dimensions, replicated as a single factor in only a few previous studies (Siddiq et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR66)), while in our sample it split into two factors. Both require digital know-how but differ in the type of knowledge applied: one ICT and the other from the domain of health and environmental protection. The latter represents a novelty, as it has not emerged in any prior study, and reflects the link between well-being and environmental sustainability, which is key for a safe digital environment (UN, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR76)).

Overall, by operationalizing both practical and cognitive sub-dimensions of digital skills, the DSATy instrument provides a new perspective for evaluating youth digital skills beyond what is explicitly captured in the current DigComp framework. It further contributes to the theoretical development of DigComp, offers an empirical basis for future research and instrument development, and enhances existing DigComp-based measures by enabling more precise measurement, differentiation, and interpretation of cognitive and practical digital skills.

However, it is important to note that some sub-dimensions and items did not replicate (due to low loadings or cross-loadings) compared to DigComp. For instance, in the sub-dimension Browsing, searching and filtering data (1.1), the item *I know how to use multiple browsers* was rated as too easy during cognitive interviews, while another item, *I know how to find and follow pages or groups that are of interest to me*, showed a low loading in the EFA. Consequently, this sub-dimension was not included in the DSATy structure.

Similarly, items related to the Content Creation sub-dimension Copyright and licences (3.3) showed limited discriminant validity in the Serbian sample. One item was rated as too complex by students in cognitive interviews (*I can distinguish between the meanings of copyright*, *copyleft…)*, whereas another (*I know how to use at least one option to check the origin of copyright…*), had a low factor loading in the EFA. Both items were therefore excluded from the final model. This does not imply that distinguishing between licence types is irrelevant for digital content creation; rather, it suggests that among Serbian youth, these skills did not empirically contribute clearly to this sub-dimension. It is possible that these skills are not yet part of young people’s digital experience in Serbia, as they have not been sufficiently recognized or systematically developed within the educational and sociocultural context. In contexts where copyright topics are formally integrated into teaching and digital media culture, similar items would likely emerge as an integral part of the instrument structure.

These results reflect differences in young people’s understanding and experience of various aspects of digital skills, suggesting that sociocultural and educational factors in Serbia may have influenced the factorial structure of the DSATy instrument. At the same time, the findings confirm that the core concept of the DigComp framework is recognizable and applicable in the Serbian sample, while additionally distinguishing cognitive and practical skills within the seven-factor structure of DSATy.

Further CFA analyses confirmed the interplay of cognitive and practical aspects within the DSATy, as well as satisfactory reliability and validity across all seven factors. Despite some factors not meeting the AVE threshold of 0.50, other strong psychometric indicators supported good convergent and discriminant validity. Furthermore, the lower AVE values of certain factors have their theoretical justification. Consistent with the literature, our findings confirmed significant interrelationships among cognitive and practical factors, highlighting the transversal nature of digital skills (Ferrari, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR22); Vuorikari et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR82)). Strong correlations were found between *Problem Solving* and all other practical factors. The cognitive factor *Evaluating Information and Content*, representing critical thinking, is integral to most digital skills and the strongest association with the practical *Problem-Solving* factor. These cross-references may explain why, in real-world contexts, cognitive and practical digital skills often appear inseparable. However, our results empirically confirm that all seven factors represent related but clearly distinct constructs within digital skills.

Building on these findings, the testing of the second-order model clarified the structural relationships between cognitive and practical digital skills. The hybrid model provided the most parsimonious fit, emphasizing the complementary nature of these skills. The three cognitive digital skills remained partially independent from the overarching digital skills construct, contributing indirectly through shared variance with the four practical ICT-related skills. This shows that, although cognitive skills are partially independent, they are essential for supporting the overall second-order digital skills construct, providing a more precise mapping of practical and cognitive sub-dimensions within the DigComp framework. These insights allow future iterations of DigComp to consider not only which skills belong to practical versus cognitive domains, but also how these sub-dimensions structurally interact within the overarching construct of youth digital skills. Unlike practical skills, cognitive digital skills are not purely digital, as they draw on know-how from non-ICT domains (critical thinking, ethical communication, and knowledge of health and environmental protection) applied in digital contexts. In interaction with practical/technical digital skills, cognitive digital skills become crucial for active and responsible digital citizenship, as highlighted by the Council Recommendation (2018). Helsper et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR34)) emphasize that technical proficiency alone often leads to passive digital participation, while combining it with critical thinking and ethical communication fosters active and constructive societal engagement. Similarly, recent studies (e.g. Bedrosova et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR5); Kasturiratna et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR43); Livingstone et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR49); Tercova & Smahel, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR70)) confirm that focusing solely on ICT knowledge and skills is associated with negative educational and social consequences, whereas teaching and promoting digital skills rooted in critical thinking and domain-specific knowledge leads to more positive outcomes.

The DSATy addresses these challenges by mapping not only practical but also cognitive skills. This structure balances technical proficiency with critical skills, offering a foundation for assessing digital competencies and shaping educational policies aligned with SDG 4.4.1 (UN, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR78)). By confirming measurement invariance across gender and educational level, the DSATy allows for reliable assessment and monitoring of the development of digital skills from adolescence to adulthood. Targeting high school and undergraduate students, the DSATy, as a national educational assessment resource, enables educational institutions in Serbia to identify strengths and weaknesses in different domains of youth digital skills and to implement interventions that foster both practical and cognitive digital skills. Cognitive digital skills, such as Evaluating Information and Content, Critical Knowledge for Online Communication, and Knowledge of Health and Environmental Protection, allow students to critically evaluate online content, communicate ethically, make informed decisions, safeguard privacy, and understand the impact of digital behaviors on personal well-being and the environment. In this way, cognitive digital skills provide the conceptual foundation for safe, ethical, and responsible ICT use. Integrating these skills into existing subjects—such as language, social studies, biology, and environmental science—can embed the digital dimension relevant to each content area. For example, students can learn to evaluate online health information or use digital tools for environmental projects (Knowledge of Health and Environmental Protection) or practice ethical online collaboration and communication (Critical Knowledge for Online Communication). From a policy perspective, DSATy results can guide curriculum design, teacher training, and educational interventions, ensuring that students progressively develop the competencies needed for responsible digital citizenship. This approach aligns with SDG 4.4.1 and the Council Recommendation (2018), emphasizing that fostering both cognitive and practical digital skills is essential for preparing youth to participate safely, ethically, and effectively in digital society.

### 5.1 Limitations and future directions

This study confirmed the latent structure of youth digital skills but has several limitations. The sample, while diverse, was not fully representative, as high school participants were drawn only from Belgrade. Future research should include a broader national sample, encompassing both public and private universities across Serbia, and conduct cross-cultural validation with international populations to examine potential variations in the factor structure across different sociocultural and educational contexts. Such studies could reveal whether item–factor relationships remain consistent or whether contextual differences influence the organization and interrelations of digital skills. Cross-national comparisons would further strengthen the theoretical and empirical basis of DigComp-based measurement models.

Although the seven-factor model demonstrated strong fit and validity, several improvements are recommended. First, increasing the number of items for certain factors with only three items (e.g., Evaluating Information and Content) could enhance reliability. Second, further refinement of latent constructs is suggested, particularly for those involving collaboration, where items could be more precisely defined to capture both operational and contextual aspects of collaborative digital practices. Finally, some broader factors, such as Health and Environmental Protection and Managing Data and Collaboration, could be divided into more narrowly defined sub-constructs with clearer conceptual boundaries. These refinements may improve measurement precision and increase explained variance.

This study did not explore causal relationships, such as mediation or moderation, as it was not its focus. Future research should investigate these mechanisms to understand how skills influence one another and under what conditions, aiding the development of tailored educational programs.

The DSATy relies on self-reporting, which may introduce biases like overconfidence and social desirability. While performance-based assessments are more objective, they are often costly and impractical, especially without prior validated tools. According to the literature, performance-based assessments are most suitable for evaluating technical and operational digital skills, whereas self-assessments are more appropriate for cognitive digital skills involving reflective and critical thinking, ethical reasoning, and contextual awareness (Aesaert & van Braak, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR2); Helsper et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13978-2#ref-CR34)).

In line with these findings, the DSATy cognitive subscales (Evaluating Information and Content, Critical Knowledge for Online Communication, and Health and Environmental Protection) can continue to be employed as self-report measures in future studies. In contrast, the practical digital skills subscales (Device Protection, Problem Solving, Managing Data and Collaboration, and Content Creation) provide a solid basis for the development of performance-based instruments aimed at validating these skills.

However, some items within the Problem Solving subscale involve more abstract, higher-order cognitive processes and would therefore require self-assessment, while certain items within the cognitive subscales could be adapted for performance-based or scenario-based assessment. Consequently, future research would benefit from adopting a mixed-method approach that combines self-report and performance-based measures to achieve a more comprehensive evaluation of digital skills.

6 Conclusion
------------

The developed and validated multidimensional DSATy is conceptually aligned with the DigComp framework. Its primary contribution lies in integrating practical ICT skills: data management and collaboration, content creation, device protection, and problem-solving, with cognitive skills such as information and content evaluation, critical knowledge for online communication, and skills for protecting health and the environment. This sophisticated, seven-factor structure of DSATy supports a holistic understanding of digital skills, contributing to the achievement of SDG 4.4.1. By empirically distinguishing practical and cognitive digital skills, the DSATy instrument provides data-driven insights that can inform and refine the DigComp framework, offering a basis for future iterations to more precisely conceptualize and measure sub-dimensions of digital competence.

Validated on a representative sample of Serbian youth, the DSATy addresses a significant gap in the national context, providing educators and policymakers with a tool for comprehensive assessment and monitoring of the development of secondary and undergraduate students’ digital skills. The DSATy offers a basis for targeted educational interventions aimed at preparing them not only for technical proficiency but also for critical, creative, and responsible engagement in digital environments. These skills are essential for achieving positive outcomes and building resilience to the negative effects of ICT use. Furthermore, the assessment and development of skills for reflective and responsible use of ICT are crucial for young adults entering the workforce and participating in today’s digital democratic society.

Additionally, DSATy is valuable not only for individual assessments but also for group-level research, facilitating evidence-based decision-making in education and policy at the macro level. Future cross-cultural research is recommended to validate DSATy, with the aim of expanding the understanding of digital competencies within the European DigComp framework and contributing to the achievement of the SDGs related to ICT education.
