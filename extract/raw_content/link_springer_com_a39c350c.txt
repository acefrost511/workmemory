Abstract
--------

Public interest has surged around artificial intelligence (AI) due to new capabilities demonstrated by new AI chatbot technologies, such as ChatGPT. This study investigates teachers’ immediate perceptions, concerns, and professional development needs with respect to AI in a large urban school district. Using a mixed methods approach, we analyzed survey responses from 1,454 teachers to understand how teacher attitudes toward AI chatbots are associated with characteristics such as grade level taught, subject area, and teaching experience. Findings identify key concerns and benefits teachers report for themselves and for their students regarding the adaptation of AI chatbot usage in educational settings. Teachers in secondary school settings were less willing to allow AI chatbot usage, relative to those in elementary settings. While elementary and middle school teachers prioritized basic AI literacy as a professional development need, high school teachers emphasized strategies for guiding students to use AI and detecting AI-generated work. These findings offer insights for developing targeted professional development programs and policies for effective AI integration across K-12 settings.

1 Introduction
--------------

Immediately after the November 2022 release of ChatGPT, there has been a mix of public reactions to what generative artificial intelligence (AI) means for the K-12 school systems. Philanthropist Bill Gates saw this as the means to “finally deliver on the promise of revolutionizing the way people teach and learn” (Gates, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR16)). Others viewed the revolution as a customizable tutoring system; educational technology organizations, like Khan Academy, immediately began work to attempt to develop that (Aleven et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR1); Mohamed et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR28); Yu & Chauhan, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR38)). Yet others saw this as a great efficiency driver in education based on early productivity studies that had come out (Chui et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR7)). Some have argued that this changes the content of what people need to teach and learn about, offering “AI literacy” as a necessary competency for all people to have and for schools to develop (Dabbagh et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR8); Hong & Kim, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR18)).

Initial reports from schools, however, were filled with wariness and caution. Early on, large school districts such as New York City Public Schools immediately banned ChatGPT, although they have since rescinded that ban (Elsen-Rooney, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR12)). Fears were rampant regarding student cheating and plagiarizing using AI technologies. Attention immediately went to the possibility of AI text detectors being a solution, although alarming cases where detectors were wrong and thus caused more problems for students appeared. Studies revealed concerning false positive rates, where legitimate student work was incorrectly flagged as AI-generated, potentially penalizing innocent students and raising due process concerns (Mathewson et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR25)). “Hallucinations” of untrue information and exacerbation of the misinformation crisis became a concern and was publicized as a major risk (Noble, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR29)). The ethical implications of technologies in education also sparked debate, particularly regarding student privacy and trust. The bias in underlying AI systems raised equity concerns, as these technologies often failed to account for diverse student experiences, especially those from the marginalized social groups, and writing styles across different cultural and linguistic backgrounds (Roshanaei, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR31)).

There is still much uncertainty about generative AI in education. Large national surveys suggested that 18% of K-12 teachers actively using AI tools and another 15% having tried them, indicating higher teacher usage compared to student adoption rates (Diliberti et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR10)). Recent empirical research examining high school students’ self-reported cheating behaviors found no significant increase in academic dishonesty following ChatGPT’s release, with rates remaining consistent at around 60–65% across pre- and post-ChatGPT periods (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR23)). Much publicized generative AI tutor projects encountered real-world challenges (Barua et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR3); Kamalov et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR21)). While we know more than before, there is still much that has not been systematically examined related to the reception of generative AI in schools. We present findings representing the perspectives of K-12 teachers in a large urban school district.

While previous studies provided valuable insights through diverse methodologies including focus groups, interviews, and national surveys (Kim, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR22); Imagine Learning, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR19)), our single-district approach enables a focused examination of individual teacher characteristics by holding institutional variables constant. There has not been a systematic examination of how teacher characteristics influence AI acceptance within a unified district with relative consistency in policies, school sizes, curriculum, and geography. This study addresses this gap by analyzing teacher responses within a large urban district, allowing us to isolate the effects of individual teacher characteristics while controlling for other variables that often confound cross-district comparisons. Granted, because it is a single district, there is the tradeoff that other districts will have important differences to consider. The overall need is for there to be several reports documenting things from many places so that the ability to understand things at different levels and grain sizes is possible.

In the following sections, we review literature on teachers and technology adoption pertinent to this work. We then describe the sample and methods used to obtain information from teachers in this district. We provide several descriptive analyses across attributes of special interest. Finally, we close with discussions of what these findings help us to understand better and where future research can further add to our understanding of generative AI in education, particularly from the perspective of teachers. Through this investigation, we examine how individual teacher characteristics such as grade level taught, subject area, and years of teaching experience influence their reception of AI chatbots in schools, offering insights that inform professional development needs and policy considerations for K-12 education.

2 Literature review
-------------------

### 2.1 Knowledge and beliefs for technology integration

Technology adoption has been a longstanding topic of interest, particularly for researchers of educational technology. The TPACK (Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge) framework developed by Mishra & Koehler ([2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR27)) sets a crucial foundation for understanding how teachers integrate technologies, such as AI chatbots, into their practices (TPACK is an extension of Shulman’s ([1986](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR35)) idea of pedagogical content knowledge—knowledge about the specific content area that is developed and especially suited for the work of teaching. Under the premise that TPACK is part of what helps teachers become more proficient in using digital technologies for teaching, we can recognize and appreciate that there is an understanding of the technology and how it may be used that should be of concern. When examining AI’s classroom adoption, the framework is especially relevant because generative AI tools present unique challenges and opportunities across different subject areas and grade levels. For instance, because of the variation in instructional objectives, an English teacher may evaluate AI writing assistance with different aspects of pedagogical content knowledge compared to a mathematics teacher’s assessment of AI-empowered computational aids. Additionally, much research on TPACK has provided limited consideration of contexts. Looking beyond the level of an individual classroom and considering differences in schools, districts, and regional policies can explain variation in the literature (Rosenberg & Koehler, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR30)).

Relatedly, others have offered perspectives complementary to TPACK and further name a range of factors that drive teacher adoption of technology. Ertmer ([1999](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR13)) has described first order and second order barriers in teacher technology adoption or integration, with first order referring to infrastructural resources and the capacity to integrate technology. This comes from adequate support, training, and time to substantively bring technology into the classroom. Second order barriers refer to factors more associated with matters considered “internal” to the teacher, such as teachers’ concerns and general perspectives of technology. This can involve beliefs about teaching, attitudes about technology, and receptiveness to change. If teachers feel that the technology used aligns with their beliefs, they are more likely to use it (Zhao & Cziko, [2001](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR39)). This means that even with adequate first order barriers addressed, how teachers feel about technology and its use in teaching factors into whether teachers will ultimately use it in their practice. This integrated theoretical framework – combining TPACK with insights about first- and second-order barriers – provides insights regarding why teachers across different subjects and grade levels may vary in their willingness to adopt AI chatbots in their pedagogical practice.

### 2.2 Instructional and demographic factors for technology integration

Teaching is not uniform with respect to students and subject matter. We naturally expect teaching in a first grade classroom to look different from an 11th-grade classroom, or to look different for language arts versus math; relatedly, we thus would expect AI chatbot use and needs to differ as well. Indeed, prior literature suggests that subject matter is predictive of differences in technology integration, although findings are inconsistent. For example, Ertmer et al.,’s ([2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR14)) study of 12 teachers considered exemplary in technology use suggests, that science teacher in middle school have a distinct affinity for educational technology relative to language, social studies, math, and general classroom teachers in elementary schools. Zhao and Frank ([2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR40)) conducted an analysis suggesting that English teachers and high school teachers were more likely to integrate technology into instruction compared to their counterparts. Hechter and Vernette ([2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR17)) found that concerns about and conditions amenable to technology integration varied by grade level, with middle and high school teachers being affected by different factors than elementary teachers. These findings guide our study to specifically examine how subject area and grade level may influence teachers’ willingness to incorporate AI chatbots into their instruction.

The role of teaching experience in technology adoption is also significant according to prior studies. Russell et al. ([2007](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR32)) examined whether the amount of time teachers have been in the teaching profession and at their particular school was associated with more or less technology use for various functions. In a sample of 22 Massachusetts school districts, they found some merit to the intuition that newer teachers were more receptive to and more likely to use technology. Teachers in the profession longer than 10 years tended to be less likely to integrate technology into their teaching. Similarly, Liu et al. ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR24)) found a negative effect of teachers’ number of years teaching experience on their confidence and comfort using technology. Accordingly, these studies guide us to incorporate the effect of years in tenure in our analysis of teachers’ decisions in AI chatbot adoption.

### 2.3 Academic integrity

Much of the concern around generative AI and chatbot technology like ChatGPT has been around concerns of integrity (Eke, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR11); Meça & Shkëlzeni, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR26)), particularly regarding teachers’ and students’ use of AI in performing school-related tasks. This is a complex matter because there is inherent ambiguity in what constitutes too much computer assistance in a task such as writing. New chatbot technology can both assist with or do all of the writing a student requests. In general, instructors are known to have some apprehension about use of technology in for fear that it is a temptation for tech-savvy students (Evering & Moorman, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR15)). Indeed, there are reports of students engaged in plagiarism and inappropriately using online connectivity in undergraduate writing (Selwyn, [2008](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR34)). Indeed, prior to the burst of attention around generative AI, large numbers of secondary students were known to engage in “cheating” behaviors, including plagiarism (Josephson Institute, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR20)). However, it is less clear that these numbers are increasing because of the presence of generative AI for high school students (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR23)). Although there is no systematically proved cause-and-effect relationship to date between the use of AI technologies and academic integrity, these concerns may largely influence teachers’ AI adoption decisions and classroom policies regarding AI chatbot usage (Bilen & Matros, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR4); Dendir & Maxwell, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR9)). By looking at teachers’ explicit perspectives toward the newly advanced technology, our studies aim to identify whether plagiarism and academic integrity are the most common concerns that inform their hesitancy or openness to incorporating AI chatbots into daily practice.

3 Research questions
--------------------

We explore teachers’ perspectives regarding generative AI and its use in schools. Specifically, we ask:

*Research question #1: to what extent were there differences in how teachers responded to generative AI use in the classroom based on time on the job, grade levels taught, and subject matter teaching responsibilities?*

*Research question #2: what risks do teachers see for generative AI use for themselves and how do they compare to risks they see for students?*

*Research question #3: what are teachers stated professional development needs, and how do they vary across grade levels, subjects taught, and years of experience?*

4 Method
--------

### 4.1 Data source

This study uses a survey instrument emerging from an established research-practice partnership between our research team and a large urban school district on the West Coast. The ongoing collaboration has allowed district leaders and researchers to understand and address pressing educational challenges. When generative AI tools became widely available in late 2022, district leaders and researchers recognized the need to systematically examine teachers’ perspectives on this transformative technology. Accordingly, for the district’s annual employee survey for all certificated educators in 2023, we added AI-focused questions adapted from prior research on AI’s impact on students’ learning experience at school (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR23)) as part of the survey. Email invitations to personalized Qualtrics links were sent to certificated educators who were employed both semesters that academic year in mid-May, close to the end of the school year. Respondents had until early July to complete the survey. Participants were provided a $15 Amazon gift card upon successful completion of the survey.

For the surveyed year, the school district had 118 schools and served about 63,629 students, with student populations that self-identify as 14% White, 30% Asian, 33% Latino, and 7% Black. The district classified about 27% of students as English Language Learners and 52% as socioeconomically disadvantaged. There were 2,535 teachers invited to participate in the survey, and the complete and effective response rate was 57.35% (66.04% for overall response rate). In particular, out of the 1674 entries of responses received, we used respondents’ responses only if they completed the entire survey (9.26% of the responses are incomplete) and filled in their grade level and subjects taught during the academic year (3.88% of the responses have missing values for either or both of these two questions).

The preprocessing left us to analyze 1,454 survey responses. Of these responses, the teachers predominantly self-identify as White (35.84%) and Asian (33.63%), followed by Hispanic or Latinx (17.02%). Black or African American educators comprise 3.51% of the workforce, while American Indigenous/First Nation/Alaskan Native educators represent 0.48% of the total. In terms of gender distribution, the majority of educators are female (70.99%), with male educators making up 28.26% of the workforce, and a small percentage (0.76%) identifying as undefined (mode detailed demographic information and is shown in APPENDIX I).

While we only receive 57.35% effective response, our balance test results (also shown in APPENDIX I) suggests that the sample is representative of the overall population of certificated educators in the district and only a minor selective bias is present. Among gender distribution, while there are statistically significant differences between the responded with overall or with non-response populations (*p* < 0.05), the magnitude of these differences is relatively small. Female educators are slightly overrepresented in our sample by 3.04 percentage points compared to the overall population and 7.21 percentage points compared to the non-response population. Conversely, male educators are slightly underrepresented in our sample by 2.86 percentage points compared to the overall population and 6.82 percentage points compared to the non-response population. Regarding ethnic distribution, most groups show no statistically significant differences between the sample and overall or non-response population (*p* > 0.05). The main exception is among Asian educators, who are underrepresented in the sample by 4.21 percentage points (*p* = 0.001), and Black or African American educators, who are underrepresented by 1.21 percentage points (*p* = 0.012). However, these differences, while statistically significant, are relatively modest and unlikely to substantially impact the generalizability of our findings to the broader population of the district’s educators.

Regarding the sampled teachers’ grade level taught, the breakdown of responses included 53.43% elementary school teachers, 16.99% middle school teachers, and 28.82% high school teachers. There were some other teachers, such as those working across multiple sites or had special roles (0.76%). The breakdown is shown in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#Fig1). While we acknowledge the imbalance in the grade-level distribution of teachers, this distribution accurately reflects the actual distribution in urban school districts (APPENDIX II demonstrates the evidence statistics).

#### 4.1.1 Survey question

Questions in survey include regular questions that general employee survey asks every year and the newly designed questions related to AI in schools. For regular questions, the survey prompted the teachers to specify their years of teaching experience at the public district, grade level taught during 2022–23 school year, and subject(s) taught during the 2022–23 school year (for middle and secondary teachers) along with other demographic information such as gender and ethnicity. For questions in the new “Artificial Intelligence (AI) Technology in Schools” section (survey wording and scale options are provided in APPENDIX III), teachers are asked to provide their prior usage of the AI chatbot in the past month and whether teachers had explicitly adopted a policy for generative AI chatbot use in their classes. Attitudes about acceptable uses for chatbot technology (AI Allowability items) were queried using a modified version of items described in Lee et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR23)) in which three different levels of writing support (generating ideas, editing or completing a paper, writing an entire paper) and two different uses of generative AI (to explain a concept, to write computer code) were presented and asked.

Open-ended questions were asked in the end of the AI section to prompt teachers provide what they believed to be major concerns about chatbot usage by teachers and students. Teachers were also asked about their professional development needs for generative AI and chatbot technology.

### 4.2 Respondent descriptives

With the preprocessed survey responses, we discuss the sample of teachers’ professional backgrounds in more detail in this section.

#### 4.2.1 Educator’s professional background

Among the completed survey results from teachers, we focus on their grade level, subject, and years of experience to illustrate their professional teaching experience, following observations in the above literature review.

In the exploratory analysis, we defined the years of teaching experience as the length of tenure since teachers joined the district. We calculated their years of service by subtracting their most recent hiring date from the survey date and, following Russell et al. ([2007](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR32)), used a ten-year tenure as a benchmark to classify teachers into “More Experienced Educator” and “Less Experienced Educator” categories for subsequent analysis. The distribution of years of teaching experience among teachers is illustrated in Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#Fig2)(a).

The survey prompted the participants to choose the subjects they teach. Because 93.44% of elementary teachers taught various subjects, we could not recategorize them into macro disciplines for analyzing purposes. These teachers were also grouped into the “Others” category, as their perspectives might differ from those teaching exclusively in STEM or Humanities. The responses to these questions demonstrate teachers’ needs and opinions regarding AI chatbots’ usage in education settings, which is distinctive for students and teachers. The responses illustrate a wide range of teachers’ attitudes and perspectives, as shown in the Result section. Take Q440 as an example: some teachers respond to this question with an answer specifying how the use of a chatbot improves their daily work by “better [using] of time, [improving] communication skills.” Some teachers may acknowledge their inexperience by answering, “I don’t know.” Moreover, others may directly claim, “I don’t think there are any benefits at all.” Accordingly, although the survey only prompted the respondents to report their perceived positive and negative use of AI chatbots in education, teachers’ responses demonstrate their attitudes, presenting more valuable insight for us to analyze. With this in mind, we analyzed these open-ended responses with more in-depth qualitative methods.

### 4.3 Analytical process for open-ended responses

To analyze the differences in perspectives regarding AI chatbots and their usage based on their teaching backgrounds, we employed both qualitative and quantitative methods to the survey results. Specifically, because the survey encompasses both closed and open-ended questions, we performed qualitative coding on textual responses and built regression models for quantitative analysis.

For quantitative analysis, the explanatory variables in our analysis include teachers’ years of teaching experiences in the district, their grade levels, and subjects taught when they fill out the survey. According to their survey responses, the response variable was their allowability for the generative use of AI. We also added interactive variables such as teacher level of experience and subject taught to have a more detailed understanding regarding how different combinations of teaching experience and subject area might affect teachers’ perspectives on AI integration for specific academic tasks (Ertmer et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR14)). This approach allows us to identify whether, for instance, experienced humanities teachers view AI differently than new STEM teachers when it comes to particular uses like writing support or concept explanation.

Because all variables were categorical variables, logistic regression was chosen to estimate the marginal probability regarding how variance in explanatory variables explained changes in teachers’ perspectives. To run logistic regression, we used Python’s *statsmodels* package to conduct the analyses and checked key assumptions. The independence of observations was satisfied through our survey design, where each teacher provided only one response. To check for multicollinearity, we calculated Variance Inflation Factors (VIFs) among our predictor variables and found all VIFs were below 3, indicating no concerning multicollinearity. It was also noted that as variables and interactions were added, the finite sample size made it so the model did not converge due to the size of the teacher population. Thus, while it might be tempting to try to segment teachers into smaller and smaller groups—such as “middle school humanities teachers who have taught for less than 10 years and have used chatbot technologies”—the ability to produce informative statistical analyses decreases, with non-convergence resulting for many such segmentations. With that in mind, there were some larger categories where non-convergence appeared and the literature suggests are worth examining, such as humanities versus STEM. When there is no convergence in these logistic regressions, a chi-square test was then used to assess the presence of potential relationships.

While informed by popular concerns in the public discourse around AI, such as those related to misinformation or plagiarism, we induced a coding scheme from the responses using thematic approaches (Braun & Clarke, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR5)). We pulled a random sample of one hundred responses for qualitative analysis and then applied live coding using exact words and phrases from responses (Saldaña, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR33)). These were then grouped into broader categories for representative codes to capture the corresponding responses’ overall meanings. This was done iteratively to produce a two-level coding scheme: one indicating the broad category of the response’s overall sentiment and themes, and the second providing specific details explaining the rationale behind each theme.

With the developed scheme, a second analyst with no prior experience with the data was brought in for reliability testing. After training on the scheme, the second analyst independently coded 100 records different from the ones used to generate the coding scheme. Cohen’s κ = 0.94 was reached from this reliability test. Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#Tab2) provides more specific reliability breakdowns.

### 5.1 Research question #1: Differences in teacher’s responses regarding generative AI use in the classroom based on time on the job, grade levels taught, and subject matter teaching responsibilities

Firstly, as mentioned in the method section, we adopted logistic regression to measure the magnitude of the impact of teachers’ grade level, subject taught, and their teaching experiences on their perspectives on the allowability of AI chatbots. In this analysis, “No Policy for this” was treated as a non-response, and we analyzed the rest of the responses to understand teachers’ allowability for the five distinct AI usage.

For the holistic analysis of how teachers’ perspectives may vary by the grade level, we treated the levels as ordinal variables to assign teachers teaching elementary school as 1, teaching middle school as 2, and teaching high school as 3 to obtain a numerical evaluation on how well the variation in grade can explain the variance in perspectives. Based on the regression results, our model showed that the increase in grade level teachers’ taught, from elementary to middle school or from middle school to high school, decreased their likelihood of allowing students to use a generative AI chatbot for all usage cases inquired in the survey. The decrease in the probability of using AI chatbots for idea generation was 12.07%, for paper editing was 11.66%, for full paper writing was 13.52%, for concept explaining was 12.49%, and for code generation was 14.99% (results are shown in Table [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#Tab4) and Fig. [5](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#Fig5)). Overall, out of the five different prompted usages, teachers have the highest probability of disallowing the use of AI chatbots to edit or complete a paper.

### 5.2 Research question #2: Perceived benefits for Teachers’ and Students’ use of AI chatbot

Teachers separately identified benefits and concerns regarding students’ and teachers’ AI usage in education. While the specified reasons are similar when explaining students’ and teachers’ benefits and concerns, teachers may hold different standards when they make these judgments for the two groups. We performed Chi-square test to compare the number of teachers marking specific concern and benefit items for teachers with the number of teachers marking concern and benefit items for students. We also ranked the items for concerns and benefits based on the proportion of teachers finishing the survey to assess whether the differences in judging AI’s usage exist.

The Chi-square results indicate that teachers hold differing opinions on the benefits (\({{\chi }^{2}}\_{dof=6} = 77.817,p = 0.000\)) and concerns (\({{\chi }^{2}}\_{dof=7} = 273.019,p = 0.000\)) regarding the educational applications of AI when considering its use for teachers versus students. The issue of overreliance is at the top of the ranking as the defined concern for teachers’ and students’ usage of AI. Overreliance issues were mentioned 10% more often for students than for teachers. In addition, 13.58% of teachers identified misinformation as a concern for themselves, while only 3.17% identified that for students. Meanwhile, plagiarism was perceived as a much more severe issue for students than for teachers, with 26.35% of teachers viewing it as a concern for students’ use of AI compared to only 3.35% for teachers.

### 5.3 Research question #3: Professional developmental needs

The survey also asked teachers to name their current needs for professional learning in light of generative AI chatbot availability. After qualitative coding and summarization, we also investigated categories of needs by teachers from different grade levels, subjects, and tenure of teaching to assess if there are systematic trends for their needs. With the frequency of teachers mentioning different needs in their short responses, we used Chi-squared tests again to evaluate if their answers significantly varied by grade level or by subjects they teach.

Based on the results, we can see that teachers’ differences in needs vary significantly by the grade levels they teach (\({{\chi }^{2}}\_{dof=21}= 63.949,p = 0.000\)), and the differences by subjects for middle and high school teachers are not significant (\({{\chi }^{2}}\_{dof=14}= 17.376,p = 0.237\)). Thus, we could tailor the professional development of AI technology to each grade level.

By examining the results more closely to view the needs across different grade levels (shown in Table [14](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#Tab14)), we found that the need to learn about using AI for pedagogical purposes was similar across all grades. Teachers wondered “how to use it effectively and safely for learning” and sought guidance on “age-appropriate applications,” while others explicitly wanted to know “how to use it academically and professionally.” As one teacher noted, “Ignoring it would be like ignoring a calculator. It is absolutely going to help everyone.” Additionally, teachers in elementary and middle schools expressed interest in learning more basic information about AI. A typical claim they made was that they wanted to learn “everything about ChatGPT,” “What it is? How to use it? Any risks?” or “Really basic. What are possible ways to use it as support….What we should be worried about. Examples of practices that others are using.” These responses indicate that teachers at present are eager to learn but lack essential foundational knowledge about AI tools.

[Full size table](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w/tables/14)

High school teachers showed more interest in learning how to instruct and manage students’ AI use (12.195%) and how to detect AI-generated work (11.707%). Specifically, they were interested in helping students develop technical skills while adapting to a world rich in powerful technology. 6 Discussion and conclusion
---------------------------

Our findings extend previous research on technology integration in education, offering insights specific to teachers’ generative AI adoption in K-12 education. Based on the qualitative and quantitative analysis, results suggest that teachers teaching distinct grade levels and having different years of teaching experience reacted differently and had distinct measurements when generative AI entered the public discourse in the 2022–2023 academic year.

Particularly, our quantitative results reveal that teachers’ resistance to AI chatbot use increased with grade level, a pattern that aligns with but also complicates prior work on technology integration patterns across educational levels (Hechter & Vernette, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR17)). The closer the students were to adulthood, the more resistance seemed to be expressed by teachers. This alignment suggests that, regardless of which types of technology are introduced to the educational system, more attention needs to be paid to addressing grade-specific pedagogical concerns, particularly at higher grade levels where teachers express greater resistance to adoption.

For tasks associated with writing, more experienced humanities teachers, in general, tended to be more conservative regarding the allowance of using AI chatbots for these tasks. Such findings reflect the distinctive nature of humanities education, where critical thinking and independent intellectual development are foundational learning objectives. Humanities teachers’ reservation to use the AI tool seems to reflect from the belief that this will eventually hurt students in developing the necessary analytical and interpretative skills, especially in those areas that require originality, subtlety, and personal involvement with the text, as shown through their short responses in over reliance and plagiarism (Applebee & Langer, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR2)).

The pattern in teachers’ reservations about AI adoption can also be connected to the recent findings about the complexities of integrating AI into writing instruction (Mohamed et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR28)). While AI has demonstrated strong potential to provide support for idea generation, content structuring, literature synthesis, data management, editing, and ethical compliance, teachers demonstrated concerns about authentic learning, as reflected through their responses. These findings provide critical insights into AI technology integration in education, particularly on the balance between expanding AI’s supportive capabilities and ensuring meaningful learning outcomes. Based on these findings, future research should prioritize developing empirically validated frameworks that address teachers’ pedagogical concerns while systematically leveraging AI’s potential to enhance student learning outcomes. Similar to the successful integration of digital media in education (Warschauer, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR37)), this process requires careful attention to both pedagogical principles and practical implementation strategies. Furthermore, comprehensive assessment of AI instruments is crucial for assisting educators in comprehending how these innovations can enhance, rather than supplant, the cultivation of students’ critical thinking and writing competencies (Mishra & Koehler, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR27); Valverde et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR36)). This would ensure that AI integration into education is grounded in sound pedagogical principles and serves the practical needs of classroom teachers.

When assessing AI chatbots’ benefits and concerns for teachers and students, teachers expressed different perspectives on the two groups’ adaptation except for the common concern of overreliance. For teachers, misinformation was considered a significant challenge, whereas plagiarism is the primary concern for students. The emphasis on plagiarism for students echoes longstanding concerns about academic integrity in digital environments (Evering & Moorman, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR15); Selwyn, [2008](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR34); Josephson Institute, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR20)), even though interestingly, recent research suggests that fears about increased cheating due to AI have been overstated (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR23)).

This disparity between perception and reality might indicate that educators’ fears are more a product of extensive public discussions and media narratives about the potential abuse of AI in educational contexts than of personal experiences, especially considering that most teachers in our research had not used AI tools independently. As Eke ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR11)) notes, discussions about AI in education have been mostly about academic integrity, potentially amplifying teachers’ anxieties beyond what current evidence can support. Bilen and Matros ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR4)) also argued that fears about widespread cheating often exceed documented cases. This pattern of heightened concern may parallel the typical trend in technological adoption in education, where people’s perceptions about adaptation usually start with initial fears about misuse.

Additionally, teachers identified that the top benefit of AI for students is information retrieval, and for teachers, the top benefit is efficiency improvement. The findings demonstrate that teachers’ evaluation of AI diverges when considering its integration into teaching versus students’ learning processes. This distinction mirrors patterns observed in previous studies of educational technology adoption, where teachers often prioritize tools that streamline administrative tasks while being more cautious about technologies that directly impact student learning processes (Kim, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR22)). The findings demonstrate that teachers’ evaluation of AI diverges when considering its integration into teaching versus students’ learning processes. The distinct patterns in perceived benefits and concerns suggest the need for differentiated approaches to AI integration, similar to what Zhao and Cziko ([2001](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR39)) found regarding teachers’ technology adoption patterns. As schools work to develop targeted professional development programs, they must address both pedagogical and practical concerns (Diliberti et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR10)), while considering how teachers’ perceptions may evolve with increased exposure and experience.

Lastly, the survey results revealed that teachers’ professional development needs for AI adaptation varied across grade levels, reflecting the contextual nature of technology integration that Rosenberg and Koehler ([2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR30)) emphasized in their TPACK framework analysis. While basic information about AI and the use of AI for pedagogical purposes are common needs across all teachers from the district, some grade levels may prefer one over the other during the limited period of professional learning sessions. This variation aligns with Ertmer et al.’s ([2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR14)) findings that technology integration needs differ significantly by grade level and subject area, suggesting that a one-size-fits-all approach to AI professional development may be ineffective. Accordingly, future district professional development design should consider such differences when selecting materials and resources to address unique needs, while also acknowledging what Liu et al. ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR24)) identified as the relationship between teacher confidence with technology and their professional learning preferences. This targeted approach could help address both the technical and pedagogical barriers to AI integration that Chan and Tang ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13350-w#ref-CR6)) identified as crucial for successful technology adoption in schools.

In sum, this research has employed survey data from the district to investigate teachers’ immediate reactions and actions after generative AI becomes much more accessible in education. The findings have highlighted differences in teachers’ adaptability to AI and perceptions of AI’s values based on different AI applications for teachers and students.

### 6.1 Limitations

While this study includes a full analysis of teachers’ reception of AI as this technology became more accessible, there are inherent limitations.

Firstly, while this study used a survey instrument that is rigorously designed and the sample proved to be representative of the studied population, there remain limitations in the methods. Our sampling strategy was based on an established research-practice partnership with a single urban school district, resulting in a self-selective response pattern where approximately 60% of invited teachers participated. While our balance tests suggest the sample is generally representative of the district’s teacher population, this self-selection could potentially introduce bias, as teachers who responded might have different attitudes toward technology than those who did not participate. Also, while the distribution of teachers across grade levels reflects the authentic distribution of the urban district, our findings may not generalize to other districts with different grade-level compositions. Additionally, the relatively small sample sizes for certain subgroups, particularly middle school teachers, limits our statistical power to detect potential relationships between variables, suggesting that some null results should be interpreted with caution. Accordingly, given the demographic characteristics, while providing authentic insights into our specific context, we suggest that the findings should be interpreted cautiously when considering applications to districts with different grade-level distributions. Particularly, due to the correlational nature of our study, no causal inferences can be drawn from these findings. Future research could strengthen these findings through more rigorous methodologies, such as randomized controlled trials or longitudinal studies that track changes in teacher attitudes and technology adoption over time.

Secondly, because this survey was collected in 2023, how teachers’ perspectives have changed since their first-year experience with AI remains unexplored. There is a lag period in academic research and dissemination, in particular given the importance and need for peer review, which takes time. At the same time, as AI becomes a more familiar concept and relevant tools for schools are deployed, teachers may find new benefits and identify new concerns from what was given in this survey. Accordingly, the suggestions regarding specifics for professional development can only be applicable in recent years. Future research could develop a dashboard with data extraction and analysis such that when new survey results come in, the suggestions regarding PD could be automatically generated for faculties to take into account. However, privacy and teacher choice about participation should always be prioritized.

Thirdly, because all teachers’ responses were captured in the survey form with limited response length and choices, other facets of teacher experience and thinking about AI are not captured in this instrument. Some initial research that has captured some of these nuances and variations has appeared, sometimes at a higher clip than surveys of this nature. Regardless, all forms of information that have been rigorously collected and analyzed can be helpful as we look to better understand and support teachers in this new age of abundance of generative AI chatbot technology.

### Appendix IV: Code schema documentation

### 4.1 Qualitative coding guidelines

This coding scheme aims to conceptualize the codes developed to qualitatively label the collected response regarding educators’ perspectives on Generative AI. The guideline will firstly provide instructions for qualitative coding, and the second section “Code Definition & Examples” presents definitions of codes with the examples sourced from the sample responses.

### 4.2 Coding instructions

![]()

1. 1.
   
   The Code is the subcategories for the Categories in this code schema, the following charts display all the categories and codes. All codes have been embedded in the dropdown menu, after you select the category, the selectable codes in the following column are its subcategories.
2. 2.
   
   To start coding, read the response on the second column.
   
   1. a.
      
      If there is **only one** statement included in the response, you can go to the “Primary Category” column, pick the category which the meaning of the statement can fall under and pick the codes within the “Primary Code”.
   2. b.
      
      If there are **several statements**, select the code in the column of “First-Mentioned Code” first, then go to the “Primary Category”, “Primary Code”, “Secondary Category”, etc.

### 4.3 Code definition & examples

#### 4.3.1 First category: Minimal information

| Insufficient Information in the Response (N/A or too vague) | Definition: The question does not apply to the respondents  Example: “n/a”, “/”, “IDK”, “Not sure yet”, “Not sure”, |
| --- | --- |
| Insufficient personal knowledge about AI | Definition: The responders don’t have enough knowledge, expertise, or clarity to offer a conclusive response to the question  Example:  “Have not used it at all at school”  “We didn’t use it” |

#### 4.3.2 Second category: Constraints in usage

| Refuse to use by choice (because of plagiarism, overreliance, more work for teacher, etc.) | Definition: These responses indicate educators’ refusal and distrust of generative AI due to their faith in the “brain,” students’ innate capability in thinking and learning, etc  Example:  “There are benefits to allowing the brain to atrophy?”  “why do we need to depend on AI when we have FUNCTIONING BRAINS and other humans to learn from”  “See no benefits” |
| --- | --- |
| Restriction from Outside of School (eg parents) | Definition: Responses include factors that reside outside of the school that prevent teachers from using generative AI in the classroom. These factors could include the constraints of families (parents in particular), guardians of students  Example:  “The parents in our grade level prefer limited technology. AI would need to be discussed with parents before using.” |
| Restriction from inside of School (eg administrators) | Definition: Responses include factors that reside inside of the school that prevent teachers from using generative AI in the classroom.These factors may include school administrators, principals, therapist  Example: “the principle refuses the integration of technology” |
| Other Constraints | This code captures any other responses that may be considered under “Constraints in Usage” |

#### 4.3.3 Third category: Concerns for the learning process

| Misinformation | Definition: This code identifies educators’ concerns in presence, creation, dissemination, or effects of false or inaccurate information within generative AI  Example:  “wrong information”  “It is not always factually accurate; hinders student growth in critical thinking” |
| --- | --- |
| Lacking social interaction | Definition: This identifies responses highlighting a perceived deficiency or absence of direct and face-to-face interaction between teachers and students with the presence of generative AI  Example:  “teaching will feel more robotic and less hands-on”  “teachers will sound like robots and will get robot responses, they spirit will be lacking”  “teacher creativity becomes sterile and robotic” |
| Plagiarism/Misuse of Generative AI | Definition: This identifies responses talking about the inappropriate and unethical use of generative AI technologies. This includes using AI for creating deceptive content, solutions to test or homework questions  Example:  “Students may not write their own work and may not experience the whole thinking, writing, and editing cycle.”  “They copy the material” |
| Students’ Overreliance Issues | Definition: This identifies responses expressing concerns about students’ overreliance on generative AI for educational purposes  Example:  “Again, I am going to focus on translation tools, since I work with newcomers, and I am already concerned about the students’ over-reliance on Google translate;”  “Becoming too dependent on technology and losing own creativity in certain tasks” |
| Teachers’ Overreliance Issues | Definition: This identifies responses expressing concerns about teachers’ overreliance on generative AI for educational purposes  Example:  “teachers becoming reliant of AI”  “teachers are lazy” |
| Other Concerns | Definition: This identifies responses that poorly specify concerns about the impacts of generative AI on students. This may involve educators worrying about students’ ability to think critically and analyze information, creativity, and verbal or written self-expression  Example:  “we do not know the long term consequences of opening this pandora’s box”  “using no creativity and their own imagination”  “Students are sometimes unable to express themselves clearly” |
| No Concern | Definition: This identifies responses stating they have no concerns regarding the generative AI |

#### 4.3.4 Fourth category: Recognized benefits

| Prepare Lesson Plans or Lesson Materials | Definition: This identifies responses discussing the use of generative AI in the creation, development, or enhancement of educational materials for students. This includes using AI to generate teaching resources, learning objectives, study guides, etc  Example:  “using it to create lesson objectives using the common core standards for a lesson and helping to create shared writing drafts and rubrics”  “to see what kind of lesson plan it would make and compare it to mine”  “Quickly writing student comments on report cards. Quickly writing mentor essays” |
| --- | --- |
| Edit, improve, or modify existing text | Definition: This identifies responses discussing the use of generative AI in checking and correcting errors in text, including grammar, spelling, punctuation, etc  Example:  “Explaining difficult word problems or providing grammar support.”  “Edit work, English practice” |
| Provides information—search for information | Definition: This identifies responses discussing the application of generative AI in conducting searches or retrieving information, definitions, and complementary learning materials  Example:  “To get quick responses to issues or questions we may have.”  “To assist them with gathering information Giving explanations of concepts” |
| Increased Efficiency other than reasons above | Definition: This identifies responses discussing using generative AI to conserve time or speed up processes  Example: “To save time!” |
| Other benefit not named above | This code captures any other responses that may be considered under “Recognized Benefits.” |

#### 4.3.5 Fifth category: Logistical issues/needs

| Inadequate Time or Compensation to Learn | Definition: This highlights comments about not getting enough pay to learn new skills and use new technology, as well as responses highlighting problems with not having enough time to learn and integrate generative AI tools into the classroom setting  Example:  “I think there are benefits but I do not have the time or capacity to think about them/explain them in this survey. Teachers should be paid for this type of labor.” |
| --- | --- |
| Others Logistical Issues | This code captures any other responses that may be considered under “Logistical Issues/Needs.”  Example:  “we don’t have the equipment/internet quality/tech support/money to pay for it” |

#### 4.3.6 Sixth category: Desire more information (specifically for Q444)

| Basic Introduction about what AI is (Advantage, Disadvantage, etc.) | Definition: This highlights comments mentioning the need to learn about the basic information related to generative AI  Example:  “risks and benefits”  “what is it ? what are the issues and advantages?”  “Everything, I don’t know what it is or ever heard of it.”  “From a teacher’s perspective, I would want more workshops about teaching technology in general, there are many teachers who are technologically illiterate especially in SFUSD, with us working in and living in Silicon Valley I think teachers should know more about coding and using it in education.” |
| --- | --- |
| How to detect AI generated work | Definition: This identifies responses mentioning the use of AI in detection of plagiarism and controlling the inappropriate or unauthorized use of content  Example:  “How to detect AI-generated plagiarized student work; Alternate ways to offer students to use AI so they do not plagiarize”  “How to manage and understand if students are using AIchat to complete work for them. Similar to "turnitin.com” |
| How to Instruct and Manage Students to Use AI (Facilitate Learning) | Definition: This identifies responses talking about usage containment for students’ usage of generative AI  Example:  “Teaching students to use it while keeping the learning benefit.”  “How to prevent students from cheating using AI chatbots.” |
| How to appropriately use AI for pedagogical purpose (draft lesson plan, engage students, etc.) | Example:  “How can teachers use AI to support their students.”  “Ways to save time and engage students with it”  “how to use it in the classroom without stifling the students learning” |
| Others Needs | This code captures any other responses that may be considered under “Logistical Issues/Needs.”  Example:  “we don’t have the equipment/internet quality/tech support/money to pay for it” |
