Abstract
--------

This study explores how Chinese university EFL learners accept and utilise ChatGPT, employing the widely recognised UTAUT2 model. A quantitative approach was utilised, collecting data from 340 participants through an online questionnaire and analysing it using structural equation modelling (SEM). The results indicate that habitual behaviour considerably influences students’ behavioural intentions (BI) and actual use behaviour (UB), underscoring the importance of fostering regular engagement with ChatGPT. Performance expectancy and social influence also positively impact BI, whereas hedonic motivation, effort expectancy, and price value show non-significant effects. Interestingly, facilitating conditions exert a significant influence on actual use behaviour, despite their weak influence on BI. The study contrasts with prior research by highlighting the minimal impact of behavioural intention on actual use, suggesting that habit and supporting resources are more critical in this context. These findings offer invaluable insights for educators and policymakers aiming to incorporate AI tools into language learning curricula, emphasising the need for habitual use and clear communication of ChatGPT’s benefits.

Introduction
------------

As artificial intelligence (AI) continues to advance, chatbot technology has permeated various domains of everyday life and found widespread use in multiple industries (Yu et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR72)). The evolution of advanced AI-driven models, such as ChatGPT (developed by OpenAI), facilitated by improvements in chatbot capabilities and language processing technology, has been hailed as the dawn of a new era.

AI technology, including advanced AI-driven language models like ChatGPT, has exhibited an impressive capacity to generate human-like textual output, addressing inquiries, and delivering explanations (Gilson et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR19)). The potential applications of these advanced language models, particularly ChatGPT, extend significantly to the realm of education, wherein they show considerable promise in enhancing pedagogical methodologies and learning outcomes. ChatGPT holds immense promise in enhancing the quality and effectiveness of educational practices, including personalised content, supporting homework tasks, and offering constructive feedback to learners (Xu et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR69)).

Incorporating ChatGPT into higher education holds the potential to transform and make far-reaching changes in teaching and learning practices by functioning as an AI-powered tool for various educational tasks (Lim et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR40)), and this integration presents transformative possibilities for pedagogical praxis. ChatGPT’s versatility allows it to operate independently or be seamlessly integrated into myriad systems and platforms used by higher education institutions. The integration of ChatGPT can enhance students’ learning experiences by generating diverse ways of articulating ideas and providing immediate, personalised feedback based on data supplied by students or instructors (Hwang & Chen, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR29)).

Building upon the preceding discourse, there has been a discernible increase in scholarly attention focused on the potential integration of ChatGPT in higher education contexts. This emerging focus encompasses a broad spectrum of academic disciplines, addressing both general pedagogical strategies and specific applications in areas such as science education. The literature reflects a growing acknowledgement of how AI technologies, such as ChatGPT, can transform educational practices and enhance learning outcomes across diverse fields.

Scholarly investigations have identified numerous advantages of integrating ChatGPT into language learning paradigms. Its application in language education is recognised for fostering meaningful interaction, providing real-time feedback, and enabling students to engage in innovative, personalised learning processes (Xu et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR69); Yan, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR71)). Through the simulation of authentic conversational scenarios, chatbots allow learners to experience target language use in contexts beyond the classroom (Liu et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR42)). Furthermore, Sysoyev and Filatov ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR63)) discuss how chatbots leverage natural language processing (NLP) to simulate human dialogue, which not only enhances student motivation but also provides opportunities for practicing language skills outside traditional classroom settings. This feature is particularly beneficial, as it helps alleviate learner anxiety by creating a non-judgemental environment for language practice.

Despite the recognised benefits of AI-powered chatbots in language learning, there is limited research on how university EFL learners perceive and use these tools for autonomous, personalised EFL learning. This gap in research is particularly concerning, presenting several critical challenges for educational practitioners and institutions.

Without a clear understanding of learners’ perceptions and usage patterns, educators may struggle to design effective ChatGPT-enhanced learning environments that align with students’ needs and preferences. Consequently, such misalignment may lead to ineffective technology integration, resulting in reduced learner engagement, decreased motivation, and suboptimal learning outcomes.

Beyond designing effective learning environments, the absence of data on learner perceptions also has broader implications for equitable and resource-efficient integration of AI technologies in education. Poorly executed technology integration efforts risk wasting institutional resources and missing opportunities to optimise language learning outcomes. Moreover, a lack of understanding can perpetuate inequitable access to technological benefits, particularly in educational contexts where AI integration is rapidly expanding.

Such challenges underscore the urgency of addressing this knowledge gap in order to develop pedagogical strategies that fully harness ChatGPT’s potential and ensure its successful integration into language education. The importance of aligning AI integration with student needs is a perspective shared by Chun ([2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR11)), who emphasised the dual necessity of leveraging technology and understanding student goals to advance foreign language education in the AI era. This understanding is crucial for effectively integrating chatbots into language education and enhancing support for student learning.

Drawing on these identified gaps, this study explicitly poses the research question: How do Chinese university EFL learners perceive and adopt ChatGPT for autonomous and personalised language learning, and what factors influence their acceptance of it within the UTAUT2 framework? This investigation directly addresses the limited understanding of learners’ perceptions and usage patterns, focusing on adoption factors specific to this population and educational context.

To effectively align AI integration with student needs, it is essential to ground investigations in robust theoretical frameworks, such as the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology 2 (UTAUT2) of Venkatesh et al. ([2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR66)), which offers a comprehensive lens for examining technology acceptance.

While UTAUT2 has been extensively employed to investigate technology acceptance, prior research using this framework has predominantly focused on conventional educational technologies, such as learning management systems, e-learning, and mobile-assisted language learning platforms (Kang et al., [2015](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR31); Moorthy et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR46); Raza et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR52)). However, AI-powered tools such as ChatGPT introduce unique capabilities that differ fundamentally from these more traditional technologies.

In contrast to traditional educational technologies, ChatGPT brings unique affordances that redefine the boundaries of technology-assisted learning. Leveraging advanced NLP, ChatGPT generates human-like responses, enabling dialogic, adaptive, and personalised interactions with learners (Rezai et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR53); Yu & Chauhan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR73)). This capability positions ChatGPT as a dynamic learning assistant rather than a static tool, offering immediate, context-specific feedback, fostering authentic language practice, and adapting to individual learners’ needs in real time. These features make ChatGPT particularly relevant for language learning, where meaningful interaction is critical for developing fluency and comprehension (Li, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR39)).

Despite these transformative features, the current body of research largely overlooks the distinct adoption patterns associated with AI-powered tools like ChatGPT, necessitating further exploration. The integration of conversational AI technologies like ChatGPT raises unique questions about performance expectancy, effort expectancy, and habit formation, given their distinct affordances and potential to redefine user-technology interactions. For example, ChatGPT’s capacity to provide personalised learning experiences and foster self-regulated learning may alter the relative importance of traditional UTAUT2 factors. This technological paradigm shift necessitates a methodologically robust investigation of how established theoretical constructs manifest in AI-enhanced learning environments.

By addressing these unique affordances and extending established frameworks, this study contributes to both theoretical advancements and practical applications. Specifically, it extends the UTAUT2 model to include ‘personal innovativeness’, a factor that captures the willingness of learners to experiment with cutting-edge technologies (discussed further below). Personal innovativeness is particularly relevant in this context due to its association with prior study habits, as learners with a propensity to explore innovative tools are more likely to have developed autonomous learning practices that align with ChatGPT’s capabilities for self-directed, personalised language practice (Agarwal & Prasad, [1998](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR2); Huang & Hamdan, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR28)). Students exhibiting higher personal innovativeness may have already developed adaptive study strategies that render them more receptive to incorporating AI tools into their existing language learning routines. Previous studies in EFL contexts have demonstrated that personal innovativeness significantly predicts technology integration in language learning (Ma, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR43)), suggesting its relevance for understanding ChatGPT acceptance patterns. While UTAUT2 effectively captures external factors influencing technology adoption, personal innovativeness addresses the intrinsic individual differences that may be particularly salient in educational technology contexts where adoption is largely voluntary, especially among Chinese EFL learners who may leverage such innovativeness to meet academic demands in a competitive educational environment. As ChatGPT represents an emerging class of AI-driven educational tools, understanding how individual and contextual factors influence its acceptance is crucial for optimising its integration into educational environments.

Context-specific focus is critical for understanding the practical implications of AI adoption in diverse educational settings. This study addresses a significant gap by analysing Chinese university EFL learners’ engagement with AI-powered tools, focusing on how these students accept and use ChatGPT and exploring factors that influence their adoption or resistance. Understanding these interactions is crucial to integrating these technologies into education.

As part of this investigation, it is vital to consider the cultural and educational demands that influence university students’ technology adoption, particularly in contexts like China where societal and academic pressures are pronounced. Unlike K-12 learners, university students are more likely to require tools for self-regulated and autonomous learning, given the increasing academic demands of higher education (Anthonysamy et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR6)). Moreover, cultural factors, such as high regard for technology in China and the societal emphasis on academic excellence (Huang et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR27); Li, [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR37)), may shape learners’ attitudes towards AI adoption differently from their counterparts in other regions. These distinctions highlight the necessity of examining this specific population to provide actionable insights that contribute to the broader field of AI in education.

Building on this understanding of cultural and societal influences, it is equally important to explore the individual characteristics and contextual factors that shape AI acceptance among university students in higher education. Compared to K-12 learners, university students benefit from a more independent learning environment, fostering greater autonomy in adopting technology (Khalid et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR32)). This independence, coupled with enhanced critical thinking skills, enables them to evaluate AI-powered tools critically and align their use with academic objectives (Li & Salleh, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR38)). These attributes highlight the relevance of university learners as a focal group for examining AI acceptance, particularly in language learning contexts.

While these traits are common among university students globally, Chinese EFL learners represent a particularly compelling group for study due to the unique interplay of cultural, societal, and educational pressures influencing their engagement with AI tools. In China, the societal emphasis on academic excellence and widespread regard for technological innovation may distinctly influence learners’ attitudes towards AI adoption. These cultural dynamics, coupled with the educational pressures faced by university students, distinguish their experiences from those of students in other regions or age groups. Examining this specific population provides actionable insights not only into the factors driving AI acceptance but also into the broader implications for integrating AI tools into diverse educational contexts. This interplay of cultural, societal, and individual factors necessitates a robust theoretical lens to understand the drivers of AI adoption, making the UTAUT2 framework particularly relevant for this study.

This study is grounded in the UTAUT2 model of Venkatesh et al. ([2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR66)). In line with previous research (Biloš & Budimir, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR9); Strzelecki et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR61)), and in addition to the seven established constructs from the UTAUT2 framework (Venkatesh et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR66)), we incorporated the construct of ‘personal innovativeness’, a construct originally proposed by Agarwal and Prasad ([1998](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR2)), capturing the willingness of individuals to experiment with emerging technologies. This construct is instrumental in elucidating how individual differences may affect the adoption and application of ChatGPT. Within this theoretical framework, behavioural intention (BI) serves as a central mediator, linking the key constructs—such as performance expectancy, effort expectancy, and social influence—with the actual use of ChatGPT (use behaviour). This mediation role reflects the premise that individuals’ intentions are a critical precursor to their actions. By analysing BI’s mediating effects, this study aims to offer a deeper insight into how various factors collectively influence students’ actual adoption and utilisation of ChatGPT in language learning contexts. This study further examines these interrelations by focusing on key constructs such as performance expectancy and effort expectancy, which are foundational in understanding ChatGPT adoption.

Drawing from extensive empirical research, this study examines the primary factors affecting AI acceptance in educational contexts through the UTAUT2 framework, with particular attention to how these factors manifest in EFL contexts. At the foundation of AI acceptance, performance expectancy (PE) emerges as a critical predictor of behavioural intention (BI) in adopting AI technologies in language learning, where users perceive AI tools as enhancing teaching and learning outcomes (Alhadiah, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR4); Zheng et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR76)). This construct has emerged as a primary driver in educational contexts, with studies showing that students’ belief in AI’s ability to enhance academic performance significantly influences adoption (Biloš & Budimir, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR9); Habibi et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR20)). Similarly, effort expectancy (EE), linked to the perceived ease of using AI tools in EFL classrooms, positively correlates with BI, particularly when learners feel confident in managing AI-assisted learning platforms (Xu & Thien, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR70)). Ease of use is particularly relevant in language learning contexts, where it determines sustained engagement (Zhang & Pan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR75)). While individual perceptions are critical, external environmental and social influences also play a significant role in shaping AI adoption patterns.

Beyond these individual perceptions, environmental and social factors play crucial roles in AI adoption. Social influence (SI), including peer and institutional encouragement, demonstrates mixed effects in EFL contexts, often shaped by cultural attitudes towards AI use (Lin et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR41); Ragheb et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR51)). Notably, SI plays a distinctive role in Asian educational settings, where peer and instructor attitudes significantly shape technology adoption (Habibi et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR20)). Furthermore, facilitating conditions (FC), such as institutional support for AI integration and technical infrastructure, have been shown to critically affect AI tool implementation in higher education (Zheng et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR76)). The perceived economic value proposition, conceptualised as price value (PV) in the UTAUT2 framework, has emerged as another significant determinant of behavioural intention, particularly in educational technology adoption where students weigh the benefits against potential monetary investments (Romero-Rodríguez et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR55)).

Equally important, and complementary to these external influences, are the personal dimensions that shape AI acceptance patterns, which provide deeper insight into learners’ engagement with AI tools. Hedonic motivation (HM) emerges as a significant factor in engaging EFL learners, where interactive and enjoyable AI tools like ChatGPT foster intrinsic motivation for language practice (Zhang & Pan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR75)). Additionally, ​ habit (H), particularly the habitual use of AI tools in language tasks, further contributes to AI acceptance (Alhadiah, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR4); Zhang & Pan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR75)). Moreover, personal innovativeness (PI) significantly influences EFL learners, especially in adopting AI for tasks like writing feedback, conversational practice, and personalised learning (Huang & Hamdan, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR28)). Understanding these factors is essential for examining how Chinese university EFL learners perceive and adopt ChatGPT in their educational practices, which this study aims to address.

Incorporating these constructs enables a nuanced exploration of how various factors interrelate, with behavioural intention serving as a pivotal mediator within this framework. By analysing these factors, this study endeavours to offer in-depth insights into the acceptance and practical use of ChatGPT among Chinese university EFL learners. By exploring learners’ attitudes and practices regarding ChatGPT, the findings will enhance our understanding of how AI tools are transforming language learning beyond traditional classroom settings. This understanding will help in developing strategies to better integrate AI tools into language learning curricula, thereby enhancing the overall quality of education and outcomes for language learners.

This research extends beyond the Chinese context by developing a comprehensive theoretical framework for understanding AI acceptance in language learning that can be adapted to various cultural contexts. While focusing on Chinese learners, the study identifies universal factors in student acceptance of AI tools while highlighting culture-specific variations, thus providing valuable methodological insights for investigating AI adoption in different educational settings.

The findings of this study will contribute to the development of evidence-based frameworks for AI integration in language education, identifying potential barriers to the adoption of AI tools among EFL learners. These insights will inform institutional policies and support educators in designing effective AI-enhanced learning environments that align with learner needs and expectations. As higher education institutions globally navigate the challenges of AI integration, this research offers timely guidance for optimising implementation strategies and avoiding potential pitfalls.

Literature review
-----------------

The UTAUT2 model builds on the original framework by including additional variables that enhance the understanding of technology acceptance. It incorporates the four principal determinants of UTAUT—performance expectancy (perceived utility), effort expectancy (perceived ease of use), social influence (social endorsement), and facilitating conditions (infrastructural support)—along with three additional constructs: hedonic motivation (perceived enjoyment), price value (cost–benefit analysis), and habit (behavioural habituation) (Venkatesh et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR66)).

The UTAUT2 is designed to explore the elements that impact technology adoption and usage in both organisational and personal contexts (Lavidas et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR36)). This model serves as a robust analytical tool for investigating factors that affect the adoption of technological innovations in diverse contexts. Its application spans a wide array of educational technologies, encompassing virtual learning environments (Kumarasamy et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR35)), mobile-based educational applications (Açıkgül & Şad, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR1)), immersive virtual and augmented reality (Du & Liang, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR15)), and institutional learning management systems (Qian, [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR50)). This broad applicability of UTAUT2 provides a foundation for examining technology acceptance in specific fields, such as EFL education, where its constructs have yielded significant empirical insights.

Empirical studies investigating technology acceptance in EFL contexts have yielded significant insights into learner behaviour and adoption patterns. For instance, research by Xu and Thien ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR70)) found that Chinese EFL learners’ performance expectancy significantly predicted their intention to use AI-powered language learning tools. Similarly, a study by Zheng et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR76)) revealed that effort expectancy, hedonic motivation, and social influence were crucial determinants of AI tool adoption in EFL learning among Chinese university students. In the context of AI-assisted language learning, Cai et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR10)) demonstrated that facilitating conditions and hedonic motivation significantly influenced EFL students’ continued use of AI-assisted learning platforms. They further posited that behavioural intention serves as a more robust predictor of learning effectiveness in ChatGPT-assisted language learning compared to performance expectancy. These empirical findings highlight the unique characteristics of EFL learners’ technology acceptance patterns, particularly in Asian educational contexts, where cultural factors and institutional support play crucial roles in technology adoption decisions.

Building on these findings, our study seeks to further examine the applicability of UTAUT2 in AI-assisted EFL learning, with an emphasis on ChatGPT technology and additional determinants such as personal innovativeness.

Our research hypothesises that the key determinants of the UTAUT2 framework affect Chinese university EFL students’ behavioural intention to use ChatGPT technology. Consistent with Biloš and Budimir ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR9)) and Strzelecki et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR61)), we include personal innovativeness into our analytical framework. Personal innovativeness (PI)—a construct linked to individuals’ openness to new technology, initially conceptualised by (Agarwal & Prasad, [1998](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR2))—has been recognised as an important determinant in technology implementation processes. Scholars suggest that traits like PI are essential for shaping technology acceptance within the realm of information technology (Biloš & Budimir, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR9); Huang & Hamdan, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR28)).

Our study seeks to investigate the impact of the factors delineated in the UTAUT2 framework, as well as personal innovativeness, on the behavioural intention of Chinese EFL university students to utilise ChatGPT. Furthermore, this investigation aims to assess these students’ perceptions of ChatGPT’s effectiveness in enhancing their language acquisition processes. This study aims to guide educational strategies and promote the effective implementation of AI tools in higher education by examining the key elements influencing the adoption and utilisation of ChatGPT.

Hypotheses development
----------------------

In the UTAUT theoretical framework (Venkatesh et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR66)), performance expectancy (PE) is defined as the user’s beliefs and perceptions about how effectively a technological system can enhance and maximise performance across various tasks. In this study, PE denotes EFL students’ belief that utilising ChatGPT will enhance their language skills and lead to better academic outcomes. Recent studies have shown that PE is a significant factor affecting behavioural intention (BI) to integrate AI tools in learning environments (Alhwaiti, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR5); Du & Liang, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR15); Lin et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR41)). Given the strong support from prior studies, we hypothesise:

### H1

PE will have a positive impact on Chinese university EFL students’ BI to use ChatGPT in language learning practices.

Effort expectancy (EE) evaluates the perceived ease of use of ChatGPT among students, indicating the effort required for effective technology utilisation (Venkatesh et al., [2003](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR65)). EE has been shown to be a vital determinant of technology adoption, directly impacting individuals’ behavioural intentions to adopt and engage with new technologies (Alhwaiti, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR5); Du & Liang, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR15)). In our study, we describe EE as the extent to which Chinese university EFL learners perceive interacting with ChatGPT to be easy and straightforward and requiring minimal effort. We hypothesise:

### H2

EE will have a positive impact on Chinese university EFL students’ BI to use ChatGPT in language learning practices.

Social influence (SI) examines the impact of interpersonal and broader institutional dynamics and educational environment on students’ attitudes towards ChatGPT. This construct suggests that users’ BI is significantly influenced by their social circles, as individuals often conform to social influences, particularly in short-term decisions such as technology adoption (Venkatesh, [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR64)). Researchers have emphasised that SI significantly affects BI across different AI applications (Kalınkara & Özdemir, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR30); Ragheb et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR51)). We define SI as the extent to which Chinese university EFL learners perceive support or encouragement from key individuals in their social networks—such as classmates or instructors—concerning their use of ChatGPT. We hypothesise:

### H3

SI will have a positive impact on Chinese university EFL students’ BI to use ChatGPT in language learning practices.

Hedonic motivation (HM) is defined as the enjoyment users experience from interacting with technology, and it has been proven to significantly affect users’ intentions (Venkatesh et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR66)). Prior investigations, such as those by Kumarasamy et al. ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR35)) and Habibi et al. ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR20)), demonstrate that students exhibit a higher propensity to sustain their utilisation of a technology if they perceive it as enjoyable. This highlights the significance of affective factors in technology acceptance, extending beyond purely utilitarian considerations. In this study, HM is operationalised as the degree to which Chinese university EFL learners find ChatGPT engaging and enjoyable, and their overall pleasure in interacting with new AI tools. We hypothesise:

### H4

HM will have a positive impact on Chinese university EFL students’ BI to use ChatGPT in language learning practices.

Facilitating conditions (FC) refer to the resources and assistance necessary to effectively use technological systems (Venkatesh, [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR64)). The UTAUT2 model highlights FC as a key determinant influencing both users’ behavioural intentions and actual usage behaviour. Studies have examined the significant connections between FC and both the intention to use and the actual utilisation of technological innovations, with a particular focus on AI (Biloš & Budimir, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR9); Strzelecki, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR59); Venkatesh, [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR64)). For instance, Strzelecki ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR59)) and Biloš and Budimir ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR9)) found that, while FC did not have a notable impact on BI, it did significantly affect actual use behaviour. We hypothesise:

### H5

FC will have a positive impact on Chinese university EFL students’ BI to use ChatGPT in language learning practices.

### H6

FC will have a positive impact on Chinese university EFL students’ ChatGPT use behaviour in language learning practices.

Habit (H) pertains to the impact of established routine behaviours on the acceptance and usage of ChatGPT, illustrating how consistently individuals engage with the technology. According to Venkatesh et al. ([2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR66)), habit plays a crucial role as a key factor that significantly predicts both BI and technology use. Recent studies have underlined the essential role of habit in determining intentions and engagement with AI technologies (Lavidas et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR36); Strzelecki, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR59)). This study conceptualises habit as the extent to which Chinese university EFL learners have established consistent patterns of interaction with ChatGPT, resulting in its automatic and regular usage for language learning purposes. This concept emphasises that frequent engagement can evolve into habitual behaviour, thereby influencing intentions and actual usage. We hypothesise:

### H7

Habit will have a positive impact on Chinese university EFL students’ BI to use ChatGPT in language learning practices.

### H8

Habit will have a positive impact on Chinese university EFL students’ actual use of ChatGPT in language learning practices.

Price value (PV) is defined as the evaluation of a system’s perceived benefits compared to its financial costs (Venkatesh et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR66)). In this study, PV pertains to the expenses incurred by Chinese university EFL students to access ChatGPT for learning. This construct assesses whether the cost of using ChatGPT is justified by the advantages it provides to students. We hypothesise:

### H9

PV will have a positive impact on Chinese university EFL students’ BI to use ChatGPT in language learning practices.

Personal innovativeness (PI) reflects an individual’s tendency to adopt and experiment with emerging technologies, such as ChatGPT, in their language learning processes. This construct measures the willingness of EFL learners to integrate innovative AI tools, in particular ChatGPT, into their language acquisition activities. High PI indicates a greater likelihood of adopting and effectively utilising ChatGPT for personalised and autonomous language learning, potentially leading to enhanced educational outcomes. We hypothesise:

### H10

PI will have a positive impact on Chinese university EFL students’ BI to use ChatGPT in language learning practices.

Behavioural intention (BI) denotes an individual’s propensity and commitment to utilise a particular technology for task completion (Cortez et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR12)). Prior research (Kalınkara & Özdemir, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR30); Lavidas et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR36)) has investigated the relationship between BI and the practical application of technological innovations. This study investigates BI to elucidated how inclined Chinese university EFL learners are to integrate ChatGPT into their language learning activities. We hypothesise:

### H11

BI will have a positive impact on Chinese university EFL students’ ChatGPT use behaviour in language learning practices.

Use behaviour (UB) seeks to elucidate users’ engagement and utilisation patterns of technology. Within this model’s framework, actual usage pertains to the frequency with which individuals use a specific technology to accomplish their task. In our study, the utilisation of ChatGPT was assessed using a seven-item scale ranging from ‘never’ (no engagement) to ‘several times a day’ (multiple daily interactions) to capture the frequency of use among Chinese university EFL students. Our analysis was guided by a research model (see Fig. [1](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#Fig1)) that incorporated seven principal elements from the UTAUT2 framework (Venkatesh et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR66)). To refine and enhance the precision and comprehensiveness of our model, we included the personal innovativeness (PI) construct as identified by Agarwal and Prasad ([1998](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR2)).

Methods
-------

### Instruments

To investigate the acceptance and use of ChatGPT among Chinese university EFL learners, we employed a modified UTAUT2 survey instrument consisting of 33 items. Participants indicated their level of agreement utilising a 7-point Likert scale, where 1 denoted strong disagreement and 7 indicated strong agreement. The survey was adapted from previous research (Agarwal & Prasad, [1998](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR2); Venkatesh et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR66)).

To ensure content validity, five experts specialising in educational technology in language reviewed the questionnaire items. Their feedback prompted necessary revisions, resulting in a final version with 33 items, used in both the pilot and the main data collection phases. Table [1](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#Tab1) outlines the questionnaire constructs, item counts, and applied scales.

### Procedure

An initial pilot study was conducted with 38 Chinese EFL students (aged 18–25) to refine the questionnaire. The participants included 20 females and 18 males from two universities in Guangzhou, which were also used for the main data collection.

Guangzhou, a first-tier city, was selected for its position as a leading educational hub in southern China, characterised by a diverse student population and significant investment in technology integration within higher education. The selected universities include both public and private institutions, providing a balanced perspective and insights into the influence of institutional contexts on technology adoption. Furthermore, Guangzhou’s emphasis on technological innovation aligns closely with the objectives of this study, making it an ideal setting to examine how societal and institutional factors influence EFL learners’ acceptance of ChatGPT.

The pilot study confirmed the reliability and validity of each variable, as well as the discriminant validity (Hair et al., [2013](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR23)). Following the pilot study, we distributed the revised online questionnaire to 340 Chinese university EFL learners, aged 18 to 25, from the same two universities in Guangzhou. The sample consisted of 85 males (25%) and 255 females (75%). Among them, 163 students (48%) were enrolled in a public university, and 177 students (52%) were from a private university. The distribution by year of study was as follows: 230 students (68%) were in their sophomore year, 65 (19%) were juniors, and 45 (13%) were in their final year of undergraduate studies. Students were informed that their responses would remain confidential, participation was voluntary, and they could respond anonymously without providing any personal identifiers.

Table [2](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#Tab2) shows that all values are within the acceptable range, confirming the normality of the data in our study. This normality supports the validity and reliability of the subsequent statistical analyses, enabling accurate conclusions and meaningful interpretations.

Results
-------

To assess the proposed model, partial least squares structural equation modelling (PLS-SEM) was employed, utilising the path weighting scheme available in the SmartPLS 4 software. We utilised default initial weights and allowed for a maximum of 3000 iterations. Additionally, bootstrapping was implemented to ascertain the statistical significance of the PLS-SEM estimates (Ringle et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR54); Strzelecki, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR60)). The validity of the constructs was assessed by analysing the indicator loadings. An indicator loading above 0.708 signifies that the construct accounts for over 50% of the variance in the indicator, demonstrating acceptable item reliability (Sarstedt et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR58)). Table [3](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#Tab3) displays the measurement scale and factor loadings, all of which exceed this threshold.

The structural model was assessed using PLS-SEM via SmartPLS software. To evaluate model fit, the standardised root mean square residual (SRMR) was calculated, yielding a value of 0.061, which satisfies the recommended threshold for good model fit (Magno et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR45)). Additionally, the squared Euclidean distance (d\_ULS) and geodesic distance (d\_G) were reported as 2.075 and 0.997, respectively, further supporting the model’s robustness (Table [6](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#Tab6)). These distance measures evaluate the discrepancy between the empirical correlation matrix and the model-implied correlation matrix, with lower values indicating better model fit. To determine good fit, bootstrap analysis with 5,000 samples was conducted, revealing that our obtained d\_ULS value (2.075) and d\_G value (0.997) were both below the 95% confidence interval upper bounds (d\_ULS 95% = 2.742; d\_G 95% = 1.322), indicating that the discrepancy is not statistically significant (Dijkstra & Henseler, [2015](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR14); Hair et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR24)). The next phase involved assessing the coefficient of determination (R2) for each construct. The model’s explanatory power was quantified by R2 values ranging from 0 to 1, with higher values indicating a greater proportion of variance explained by the model. As proposed by Hair et al. ([2011](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR22)), R2 values can be classified into three levels: high (0.75), moderate (0.50), and weak (0.25). Additionally, f2 values, which evaluate the impact of an independent variable, are categorised minimal (0.02), moderate (0.15), or significant (0.35), with values below 0.02 indicating an insignificant effect (Wall Emerson, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR67)). Figure [2](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#Fig2) presents the PLS-SEM results, displaying standardised regression coefficients and R2 values within the variable circles. The results demonstrate that habit (H) exerts the most substantial effect (β = 0.436) on behavioural intention (BI), followed by personal innovativeness (PI) with a beta value of 0.219, performance expectancy (PE) at β = 0.193, and social influence (SI) with β = 0.147. Discussion
----------

This study examined the factors influencing Chinese university EFL learners’ adoption and application of ChatGPT in their educational processes. The findings offer a comprehensive and multifaceted understanding of AI tool adoption in education, with some results aligning with previous research while others diverge.

The analysis identified habit (H) as the most significant factor of behavioural intention (BI) (β = 0.436), indicating that regular engagement with ChatGPT significantly boosts students’ intentions to use the tool. This aligns with prior research suggesting that habitual behaviour strongly influences technology adoption (Lavidas et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR36); Strzelecki et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR61)). Strzelecki ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR60)) also emphasised the crucial role of habitual behaviour in fostering acceptance of AI-driven educational tools within learning settings, reinforcing the current findings. However, our results contrast with the findings of Du and Liang ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR15)), Fithriya et al. ([2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR16)), and Ain et al. ([2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR3)), who observed no direct relationship between habit and behavioural intention. This discrepancy suggests that the impact of habit may be context-dependent, varying across different technological tools, highlighting the need for further investigation.

Personal innovativeness (PI) also emerged as a crucial determinant for BI (β = 0.219), highlighting the importance of students’ willingness to embrace new technologies in enhancing their behavioural intentions. This result aligns with prior research that has linked PI to technology acceptance. For instance, Strzelecki ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR59)) and Biloš and Budimir ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR9)) found that PI exerts a positive impact on BI, although to a lesser extent than other factors.

Performance expectancy (PE) (β = 0.193) and social influence (SI) (β = 0.147) also positively influence BI, albeit to a lesser extent. These results suggest that students’ perceptions of ChatGPT’s capacity to foster language proficiency and the influence of peers and educators are pivotal in determining their intention to adopt the tool. These results are consistent with the UTAUT2 framework, which emphasises the relevance and importance of PE and SI in technology adoption (Venkatesh et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR66)). Similarly to our findings, Habibi et al. ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR20)) reported significant positive correlations between PE and BI in their studies of ChatGPT and other educational technologies. Additionally, our findings align with previous studies (Biloš & Budimir, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR9); Habibi et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR20)), which have demonstrated that social influence (SI) is a key factor influencing behavioural intention. However, this finding contradicts the study of Strzelecki ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR59)) on ChatGPT in higher education, which found minimal influence of SI on BI.

In contrast, hedonic motivation (HM) (β = 0.062, p = 0.407) and facilitating conditions (FC) (β = 0.069, p = 0.305) showed weak, non-significant effects on BI. This suggests that the enjoyment derived from interacting with ChatGPT and the accessibility of resources and support have a negligible impact on students’ behavioural intentions. Our findings contradict those of several studies on AI adoption in higher education (Habibi et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR20); Strzelecki, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR59), [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR60)) which reported significant positive effects of hedonic motivation on behavioural intention, differing from the non-significant influence observed in this research. Similarly, Kalınkara and Özdemir ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR30)), Venkatesh ([2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR64)), and Lavidas et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR36)) identified FC as an important and significant variable affecting BI. Specifically, Habibi et al. ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR20)) revealed that FC emerged as the most crucial factor influencing students’ BI towards ChatGPT usage in universities across Indonesia, which is inconsistent with our results.

Interestingly, effort expectancy (EE) (β = − 0.070) and price value (PV) (β = − 0.073) demonstrated non-significant negative relationships with BI, suggesting that neither perceived ease of use nor cost considerations play crucial roles in students’ intentions to adopt ChatGPT. Our findings challenge the common trend in technology adoption studies (Azizi et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR7); Du & Liang, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR15)), which report positive impacts of EE and PV on students’ BI. Instead, as mentioned above, we observed different dynamics between these variables in the adoption of AI technologies for language learning among Chinese university EFL learners. This insignificance arises because university EFL learners regard Internet-based resources as everyday utilities, making the use of ChatGPT relatively effortless in students’ learning processes. Similarly, the insignificant impact of PV on BI to use ChatGPT can be attributed to its availability for free at the time of the study, with respondents likely utilising the free version, ChatGPT 3.5. These findings align with Neves et al. ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR49)), who found EE and PV were not significant in influencing behavioural intention in their study.

When examining use behaviour (UB), habit (H) again proved to be the most significant factor (β = 0.534), with facilitating conditions (FC) identified as the second most significant factor (β = 0.181). This indicates that habitual use and the availability of supportive resources significantly contribute to the actual usage of ChatGPT. Behavioural intention (BI) (β = 0.078), while positive, showed a relatively weak influence on UB, suggesting that other factors beyond intention, such as established habits and facilitating conditions, are more critical in determining actual use. These results align with Habibi et al. ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR20)), who also observed a strong correlation between facilitating conditions and the utilisation of ChatGPT. The strong impact of habit on actual use behaviour is further supported by studies on e-learning platforms in college English learning (Deng et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR13)) and mobile-assisted language learning platforms (Moorthy et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR46)). However, the relatively weak influence of BI on UB contrasts with findings by Venkatesh et al. ([2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR66)), who emphasised that BI is a crucial factor in determining patterns of technology usage in their UTAUT model. This discrepancy may suggest that in the context of ChatGPT adoption among Chinese EFL learners, habitual behaviours and facilitating conditions play more dominant roles than mere intention. As indicated previously, habit can exert a direct impact on human behaviour and may diminish the impact of behavioural intention (Kumar & Bervell, [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR34)). In other words, the establishment of habitual usage patterns effectively reduces reliance on conscious decision-making processes; when students integrate ChatGPT into their routine practices, their engagement transitions from a deliberate choice to an automatic behaviour, thereby eliminating the need for intentional contemplation before use. This indicates that once students have developed a habit of using ChatGPT and have favourable facilitating conditions (e.g., access, resources, support), their actual use behaviour may be more strongly influenced by these factors than by their initial intentions.

The findings of this study, while focused on Chinese university EFL learners, offer valuable insights that may extend to other cultural contexts, albeit with crucial caveats. The strong influence of habit on both behavioural intention and use behaviour may manifest differently across cultures with varying degrees of technology integration in education. For instance, in Western educational contexts, where learner autonomy is often emphasised, habit formation might be more individually driven and self-directed, whereas in collectivist cultures like China, habit formation could be more influenced by institutional structures and peer practices (Hofstede et al., [2010](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR26); Moradi, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR47)).

The role of personal innovativeness identified in this study may have different implications in cultures with varying attitudes towards innovation and risk-taking. Societies with higher uncertainty avoidance (Hofstede et al., [2010](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR26)) might exhibit more resistance to AI adoption regardless of individual innovativeness, while those with lower uncertainty avoidance might demonstrate accelerated adoption rates. Future cross-cultural studies could examine how cultural dimensions moderate the relationship between personal innovativeness and AI acceptance.

The influence of motivation factors may also vary significantly across cultural contexts. While hedonic motivation showed minimal impact in our study of Chinese EFL learners, this finding might differ in educational cultures that place greater emphasis on enjoyment in learning. Similarly, language anxiety—a factor not explicitly measured in our study—could interact with technology acceptance differently across cultures. In contexts where face-saving is culturally significant, AI tools like ChatGPT might offer a low-stakes environment for language practice that reduces anxiety, potentially strengthening the performance expectancy factor (MacIntyre & Vincze, [2017](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR44); Muthmainnah et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR48); Wang, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR68)). Future research should explicitly incorporate cultural dimensions as moderating variables in technology acceptance models when studying AI adoption in language learning contexts. Such research could elucidate how motivational factors, anxiety reduction, and social dynamics interact with cultural variables to influence AI acceptance and use across diverse educational settings.

Limitations
-----------

It is important to acknowledge several constraints in this study. First, the sample is limited to Chinese university EFL learners, which may impinge upon the generalisability of the results to other contexts or populations. Second, the cross-sectional approach captures a snapshot of the situation at a distinct temporal reference, thereby precluding the establishment of causal relationships between the variables under investigation. While this design effectively identifies associations among variables, it does not account for temporal changes or unmeasured factors that may influence the relationships between UTAUT2 constructs and behavioural intention. Future research should adopt longitudinal designs to track learners’ attitudes and behaviours over time, enabling a more robust analysis of causal pathways and the long-term impact of ChatGPT on language learning outcomes.

Third, this study does not explicitly account for the potential influence of cultural factors on ChatGPT usage among Chinese EFL learners. Cultural norms such as societal attitudes towards technology adoption and the emphasis on academic excellence in China may uniquely shape learners’ acceptance and use of AI-powered tools like ChatGPT. These cultural dynamics may play a significant role in shaping behavioural intention and actual use behaviour, suggesting the need for future research to explore these cultural dimensions more comprehensively. Furthermore, the study did not assess the potential influence of English language proficiency on students’ adoption of ChatGPT. Variations in language competence could potentially result in differing experiences and perceptions of ChatGPT’s utility, thereby influencing students’ acceptance and usage patterns. For instance, learners with higher proficiency might utilise ChatGPT more effectively for complex tasks, while those with lower proficiency might encounter barriers in interacting with the tool. Future research could address this limitation by categorising participants based on their English language proficiency levels to investigate how this factor mediates the adoption of AI tools in language learning contexts. Additionally, the rapid evolution of AI technologies like ChatGPT may lead to changes in user perceptions and behaviours that this study does not account for. Future research should consider longitudinal designs and diverse populations to confirm and extend these insights.

Implications
------------

This investigation yields significant empirical evidence that can inform strategic decision-making regarding the incorporation of AI language models, particularly ChatGPT, into language learning curricula for Chinese university EFL learners. Emphasising the development of habitual usage through regular and structured engagement with ChatGPT can significantly enhance students’ acceptance and use of the tool. This can be achieved through daily tasks, interactive sessions, and continuous support, making ChatGPT an indispensable part of the learning process.

Promoting a setting that nurtures individual creativity and innovativeness and emphasises the practical advantages of using ChatGPT in terms of enhancing performance may significantly stimulate its adoption. Highlighting success stories, offering incentives for early adopters, and integrating ChatGPT into various aspects of the curriculum can help students see the tangible benefits of using this tool. Factors including hedonic motivation, effort expectancy, and price value appear less critical, suggesting that promotional and training efforts might not need to heavily focus on these aspects. Resources can be better allocated towards areas that significantly impact students’ behavioural intentions and actual use. While social influence shows moderate importance, it could become more critical as AI tools such as ChatGPT gain wider acceptance and institutional support. As more students and faculty members adopt these technologies, peer influence could play a larger role in encouraging others to follow suit. Therefore, creating a supportive community that champions the use of AI in education can yield significant benefits.

When developing pedagogical strategies, it is crucial to consider varying levels of English proficiency among university EFL learners. For beginners and lower-intermediate learners, educators should implement scaffolded approaches where ChatGPT interactions are initially structured with predefined prompts and guided tasks (Tran, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR100)). These may include template-based conversations, error correction exercises, and vocabulary building activities that align with the students’ proficiency level (Billingsley & Gardner, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR8); Sun et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR62)). Instructors should provide explicit instruction on formulating effective prompts that correspond to beginners’ language capabilities, gradually increasing complexity as proficiency develops.

For intermediate learners, a balanced approach combining guided and autonomous interactions is recommended. These students can benefit from utilising ChatGPT for more complex tasks, such as paragraph-level feedback, contextual vocabulary enhancement, and simulated dialogues on familiar topics. The strong relationship between habit and use behaviour identified in our study suggests the implementation of regular, structured ChatGPT interaction sessions for these learners would be beneficial in developing productive usage patterns (Strzelecki, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR59); Zare et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR74)). Advanced learners can leverage ChatGPT more autonomously for sophisticated language development. Given our findings regarding personal innovativeness, instructors should encourage these students to explore creative applications such as stylistic analysis, academic writing support, and cultural nuance discussions. For this group, the emphasis should be on critical evaluation of AI-generated content rather than mere acceptance, fostering both language proficiency and digital literacy (Sandu et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-025-00530-4#ref-CR57)). Regular reflection activities can assist students in articulating their experiences with ChatGPT, fostering metacognitive awareness and promoting responsible AI use in language learning.

A tiered implementation framework is therefore recommended, with differentiated objectives, prompting strategies, and assessment methods based on proficiency levels. This differentiated approach acknowledges that the factors influencing technology acceptance identified in our study—particularly habit, personal innovativeness, and performance expectancy—may manifest differently across proficiency levels. While habit formation is universally important, as indicated by our results (β = 0.534), the complexity of tasks that become habitual should align with proficiency levels. Similarly, performance expectancy (β = 0.193) might be fulfilled differently, with beginners valuing basic communication improvements and advanced learners seeking sophisticated linguistic development.

By leveraging these insights, educators and policymakers can strategically promote AI integration and take targeted measures to encourage the uptake and optimised use of ChatGPT, ultimately enhancing the language learning experience for Chinese university EFL learners.

Conclusion
----------

Using the UTAUT2 model, widely recognised in technology acceptance studies, this study underscores the substantial influence of habitual behaviour in driving Chinese university EFL learners’ acceptance and use of ChatGPT. Through the promotion of regular use and emphasising the innovative aspects of ChatGPT, educators can effectively promote its integration into language learning practices. The notable correlation between performance expectancy and behavioural intention emphasises the need for educators to clearly communicate the practical benefits of ChatGPT in enhancing learning outcomes. Conversely, the non-significant influences of hedonic motivation, effort expectancy, facilitating conditions, and price value highlight areas where this study’s results diverge from previous research, suggesting that these factors may play different roles in this research context. Overall, this research contributes to the expanding scholarly discourse regarding the integration of AI tools in higher education and offers practical recommendations for leveraging ChatGPT to improve language learning experiences.
