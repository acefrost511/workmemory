## Abstract
To open this special issue of The Journal of Educational Research, I have collected a range of research reports on teaching and learning with generative artificial intelligence (GAI). To guide readers in exploring the contents of this special issue, I have provided a summary of each of the included research reports. I have organized the articles by educational context to provide readers with additional context for the articles and enhance the potential for translating the research into practice. I open and conclude my introduction by positioning the adoption of GAI as considering and exploring GAI in K‑12 learning as an educational innovation and the opportunities and limitations for embracing GAI as a tool for learning and teaching.

## Introduction
We have entered a new era of technological innovation with the rapid popularization of artificial intelligence (AI). The access and integration of both predictive and generative artificial intelligence has become widespread with a broad and expanding range of potential applications. While predictive artificial intelligence (PAI) has been mainstreamed for many years (e.g., spelling check in word processing software), generative artificial intelligence (GAI) has been a relatively recent introduction to the general public. Generative artificial intelligence has a wide range of capabilities, including generating text, pictures, images, sounds, voices, and movies, including animation. The breadth of the potential of GAI continues to expand as the accumulation of extant data into repositories increases. The expansion of data accessible for GAI creations has increased the potential usefulness in a wide diversity of educational settings.
We dedicated this special issue of the Journal of Educational Research to research reports on the potential and current applications of GAI in K-12 educational settings. My introductory chapter is intended to provide a roadmap and synthesis for exploring the reports published in this issue detailing the evidence-based practices that include integrating GAI in K-12 education. For this issue, I have curated a diversity of empirical research reports detailing how students and teachers are using GAI to enhance learning and teaching effectiveness. The range of contexts and locations in which the research took place, as detailed in the included reports in this special issue, are a testament to the global use of GAI in K-12 education. Further, the reports reflect how GAI may be transforming teaching and learning in alignment with the activities of 21st-century societies.
## Teacher GAI adoption and instructional use
As with many other education innovations, the adoption of GAI in K-12 for teaching and learning appears to be aligned with the fundamental principles of diffusion theory (Rogers, [Citation1995](#)). As Nadelson et al. ([Citation2025](#)) report, there is a wide diversity of justifications and supports for teachers considering (or not considering) GAI for teaching and learning. The association between the adoption and use of GAI with professional development is consistent with the Nadelson and Seifert ([Citation2016](#)) report on teacher embrace and integration of integrated STEM. In their exploration of teacher experiences with GAI, Döğer and Göçen ([Citation2025](#)) examined the related issues across 15 countries, finding educators tended to maintain a critical perspective on using GAI for teaching and learning. The research provides a unique baseline for future analysis that may focus on the evolution of teachers’ adoption of GAI.
Embracing a similar framework to Nadelson et al. ([Citation2025](#)), Isik ([Citation2025](#)) examined teacher adoption of GAI in alignment with other models of technology and innovation adoption. Isik explored the adoption based on tangible indicators such as usefulness and ease of use among a population of teachers engaged in professional development offerings focused on using GAI for teaching and learning. The research provides empirical documentation for the potential professional development may have on teacher integrations of GAI in their teaching and for their student learning
In their exploration of the perceptions and use of AI tools in eTwinning environments, Döğer and Göçen ([Citation2025](#)) conducted semi-structured interviews of teachers from a range of participating countries. They found that teachers tended to find benefits to using AI, including increased efficiency in lesson planning, assessing student work, and opportunities for collaboration. Their research also revealed a number of common issues, such as ethical concerns, bias, overreliance, and privacy. Döğer and Göçen also raised concerns about the potential for controlled access to and knowledge of AI to exacerbate social problems, such as broadening the digital divide and supporting AI-driven capitalism. They conclude their report by offering recommendations for evidence-based practices and a roadmap for integrating AI effectively and thoughtfully into teaching and learning.
In their exploration of how teachers perceive and use GAI in teaching foreign languages, Drożdż et al. ([Citation2025](#)) gathered a combination of qualitative and quantitative data, seeking to empirically document the teachers’ practices. The researchers found the teachers perceived the use of AI-based corpora to be helpful in enhancing their teaching effectiveness and their students’ learning. The teachers noted that the personalization offered by GAI to their students in their language learning was highly beneficial and enhanced student understanding and their acquisition of foreign language. However, nearly half of the teachers in the study indicated they were unfamiliar with using AI to learn languages, suggesting a high potential for emerging uses of GAI in foreign language learning.
To explore how teachers may be using GAI to develop curriculum and learning activities, Delaney et al. ([Citation2025](#)) conducted a case study research on two high school teachers. Through the lens of a co-design partnership, Delaney et al. ([Citation2025](#)) examine how teachers learn and subsequently adopt educational innovations, utilizing technology to enhance their practice. Examining how the two teachers were both learning about GAI resources and applications and applying their GAI knowledge to develop curriculum and instructional materials. The researchers found that there is a benefit for teachers to learn about GAI in the context of teaching and learning rather than in a discipline-specific context. In their conclusion, Delaney et al. ([Citation2025](#)) recognize the need for more research on teacher-generated curriculum and activities, which may be particularly critical as teachers develop more custom instructional materials using GAI.
## Administrators
As leaders in their schools and districts, educational administrators can significantly influence teacher consideration and adoption of educational innovations (e.g., Hsieh et al., [Citation2024](#)). Given the potential influence, there was high justification for the Göçen and Döğer ([Citation2025](#)) exploration of how school administrators across 20 countries used GAI in their leadership activities. The adoption of GAI by administrators for their practice will likely influence school policy regarding the use of GAI and teacher integration of GAI into their practice. Furthermore, as Göçen and Döğer discuss, it is also essential to consider the limitations of GAI and maintain a balance with social interactions to achieve effective leadership.
## Elementary education
In their exploration of GAI integration for elementary-level student learning, Tetteh et al. ([Citation2025](#)) leveraged a culture-techno-contextual approach (CTCA). Students taught using the CTCA method experienced higher learning gains compared to their peers taught using traditional lectures for instruction. Further, the students recognized the affordances of CTCA, which has implications for their use of AI beyond traditional learning conditions. The potential of AI to shift to a more student-centered focus in instruction may be a critical consideration for both research and teaching. As the work of Tetteh et al. suggests, integrating GAI may lead to gains in achieving learning objectives and increased student self-directed learning.
Taking a different approach to using AI-driven vocabulary builders, Temiz and Kafadar ([Citation2025](#)) compared student gains achieved with the AI software to those obtained using traditional flashcards. The prediction was that using adaptive and generative AI in the computer-based vocabulary builder would enhance student learning in ways superior to using flashcards. The results showed significant short-term gains but no significant differences in the long term as students working with the flashcards performed higher and equivalent on the delayed post-test. The research exemplifies how AI may impact short-term learning compared to traditional methods, but in the long term, some forms of AI-based instruction may not result in significant differences compared to conventional approaches. The research provides a useful model for comparing traditional and AI-based learning and a model for framing AI research in terms of meeting learning objectives rather than achieving superior outcomes.
## Middle school education
As many secondary students have extensive access to the internet (Haidt, [Citation2024](#)) and are likely engaging with GAI for a range of reasons, there is a particular need to explore in depth how secondary students are engaging with GAI in their learning. Adisa and Adefisayo ([Citation2025](#)) explored how middle school students conceived and perceived GAI should be integrated and used for learning. The students shared perceptions of the importance of teacher supervision of student GAI use and the need to restrict the use of GAI in assessments. The students had mixed preferences for using GAI compared to collaborating with other students. The students’ perspectives offer a unique insight into how students may consider the limitations and ethical uses of GAI, a line of research in which data is collected from teachers or administrators.
Also focusing on using GAI in middle school, Chen et al. ([Citation2025](#)) explored how the use of GAI could be used to shift history teaching from the common pedagogical model of teacher-led, text-based learning to an approach that is more interactive and personalized for students. Their quasi-experimental design revealed the use of GAI was effective in enhancing student historical knowledge and motivation for learning. The outcome suggests that, when used effectively, GAI can lead to significant learning gains, greater student engagement, and increased creative learning experiences.
## High school education
As students develop, a level of sophistication for learning about and with GAI is likely to increase, as reflected by Smyslova et al. ([Citation2025](#)). In their pilot study, they investigated the development of student AI literacy. Focusing on both predictive and generative AI processes, the researchers found significant gains in understanding predictive AI text generation. However, the research also revealed challenges in transitioning their understanding to generative AI processes, with some misconceptions persisting. Further, their research noted gender differences. The research report highlights the importance of moving beyond the mechanics and ethics of GAI use to exploring the actual processes so that students can develop an appreciation of how GAI uses extant data to assemble seemingly unique outputs.
Using the reflective lens of a researcher-practitioner teaching high school history and literature, Wall et al. ([Citation2025](#)) gathered data to develop a set of evidence-based principles guiding the use of GAI for teaching and learning. Wall et al. moved beyond the approach of limiting student access and trying to control the presence of GAI to a more proactive approach of enhancing students’ GAI literacy. Their proposed principles focus on educating students about GAI, enhancing students’ critical analysis of GAI input and output, and considering student learning holistically. In alignment with their embrace of evidence-based practices, they demonstrate how GAI can enhance student learning.
Extending the empirical documentation of student GAU use, Bieńkowska et al. ([Citation2025](#)) gathered data from students of different ages, genders, community structures, and schools. The researchers investigated how students used GAI and the impact of GAI use on various variables, including motivation to learn, relationships with teachers, independent thinking, and time management. Bieńkowska and colleagues also examined the students’ perceptions of the advantages and disadvantages of GAI use for learning. They found the students had moderate engagement with GAI and used GAI for a range of tasks. Their research also revealed differences in engagement and motivation for the use of GAI, which were associated with the student’s age, gender, school type, and the community location of the schools the students attended. The results reveal multiple indicators of potential differences in GAI use based on student demographics and expose an array of possible influences that may catalyze the broadening of the digital divide.
Mixing the research on the instructional use of GAI and elements of AI literacy, Liu et al. ([Citation2025](#)) examined the benefits and limitations of integrating GAI into teaching high school geography. Starting with a comparison of GAI systems in China and the United States, Liu et al. proceed to explore issues related to the accuracy of GAI output, student dependence, ethical use of intellectual property, and equitable access. Thus, while GAI holds the potential for enhancing student geography learning, there are notable limitations that need to be recognized and perhaps resolved to consider GAI as a highly valuable instructional tool.
Moving beyond instructional use and learning using GAI, Meşe et al. ([Citation2025](#)) explored the critical variable of learning engagement. Using the context of GAI use in learning English, Meşe et al. examined the levels of cognitive, emotional, and behavioral engagement among tenth-grade students who used GAI in a writing course. Their semi-structured interviews revealed evidence of student persistence in seeking feedback and support for correcting errors. The students also engaged in critical thinking, seeking to enhance their writing by identifying and correcting errors in their work. Further, the students’ use reflected elements of curiosity and enjoyment. The research of Meşe et al. underscores the need to move beyond cognitive limitations to include emotional variables as well.
Similar to Meşe et al. ([Citation2025](#)) focus on English language learners, Woo et al. ([Citation2025](#)) explored the use of GAI by high school students learning English. In their research, Woo et al. examined how students crafted GAI prompts to create English-language text as part of their writing assignments. The results indicated that students require specific instructions and practice to develop GAI prompts that result in meaningful and useful outputs. The research by Woo et al. reinforces the need for GAI literacy and instruction focused on strategically generating GAI prompts to garner desired and meaningful outcomes.
## Curriculum integration
In a unique study examining the use of GAI as a mechanism for curriculum integration, Kepceoğlu and Oguz-Hacat ([Citation2025](#)) examined how middle school-level students used GAI to complete assignments that required the students to integrate concepts from mathematics and social studies. In their qualitative exploratory study, Kepceoğlu and Oguz-Hacat found support for using GAI as an instructional context for integrating curriculum. The research found the levels of critical thinking skills of the participating students led to differences in the use and reliance on GAI. The outcomes of the study reflect the potential for using GAI to integrate curriculum and the need to consider and attend to students’ critical thinking skills for meaningful GAI input and their critical examination of GAI output.
## Book review
Closing out this special issue on generative artificial intelligence is a book review by Nufninu et al. ([Citation2025](#)) of \*Empowering K-12 Education with AI: Preparing for the Future of Education and Work\* (Chiu, [Citation2024](#)). The review highlights the focus on GAI for learning and teaching, with many of the ideas well-aligned with the research presented in this issue. In their review, Nufninu et al. emphasize Chiu’s ([Citation2024](#)) primary argument for using GAI as a catalyst for substantial change in K-12 education. As I argued at the beginning of this introduction, GAI is a significant educational innovation that has the potential for a profound impact on teaching and learning. However, as Nufninu et al. also highlight, Chui emphasizes preparing students for a workplace where GAI is integrated as an essential workplace tool.
## Conclusion
Teaching and learning are complex processes that may be constrained by the historical schema of school, limiting the potential for integrating educational innovations. Unlike the introduction of past educational innovations, the presence and use of GAI is impacting nearly all facets of society, business, industry, education, social media, entertainment, sports, and general communication. Given the ubiquitous presence, it will be nearly impossible to stop or even limit the presence of GAI in K-12 education. Thus, rather than trying to prevent or restrict the use of GAI, it is wise we continue to explore and research teaching and learning with the innovation so that we may engage in evidence-based decision-making. The research in this special issue provides a strong foundation for future research on K-12 teaching and learning with GAI.
