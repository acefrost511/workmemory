## Abstract
Despite increasing interest in using Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) in education, little is known about how students with disability engage with GenAI to support their own learning. This study investigates the potential of ChatGPT to support the learning agency of adolescents with disability in a secondary science classroom in Australia. Guided by sociocultural and socio-material conceptualisations of agency, the study explored the mediated choices and capabilities of three students with disability to use ChatGPT to facilitate their learning. The study was conducted in a class comprising students of varying ages clinically diagnosed with diverse learning needs. Data sources included student interviews, the students' conversations with ChatGPT, teachers' lesson worksheets and video recordings of the lesson. Thematic analyses reveal that while students expressed clear and meaningful choices to use ChatGPT to support their learning, they faced metacognitive challenges and cognitive constraints, resulting in a misalignment between their choices and actual capability. The findings identify key theoretical perspectives and practical considerations for supporting students with disability in using GenAI to develop their learning agency. The study recommends customising GenAI for specific learning needs in line with its function as a cognitive prosthesis for students with disability and for better alignment with Universal Design for Learning, thereby supporting students' learning agency.
### Practitioner notes
What is already known about this topic
\* The development of students' learning agency has been widely explored in the secondary context, but not for students with disability.
\* There is increasing interest in using Generative AI (GenAI) to support inclusive education.
\* Current theoretical frameworks can inform the exploration of the learning agency of students with disability using GenAI.
What this paper adds
\* Foregrounds the learning choices of students with disability as indicators of their emergent learning agency.
\* Identifies learning challenges facing students with disability that misalign their learning choices and capabilities.
\* Examines GenAI's potential as a socio-material mediator facilitating the material and relational agency of students with disability.
Implications for practice and/or policy
\* Consulting students with disability on how GenAI can support their learning, providing opportunities for them to express their learning preferences.
\* Customising GenAI tools in line with the Universal Design for Learning guidelines to address the specific learning challenges of students with disability.
\* Clarifying the roles of teachers and education assistants in facilitating the learning agency of students with disability in light of customising GenAI to mediate their learning agency.
## INTRODUCTION
With the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development in sight, the United Nations ([2024](#bjet70048-bib-0046)) reports that, in 2021, 47% of countries provided appropriate learning materials to students with disability, up from 34% in 2016, and 41% provided human resources for the implementation of policies on inclusive education to address the needs of children with disabilities, up from 25% in 2016. Despite the progress made over 5 years, the report concluded that most countries were not on track to achieve the Sustainable Development Goals relevant to persons with disabilities. In Australia, the Disability Standards of Education ([2005](#bjet70048-bib-0013)) requires educational institutions to make reasonable adjustments to ensure inclusive education and equitable learning outcomes for students with disability (ACARA). In 2024, 25.7% of students received an educational adjustment due to a disability (ACARA, [2024](#bjet70048-bib-0004)). Of these, 53.9% were for students with cognitive disability, 35% for social–emotional disability, 8.6% for physical disability and 2.5% for sensory disability (ACARA, [2024](#bjet70048-bib-0004)). Even with supports such as Individual Education Plans and Education Assistants (EA), teachers continue to face significant challenges addressing the diverse needs of students with disability, leading to calls in Australia to harness a range of educational technology to address their needs (AITSL, [2024](#bjet70048-bib-0005)).
There is increasing interest in how emerging tools such as Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) can support inclusive education (Rappa & Nonis, [2026](#bjet70048-bib-0051)). Despite its rapid uptake, research on how students with disability use GenAI remains limited, particularly in terms of understanding how it might support students' learning agency, defined in this study as socioculturally and socio-materially mediated choices and capability to undertake actions to learn. This exploratory multiple-case study addresses that gap by focusing on the agentic use of ChatGPT in an inclusive classroom. The aim of this study was to investigate the potential and limitations of current GenAI tools, such as ChatGPT, to support the learning agency of adolescents with disability in a secondary school science classroom in Australia.
### Theorisations of agency in learning
Following Moje and Lewis ([2007](#bjet70048-bib-0031)), we adopt a sociocultural view of learning agency as a process of strategic transformations, where learners act purposefully within specific sociocultural and power-laden contexts to shape their identities and learning trajectories, as well as a socio-material view of learning agency that is shaped by distributed networks of human and non-human actors (Rappa & Tang, [2017](#bjet70048-bib-0100); Leander & Lovvorn, [2006](#bjet70048-bib-0024); Thrift, [1995](#bjet70048-bib-0045)). In this study, we conceptualise learning agency as an emergent property of the interaction between the learner, the socio-material tools they engage with, and the broader cultural and institutional contexts in which learning takes place (Dodge et al., [2008](#bjet70048-bib-0014); Holland et al., [1998](#bjet70048-bib-0022)).
To capture this interplay, we propose a model (Figure [1](#bjet70048-fig-0001)) comprising three nested levels: the personal level (where agency emerges through learners' choices and capability), the socio-material level (where GenAI functions as a mediator) and the cultural-contextual level (which shapes norms, expectations and affordances for agency). These layers do not operate in isolation but co-constitute each other. The inner and middle layers are key to the conceptualisation of this study and deserve closer attention. As such, this study sheds light on how students with disability expressed and demonstrated their choices and/or capability to support their own learning and how GenAI served as a socio-material mediator in the actions undertaken by these students within the context of a science class catering specifically for students with disability.

Learning agency as conceptualised within the context of this study.
While choice is an important component of agency, it is not sufficient. A student may choose to undertake certain actions to learn, but may lack the personal capability and the tools, supports or sociocultural affordances to translate that choice into action. Thus, we conceptualise learning agency as emergent—neither entirely personal nor entirely structural, but arising from the interaction of personal goals, capabilities, tools, relationships and constraints within their environments.
Drawing on Latour's ([2005](#bjet70048-bib-0023)) Actor-Network Theory (ANT), we further view GenAI as a non-human actant—an entity with agency-like effects that co-produces meaning, knowledge and action through its interactions with humans (Tang & Cooper, [2025](#bjet70048-bib-0050)). In Latour's terms, agency is not located within individuals but is shared and distributed across the network of relationships between actors (human and non-human). In this framework, GenAI systems such as ChatGPT act as socio-material mediators: they do not simply transmit information but co-construct the learning environment by reshaping how learners ask questions, make connections, receive feedback and reflect on what they know, with GenAI as a dialogic partner (Tang et al., [2024](#bjet70048-bib-0049)).
The framing of sovereign, new material and relational agency (Charteris & Smardon, [2018](#bjet70048-bib-0011)) allows us to move beyond purely individualistic understandings of learning agency and instead consider how it arises through interactions among learners, technologies and sociocultural contexts. In this study, we are particularly interested in how GenAI, as a socio-material actant, mediates, supports or hinders students' learning agency in the classroom.
In the case of students with disability, GenAI is an enabler for new material agency as it can offer knowledge outputs in different formats, genres and modalities, structured learning experiences, motivation to learn and scaffolded dialogue to co-construct conceptual knowledge when human support is not immediately available (Farhah et al., [2025](#bjet70048-bib-0016); Gabriel, [2024](#bjet70048-bib-0019); Mitre & Zeneli, [2024](#bjet70048-bib-0030)). While extensive work on learning agency in secondary schools has been documented in a systematic review by Schoots-Snijder et al. ([2025](#bjet70048-bib-0041)), our model situates these forms of agency within socio-material and cultural assemblages.
### Generative Artificial Intelligence for students with disability
AI has gained considerable interest and support for its use in special education contexts over the last 10 years. Barua et al. ([2022](#bjet70048-bib-0006)) identified a comprehensive range of AI-enabled personalised assistive tools for students with Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder, Dyslexia and Autism Spectrum Disorder that have improved learning, engagement and social skills. They proposed developments in AI tools for real-time personalised learning. Alkan ([2024](#bjet70048-bib-0001)) grouped AI tools by functionality and recommended diversification of AI tools, with a particular focus on expert systems and adaptive tutoring systems to meet the needs of students with disability. However, Barua et al. ([2022](#bjet70048-bib-0006)) acknowledged that there was limited data on students with severe disabilities and the effectiveness of AI use. The research on GenAI in this context is even more limited. Nascent literature points to GenAI's potential to create learning resources that meet individual needs. In Rizos et al.'s ([2024](#bjet70048-bib-0038)) study, teachers used ChatGPT to create individualised Maths worksheets adapted to the interests and preferences of their Grade 8 students with disability. In Farhah et al.'s ([2025](#bjet70048-bib-0016)) study, GenAI was used to modify learning content to match the needs of students with ADHD, Dyslexia and Sensory Visual and Auditory impairment in real time.
Nevertheless, the dearth of research on GenAI in secondary schools and more so at the intersections of GenAI, students with disability and learning agency is a gap in the literature that needs to be addressed. This study explores whether and how students with disability acted agentively when using ChatGPT to support their own learning, and the affordances and constraints of ChatGPT for fostering their learning agency:
1. How does ChatGPT mediate, support or hinder the learning agency of students with disability?
2. What challenges do students with disability face when using ChatGPT to learn agentively, and how might GenAI be better designed to meet their needs?
## MATERIALS AND METHODS
This pilot study utilised an exploratory multiple-case study design (Yin, [2014](#bjet70048-bib-0048)) to present illuminating cases that bring to the fore theoretical connections (Mitchell, [1983](#bjet70048-bib-0029)) between agency according to Charteris and Smardon ([2018](#bjet70048-bib-0011)) and Latour's ([2005](#bjet70048-bib-0023)) ActorNetwork Theory (ANT) when exploring students with disability's emergent agency as they position GenAI as a socio-material mediator.
### Research context and participants
The study investigated the use of ChatGPT by students with disability in a secondary science class in Australia. Participants purposively sampled based on specific characteristics and context included six students with disability from Years 6 to 10. Data were collected over the final term of 2024, encompassing 5 weeks of lessons and 2 weeks of interviews with students. Of the six students, two struggled to use ChatGPT independently; one was unavailable for interviews, and the other had communication difficulties. A third student refused to use ChatGPT. Consequently, data from the remaining three students were analysed. Attendance was generally regular, though some participants were occasionally absent, leading to missing data for certain lessons (see Table [1](#bjet70048-tbl-0001)).

The study proceeded with three cases based on information power, which theorises that the more information each case holds that is relevant to the study aims, the lower the number of participants required, contingent on the study aims, sample specificity, use of established theory, quality of dialogue and analysis strategy (Malterud et al., [2016](#bjet70048-bib-0025)). The study aims were narrowed to drill down into comprehensive analyses of each case, reflecting variations in physical and learning needs, and focusing on particularities of experiences with ChatGPT that had hitherto not been documented. Substantive theoretical development of learning agency facilitated the synthesis of existing knowledge beyond the empirical data. The quality of the interview data was enriched by and triangulated against the multiple data sources, including participants' logged conversations with ChatGPT, teachers' worksheets and video recordings, bringing to light new insights on how participants acted agentively in their interactions with ChatGPT. Finally, given each case's specific disability, the cases were standalone and not intended for comparison. Yet the variation across the three cases presented different patterns salient to the study's aims, demonstrating variation in how agency was enacted. From the perspective of information power, the three cases collectively illuminate our understanding of how students with disability acted agentively in their interactions with ChatGPT functioning as a social mediator.
### Context of lessons
The science lessons covered push and pull forces, friction, gravity, mass and motion. Lesson 2 was designed by the researchers, while Lessons 1 and 3 to 6 were teacher-designed. Each weekly 1.5-hour lesson began with a whole-class discussion to assess students' prior knowledge and misconceptions, followed by the introduction of new concepts and their interrelationships. Teachers encouraged participation and provided relatable examples, such as the friction experienced while cycling over different terrains and the force of gravity on a steeper gradient.
Students in groups then conducted science investigations using everyday objects to concretise their understanding. Students made predictions, conducted each science investigation thrice, calculated averages and recorded their observations. These science investigations included:
\* L1 (Push and Pull Forces) and Introduction to ChatGPT.
\* L2 (Gravity): Investigating whether a grape and a watermelon would sink or float.
\* L3 (Friction and Road Surfaces): Determining which surface allowed a toy car to travel the furthest.
\* L4 (Car Brakes, Friction and Gravity): Comparing the effectiveness of front and back brakes on a toy car.
\* L5 (Mass and Gravity): Examining whether heavier or lighter toy cars travel further.
\* L6 (Motion, Inertia and Rest): Assessing if a Lego passenger would fall out when a toy car stops suddenly on different slopes.
### Intervention: Using ChatGPT to learn about science topics
Each science investigation was followed by students' use of ChatGPT to explore the science topics taught at each lesson. The intervention lasted between 18 and 25 minutes and included time spent assisting students to log into their ChatGPT accounts, the teacher modelling the use of ChatGPT and students using ChatGPT in class. The researchers, teachers and EAs then assisted the students to email the conversation data to an email address set up specifically for this project.
During this intervention, the teachers explored how best to engage their students in using ChatGPT, and their approach became increasingly scaffolded. They issued general instructions in Lesson 3. They progressed to suggesting questions their students could ask ChatGPT and modelling how their students could ask ChatGPT for a simpler explanation in Lesson 4. They modelled how students could ask ChatGPT for examples in Lesson 6. They drew their students' attention to examples of ChatGPT's response in both Lessons 4 and 6. This approach is consistent with the view that cognitive diversity is not a deficit, but a condition that requires responsive scaffolding (Puntambekar, [2022](#bjet70048-bib-0037)).
### Data sources
The data sources included (a) the teachers' lesson worksheets, (b) video recordings of whole-class teaching and group interactions during the science investigations, (c) students' ChatGPT conversation data, some of which were keyed in by their EA depending on the students' disability, (d) a researcher's field notes and (e) interview data of students, teachers and EAs. For the research aims of this paper, the analysis included three students with the most complete set of ChatGPT conversation data and interview data, and this analysis was informed by the teachers' lesson worksheets and video recordings (see Table [1](#bjet70048-tbl-0001)).
### Data analysis and representation
Case study methodology involves analytic generalisation or induction to identify logical classes of the agency enacted by students with disability in their interactions with ChatGPT (Mitchell, [1983](#bjet70048-bib-0029); Yin, [2014](#bjet70048-bib-0048)). Abstracting theoretically significant elements within the data to establish the essential features of such agentic enactments in each case required understanding contextual conditions that shaped the agentic moves in each case. Drawing on multiple sources of evidence for this intervention undertaken in an authentic context, the cases produced a ‘causally self-contained system’ (Mitchell, [1983](#bjet70048-bib-0029), p. 179), valuable for their ‘explanatory power’ (Mitchell, [1983](#bjet70048-bib-0029), p. 177) underpinned by cogent theory, rather than for their typicality. These cases are generalisable to the theoretical proposition of students' enactment of agency with ChatGPT functioning as a social mediator, providing description, illustration and explanation to inform future inquiry.
The data were initially analysed by the first author using the empathy map canvas designed to ‘develop deep, shared understanding and empathy for other people’ (Gray, [2017](#bjet70048-bib-0021)). During the process of categorising the data on a spreadsheet segmented to include all components of the empathy map canvas, the interview data, followed by the ChatGPT conversation data, were layered on. The teachers' lesson worksheets and the classroom video recordings were referred to for data triangulation (Miles et al., [2018](#bjet70048-bib-0028)). The data were then thematically coded using the processes outlined by Saldana ([2025](#bjet70048-bib-0039)). Initial categories were derived from the empathy map canvas questions on seeing, saying, doing, thoughts and feelings to make them more specific to the study and applicable to all three cases, and a new category, ‘future goals’, was added to capture the students' views on how they would like to use ChatGPT going forward. The secondary categories, derived from the primary categories, were written from the viewpoint of the individual case, using a first-person perspective, and the secondary categories pertinent to learning agency informed the construction of the theme for each case. The authors discussed the initial and secondary categories and themes, and, in line with the interpretive paradigm of this qualitative study, arrived at a ‘group consensus as an agreement goal’ (Saldana, [2025](#bjet70048-bib-0039), p. 59).
According to Yin ([2014](#bjet70048-bib-0048)), the individual can be considered ‘the primary unit of analysis’ and ‘several such individuals or “cases” might be included in a multiple-case study’ (p. 21). The intent of this multiple-case study design was to ensure balance and diversity across purposively sampled cases to facilitate an in-depth study of the phenomenon and create opportunities to learn about how students with disability engage with GenAI (Stake, [2005](#bjet70048-bib-0043)). Therefore, each student is presented as a case depicting different aspects of the phenomenon of students' learning agency with ChatGPT within the bounded systems of ChatGPT and the science class (Stake, [2005](#bjet70048-bib-0043)). These cases are presented not necessarily to imply generalisability or typicality, but ‘because it is believed that understanding them will lead to better understanding, perhaps better theorising, about a still larger collection of cases’ (Stake, [2000](#bjet70048-bib-0042), p. 437) on the use of GenAI among students with disability.
## RESULTS
The learning agency of students with disability is shaped not only by their choices but also by the mediating role of GenAI and the cognitive and metacognitive demands it places on them, hence revealing a misalignment between their desire to act agentively and their capability to do so without targeted support.
### Student choice to learn with ChatGPT
The following cases illustrate the sovereign agency of students with disability through their expression of choices on how to use ChatGPT. Material agency within this assemblage is reflected in the role they assigned to ChatGPT and relational agency in how they interacted with ChatGPT. The themes were based on each case, and a pseudonym is used for each student.
#### Arthur viewed ChatGPT as an additional source of information
Arthur expressed a strong personal interest in science and viewed ChatGPT as a useful source of information to extend what he already knew. During the interview, he shared his choice to use ChatGPT to explore ideas related to gravity, and his understanding that it can ‘not make a human fly’ and ‘when you're up in space, you're floating’, and stated, ‘I wanted to find [information on gravity] back from far’. His comments reflected a desire to build on prior knowledge, including references to Isaac Newton and Albert Einstein, and to verify factual distinctions, such as between discovery and invention. When asked why he used multiple information sources, including ChatGPT, Arthur replied, ‘Because that's what I do’, suggesting a self-directed orientation to learning.
Arthur's conceptualisation of learning aligned with a sovereign notion of agency—seeking to take ownership of his learning and independently expand his understanding. His case illustrates the choice component of sovereign agency, where he identified a learning goal and made a purposeful decision to engage with a tool to achieve it. However, while he positioned ChatGPT as a means to access knowledge, he offered little elaboration on what he had learnt directly from the tool or how he evaluated the responses he received. This suggests that while the choice to learn was clearly articulated, the follow-through remained constrained—an early indication of the gap between choice and capability.
#### Inez wanted to connect information from ChatGPT with real-world experiences
Inez verbalised her desire to use ChatGPT to make science more personally meaningful and applicable to everyday life. Her learning choices emerged through her focus on real-world scenarios, particularly in relation to safety while driving—a concern grounded in her own family's experiences. She mentioned that her relative was involved in many accidents and that she herself would like to learn how to ‘brake safely’ so that she would not ‘crash into other people’ when she started driving. During the interview, she recalled asking ChatGPT questions such as ‘What happens if I crash into a pole?’ and ‘How would we survive?’ Although these questions diverged from the immediate focus of Lesson 6 on motion, inertia and rest, the Lesson 6 worksheet image of a rider falling forward when a bicycle stopped abruptly (Figure [2](#bjet70048-fig-0002)) and the multiple science investigations using toy cars over several weeks plausibly reminded Inez of brake-induced friction, prompting her to ask about car crashes, and reflecting her efforts to understand science through the lens of lived experience. When she was asked what she wanted ChatGPT to do for her in science class, she emphasised that she wanted ChatGPT ‘to talk about… more like experience… that's interesting stuff because I like to learn more’.

This approach showed a choice to personalise science learning—an agentic move grounded in relevance and curiosity. Inez's case reflects an effort to engage in self-directed inquiry, drawing connections between classroom content and future concerns. Her agency was expressed through a desire for personally contextualised understanding, indicating a form of meaning-making and goal-setting. However, the alignment between her questions and the scientific focus of the lesson was tenuous at times, foreshadowing the challenges she would later face in maintaining topical relevance during interactions with ChatGPT.
#### Louis treated ChatGPT as a more knowledgeable peer
Louis approached ChatGPT as both a source of knowledge and a conversational partner. His enthusiastic engagement was evident in his eagerness to access the tool at the start of each lesson and type his teachers' suggested questions on the given science topic into ChatGPT. Across Lessons 3 to 6, Louis asked ChatGPT about friction, car brakes, inertia and motion, including questions like ‘Why does the car travel fast on ice?’, ‘How do brake callipers work?’, ‘Does gravity help cars travel faster downhill?’ and ‘Give me an example of inertia in bike riding’. This pattern of behaviour demonstrated clear choices, with Louis using the tool to generate relevant information.
At the same time, Louis often shifted between science-focused questions and personal queries such as ‘Do you like Taylor Swift?’ or ‘Do you have any pets?’—suggesting he viewed ChatGPT not only as a knowledge source but also as a social entity. He was attempting to identify life experiences that he and ChatGPT shared. His case illustrates a hybrid form of agency: sovereign in his choices on how to use the tool, and relational and new material in his treatment of ChatGPT as a conversational partner who could scaffold his learning. Louis's desire to engage both cognitively and socially with the tool highlights his choices, though the blending of purposes points to emerging tension between curiosity and focus—an issue explored further in the next section.
### Challenges to enacting learning agency with ChatGPT
While students exercised their sovereign agency through their expressed choices on how to learn with ChatGPT, they faced challenges owing to their disability, which included Autism Spectrum Disorder, mild intellectual disability, impaired hearing, cerebral palsy and a slower processing speed impacting communication. Notably, their capability to enact sovereign agency in its fullest sense was constrained by two major challenges: fragmented discourse owing to their topical diversions and the high discourse volume, density and complexity of ChatGPT's responses that placed high cognitive demands on students with disability to process these responses. These challenges reveal the misalignment between students' choices and their capability to enact these choices within the study's sociocultural context.
#### Fragmented discourse
The topical digressions in their conversations with ChatGPT might have been meaningful for the students with disability, but they rendered the discourse incoherent, obfuscating its message and meaning in relation to the science topic being discussed during the lesson. All three students produced fragmented discourses in different ways during their interactions with ChatGPT.
Arthur frequently drifted into unrelated topics, such as the Australian Football League or historical landmarks, even when his teacher had just introduced a science-related question. In Lesson 3, after completing a science investigation on friction, he queried ChatGPT about unrelated historical sites instead of the effects of different surfaces on motion. In Lesson 4, with direct teacher support, Arthur stayed on task and successfully repeated the prompt, ‘How does friction work in a car?’, suggesting that he could act more agentively when external regulation was provided. Inez also displayed tangential questioning. While Inez's questions during Lesson 6 (eg, ‘What happens if I crash?’) were connected to her lived experiences, other queries, such as ‘Why are cheetahs so fast?’ or ‘Why do female lions hunt?’ were unrelated to the lesson objectives. These shifts, prompted by personal interests, detracted from the lesson focus. Louis, despite his enthusiasm, regularly alternated between relevant science questions and social prompts directed at ChatGPT (Figure [3](#bjet70048-fig-0003)).

#### High volume, density and complexity discourse
The high volume, density and complexity of ChatGPT's discourse made it difficult for students with disability to make sense of the information being shared with them and how this information could be integrated with their current understanding. ChatGPT's verbose and complex explanations often included unfamiliar terminology and extended reasoning sequences (Figure [4](#bjet70048-fig-0004)).

Louis described his experience with ChatGPT's responses as ‘crazy… a lot of words’, and expressed a preference for ‘simple words’. Although students were advised by teachers in Lesson 4 to ask ChatGPT to explain its ideas more simply and, in Lesson 6, to request examples, there was little evidence that students used these strategies independently. ChatGPT's responses delivered through a text-to-speech function did not mitigate the challenge, as the content remained dense and difficult to process. For students like Inez, who had documented difficulties with spelling, the high linguistic complexity of ChatGPT's default responses was a barrier to agentic engagement. The tool itself lacked the adaptability to meet them at their level of comprehension. These challenges show that while students were motivated and curious, they needed scaffolded mediation to sustain their learning agency. Without it, the design of GenAI, optimised for general users, rendered the information shared incomprehensible.
In sum, the findings provided insights into the ways students with disability demonstrated clear and diverse choices to use ChatGPT in agentive ways—reflecting their sovereign, material and relational agency in their expressed desire to seek knowledge, make real-world connections and interact socially. The data suggest that an aspect of sovereign agency is inherent, enacted through choice and specific to the individual. At the same time, through their conversations with ChatGPT, we found their expressed choices to learn with ChatGPT had not translated into the capability to use ChatGPT to learn agentively in class, resulting in a disjunction between choice and capability that set limits on their sovereign agency. The students with disability faced metacognitive and cognitive challenges, owing in part to learning routines and practices that had yet to be established and to constraints they encountered when using a GenAI platform catering for the general populace, reflecting limits in material agency arising from GenAI's design. Nevertheless, Louis's case, in particular, highlighted ChatGPT's perceived social presence and its potential to serve as a peer, and is indicative of new relations evolving between humans and objects that facilitate not only new forms of material agency but also relational agency.
## DISCUSSION
This study set out to examine the potential of GenAI—specifically ChatGPT—to support the learning agency of students with disability in a secondary school science classroom. The discussion below critically reflects on the study's research questions.
> RQ1: How does ChatGPT mediate, support or hinder the learning agency of students with disability?
The expressions of choice by students with disability on how they wanted to use ChatGPT to help them learn aligned in part with their sovereign agency and speak to their sophisticated understanding of learning and learning processes. The findings also underscore the importance of giving choice and voice to students with disability, which entails offering customised learning opportunities. However, the gap between their stated choice and their limited capability to sustain their inquiry reinforces the view of agency as emergent, as a mediated process and context-dependent, shaped by the design of tools like ChatGPT and the presence, or absence, of timely human support. When scaffolded by teachers or EAs, students were more likely to stay on task and engage meaningfully with the science topics. In contrast, without support, their interactions with ChatGPT often drifted off-topic, and they struggled to understand its responses, reflecting their need for cognitive and metacognitive support from people and tools, and underscoring the importance of material and relational agency.
ChatGPT played a dual role as both an enabler and a constraint in the material agency of students with disability. From a socio-material perspective, the tool acted as a mediator in students' learning processes—delivering explanations, prompting questions and, in some cases, sustaining engagement through dialogic interaction. At the same time, the design of ChatGPT, particularly its default verbosity, lack of adaptive scaffolds and general-purpose interface, often posed barriers to sustained engagement. Students were not always able to adjust its responses to match their metacognitive and cognitive processing needs, and the tool lacked built-in mechanisms to help redirect off-topic queries or simplify explanations and illustrations without external prompting. In this sense, the tool's socio-material affordances were not fully aligned with the learning needs of students with disability. This reinforces the need to reconceptualise GenAI tools not just as information providers, but as active participants in the socio-material assemblage, shaping what students can do, and how they can do it, giving rise to the following questions by Bord et al. ([2023](#bjet70048-bib-0007)) regarding choices: (1) What can be chosen? (2) Who has choice, the student, GenAI or the teacher? (3) When can choices be made? (4) Where can choices be made?
From a broader perspective, these findings are consistent with Moon et al.'s ([2025](#bjet70048-bib-0032)) proposed triangular framework of guided interaction in AI chatbot interaction, depicting the flexible interactions between pedagogical design, student agency and technology integration through scaffolding, alignment and adaptability to provide different levels of support. Going forward, in reference to Strnadová et al.'s ([2024](#bjet70048-bib-0044)) identification of the most pervasive practices used to holistically support students with disability, namely, visual supports, peer-mediated instruction and intervention, systematic instruction, self-management, time delay and prompting, we can anticipate various roles that GenAI can play in enabling the material agency of students with disability.
> RQ2: What challenges do students with disability face when using ChatGPT to learn agentively, and how might GenAI be better designed to meet their needs?
The findings identified two major challenges: fragmented discourse and high volume, density and complex discourse. Both are linked to the metacognitive and cognitive profiles of students with disability. The learning difficulties of students with disability are attributed to the students' cognitive and neurological profile (Büttner & Hasselhorn, [2011](#bjet70048-bib-0009)). Metacognition plays a critical role in activating execution and regulation strategies (Schneider & Lockl, [2002](#bjet70048-bib-0040)), facilitating reflections that enable a shift from reactive to agentic approaches to learning (Marulis et al., [2020](#bjet70048-bib-0027)). However, adolescents with learning disabilities, particularly those with severe learning disabilities, exhibit lower performance, self-regulation and metacognition compared to neurotypical peers (Nader-Grosbois, [2014](#bjet70048-bib-0033); Schneider & Lockl, [2002](#bjet70048-bib-0040)).
The misalignment between GenAI's default design and the cognitive and metacognitive needs of students with disability offers grounds for interrogating ChatGPT's current role as a socio-material mediator. It emphasises the need to design GenAI to be responsive to the cognitive and metacognitive profiles of students with disability to enable new material agency that would help sustain purposeful inquiry. Marino et al. ([2023](#bjet70048-bib-0026)) refer to AI's potential role as a ‘cognitive prosthesis’, amplifying, complementing or enhancing the cognitive ability of students with disrupted or impaired cognitive functions.
With research pairing GenAI and adaptive learning environments reporting promising results for students with learning disabilities (Farhah et al., [2025](#bjet70048-bib-0016)), the incorporation of cognitive and metacognitive supports into the design features of GenAI could enable it to function effectively as a cognitive prosthesis. In an adaptation of Federiakin et al.'s ([2024](#bjet70048-bib-0017)) prompt engineering framework, which reverses interaction roles, assigning responsibility for prompting to GenAI, these supports for students’ cognition and metacognition could include GenAI undertaking the following actions:
1. Structured content: Initiating queries based on prompt structures (e.g. Microsoft ([n.d.](#bjet70048-bib-0052)) Goal-Context-Expectation-Source prompt for Copilot) customised for individual student needs;
2. Literacy support: making communications with students more accessible by providing simple explanations of these options in text, and in auditory and visual formats, and adopting adjustable pacing of interactions;
3. Flexible methods: adopting methods customised to individual needs such as offering and modelling a set of viable options for students to progress in a task, presenting scaffolds such as embedded modelled questions, simplification and adaptive prompts;
4. Reflexive and responsive interactions: initiating regulatory cues such as summarising previous questions, suggesting clarifying follow-ups, or gently redirecting the learner back to the topic with metacognitive nudges, akin to interventions undertaken by teachers and EAs arising from relational agency that help students stay on track.
The relationship between scaffolding and agency is illuminated by Moon et al.'s ([2025](#bjet70048-bib-0032)) finding that, along a continuum of least to most guided scenarios, their participants demonstrated the most sophisticated practices when they were most guided.
This project was implemented when AI-generated images were not available in ChatGPT. Nevertheless, going forward, GenAI's growing multimodal capacities present new opportunities for inclusive practices that reflect students' preferred ways of learning, transforming and expanding its role as a socio-material mediator. These design features would align with the Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles, offering multiple means of representation, engagement, and expression (CAST, [2024](#bjet70048-bib-0010)), making it easier for students with disability to access and process information independently. The use of GenAI in alignment with the UDL framework in a university context presented significant learning assistance in teaching, writing, reading and research, or self-organisation (Pierrès et al., [2024](#bjet70048-bib-0036)). As suggested by Fackler et al. ([2024](#bjet70048-bib-0015)), modelling scientific thinking and practices through multiple modes can support students in constructing meaning using diverse cognitive pathways.
Conceptualisations of GenAI integration to better support students with disability and equity in general are ongoing and highlight possibilities, risks, limitations and key considerations such as AI literacy and alignment with CAST's ([2024](#bjet70048-bib-0010)) principles of Universal Design for Learning (see Rappa & Nonis, [2026](#bjet70048-bib-0051); Gabriel, [2024](#bjet70048-bib-0019)). Even so, such conceptualisations should enable ‘bricolage practices’ (Moon et al., [2025](#bjet70048-bib-0032)), affording opportunities for students with disability to creatively adapt AI chatbot affordances. In sum, GenAI should be designed to provide a comprehensive range of supports that address students' cognitive, metacognitive and affective needs and customised options that would facilitate sovereign, material and relational agency for students with disability.
## CONCLUSION
This study offers the view that agency must be understood as emergent and mediated—a dynamic process shaped by the interaction of personal choices, technological tools, social supports and the cultural context of the classroom. GenAI can support this process, but only when it is designed and integrated with the specific needs of students with disability in mind.
### Recommendations
With continued developments in GenAI, the education sector must move beyond one-size-fits-all designs to the customisation of multiple agents to meet the specific learning needs of students with disability while enhancing their learning agency. The following recommendations outline a direction for research, development and classroom practice to better align GenAI with the learning agency of students with disability.
#### Personalising learning through a ground-up approach
A ground-up approach to designing GenAI beginning with students' lived experiences and learning choices, and advancing through careful design focused on adaptive mechanisms, interactive learning opportunities, real-time assessment and feedback, and targeted instructional strategies (Vorobyeva et al., [2025](#bjet70048-bib-0047)) can make personalised learning through GenAI a reality. Such an approach is consistent with Australia's plan for personalised learning and support for people with disabilities (Department of Education, Skills and Employment, [2020](#bjet70048-bib-0012)).
#### Clarifying the role of human facilitators
There is a disparity in the perceptions of the roles played by EAs and the importance of these roles (Brock et al., [2020](#bjet70048-bib-0008); Gibson et al., [2016](#bjet70048-bib-0020)). Layering in GenAI adds to this complexity. Hence, clarification of teachers' and EAs' roles in facilitating the learning agency of students with disability using GenAI is needed. Within the outermost circle representing sociocultural contexts (Figure [1](#bjet70048-fig-0001)), the interventions of educators not only support students in overcoming cognitive and metacognitive challenges but also foster a classroom culture validating students' learning choices and promoting relational agency. As such, professional learning for educators should include not only technical training in GenAI tools but also strategies for fostering students' learning agency through coordinated human–AI interaction. As a starting point, Schoots-Snijder et al. ([2025](#bjet70048-bib-0041)), in examining curricular elements that enable student agency, outline the teacher's role in developing student agency. They identify key practices that need to be introduced or transformed, some of which could potentially be distributed to and enacted by EAs and GenAI. Nevertheless, we advocate a cautious and phased approach to implementing interventions. Until the results of customising GenAI to meet students with disability's needs on a smaller scale are known, we advise against scaling up interventions for this vulnerable group of learners, and against their learning independently with GenAI, given persistent issues with the use of AI, particularly, humans being manipulated into making decisions about their learning (Fu & Weng, [2024](#bjet70048-bib-0018)).
### Challenges and limitations of the study
Students with disability have complex physical and learning needs. These needs, together with the priority of ensuring that students take brain breaks and their medication at scheduled times and go for scheduled appointments with psychologists during class, make data collection challenging. This pilot study presents its findings based on three students who had the most complete set of data. The triangulation of data presents us with a rich and complex picture of the learning agency of students with disability to use ChatGPT to learn. Nevertheless, we acknowledge that the small number of participants and the uniqueness of the study context can limit the transferability of its findings. In returning to the United Nations ([2024](#bjet70048-bib-0046)) report, the digital divide persists globally, with only 47% of students with disabilities having Internet access during school closures arising from the global pandemic, and 9 in 10 did not have access to the information and communications technology they needed to learn remotely. As such, even though the customisation of GenAI to meet the specific needs of students with disability is anticipated to make a major difference in learning for these students, access to technological resources remains an issue worldwide for most students with disability and should be a key focus in educational interventions.
