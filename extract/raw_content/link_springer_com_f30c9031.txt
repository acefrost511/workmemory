Abstract
--------

This study conducted a comprehensive bibliometric and content analysis to explore the integration of artificial intelligence in students’ cognitive learning outcomes. A structured TITLE-ABS-KEY search was performed in the Scopus database using keywords such as “Artificial Intelligence,” “AI,” “Students,” and “cognitive learning outcomes,” resulting in 318 documents published between 2016 and 2025. After filtering, a final dataset of 246 research articles and conference papers was analyzed. The methodology includes bibliometric performance analysis (covering publication trends, countries, affiliations, authors, and journals) and network analysis (comprising co-word, citation, co-authorship, and bibliographic coupling). Additionally, content analysis was conducted on the ten most cited and ten focused articles addressing AI’s impact on cognitive learning outcomes. VOSviewer software was used for data analysis and visualization. Findings indicate increased research output post-2023, driven by digital transformation and global collaboration. Leading affiliations include The University of Hong Kong and Carnegie Mellon University, with the United States, China, and India as top contributing countries. Influential journals and funding bodies include the National Science Foundation and the National Natural Science Foundation of China. Notable authors include Chiu and Cukurova, while Kit Ng, Zhong, and Liu are prominent in bibliographic coupling, emphasizing AI adoption. Co-authorship analysis shows collaboration primarily among developed nations. Co-word analysis reveals Key research themes include contrastive learning, adversarial machine learning, and federated learning. Content analysis highlights AI’s transformative potential for learning, teaching, cognitive learning, and innovation. This study provides managerial and practical recommendations for students, universities, and policymakers. This study has several limitations that future studies will consider.

1 Introduction
--------------

Historically, educational technology introduced computers and the Internet into classrooms to support student learning. AI was not seen as a practical tool for enhancing cognitive development [[1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR1)]. However, the conversation has evolved. AI is now deeply integrated into educational systems through features like adaptive learning platforms, the metaverse, gamification, intelligent feedback, virtual avatars, and virtual and augmented reality. These technologies are creating more tailored and effective cognitive learning experiences for students [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR2)].

Cognitive learning outcomes represent the development of students’ intellectual abilities, including critical thinking, problem-solving, understanding, and knowledge retention. These outcomes are essential indicators of how effectively learners process, internalize, and apply new information across various educational contexts [[3](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR3)]. Enhancing cognitive learning is a central goal in education, as it facilitates academic achievement and prepares students for complex real-world challenges by fostering higher-order thinking skills. In recent years, innovative educational technologies, such as artificial intelligence, have shown promise in supporting and improving cognitive learning outcomes by providing personalized, adaptive, and interactive learning experiences [[4](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR4)].

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is playing a growing role in improving students’ cognitive learning outcomes. AI-driven educational tools adapt to individual learning styles and provide personalized support, helping students understand complex concepts better [[5](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR5)]. These technologies enhance critical thinking, problem-solving, and memory retention, creating more profound and compelling learning experiences. AI eases the workload of educators and administrators by handling routine administrative tasks, freeing them to focus on strategic and innovative educational efforts. It streamlines processes such as registration, grading, and resource management, enhancing efficiency and enriching the overall experience for students [[6](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR6)].

Artificial Intelligence (AI) has transformed education, enhancing students’ cognitive learning outcomes. AI-driven adaptive learning platforms can customize instructional content to align with individual student’s cognitive abilities, learning styles, and progress rates [[7](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR7)]. This personalization allows learners to engage more deeply with material suited to their understanding, promoting better retention and critical thinking. Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS), Adaptive Learning Platforms, and Natural Language Processing (NLP) tools like chatbots and virtual assistants. ITS provides personalized, adaptive instruction and real-time feedback tailored to each student’s cognitive needs, helping deepen understanding and skill mastery by adjusting difficulty and guiding problem-solving processes [[8](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR8)]. Adaptive Learning Platforms dynamically customize content based on learners’ progress, preferences, and knowledge gaps, improving engagement and cognitive retention through individualized learning pathways. Meanwhile, NLP-powered tools offer immediate support by answering questions, explaining concepts, and assisting with language and writing tasks, enhancing comprehension and critical thinking through interactive dialogue [[9](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR9)]. These AI tools combine personalized learning, instant feedback, and interactive support, key elements that effectively boost cognitive learning outcomes. AI’s integration into learning environments fosters a more engaging and efficient cognitive development process, preparing students for complex problem-solving and analytical tasks in their academic journey [[10](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR10)].

The swift adoption of AI technologies such as machine learning, natural language processing, and predictive analytics has introduced innovative opportunities to enhance teaching methods and increase student engagement. This rapid evolution highlights the importance of developing sustainable strategies to leverage AI’s capabilities fully. As higher education institutions embrace these advancements, ongoing research and innovation are essential to maximize AI’s benefits while effectively addressing its challenges [[11](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR11)].

In recent years, numerous empirical studies have explored the impact of artificial intelligence on student’s cognitive learning outcomes. For instance, A case study of the impact of artificial intelligence on student reliance on exam answers in Indonesia, the study explores how students at Ibnu Rusyd College of Tarbiyah (IRCT) Indonesia rely on AI tools to answer exam questions and examines the impact of this reliance on learning, understanding, and academic performance [[12](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR12)]. Factors influencing university students’ continuance intentions towards self-directed learning using artificial intelligence tools: insights from structural equation modeling and fuzzy-set qualitative comparative analysis offer insights into the various cognitive and motivational factors shaped by AI integration in learning environments [[13](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR13)]. These studies, among others, have significantly contributed to the growing body of literature demonstrating AI’s potential to improve student cognitive outcomes in education. Through comprehensive literature analysis, researchers gain a deeper understanding of how AI supports and transforms cognitive learning processes, ultimately aiding educational development.

No comprehensive bibliometric and content analysis has specifically examined the relationship between artificial intelligence and students’ cognitive learning outcomes. Previous studies have focused on related but distinct areas, exploring various educational contexts and employing different AI tools, such as the study by Abdur Rahman et al. [[14](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR14)] A comprehensive bibliometric and content analysis of artificial intelligence in language learning: systematically analyze the existing scientific literature to map the development of AI applications in language learning from 2017 to 2023. This study identifies key authors, influential institutions, leading countries, prominent documents, and thematic research clusters, providing a comprehensive overview of trends and content in the field through bibliometric and content analysis methods. Another study by Lin and Yu [[15](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR15)], A bibliometric analysis of artificial intelligence chatbots in educational contexts. The study reviews the rising use of artificial intelligence chatbots in education, especially post-COVID-19, using bibliometric and citation network analyses to explore key research themes, application areas, and challenges. It highlights chatbot use in language learning, educational services, and healthcare education. Another study by Li and Wong [[16](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR16)] provides a comprehensive bibliometric analysis of artificial intelligence in personalized learning from 2000 to 2022, highlighting key publication trends, major research topics, and emerging areas like big data and deep learning. It outlines the intellectual foundations and evolving trends, emphasizing the need for future research on pedagogical approaches and teachers’ roles in AI-supported personalized learning. However, these studies do not directly address students’ cognitive learning outcomes, leaving a critical gap in the existing literature. To address this gap, the current study adopts the following objectives presented in Table [1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#Tab1).

2 Methodology
-------------

This study employed a hybrid review methodology, combining bibliometric analysis with content analysis, to highlight best practices in disseminating scientific knowledge, particularly concerning artificial intelligence and students’ cognitive learning outcomes [[17](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR17)]. This dual approach captures the development of academic insights in the field by integrating quantitative bibliometric techniques with in-depth qualitative evaluation through a systematic review. The analysis utilized quantitative and qualitative methods, drawing on Scopus-indexed literature on AI and students’ cognitive learning outcomes [[18](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR18)].

### 2.1 Study selection process

Selecting a suitable and reliable database is critical for ensuring the validity and appropriateness of data collection. In line with the research objectives, this study utilized the Scopus database. This choice was strategically made due to several advantages of Scopus, such as comprehensive coverage, timely updates, data debugging, and processing capabilities, which can help researchers ensure the accuracy and integrity of the data [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR2)]. Scopus was chosen for bibliometric analysis due to its broad coverage of peer-reviewed journals across various disciplines. It offers reliable citation data and advanced analytical tools for mapping research trends. Additionally, Scopus ensures high-quality, curated content, making it a trusted source for bibliometric research [[19](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR19)]. Scopus offers a more curated and academically rigorous collection of sources, as it excludes non-peer-reviewed materials often included in databases such as Google Scholar. Although the Web of Science is similarly reputable, its journal coverage is relatively limited, particularly regarding non-English publications [[20](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR20)]. For this study, a TITLE-ABS-KEY-specific search using the keywords “Artificial Intelligence,” “AI,” “Students,” and “cognitive learning outcomes” was conducted on May 15, 2025, yielding 318 documents published between 2016 and 2025. After removing duplicate entries and excluding non-research items such as editorials, notes, and reports, as well as those limited to English, the final dataset was refined to 246 research articles and conference papers. The architecture for retrieving the relevant literature employed in bibliometric investigation is depicted in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#Fig1).

### 2.2 Data analysis process

This study utilized two primary bibliometric methods: bibliometric performance analysis, which explored publication trends, key contributing countries, institutions, authors, and journals, and network analysis, encompassing co-word analysis, citation analysis, co-authorship mapping, and bibliographic coupling. Additionally, a content analysis was conducted on a set of highly cited articles related to artificial intelligence and students’ cognitive learning outcomes, along with ten selected articles that specifically investigate the effects of artificial intelligence on students’ cognitive learning outcomes [[21](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR21)]. Moreover, it investigates the field’s core themes and intellectual structure using science mapping techniques, including co-word analysis, citation analysis, co-authorship analysis, bibliographic coupling, and a content review of the five most highly cited articles on artificial intelligence and students’ cognitive learning outcomes. For data analysis and visualization, the study employs VOSviewer, a powerful distance-based mapping tool, to represent relationships visually. VOSviewer displays results as networks and color-coded clusters, illustrating connections, link strength, and total link strength between articles [[22](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR22)].

### 3.1 Bibliometric performance analysis

A bibliometric performance analysis was conducted to assess the research landscape, focusing on top contributing authors, journals, countries, affiliations, and annual publication trends. Figure [3](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#Fig3) illustrates the number of publications from 2016 to 2025. Research on artificial intelligence in relation to students’ cognitive learning outcomes has shown a sharply upward trend since 2023, with continued growth observed in 2025. Notably, 2024 recorded the highest number of publications, reaching 96. This sustained growth reflects a rapidly expanding body of literature and increasing institutional interest in leveraging artificial intelligence to enhance cognitive development in higher education. These findings are consistent with Garzón et al. 2025 [[23](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR23)], who, in their systematic review of AI in education from 2015 to 2025, similarly reported a significant surge in publications beginning in 2022, particularly driven by generative AI applications such as ChatGPT. Their study highlights not only the momentum of research in this domain but also the growing recognition of AI’s potential to transform learning outcomes, while acknowledging the accompanying challenges of digital dependency and ethical concerns (Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#Fig2)).

### 3.2 Network analysis

This analysis adopts a relational approach to explore and classify bibliographic data through methods such as co-word analysis, citation analysis, co-authorship, bibliographic coupling, and co-citation.

#### 3.2.1 Co-word analysis

Co-word analysis is a widely employed bibliometric method that examines the co-occurrence patterns of keywords within academic publications to uncover the conceptual structure of a research field. By analyzing the frequency with which specific terms appear together in titles, abstracts, or complete texts, this technique facilitates the identification of thematic clusters and the relationships among key concepts [[31](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR31)]. The resulting co-word networks enable researchers to visualize and interpret the intellectual landscape of a domain, revealing dominant research areas, emerging trends, and the evolution of scholarly discourse over time. As such, co-word analysis is a valuable tool in bibliometric studies. It contributes to a more systematic understanding of knowledge development and informs strategic decisions in research management and policy formulation, allowing researchers to place their work within dynamic academic contexts [[32](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR32)].

In bibliometric analysis, thresholds are used to set minimum inclusion criteria for elements within a network. For keyword co-occurrence analysis, a commonly accepted threshold is a minimum of five appearances, ensuring the inclusion of only the most relevant terms while preventing data fragmentation [[33](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR33)]. Similarly, citation link strength, which reflects the degree of connection between two publications based on shared references, is often set at 10 or more to emphasize significant academic relationships. In this study, we applied a threshold of at least five keyword occurrences to analyze co-occurrence patterns related to artificial intelligence and its influence on students’ cognitive learning outcomes [[34](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR34)].

Table [6](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#Tab6) highlights the co-occurrence of key terms such as Students, artificial intelligence, and contrastive learning, which appear most frequently in the literature, underscoring the growing academic focus on AI’s role in education and students’ cognitive learning outcomes. Within this context, artificial intelligence transforms cognitive learning outcomes by enabling personalized instruction, adaptive assessments, and real-time feedback tailored to individual student needs [[35](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR35)]. These AI-driven approaches support enhanced student understanding and academic performance. Moreover, advanced AI techniques such as machine learning and natural language processing are accelerating educational research, improving learning data analysis, and refining strategies that directly impact students’ cognitive development and success [[36](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR36)].

Other frequently occurring themes, such as adversarial machine learning, teaching, federated learning, and learning outcomes, highlight the strong connection between artificial intelligence and students’ cognitive learning outcomes. AI supports cognitive learning outcomes by delivering personalized learning paths that adapt to individual student needs, boosting engagement and academic achievement. Artificial intelligence is transformative in enhancing students’ cognitive learning outcomes through its integration into modern curricula, intelligent learning systems, and flexible e-learning platforms [[37](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR37)]. AI-enabled curricula are increasingly designed to be adaptive and personalized, allowing students to engage with content that matches their learning pace and cognitive level. Advanced learning systems leverage machine learning algorithms to provide tailored instructional pathways and immediate feedback, fostering deeper comprehension and critical thinking. In e-learning environments, AI facilitates interactive, student-centered experiences such as virtual tutors and real-time support that promote active engagement, knowledge retention, and the development of higher-order thinking skills [[38](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR38)].

#### 3.2.2 Citation analysis

Scholars use citations to evaluate the influence and importance of academic work within the scholarly community. A research article with many citations generally indicates its considerable impact on the discipline [[57](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR57)]. Additionally, the pattern of citations over time can reflect how a study has shaped and influenced other researchers [[58](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR58)].

Table [7](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#Tab7) highlights the five leading articles ranked by their citation index. Cristina Conati et al. [[59](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR59)], has the highest citation number. This study improved the Adaptive Constraint Satisfaction Problem (ACSP) educational tool by adding explanation features to AI-generated hints. Using a controlled experiment with students, it showed that explanations boosted trust, usefulness, and willingness to reuse the system. Importantly, the study also highlighted that learner traits (Need for Cognition, Conscientiousness, Reading Proficiency) shaped the effectiveness of XAI explanations. The high citation count reflects both its methodological rigor and its practical contribution to designing more personalized intelligent tutoring systems.

Another highly cited article by Cathryn A. Manduca et al. [[60](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR60)], investigated whether geoscience faculty had shifted from lectures to active learning, and assessed the impact of the “On the Cutting Edge” program. Using large-scale surveys, classroom observations, and interviews, the study provided robust evidence that professional development workshops significantly improved teaching practices. Its influence stems not only from strong empirical evidence but also from its practical application in faculty training programs that are replicable across disciplines.

The third most cited article, Carolyn P. Rosé et al. [[61](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR61)], Explanatory learner models: Why machine learning (alone) is not the answer, offers a conceptual but highly influential contribution. It argued that predictive “black box” models are insufficient for educational contexts and advocated for explanatory learner models that are interpretable and pedagogically meaningful. While not empirical, its widespread citation reflects its theoretical impact in shaping debates about transparency and interpretability in AI for education.

Ofra Amir et al. [[62](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR62)], examined interactive advice-seeking strategies in simulated environments. The study demonstrated that shared decision-making between teacher and AI learner reduced the teacher’s workload without compromising student outcomes. Though simulation-based, its rigorous design and focus on human AI collaboration explains its strong influence in subsequent research on interactive learning systems.

Finally, Ali M. Fazlollahi et al. [[63](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR63)], Effect of Artificial Intelligence Tutoring vs. Expert Instruction on Learning Simulated Surgical Skills Among Medical Students: A Randomized Clinical Trial, provided high-quality empirical evidence by comparing AI tutoring with expert instruction and control groups in surgical training. Results showed AI tutoring could match or exceed expert instruction in certain areas, underscoring the practical potential of AI systems in specialized, high-stakes education. Its randomized controlled design and clear practical application explain both its quality and its high citation count.

#### 3.2.4 Bibliographic coupling analysis

Bibliographic coupling occurs when two or more research papers cite the same earlier sources, indicating a shared foundation in the literature. This method explores and maps the current landscape of active research within a particular domain [[76](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR76)]. By analyzing overlapping references, bibliographic coupling helps uncover relationships between studies and reveals how closely connected different works are regarding their conceptual basis. It is a valuable tool for understanding an academic discipline’s intellectual structure and emerging trends. It offers insights into how ideas evolve and where research efforts are concentrated [[77](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR77)].

Table [9](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#Tab9) lists the five most frequently cited publications based on total link strength from bibliographic coupling analysis. These works are not only widely recognized but also stand out in terms of methodological rigor, reliability of findings, and their practical application in educational settings. For instance, the study by Ng et al. [[78](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR78)], which designed and validated the AI Literacy Questionnaire (AILQ), represents a high-quality contribution with substantial influence. By combining theoretical reviews, expert judgment, pilot testing, and confirmatory factor analysis, the authors established a robust four-factor tool encompassing affective, behavioral, cognitive, and ethical dimensions of AI literacy. Its value lies in offering educators a valid and trustworthy instrument to assess students’ AI knowledge, attitudes, and skills, making it highly applicable for improving instructional design. Similarly, the work of Zhong and Liu [[79](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR79)] provides another significant methodological advancement through the development of the AI Literacy Scale (AILS). Drawing on Piaget’s epistemology and Bloom’s taxonomy, the study validated its framework with a large-scale sample of 1,392 students. The rigorous process of scale refinement, including Rasch analysis and confirmatory factor analysis, ensures reliability, while its findings on gender differences add further depth to understanding student learning outcomes. The impact of this study lies in its potential for cross-cultural validation, making the tool adaptable across diverse educational contexts.

Equally important is the study by Dahri et al. [[80](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR80)], which explores the role of ChatGPT in shaping academic success and career readiness. This work is notable for its empirical focus, examining real-world applications of generative AI in education. Its strength lies in highlighting both positive outcomes such as improved performance and readiness and potential risks, including over-reliance and diminished critical thinking. By doing so, it offers balanced insights that can inform responsible adoption of AI tools in schools and universities. Complementing this, Fu et al. [[81](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR81)] provide a scoping review of AI learning tools in K-12 education. Their contribution lies in mapping the landscape of existing tools and stressing the need for standardized assessments, which is crucial for scaling AI education reliably. Finally, Jose et al. [[82](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR82)] address the integration of AI-based chatbots in higher education. Their systematic review identifies benefits such as scalability and personalized learning while underscoring the importance of ethical considerations and effective curricular integration. Collectively, these five publications shape both the conceptual foundations and applied practices of AI in education, balancing innovation with critical reflection.

Table [11](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#Tab11) presents the key methodological approaches and research purposes of the ten most highly cited articles on artificial intelligence and student’s cognitive learning outcomes. Table [12](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#Tab12), based on the findings of these articles, synthesizes results into five major themes and related sub-themes personalization and adaptive learning, feedback and assessment, explainability and human interaction, engagement and motivation, and ethics and AI literacy highlighting their major contributions. Table [13](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#Tab13) summarizes the reported limitations of the ten studies alongside recommendations for future research, pointing to the need for broader validation, longitudinal studies, and stronger ethical integration in AI applications for education.

Conati et al. [[59](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR59)] investigated the value of personalized explanations in explainable artificial intelligence (xai) within an intelligent tutoring system. Their controlled experiment showed that explanations not only improved learning outcomes but also enhanced student trust and willingness to reuse the system. Interestingly, the benefits were not uniform students with higher Need for Cognition and reading proficiency gained more, highlighting the importance of adapting explanations to individual learner traits. This study demonstrates that personalization in AI systems can significantly influence both cognitive and affective aspects of learning.

Similarly, Sajja et al. [[94](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR94)] developed and tested an AI-enabled intelligent assistant, known as the Virtual Teaching Assistant (VirtualTA), for higher education. While Conati et al. [[59](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR59)] emphasized personalization in explanations, Sajja et al. [[94](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR94)] focused on providing adaptive support through a wide range of intelligent services such as quizzes, flashcards, and automated feedback. Their findings confirm that adaptive assistants can improve engagement and self-regulated learning while also reducing instructor workload. Taken together, these studies reinforce the conclusion that personalization and adaptivity are central to maximizing the impact of AI in education.

Fazlollahi et al. [[63](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR63)] explored a related dimension of AI’s educational role in the context of medical training. In a randomized clinical trial, they compared the AI-based Virtual Operative Assistant to expert instruction and no-feedback conditions in surgical simulation. Results revealed that the AI tutor significantly outperformed both expert and control groups in procedural performance, while not increasing cognitive load or emotional strain. This study provides strong empirical evidence that AI feedback systems can rival or even surpass human instructors in certain specialized domains.

Fu et al. [[95](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR95)] expanded the conversation on feedback and assessment by examining how AI-enabled automatic scoring applications influence continuous learning intentions in language learning. Their mixed-methods study identified social presence, peer influence, and immediate benefits as key features that sustain learner engagement. Interestingly, while accurate speech recognition directly influenced intention to use the apps, it did not significantly foster engagement. This complements Fazlollahi et al. [[63](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR63)], showing that while AI can improve technical performance, its design features particularly those that promote social and emotional connections are equally important in sustaining motivation and long-term learning.

Rosé et al. [[61](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR61)] argued that predictive accuracy alone is insufficient for meaningful AI use in education, advocating instead for explanatory learner models. Their conceptual paper stressed the need for interpretability, so that models can be used by educators to support metacognition, detect misconceptions, and guide instructional design. This call for explainability is echoed by Ghai et al. [[96](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR96)], who empirically tested Explainable Active Learning (XAL). Their study found that while explanations improved trust calibration and elicited richer feedback, they also introduced risks of anchoring bias and cognitive overload. Amir et al. [[62](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR62)] extended this discussion by showing that interactive advice-seeking strategies allowed AI learners to reduce teacher workload while maintaining performance. Together, these three studies converge on the idea that transparency and human interaction designs are critical to building effective and trusted AI systems, but they also caution that explainability must be implemented carefully to avoid unintended consequences.

Manduca et al. [[60](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR60)] approached AI’s educational role more indirectly by focusing on faculty professional development in STEM education. Their mixed-methods study revealed that faculty who participated in discipline-based training programs, such as “On the Cutting Edge,” were significantly more likely to adopt active learning practices. Although not focused on AI tools directly, this study highlights that professional development and supportive teaching ecosystems are essential for successfully integrating AI-enhanced learning practices. It also aligns with the findings of Fu et al. [[95](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR95)] and Sajja et al. [[94](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR94)], where sustained learner engagement was shown to depend not just on technology, but also on the broader pedagogical and social environment.

Ng et al. [[78](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR78)] contributed to the field by developing and validating the Artificial Intelligence Literacy Questionnaire, which confirmed literacy as a multidimensional construct encompassing affective, behavioral, cognitive, and ethical components. This work provided a reliable measurement framework for assessing student preparedness in AI-related contexts. Similarly, Sanusi et al. [[97](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR97)] examined the competencies needed for K-12 students in Nigeria to succeed in AI education. Their findings revealed that ethics and human-tool collaboration were the strongest predictors of student comprehension of AI content. Both studies converge on the view that ethics and literacy are not supplementary but central to effective AI education.

Finally, Ghai et al. [[96](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR96)], while primarily focused on annotation and active learning, also emphasized the ethical implications of explanation design. Their findings reinforce Ng et al. [[78](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR78)] and Sanusi et al. [[97](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR97)], suggesting that without careful design, AI tools can unintentionally undermine fairness, cause cognitive strain, or mislead users. Together, these three studies remind us that literacy, ethics, and explainability are tightly interlinked and must be prioritized if AI is to have a sustainable and responsible role in education.

5 Managerial implications
-------------------------

This study uses a bibliometric method to trace how research on artificial intelligence has developed about students’ cognitive learning outcomes. As AI becomes increasingly important in this domain, understanding the trajectory of past and existing studies is crucial for shaping and informing future research efforts.

By conducting a bibliometric performance analysis, this research fulfills its first aim by outlining key statistical insights based on publication data. Analyzing trends in publication volume and identifying the most prominent journals, authors, institutions, and countries offers proper direction for aspiring researchers, academic professionals, and students entering the field. The second objective of this study is addressed through network analysis, which maps the expected relationships among various components. Findings from co-word, citation, and bibliographic coupling analyses reveal the main research themes, influence, and evolving directions within artificial intelligence and students’ cognitive learning outcomes. Co-authorship analysis further uncovers patterns of international collaboration. Researchers have identified key trends in research focus and partnerships that are important for future exploration. Specifically, the network analysis clusters commonly used terms such as students, artificial intelligence, contrastive learning, adversarial machine learning, teaching, federated learning, learning outcome, learning systems, curricula, and e-learning, highlighting the current artificial intelligence issues in students’ cognitive learning outcomes. These themes can serve as a foundation for forming hypotheses, examining correlations, or proposing new research initiatives. Faculty members may use these insights to enrich their teaching and scholarly work. At the same time, students can apply them to design creative projects, enhance their academic experience, and pursue new opportunities in technology-focused careers. Secondly, the results suggest that researchers and institutions working on artificial intelligence and students’ cognitive learning outcomes strengthen the research community by promoting collaboration between established and emerging markets. Broadening the research network and reinforcing the field will help deepen the understanding of AI and student cognitive learning outcomes. Thirdly, the development of AI research in this sector highlights key authors who have made notable academic contributions. Educators can benefit from studying these influential researchers to enhance their current AI-related policies, frameworks, and standards for students’ cognitive learning outcomes within higher education sectors.

6 Limitations and future research directions
--------------------------------------------

Artificial intelligence plays a significant role in shaping students’ cognitive learning outcomes, offering personalized learning experiences, adaptive tutoring, and predictive analytics that enhance engagement, problem-solving, and critical thinking. However, the rapid integration of AI also raises important ethical concerns and demands practical, actionable strategies to ensure its responsible and effective implementation in education.

One of the most pressing ethical considerations is the issue of student data privacy. AI tools rely on large volumes of sensitive data, including learning behaviors, performance records, and in some cases biometric information. Without clear safeguards, this information could be misused or exposed, compromising student autonomy and security. To address these risks, institutions must adopt transparent consent processes, secure data management systems, and policies aligned with international standards such as GDPR (general data protection regulation**)** to protect students’ rights and build trust in AI adoption [[3](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR3)].

Another critical concern relates to algorithmic fairness. If AI models are trained on biased or incomplete datasets, they can perpetuate or even amplify existing inequalities in education. This may result in unfair grading, skewed recommendations, or unequal access to opportunities. To prevent such outcomes, institutions and developers should carry out regular bias audits, integrate diverse datasets, and establish independent oversight mechanisms to ensure AI systems serve all learners equitably [[98](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR98)].

The challenge of potential biases and transparency further complicates AI adoption. Many AI systems operate as “black boxes,” producing decisions without clear explanations. This lack of interpretability can undermine trust and limit the ability of educators and students to question or correct flawed outputs. Emphasizing explainable AI (XAI) is therefore essential, as it promotes transparency, accountability, and trust by allowing users to understand how decisions are made [[99](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR99)].

At the same time, educators must be cautious of student over-reliance on AI tools. While AI enhances efficiency and engagement, excessive dependence may reduce opportunities for self-reflection, independent thinking, and problem-solving. Encouraging digital literacy, critical evaluation of AI tools, and balanced use alongside human instruction can help ensure AI acts as a cognitive enhancer rather than a replacement for student reasoning [[100](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR100)].

Finally, institutional and policy responsibilities are central to ethical AI integration. Educational leaders and policymakers must design governance frameworks that prioritize fairness, accountability, and inclusivity in AI adoption. This requires close collaboration between educators, technologists, and regulators to ensure AI supports education as a human-centered process while upholding ethical values [[101](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR101)].

Alongside these ethical considerations, more actionable recommendations are needed to guide educators and institutions in the practical adoption of AI. Integrating AI literacy modules into curricula can equip students with the skills to critically evaluate AI tools and use them responsibly. Similarly, professional development programs for teachers are essential to build confidence and expertise in using AI for instruction, assessment, and student support. Institutions should also establish clear governance policies addressing data use, transparency, and accountability to strengthen trust among all stakeholders [[102](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR102)].

Educational design should focus on blended learning models where AI supports rather than replaces traditional teaching, ensuring that human interaction and critical inquiry remain at the center of learning. Equitable access to AI must also be prioritized, particularly in underfunded schools and developing regions, through targeted investments in digital infrastructure and resources. Future research should extend beyond short-term performance outcomes and focus on evaluating the long-term effects of AI use on cognitive skills such as independence, critical thinking, and problem-solving [[103](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00865-0#ref-CR103)]. The integration of AI in education holds transformative potential, but its success depends on careful attention to ethics, equity, and practical implementation. By balancing technological innovation with responsible oversight and actionable strategies, educators and policymakers can ensure that AI enhances students’ cognitive learning outcomes while safeguarding fairness, transparency, and inclusivity in educational environments.

This study is subject to several limitations that should be acknowledged. First, a database limitation arises from the exclusive use of Scopus. Although Scopus is widely regarded as a comprehensive database, relying on a single source may have excluded relevant studies indexed elsewhere, thereby narrowing the scope of the analysis. Future research should therefore include multiple databases such as Web of Science, Google Scholar, or ERIC to achieve broader coverage. Second, there is a language restriction, as the study focused only on English-language publications. While this approach facilitated consistency in analysis, it may have overlooked valuable research conducted in other languages, particularly studies from non-English-speaking regions where AI in education is actively developing. Future investigations should include multilingual sources to capture diverse global perspectives. Third, the study faces a methodological limitation due to its reliance on bibliometric and content analysis. Although these methods provide valuable insights into research trends and intellectual structures, they do not capture real-world practices or contextual experiences. To address this gap, future studies should incorporate qualitative methods such as interviews, surveys, or case studies, as well as longitudinal approaches to better understand how AI impacts students’ cognitive learning over time.

Finally, there is a narrow source scope, as books, review articles, and other academic outputs were excluded. This restriction may have limited the theoretical depth and interdisciplinary connections that could enrich the findings. Future research should therefore consider a wider range of sources to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the field.

7 Conclusion
------------

This study employed bibliometric and content analysis to investigate how artificial intelligence (AI) supports students’ cognitive learning outcomes from 2016 to 2025. The results show a sharp rise in AI-related educational research after 2023, driven by digital transformation, the COVID-19 pandemic, and growing international collaboration. Leading institutions such as the University of Hong Kong and Carnegie Mellon University, along with countries including the United States, China, and India, have played central roles in shaping this field.

The analysis highlights that AI tools such as adaptive learning platforms, intelligent tutoring systems, and predictive analytics hold strong potential to enhance students’ critical thinking, problem-solving, and personalized learning experiences. At the same time, the study identifies critical challenges, including concerns about data privacy, algorithmic bias, and the risk of student overreliance. These issues emphasize the need for transparent, ethical, and responsible adoption to ensure that AI complements, rather than replaces, human-centered teaching.

Overall, this research not only maps the intellectual structure of AI and cognitive learning but also provides directions for advancing responsible integration. However, the study is limited by reliance on a single database and English-language publications, which may exclude diverse perspectives. Future research should expand coverage, include multilingual sources, and adopt longitudinal approaches to capture the long-term impact of AI on student cognitive learning outcomes more comprehensively.
