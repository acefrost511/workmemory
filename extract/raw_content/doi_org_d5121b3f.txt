## Abstract
This article examines the challenges primary and secondary teachers face in implementing formative assessment, with a particular focus on the use of digital technologies and the emerging potential of artificial intelligence (AI), including generative AI (GenAI) and agentic AI. Drawing on empirical research and theoretical perspectives, we explore how formative assessment—an established pedagogical practice with significant impact on student learning—has evolved alongside technological developments. We revisit a well-established framework of five key formative assessment strategies, analysing how it has been extended to integrate digital technologies and how this influences the roles of teachers, learners and tools in classroom decision-making. Our central research question asks: \*How can GenAI be integrated into formative assessment practices to enhance student learning while supporting teacher agency and professional judgement?\* We argue that GenAI, when critically and thoughtfully deployed, can create new opportunities for personalised feedback, dynamic learning pathways and the co-construction of knowledge between teachers and students. Moreover, GenAI's ability to support ‘moments of contingency’ enables teachers to respond more effectively to emerging learning needs, thus fostering self-regulation and deeper engagement. However, we stress that AI agency is agency without intelligence. The value of these technologies depends on how they are interpreted and implemented by educators, which requires ongoing reflection, collaboration and theoretical understanding. With deep implications for teacher education programmes, our analysis suggests that teacher quality in this evolving pedagogical landscape should be understood as adaptive, multifaceted and grounded in both technological fluency and sound formative assessment principles, moving beyond the narrow and prescriptive definitions that dominate recent educational policy in England.
### Practitioner notes
What is already known about this topic
\* Formative assessment is well established in education, with strong empirical support showing high impact on learning.
\* Digital technologies have shown more variable and generally moderate impact on learning, despite long-standing interest.
\* Teachers often face challenges implementing formative assessment effectively due to limited training and systemic constraints.
\* Very little is known about how generative AI (GenAI) will influence formative assessment practices, given its novelty and potential to disrupt traditional teaching roles.
What this paper adds
\* This paper examines how formative assessment can be enhanced—and complicated—by digital technologies, especially new forms of AI.
\* A formative assessment framework is reviewed, outlining how GenAI and \*agentic AI\* could support each of its five core strategies.
\* The paper offers a timely analysis of AI's pedagogical potential, portraying it as a dynamic but fallible agent in learning.
Implications for practice and/or policy
\* Teacher education must now include AI literacy alongside formative assessment pedagogy, enabling ethical and informed use of GenAI.
\* Educators should be equipped to evaluate and adapt emerging technologies while protecting student agency and pedagogical intent.
\* Schools and policymakers must foster conditions for early-career teachers to explore and refine AI-supported approaches.
\* Ethical safeguards are needed to address concerns around feedback quality, learner autonomy and dependency on automation.
\* Policy should promote collaborative design of pedagogies that integrate AI responsibly, centring on deep learning and teacher–student relationships.
## INTRODUCTION
Developments in digital technologies have held promise for transforming pedagogy in schools for more than 30 years. While there is good evidence that many beneficial changes in learning and teaching have been achieved through teachers deploying digital technologies (see, eg, Dahl-Leonard et al., [2024](#bjet70051-bib-0014); Zou et al., [2025](#bjet70051-bib-0052) for a review), research continues to find that transformation of pedagogy is complex and elusive (Cox & Webb, [2004](#bjet70051-bib-0013); Timotheou et al., [2023](#bjet70051-bib-0037)). Furthermore, making good use of technologies continues to be challenging for teachers, especially for beginning teachers (Clark-Wilson et al., [2020](#bjet70051-bib-0011); Colognesi & Hanin, [2024](#bjet70051-bib-0012); Cox & Webb, [2004](#bjet70051-bib-0013); Webb, [2014](#bjet70051-bib-0041)). In parallel with developments in the use of digital technologies for learning, the publication of Black and Wiliam's ([1998](#bjet70051-bib-0005)) review of research revealed high effect sizes for formative assessment interventions from a number of meta-analyses. Subsequently, the importance of formative assessment for learning has become established globally.
Evidence from a more recent major analysis of meta-analyses (Hattie, [2009](#bjet70051-bib-0023)) found that the effect sizes for interventions based on formative assessment were high, whereas interventions based on the use of digital technologies had more varied outcomes and generally no more than medium effect sizes. In recent years, however, opportunities for AI to support learning have been accelerating particularly with the development of Generative AI (GenAI). GenAI has transformed the AI landscape considerably by introducing models capable of generating human-like text, images and other media based on probabilistic patterns derived from vast datasets.
Before GenAI came about, Black and Wiliam ([2009](#bjet70051-bib-0006)) and Leahy et al. ([2005](#bjet70051-bib-0028)) introduced a framework outlining five central formative assessment strategies, which was later expanded by Webb ([2014](#bjet70051-bib-0041)) to include various digital technologies. As we will show later, this extended framework offers a structure for clarifying the roles of primary and secondary teachers, learners and technological tools. The framework takes into account the settings within which learning takes place, and the histories of those involved but focuses on ‘moments of contingency’ which provide opportunities for pedagogical decisions that better support learning processes in the classroom (Wiliam, [2014](#bjet70051-bib-0046)).
In this context, our first aim in this article is to review the theoretical tenets of formative assessment and the impact and challenges of using it in schools prior to the popularisation of GenAI tools in education. Second, we use the scholarship of formative assessment to theorise how GenAI could transform the way we conceptualise and do formative assessment in compulsory education. We aim to examine how GenAI could take on various roles within this framework and how these interactions could affect student-to-student and student-to-teacher relationships and influence both student and teacher agency. Thus, our central research question asks: \*How can GenAI be integrated into formative assessment practices to enhance student learning while supporting teacher agency and professional judgement?\*
In the sections below, we first review the significance of formative assessment in education. Then, we analyse and characterise the challenges for teachers in formative assessment, especially those involved in using digital technologies for enabling formative assessment. We do this by analysing empirical research into teachers' professional practice. We then examine the theory of formative assessment by drawing on earlier work by Black and Wiliam ([2009](#bjet70051-bib-0006)) and other researchers from King's College London, as well as more recent research. Turning to possible future opportunities, we analyse the existing capabilities of AI as well as considering expectations of future developments, including tailoring for specific educational uses, to examine the extent to which AI has the potential to enable and support teachers and learners to use formative assessment.
## THE TRANSFORMATIVE IMPACT OF FORMATIVE ASSESSMENT
Definitions of formative assessment have varied, and there is no generally accepted definition (Black & Wiliam, [2009](#bjet70051-bib-0006); Yan et al., [2021](#bjet70051-bib-0048)). This lack of conceptual clarity was identified by Yan et al.'s ([2021](#bjet70051-bib-0048)) systematic review as one of the key challenges inhibiting teachers' use of formative assessment. In this article, we define formative assessment, as proposed by Black and Wiliam ([2009](#bjet70051-bib-0006)), when they stated that an assessment functions formatively:
> to the extent that evidence about student achievement is elicited, interpreted, and used by teachers, learners, or their peers, to make decisions about the next steps in instruction that are likely to be better, or better founded, than the decisions they would have taken in the absence of the evidence that was elicited. (Black & Wiliam, [2009](#bjet70051-bib-0006) p. 9)
This definition emphasises the assessment's function in providing evidence that enables students to take their next step(s) in learning. Beyond the importance of assessment function, another compelling reason for adopting this definition is that, as Black and Wiliam ([2009](#bjet70051-bib-0006)) argued, we see this as directing attention to the significance of ‘moments of contingency’ in instruction for regulation purposes. These moments are critically important yet challenging for teachers to manage during real-time interactions with students—a challenge that might potentially be addressed by AI-enabled systems, as will be discussed later.
The terms assessment for learning and formative assessment are often used interchangeably; however, while assessment for learning focuses on the purpose for which the assessment is designed, it only becomes truly formative when the evidence is actually used to promote learning (Black et al., [2002](#bjet70051-bib-0003)). Nevertheless, the concept of assessment for learning has proven valuable for teachers when designing assessments, and much research literature employs this terminology. Thus, formative assessment is part of a process and can be considered transformative for pedagogy because, at its core, it fundamentally shifts the traditional paradigm of teaching and learning from a one-way transmission model to an interactive, dynamic process.
Based on their initial review (Black & Wiliam, [1998](#bjet70051-bib-0005)) and subsequent research and professional development initiatives with teachers (Black et al., [2003](#bjet70051-bib-0004); Wiliam et al., [2004](#bjet70051-bib-0047)), feedback, questioning, self-assessment and peer-assessment were identified as the four major formative assessment strategies. By the very nature of those four strategies, central to formative assessment is the dynamic interaction between teachers and students, which generates rich, actionable data on learners' existing knowledge, misconceptions and learning needs. This interaction provides critical insights for educators, allowing them to tailor instructional strategies effectively to meet diverse learner requirements and promote deeper understanding (Black et al., [2003](#bjet70051-bib-0004); Black & Wiliam, [1998](#bjet70051-bib-0005)). Students engaged in formative assessment regularly practice peer-assessment which helps them to develop their self-assessment capabilities, thus acquiring a clearer understanding of their strengths, weaknesses and learning objectives (Black et al., [2003](#bjet70051-bib-0004)). Such engagement not only builds intrinsic motivation and a growth mindset (Dweck, [2000](#bjet70051-bib-0016)) but also promotes deep reflective practices and critical self-awareness, significantly enhancing learning efficacy (Black et al., [2003](#bjet70051-bib-0004)). Strategic questioning methods, including open-ended and probing questions, enable educators to delve deeply into students' understanding, identify misconceptions and guide subsequent teaching strategies (Wiliam et al., [2004](#bjet70051-bib-0047)). Effective feedback, an integral aspect of formative assessment, transcends simple corrections, explicitly outlining the necessary steps students must undertake to bridge their current understanding with desired learning outcomes (Black et al., [2003](#bjet70051-bib-0004); Wiliam, [2007](#bjet70051-bib-0045)). Teachers' decisions about curriculum adjustments, instructional planning and personalised interventions must, therefore, be driven by careful interpretation and application of assessment data, supporting a responsive and adaptive teaching practice (Black et al., [2003](#bjet70051-bib-0004); Boyle & Charles, [2010](#bjet70051-bib-0009)).
Black and Wiliam ([2009](#bjet70051-bib-0006)) identified that the aim of formative assessment to make students the owners of their learning aligns closely with theories of self-regulated learning (SRL). In recent years, many researchers have also examined the interplay between formative assessment and SRL (eg, see Hopfenbeck et al., [2023](#bjet70051-bib-0024) for a review). In summary, formative assessment supports the development of SRL, and self-regulated learners have good peer- and self-assessment skills. In recent years, theories of SRL have advanced rapidly, and SRL is recognised widely as an important aspect of student learning (Prasse et al., [2024](#bjet70051-bib-0033)). In their systematic umbrella review of 27 systematic reviews and four meta-analyses, Prasse et al. identified a range of different theoretical models that recent studies have used. Some of these models go beyond individual SRL to characterising co-regulated learning and socially shared regulation of learning, thus more fully supporting a social constructivist view of learning.
A crucial insight from Black and Wiliam ([2009](#bjet70051-bib-0006)) and Wiliam ([2014](#bjet70051-bib-0046)), as pointed out earlier, is that formative assessment must create and capitalise on moments of contingency. Such moments may occur in real time during classroom interactions and hence require instant decision-making based on whatever data are immediately available. A framework of five key strategies of formative assessment (Black & Wiliam, [2009](#bjet70051-bib-0006); Leahy et al., [2005](#bjet70051-bib-0028)), which was further developed to incorporate the use of a range of digital technologies (Webb, [2014](#bjet70051-bib-0041)) (see Figure [1](#bjet70051-fig-0001)), provides a structure for elucidating the roles of learners, teachers and technologies. This framework takes into account the settings within which learning takes place and the histories of those involved but focuses on ‘moments of contingency’ (Wiliam, [2014](#bjet70051-bib-0046)). The framework identifies how formative assessment practices reflect reciprocal teaching, and the activation of students as instructional resources and as owners of their own learning. In this model, teachers clarify learning intentions and success criteria, engineer discussions that elicit evidence of understanding, provide feedback that moves learning forward and support peer- and self-assessment. These strategies are intended not just to measure progress but also to shape it, forming a contingent and dynamic process whereby teachers adapt instruction based on student responses. This framework also has the advantage that it is context and content free and can be applied to any learning situation face to face, online or blended. Thus, the framework could be applied from early years through to higher education. Here, we are focusing on technology use in compulsory education, both primary and secondary (aged 5–16). Subsequently, in the section ‘A Framework for Integrating AI into Formative Assessment’, we discuss AI integration within the five strategies shown in Figure [1](#bjet70051-fig-0001).

However, despite the evidence that formative assessment can be effective for improving learning, formative assessment practices have continued to prove difficult for many teachers to implement successfully with or without technologies (Børte et al., [2023](#bjet70051-bib-0008); Boyle & Charles, [2010](#bjet70051-bib-0009)). Reviews also show that digital technologies can be deployed in various ways for formative assessment (Webb et al., [2018](#bjet70051-bib-0044)), but these pedagogical decisions also present challenges for teachers (Børte et al., [2023](#bjet70051-bib-0008); Webb et al., [2018](#bjet70051-bib-0044)). Research and development have now increased attention on whether the addition of AI to the technological landscape can assist in enabling formative assessment. Hopes for the transformation of learning through the use of digital technologies also emphasised interaction and learner-centred pedagogies (Webb & Cox, [2004](#bjet70051-bib-0053)). Perhaps, therefore, focusing digital technology use on formative assessment could harness the potential of both elements.
## SUPPORTING TEACHERS TO IMPLEMENT FORMATIVE ASSESSMENT SUCCESSFULLY
Despite the substantial benefits, implementing formative assessment effectively encounters notable challenges, both personal and contextual, as indicated by Yan et al.'s ([2021](#bjet70051-bib-0048)) systematic review of 52 studies of teachers' formative assessment practices. Many educators lack adequate pedagogical training and professional support, restricting the widespread adoption and sustained practice of formative assessment methods (Black, [2015](#bjet70051-bib-0002); Black & Wiliam, [1998](#bjet70051-bib-0005); Børte et al., [2023](#bjet70051-bib-0008); Yan et al., [2021](#bjet70051-bib-0048)). As Hopfenbeck et al. ([2023](#bjet70051-bib-0024)) have argued, teachers struggle to manage formative assessment because they must ascertain how each student is learning and provide appropriate support accordingly. This requires teachers to possess a range of knowledge and skills and to employ a variety of formative assessment approaches, collecting data about students' learning through ongoing interaction among peers and between students and the teacher. Moreover, teachers frequently face pressures from rigid curricular frameworks and standardised assessment regimes, leading to superficial implementation or oversimplified strategies rather than deep, formative practices (Boyle & Charles, [2010](#bjet70051-bib-0009)). Nevertheless, a highly supportive management and policy framework may not suffice for optimum use of formative assessment.
As an example, a professional development project in the State of Jersey, where the self-governing administration had eliminated the standardised assessments imposed in England and teachers were receiving extensive professional development, significant classroom challenges remained (Webb & Jones, [2009](#bjet70051-bib-0043)). Implementing robust formative assessment practices necessitated more than merely employing techniques and strategies; rather, it demanded a fundamental cultural transformation involving alterations in the beliefs and roles of both teachers and students. Learners needed not only to assume responsibility for their own learning but also to assist their peers and acknowledge that willingness to attempt difficult challenges and hence to risk making errors constitutes an essential component of the learning process. Teachers conceptualised formative assessment as providing a learning philosophy centred on students taking ownership of their learning by developing awareness of both what and how they were learning through reciprocal feedback processes. This transformation was facilitated by the formative assessment philosophy, various mediating artefacts and the sharing and evaluation of experiences, including both achievements and setbacks. In this supportive climate and culture, many teachers were successful, and many students developed well as autonomous learners (Webb & Jones, [2009](#bjet70051-bib-0043)). Success ultimately depended upon teachers' determination, pedagogical expertise and their judicious selection of mediating artefacts (Webb & Jones, [2009](#bjet70051-bib-0043)).
## NEW POSSIBILITIES AND CHALLENGES TO TRANSFORMING FORMATIVE ASSESSMENT SUPPORTED BY GENAI AND AGENTIC AI
Recent developments in GenAI have significantly expanded opportunities for AI-supported learning. Large language models (LLMs), trained on extensive text corpora to process and generate human language, exemplify this advancement. A group of GPTs (generative pre-trained transformers), developed by OpenAI, represent a prominent family within this paradigm, utilising the transformer architecture introduced by Google researchers in 2017 (Vaswani et al., [2017](#bjet70051-bib-0039)). These models predict subsequent words or phrases based on contextual patterns learnt from training data, employing sophisticated attention mechanisms through pretraining and fine-tuning processes, though they lack inherent access to verifiable factual information or external validation mechanisms. While previous chatbots existed, ChatGPT, launched in November 2022, was the first GPT-based conversational AI to achieve mainstream adoption catalysing widespread public engagement with GenAI. AI had been integrated into education over many years, playing an increasingly significant role through developments such as bespoke learning platforms, adaptive assessment tools, predictive analytics and conversational agents (Akgun & Greenhow, [2022](#bjet70051-bib-0001); Webb et al., [2021](#bjet70051-bib-0042)). Yet, the capacity of GenAI to produce human-like text responses to a large variety of requests constituted a significant change. Following the release of ChatGPT, a number of similar and competing families of LLMs have been developed using GPT and other architectures, for example, Claude (Anthropic), LlaMA (Meta), DeepSeek (China) and Gemini (Google) (see Ferrag et al., [2025](#bjet70051-bib-0018) for an overview).
One emerging concept is ‘agentic AI,’ where AI functions as a system of autonomous agents with the ability to make judgements and decisions based on new data (Sapkota et al., [2025](#bjet70051-bib-0035)). Unlike traditional AI agents that operate individually, agentic AI systems consist of multiple specialised agents that collaborate through structured communication, shared memory and dynamic role assignment to achieve complex objectives.
These systems exhibit significantly greater autonomy and adaptability compared to traditional AI agents, which were designed for specific tasks based on predefined rules or training data. While traditional AI agents focus on handling narrow, well-defined tasks within specific domains, agentic AI coordinates multiple agents to work towards broader objectives by decomposing complex goals into subtasks, planning and executing multi-step tasks, learning from interactions and adjusting its approach in real time. Modern agentic AIs use large LLMs and advanced reasoning capabilities to analyse complex situations, develop strategies and execute actions to achieve specific objectives. While agentic AI typically incorporates LLMs and therefore shares some of the accuracy limitations of GenAI, it represents a fundamental shift in how AI systems operate by emphasising goal-oriented behaviour and adaptive decision-making rather than simply following predefined rules. Agentic AI is increasingly used across business and industry and is gaining traction in education through intelligent tutoring systems and adaptive learning environments (Kamalov et al., [2025](#bjet70051-bib-0026)). In these complex settings, explainability and transparency remain significant challenges, along with concerns around bias and fairness.
Thus, in recent years, GenAI development has progressed rapidly beyond language models based only on transformer architecture to incorporate retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems and multi-agent frameworks, enabling AI systems to access real-time information, collaborate dynamically and tackle complex tasks through coordinated autonomous decision-making (Ferrag et al., [2025](#bjet70051-bib-0018)).
These technologies have the potential to disrupt many traditional educational processes but also present many opportunities for learning and teaching. There are several tensions in the use of GenAI in assessment. First, the statistical nature of prediction in LLMs, combined with the absence of genuine world understanding, means that the issue of inaccuracies and producing fluent and coherent outputs that are factually incorrect or entirely fabricated, sometimes referred to as ‘hallucinations’, may not be fully resolved in the near future. Intensive efforts are being made to fine-tune or edit LLMs to counteract their inherent propensity for inaccuracies, particularly in specific domains; however, the fundamental stochastic nature of these models means that, while benefitting from GenAI, users should always question the validity of the information LLMs provide (Zhang et al., [2024](#bjet70051-bib-0051)).
As Sapkota et al. ([2025](#bjet70051-bib-0035)) have explained, if education adopts agentic AI systems, these would present even greater challenges regarding trust and explainability, due to their distributed, multi-agent architecture that fundamentally complicates accountability and traceability. While interpreting the behaviour of a single large language model-powered agent is already non-trivial, this complexity is substantially multiplied when multiple agents interact asynchronously, as each agent may possess its own memory, task objective and reasoning path, resulting in ‘compounded opacity where tracing the causal chain of a final decision or failure becomes exceedingly difficult’ (Sapkota et al., [2025](#bjet70051-bib-0035), p. 25). The distributed nature of these systems creates accountability gaps where responsibility for errors or unintended consequences becomes ambiguous, particularly problematic when multiple agents collaborate to produce outcomes. Furthermore, Sapkota et al. ([2025](#bjet70051-bib-0035)) argue that agentic AI systems are susceptible to emergent behaviours—complex system-level phenomena arising from agent interactions that can yield unintended outcomes diverging from task objectives or generating potentially harmful actions, particularly in high-stakes domains. As the number of agents and complexity of their interactions increase, so too does the likelihood of system instability.
These rapid developments in AI systems together with their widespread deployment across business, society and education mean that AI literacy has become an essential requirement for both students and teachers. Analysis of 124 pre- and post-GenAI studies revealed that AI literacy conceptualisations typically address three dimensions: technical detail, tool-based applications and sociocultural perspectives (Gu & Ericson, [2025](#bjet70051-bib-0021)). Building on contemporary research and policy frameworks, AI literacy is considered to be the integrated set of knowledge, skills and attitudes that enables individuals to understand how AI works and affects society, use AI tools responsibly and effectively, critically evaluate AI outputs and limitations, communicate or collaborate with AI systems, and—at advanced levels—design or adapt AI solutions (Chiu et al., [2024](#bjet70051-bib-0010); Long & Magerko, [2020](#bjet70051-bib-0029); OECD & European Commission, [2025](#bjet70051-bib-0032)). Beyond general understanding and evaluation of AI outputs, AI literacy should encompass practical competencies in \*prompt engineering\*, that is, crafting and iterating prompts purposefully to a GenAI to achieve pedagogical aims (Gu & Ericson, [2025](#bjet70051-bib-0021)). Such skills underpin responsible and effective classroom use of GenAI, linking technical fluency with pedagogical intent.
## OPPORTUNITIES AND TENSIONS IN INTRODUCING AI INTO FORMATIVE ASSESSMENT
Recent advances in AI, discussed above, introduce a potential conceptual shift in how technology operates within formative assessment. Rather than functioning solely as an instrument or tool as was the case with the technologies illustrated in Figure [1](#bjet70051-fig-0001), AI has the potential to act as an autonomous agent. As the philosopher Floridi ([2023](#bjet70051-bib-0019)) has argued, AI systems demonstrate ‘agency without intelligence’—the capacity to act in the world without possessing human-like intelligence. Floridi characterised AI agency through three elements: interaction with the environment (processing inputs and producing influential outputs), learning from feedback (adjusting behaviour to improve performance) and the ability to modify internal rules (adapting to new contexts). This framework positions AI systems in a distinctive space between simple natural forces and human intelligence. Floridi contends that this behaviour constitutes an unprecedented form of agency whereby systems can function efficaciously and enhance their performance without comprehension or intentionality.
Adaptive feedback mechanisms have been used for some years in intelligent tutoring systems which can adapt teaching and guide and assess learners, and such systems have increasingly used machine learning (Mousavinasab et al., [2021](#bjet70051-bib-0031)). While adaptive learning systems are not yet widely used in schools, they have been used in higher education for some years. According to a systematic literature review of 63 articles about the development and use of adaptive learning systems in higher education (Gligorea et al., [2023](#bjet70051-bib-0020)), such systems have increasingly been using a range of different AI-based techniques including machine learning. Although GenAI tends to be the most talked about and evident form of AI in education systems, most companies developing AI systems for education are actually now using agentic AI incorporating multi-AI systems. In recent AI-enhanced educational systems, such as Squirrel AI or Khanmigo (Khan Academy), different types of AI are increasingly working together. GenAI is used on the front end, where students interact with the system; for example, it generates feedback, explains concepts or simulates a tutor's dialogue. Behind the scenes, other AI techniques like discriminative AI (which makes predictions and classifications), knowledge tracing (which tracks what a learner knows) and reinforcement learning (which adapts content based on performance) form the system's adaptive engine. This hybrid model allows educational platforms to deliver personalised instruction, monitor learner progress in real time, scale up content creation and maintain greater accuracy in tracking and supporting student learning than earlier and more traditional systems (Dutta et al., [2024](#bjet70051-bib-0015)). These hybrid systems support core functions such as predictive analytics, skill tracking, adaptive sequencing of learning materials and ‘intelligent interfaces’ that respond to each learner's needs. Researchers are now developing specialised automated feedback systems by training LLMs for specific educational contexts.
Given the developments outlined above, AI now has the capacity to support formative assessment by facilitating dialogic engagement with students, co-constructing meaning, eliciting reflection, emulating feedback mechanisms, fostering the development of self-regulation and metacognitive strategies and delivering dynamic, responsive commentary to students' enquiries and submissions. These functionalities are available to be developed and adapted by researchers and educators by building prompts with LLMs. Therefore, studies of learning with LLMs are proliferating especially in higher education. For example, Wang and Fan ([2025](#bjet70051-bib-0040)) conducted a meta-analysis on 51 experimental studies that used ChatGPT to support learning in various ways. While that meta-analysis did show positive effects of students using ChatGPT, the value of using an LLM depended on many different factors that are only beginning to be investigated (Wang & Fan, [2025](#bjet70051-bib-0040)) For example, an important issue examined by Zhang et al. ([2025](#bjet70051-bib-0049)) is how students respond to feedback generated solely by AI or in conjunction with AI compared with tutor feedback.
AI interacts with all elements of the learning system, potentially acting as both a mediating artefact and a new kind of participant in the activity. When planning and implementing formative assessment processes, AI could significantly contribute to transforming teaching and learning practices.
However, these capabilities prompt critical questions regarding the appropriate scope for AI agency within educational settings, particularly classroom environments. Finding out the optimum balance between teacher agency, student agency and AI agency is context dependent and a new practice and research challenge. AI-based feedback systems are expected to improve as LLMs advance through better training data and innovative approaches. However, AI systems still face several challenges including accuracy issues, a tendency to reveal answers, poor suggestions, limited diagnostic value and an inappropriately positive tone (Scarlatos et al., [2024](#bjet70051-bib-0036)). One promising direction is the development of multi-agent systems (Guo et al., [2024](#bjet70051-bib-0022)), as discussed earlier. This approach represents a significant advancement over traditional rule-based AI agents, which were limited by their narrower capabilities and inability to adapt to complex learning contexts. These newer agentic systems can dynamically coordinate their specialised functions while responding to the unique needs of individual learners.
Whereas the early development of AI in education focused on replicating the role of the teacher and developing ‘intelligent tutoring systems’ which acted as individual tutors, more recent developments in AI have opened up opportunities for AI developers to consider a range of different pedagogical approaches including those in which systems can be envisaged as human-AI hybrids such that teachers and learners can be in control of the pedagogical processes (Molenaar, [2022](#bjet70051-bib-0030)). Thus, going forward, AI systems development has the potential for supporting a range of different pedagogical approaches. At the same time, the emergence of GenAI in the form of accessible applications such as ChatGPT has democratised access to personalised feedback through LLMs. Recent surveys indicate that higher education students increasingly use GenAI for explanations and support during independent study (see eg, ). In these scenarios, students must independently evaluate the accuracy and usefulness of AI-generated advice. Whether all students, especially younger students, can benefit from use of such systems without specific customisation to constrain the data in use and the responses given remains an open question.
Nevertheless, as extensively documented in the academic discourse following the widespread accessibility of GenAI technologies, significant concerns about students' learning have emerged. These primarily relate to learners potentially employing such technologies merely to accomplish product-oriented objectives rather than to genuinely support their cognitive development. Furthermore, there exists the risk that students might develop dependence on these technological tools, consequently failing to cultivate their own intrinsic capabilities. These considerations present a pedagogical tension that a focus on the development of ‘ownership of their own learning’, as indicated by formative assessment theory and practice, could potentially help to alleviate. Therefore, developing AI literacy among students in compulsory education should be prioritised to enable them to utilise the expanding array of AI-based tools to enhance their learning processes. One particularly important element of AI literacy is to enable learners, in taking responsibility for their own learning, to critically evaluate GenAI output and to recognise that initial struggle can be productive for deeper learning. Kapur's ([2016](#bjet70051-bib-0027)) productive versus unproductive successes and failures framework helps determine when students should attempt tasks independently before receiving AI assistance, avoiding the illusion of learning that immediate AI support might create. Kapur ([2016](#bjet70051-bib-0027)) identified that failure can be productive for learning whereas success can be unproductive.
Ultimately, realising the full potential of digital technologies within formative assessment necessitates both digital and AI literacy, comprehensive professional training, careful pedagogical planning and iterative refinement of assessment practices. Ensuring that digital solutions meaningfully enhance rather than merely digitise existing practices requires continuous innovation, robust frameworks and sustained educational support.
## A FRAMEWORK FOR INTEGRATING AI INTO FORMATIVE ASSESSMENT
Building on the framework shown in Figure [1](#bjet70051-fig-0001), we can see that all of these five strategies could be supported by AI. However, the extent to which AI should be involved and in what role is an important consideration affecting the need for empowering both learners and teachers and making informed decisions about the extent to which AI should be permitted to have agency. Such decisions are likely to be dependent on the context and pedagogical aims. Fundamentally, it is an issue of empowerment and responsibility. While AI systems have agency (Floridi, [2023](#bjet70051-bib-0019)) and are likely to be increasingly capable of supporting learning, essential human elements of teaching and learning, including moral responsibility, contextual judgement and authentic cognitive development, cannot and should not be delegated to AI. For younger learners, responsibility rests with the teacher, but older learners need to be empowered to take responsibility for their own learning and to develop AI literacy to ensure that they use AI to support deep learning.
The adaptive learning systems, outlined above, have been designed typically to work as tutors for individuals rather than to facilitate collaborative learning in group contexts (Dutta et al., [2024](#bjet70051-bib-0015)). Private tutoring has gained significant traction globally, although its efficacy depends upon various factors and does not invariably yield positive outcomes (Zhang & Liu, [2022](#bjet70051-bib-0050)). Nevertheless, AI-enabled adaptive learning systems, exemplified by systems developed by Squirrel AI, a Chinese and American-based organisation, are increasingly adopted for personal use.
When examining how such systems might correspond to the framework illustrated in Figure [1](#bjet70051-fig-0001), we observe that they could potentially supplant the teacher's role in Strategies 1, 2 and 3. Educators would generally regard such a system as possessing limited pedagogical value, perhaps being most suitable for individualised practice and consolidation of specific concepts and competencies. For instance, adaptive systems have been proliferating within the domain of language acquisition, and educators might recommend that pupils practice their English pronunciation using one of the numerous pronunciation applications that support such endeavours while conforming to some social constructivist principles (Fang & Webb, [2023](#bjet70051-bib-0017)). As these adaptive systems evolve to demonstrate enhanced capabilities across a broader spectrum of domains, it is reasonable to anticipate an expansion of contexts in which such individualised practice might be deemed pedagogically appropriate.
A hybrid human/AI formative assessment approach might involve AI systems operating under teacher and/or student direction, assisting with particular tasks or assuming specific roles within the strategies of the formative assessment framework. Other roles would remain with teachers and students engaged as peers in collaborative learning environments. Such approaches would more efficiently preserve and enhance teacher and student agency. An obvious and useful role for AI would be to provide suggestions to teachers and/or students about which technological tools to use to support their formative assessment strategies in particular contexts. Thus, the problems previously experienced by teachers in utilising software (Webb, [2014](#bjet70051-bib-0041)) could be overcome by advice from an LLM.
An AI could also take on specific roles in relation to each strategy of formative assessment in the framework in Figure [1](#bjet70051-fig-0001), as follows:
\* Strategy 1 is typically controlled by the teacher implementing curriculum expectations. Here, there is a potential role for a GenAI Chatbot in clarifying learning intentions at the behest of a student.
\* Strategy 2 is a task that has been much touted as a role for GenAI owing to its capability to adapt tasks in order to differentiate and thus provide personalised support. Studies to demonstrate this personalisation are in their early stages but one small study in primary schools (Jauhiainen & Garagorry Guerra, [2024](#bjet70051-bib-0025)) suggested that not only could ChatGPT 3.5 adapt materials to present learning opportunities at different knowledge levels based on test data from students but also further adapt during the lesson as more test data were collected. Thus, the GenAI system was engineering an activity that collected data and provided feedback and further instruction in real time.
\* Strategy 3. The findings from the study by Jauhiainen and Garagorry Guerra ([2024](#bjet70051-bib-0025)) suggested that the learners were generally positive about their experiences. However, this example used only text and graphics for individual students to study so does not represent the rich learning environments suggested by ‘engineering effective discussions and other learning tasks’ (see Figure [1](#bjet70051-fig-0001)).
\* Strategy 4 is backed by extensive classroom research indicating that peer interaction, particularly peer feedback, enhances students' ability to self-assess (Black et al., [2003](#bjet70051-bib-0004)). It is entirely feasible to train an AI, including a fairly simple GPT-based system to take on the role of peer in a particular learning situation. In fact, one approach might be to encourage students to view their interactions with GenAI tools like ChatGPT as peer collaborations. This perspective would prompt students to consider AI suggestions thoughtfully while maintaining awareness that such systems may make errors requiring verification and critical evaluation. Although potentially valuable, these AI systems would have inherent limitations compared to authentic peer feedback, with effectiveness varying by educational context. A more pedagogically appropriate approach, albeit more challenging to implement, would involve AI assuming the role of a peer within group collaborative learning environments. This would combine the benefits of student-to-student collaboration with the advantages of AI-supported learning.
\* Strategy 5, activating students as owners of their own learning, is complex and students are likely to achieve this ownership only after substantial experience of the other four strategies together with encouragement to take responsibility. Self-assessment is important and that may be developed through peer assessment. However, in addition, students need to be motivated and empowered to believe that they can own their own learning. Developing self-regulated learning is closely related to this process but how this ‘activation’ can be achieved in practice is likely to be very dependent on the context and subject matter. Therefore, designing an AI to support this strategy is also challenging but potentially useful.
A crucial mediating skill across all five formative assessment strategies is the ability to formulate and refine effective prompts. Mastery of prompting directly influences the intent, precision and reliability of AI-generated feedback (Qian, [2025](#bjet70051-bib-0034)), thereby shaping the quality of evidence elicited, tasks engineered and feedback produced. Developing students' and teachers' ability to design, evaluate and iteratively improve prompts would support metacognitive monitoring and self-regulation. Within teacher education, explicit attention to prompt design would enable teachers to engage critically with AI-mediated formative assessment, recognising how the phrasing of prompts affects learning dialogues, peer interactions and the ownership of learning.
‘Moments of contingency’ are integral to the formative assessment process in the framework and are critical points when teachers make real-time assessments to guide a student's next learning step. Teachers struggle to create these valuable moments when managing large groups of students. AI systems can potentially enhance and multiply these opportunities under teacher and learner direction. Modern agentic AI can adapt to individual learner responses, identify misconceptions and offer personalised guidance at scale. However, given AI's architectural limitations, reliability challenges, inherent biases and the paramount importance of empowering both teachers and students, we need careful analysis to determine the appropriate balance of human and AI roles in each learning situation.
To what extent should AI function independently as an autonomous agent, making decisions about learner progression and feedback, versus serving as a supportive tool under teacher or student control? This question requires thoughtful planning and consideration of multiple factors: the subject domain, assessment purpose and the specific capabilities of the AI system being employed. Just as with previous developments in digital technologies, it is not these technologies themselves that determine their benefits for learning but how they are deployed by learners and teachers. The implementation context matters significantly—considering key factors such as the age, academic ability and AI literacy of the students, teachers will need to decide to what extent students can take on the management and critical evaluation of advice and feedback from an AI system. Teachers must also consider whether the AI system can provide appropriate monitoring information to help teachers understand how their students' learning is progressing while maintaining student agency and ownership over their learning journey. This includes evaluating how AI-generated data might complement rather than replace teachers' professional judgement and how such systems might support rather than undermine students' development of metacognitive skills and self-regulated learning strategies.
Our analysis indicates that teacher quality in this evolving pedagogical landscape must be multifaceted and adaptive, as shown in research by Towers et al. ([2023](#bjet70051-bib-0038)) on beginning teachers during the pandemic, rather than confined to the narrow, prescriptive definitions embedded in England's \*Teachers' Standards\* and related policy frameworks (Towers et al., [2023](#bjet70051-bib-0038)). Towers and colleagues highlight how policy constructs teacher quality as a ‘problem’ addressed through regulation and performative accountability, reducing professionalism to measurable behaviours. In contrast, teacher educators and school leaders emphasise a broader conception centred on resilience, inclusivity, reflection and moral purpose—qualities that became particularly visible during the COVID-19 crisis. This tension underscores the need to reconceptualise teacher quality as dynamic, context-responsive and ethically grounded rather than a fixed set of standards.
Within the context of rapidly developing AI possibilities in education, both experienced and beginning teachers must be supported to collaboratively and creatively explore emerging learning opportunities while critically evaluating outcomes. Both learners and teachers require a comprehensive understanding of these technologies, including the nature of their underlying technical functionality, their educational potential, limitations and associated risks. Teachers need to develop proficiency in (1) using these applications; (2) conducting a predictive evaluation of the software using their understanding of theoretical frameworks and pedagogical knowledge (Fang & Webb, [2023](#bjet70051-bib-0017)); and (3) exploring, designing and implementing pedagogical practices with AI-enabled software to enhance learning processes.
While experienced teachers typically possess the pedagogical content knowledge (PCK) needed to evaluate these new opportunities, beginning teachers need to develop both traditional PCK and understanding of AI-enabled functionalities. The GenAI-based systems that support teachers in planning lessons pose a risk for beginning teachers who might believe that they can bypass the professional knowledge development needed and rely on AI systems to plan their lessons for them. Thus, high-quality teacher education which prioritises critical thinking and development of PCK remains crucial.
## CONCLUSIONS AND DIRECTIONS TOWARDS AI-ENHANCED FORMATIVE ASSESSMENT
Our aim in this analysis was to examine whether and how new technologies, including AI, could support formative assessment. To this end, we used a framework, based on one developed previously (Black & Wiliam, [2018](#bjet70051-bib-0007); Leahy et al., [2005](#bjet70051-bib-0028); Webb, [2014](#bjet70051-bib-0041)), which enabled analysis of how digital technologies including AI might support five key formative assessment strategies: clarifying learning intentions, engineering effective discussions, providing feedback, activating students as instructional resources for one another and activating students as owners of their learning. This framework forefronts ‘moments of contingency’ that provide opportunities to regulate learning. Our analysis showed that AI might support each of the five key formative assessment strategies as a tool or as an agentic system capable of autonomous decision-making, adaptive responses and goal-oriented behaviour. We conceptualised AI agency as ‘agency without intelligence’—the capacity to act in the world without possessing human-like intelligence (Floridi, [2023](#bjet70051-bib-0019)). We argued that AI agency risks undermining teacher and student agency. Therefore, considerations of AI's role should prioritise the need to empower both learners and teachers. Pedagogical approaches might involve AI systems operating under teacher and/or student direction, assuming specific roles within formative assessment while preserving teacher and student agency. As GenAI tools become embedded in formative assessment, prompt engineering should be viewed as a pedagogical skill, integral to both AI literacy and formative assessment pedagogy. Embedding these practices in teacher education will strengthen teachers' capacity to scaffold students' self-regulated learning with AI.
We advocate for supporting new teachers to collaboratively explore emerging learning opportunities alongside their development of PCK while critically evaluating outcomes. Both learners and teachers require comprehensive understanding of AI technologies, including their potential, limitations and associated risks. We envisage that as learners develop to take ownership of their own learning, with good AI literacy and capabilities with the technology, they will be able to work with their peers and with AI in partnership. Understanding AI's limitations, they will be able to engage in formative assessment by asking questions, seeking advice, obtaining feedback and evaluating the AI's responses and thus take their learning forward. Teachers, with their understanding of pedagogy and the learners, will be able to work with developers to identify and build on new developments in technology including AI to truly transform learning.
Looking ahead, we anticipate several important developments in the integration of AI with formative assessment practices. The first is the use of sophisticated multi-agent AI systems specifically designed for educational contexts, where different AI agents specialise in various specific aspects of formative assessment, from generating questions to providing feedback tailored to individual learning pathways. It is also likely that real-time analytics could enhance teachers' ability to identify and capitalise on ‘moments of contingency’ by processing complex classroom data instantaneously and suggesting tailored interventions. For example, an AI-enabled system could identify important student misconceptions during their interactions with the technology. Thus, while students are working with the AI system, it might detect a significant misunderstanding and alert the teacher. The teacher can then approach the group, engage them in a discussion to highlight the misconception and guide them in overcoming it. In some cases, the teacher may choose to empower the AI system to directly support students in addressing the identified misconception. However, it is crucial that the teacher maintains oversight and makes deliberate decisions about which aspects of learning should be delegated to the AI and where the teacher's direct intervention is most appropriate. The teacher's professional judgement is essential in managing the situation and determining the optimal balance between AI support and human instruction based on the specific learning context and student needs.
By strategically utilising the AI system's capabilities while retaining their central role in the learning process, teachers can harness the benefits of AI-enhanced formative assessment to identify and address student misconceptions in a targeted and timely manner. This approach allows for a productive partnership between human and AI, with the teacher maintaining ultimate control over the learning experience.
One limitation of our analysis is that it has focused on compulsory education in situations such as in England where class sizes are manageable and teacher professional development is considered a priority. In different contexts, where, for example, there is a shortage of teachers, or too large a number of students per classroom, the type of partnership between teacher and AI may vary significantly and encounter additional challenges. Nevertheless, across all educational contexts, there are some key expectations regarding formative assessment and AI use. Student-directed AI tools may evolve to support self-regulated learning more effectively, helping learners track their progress, identify their own learning needs and select appropriate resources. Ethical frameworks specific to AI use in formative assessment will need development, addressing concerns about agency, data privacy, bias and the changing nature of teacher–student relationships. All of those uses of AI make the development of AI literacy by teachers and students essential. Teacher education programmes will need to focus not only on technical understanding but also on the pedagogical integration of technologies including AI within formative assessment. As these technologies continue to evolve, maintaining a focus on the core principles of effective formative assessment will be crucial. The potential benefits will depend not on the technologies themselves, but on how thoughtfully teachers and learners deploy them within pedagogically sound frameworks that preserve human agency and promote learning.
