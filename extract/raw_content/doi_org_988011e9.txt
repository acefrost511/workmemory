## Abstract
This study examines the effects of generative artificial intelligence (GAI)-enhanced pedagogical agents in the metaverse (GPAiM) on elementary school students' conceptual understanding of traditional festival customs and on the students' cognitive engagement patterns. The participants included 116 students from three intact classes. These classes were randomly assigned to two experimental groups (with 2D-GPAiM and 3D-GPAiM, respectively) and one control group (without GPAiM but with a real-person teacher). All the participants learned in the metaverse, and students in different groups were allowed to interact with 2D-GPAiM, 3D-GPAiM, and the real-person teacher during their learning, respectively. This study was conducted under a three-week AI literacy project with the learning topic of traditional festival customs. The results showed that the experimental groups (both 2D-GPAiM and 3D-GPAiM) had a positive impact on the students' conceptual understanding of traditional festivals, while the control group did not. More importantly, the 2D-GPAiM group showed a significantly positive difference in the participants’ conceptual understanding compared with the control group. In addition, regarding cognitive engagement, the 2D-GPAiM group showed a \_highly interactive, low-fluctuating, and high-level cognitive engagement\_ pattern; The 3D-GPAiM group demonstrated a \_highly interactive, highly fluctuating, medium-level cognitive engagement\_ pattern, while the control group exhibited a \_low-interactive, low-fluctuating, low-level cognitive engagement pattern\_. These findings provide valuable insights into future GAI-assisted pedagogical designs.
## Introduction
The \_Convention for the Safeguarding of the Intangible Cultural Heritage\_, released by UNESCO (Kurin, 2004), emphasizes the importance of protecting and transmitting intangible cultural heritage from national and international perspectives. One of the most important aspects of protection and transmission pertains to traditional festival customs (Smith & Akagawa, 2018). The sustainable development of traditional festival customs plays a significant role in national tourism (Suntikul, 2018), social interconnection (Akintan, 2013), and regional economic development (Apostolakis & Viskadouraki, 2017). However, studies have shown that interest in some traditional festivals is gradually fading among school students (Chen & Tao, 2017; LearnHub, 2024), which may lead to a lack of strong willingness to spontaneously promote traditional festival customs. Therefore, scholars have suggested that the learning of traditional festivals can adapt to the trend of technologies and make good use of their potential benefits (Chen, 2024, Chen, 2024; Kye et al., 2021).
Nowadays, advancements in technology have helped promote traditional festivals learning. For example, virtual reality technology was used in Spain during the Covid-19 pandemic for local festival management, offering new ideas for employing technology to celebrate traditional festivals (Estanyol, 2022). Researchers have indicated that virtual environments can authentically recapture the era and setting of traditional festivals, offering immersive experiences that transcend physical limitations (Anthes et al., 2016). However, delivering personalized guidance and immediate feedback to learners in virtual settings remains a challenge. Under these circumstances, learners with limited background knowledge of traditional festival customs may find their study efficiency compromised (Serral et al., 2016). According to previous literature, pedagogical agents with humanlike appearance and synthesized voice designs (Craig & Schroeder, 2017; Siegle & Craig, 2024) have been considered effective tools for providing support in virtual learning environments (Davis, 2018; Park, 2015; Zhang et al., 2024). For example, a meta-analysis conducted by Davis et al. (2023) indicated that pedagogical agents significantly increased learning outcomes, with notable effect sizes across various types of test questions. However, previous pedagogical agents were mostly used for directing attention with mechanical and predetermined algorithms rather than engaging in in-depth, immediate, and human-like interactions (Castro-Alonso et al., 2021; Schneider et al., 2022). This contradicts the modern educational philosophy of student-centered embodied learning and experiential learning (Fitton et al., 2020). Therefore, researchers have introduced the concept of “GAI-enhanced pedagogical agents” in the virtual metaverse to address this issue (Sikström et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024).
On the one hand, empirical studies have shown that powered by a multi-head attention and feed-forward mechanism, transformer-based GAI has significant potential in education (Ghafouri et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Previous research has valued the design of pedagogical agents embedded with GAI in many learning subjects (Chen et al., 2024; Lan & Chen, 2024; Mittal et al., 2024). On the other hand, researchers have recently recognized three-dimensional virtual metaverse learning environments as suitable digital landscapes for pedagogical agents (Chamola, Sai, et al., 2024; Zhai et al., 2023). In the metaverse, learners can experience traditional festival customs virtually through avatars and engage with GAI-enhanced pedagogical agents with the perceived characteristics of immersion, sociability, and exploration (Kye et al., 2021). However, the application of GAI-enhanced pedagogical agents in the metaverse (GPAiM) is scarce. Thus, this study aimed to examine the effects of GPAiM (both 2D-GPAiM and 3D-GPAiM) on elementary school students’ conceptual understanding and cognitive engagement concerning traditional festival customs through an empirical study based on the theory of embodied cognition (Shapiro & Spaulding, 2014).
