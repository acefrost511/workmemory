## Abstract
The rapid integration of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) into higher education has precipitated a critical tension between the pursuit of efficiency and the imperative for deep, transformative learning. While GenAI offers unprecedented speed in knowledge processing, it raises concerns that efficiency-driven motivations may erode the cognitive struggle essential for intellectual growth. This study investigates how Human-GenAI pedagogical partnerships influence transformative learning through the dual cognitive pathways of vigilance and offloading, moderated by efficiency orientation. Employing a rigorous mixed-methods design across three cultural contexts (China, Europe, and the United States, N = 912), we combine structural equation modeling, importance–performance map analysis (IPMA), fuzzy-set qualitative comparative analysis (fsQCA), and semi-structured interviews to unpack these complex dynamics. The findings reveal a counterintuitive paradox: pedagogical partnerships simultaneously activate cognitive vigilance (critical evaluation) and cognitive offloading (strategic delegation). Contrary to traditional assumptions that delegation diminishes learning depth, our results indicate that strategic cognitive offloading—when exceeding specific thresholds—liberates mental resources for higher-order reflection, thereby enhancing transformative learning experiences. Furthermore, efficiency orientation acts not as a barrier but as an amplifier, strengthening both vigilance and offloading behaviors through mechanisms of pragmatic risk management. These findings reconceptualize student-AI dynamics from a zero-sum resource competition to a synergistic collaboration, offering a nuanced framework for educators to integrate GenAI in ways that preserve human agency and foster quality education in the digital age.

## Introduction
The integration of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) into higher education represents a watershed moment in educational technology, fundamentally reshaping how students engage with knowledge acquisition and creation (Bond et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR3); Ursavaş et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR37)). The widespread adoption of tools such as ChatGPT in academic environments has created a critical tension: while these technologies offer remarkable efficiency in supporting learning activities, they simultaneously challenge the cognitive engagement necessary for deep, transformative learning (Abbas et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR1); Liu et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR26)). In this study, generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) refers to AI systems built on large language models and multimodal foundation models that are capable of producing novel, contextually relevant content—including text, code, images, and structured data—in response to natural-language prompts (Dwivedi et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR9); Küchemann et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR22)). Unlike predictive analytics or intelligent tutoring systems that operate within predefined parameters, generative AI engages users in interactive, open-ended knowledge construction, making it a qualitatively distinct technology for educational contexts (Kasneci et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR20)). Our investigation focuses specifically on commercially available, general-purpose generative AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) that students independently access and voluntarily integrate into their academic workflows, rather than institutionally developed or customized AI applications. This focus captures the authentic, self-directed interaction patterns through which students organically develop partnership orientations, cognitive strategies, and motivational dynamics with these technologies. This paradox intensifies as students increasingly frame AI as collaborative partners rather than passive instruments, raising fundamental questions about preserving active learning pedagogies in an era when computational systems can execute tasks traditionally requiring human cognition (Leonardi, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR24); Zhang et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR42)). Understanding these dynamics becomes urgent as higher education institutions worldwide develop integration policies that must balance technological affordances with pedagogical quality, acknowledging that AI adoption is both irreversible and consequential (Nyberg et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR30)). This study examines how efficiency-driven motivations reshape student learning processes when generative AI transforms from instrumental tool to intellectual partner, with findings applicable to educational practice, institutional policy, and lifelong learning design.
Transformative learning theory offers a rigorous framework for understanding how learners reconstruct perspectives through critical reflection and disorienting dilemmas (Mezirow, [1991](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR28)), yet its application to technology-mediated educational environments remains underdeveloped (Labadze et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR23)). Recent scholarship explores human-AI partnerships as collaborative relationships marked by mutual influence and shared agency, transcending conventional tool-use paradigms (Ramaul et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR32); Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR38)). Parallel research examines cognitive processes during AI interactions, particularly cognitive vigilance—critical evaluation of AI outputs—and cognitive offloading—strategic delegation of intellectual tasks to computational systems (Gilbert et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR13); Wei et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR39)). Efficiency orientation, grounded in achievement goal theory, captures students' prioritization of expedient completion over thorough understanding (Elliot & McGregor, [2001](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR10); Greenbaum et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR14)). These theoretical foundations converge around a central tension: while partnerships theoretically deepen engagement through shared accountability, efficiency motivations may undermine collaborative dynamics by privileging speed over scrutiny (Ishowo-Oloko et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR18)). These constructs collectively capture the multifaceted nature of human-AI educational interactions, where relational framings, cognitive strategies, and motivational orientations intersect to shape learning outcomes.
Despite growing attention to AI's educational impact, critical knowledge gaps persist regarding how partnership orientations interact with efficiency motivations to reshape cognitive engagement and learning quality. Existing research typically examines trust versus skepticism in human-AI interactions as binary constructs (Dietvorst et al., [2015](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR8); Logg et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR27)), missing the possibility that partnerships might simultaneously activate both vigilant and offloading behaviors. Furthermore, while cognitive offloading theory traditionally positions delegation as detrimental to learning (Sparrow et al., [2011](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR34)), AI partnerships' unique affordances may fundamentally alter this relationship through strategic resource reallocation. This study addresses three questions: (1) How do Human-GenAI pedagogical partnerships influence the dual cognitive pathways of vigilance and offloading? (2) Through what mechanisms do these cognitive processes translate into transformative learning experiences? (3) How does efficiency orientation moderate these relationships? The contributions include extending transformative learning theory to AI-mediated contexts, reconceptualizing cognitive offloading as potentially beneficial when strategically deployed, and revealing how efficiency orientations can paradoxically enhance critical thinking in partnership frameworks. Employing a rigorous mixed-methods design across three cultural contexts (China, Europe, and the United States, N = 912), we combine structural equation modeling (PLS-SEM), fuzzy-set qualitative comparative analysis (fsQCA), importance–performance map analysis (IPMA), and semi-structured interviews to unpack these complex dynamics. This multi-method approach is essential because the paradoxical, nonlinear, and configurational nature of Human-GenAI interactions cannot be fully captured by any single analytical technique. PLS-SEM tests the hypothesized mediation and moderation pathways; fsQCA reveals equifinal configurational pathways to transformative learning; IPMA translates statistical findings into actionable pedagogical priorities; and qualitative interviews illuminate the process-oriented mechanisms underlying the quantitative patterns. A three-wave, time-lagged survey design minimizes common method bias by temporally separating predictor and criterion variable measurement. These combined methods provide evidence-based insights for educators integrating generative AI while maintaining active learning principles.
To address these research questions, this study adopts a rigorous mixed-methods design organized into four complementary studies. Study 1 employs partial least squares structural equation modeling (PLS-SEM) to test the hypothesized mediation and moderation pathways. Study 2 conducts an importance–performance map analysis (IPMA) to translate statistical findings into actionable pedagogical priorities. Study 3 uses fuzzy-set qualitative comparative analysis (fsQCA) to identify configurational, equifinal pathways to transformative learning. Study 4 triangulates these quantitative findings with semi-structured interviews from 45 business students, illuminating the process-oriented mechanisms underlying the statistical patterns. Data were collected through a three-wave, time-lagged survey across three cultural contexts (China, Europe, and the United States, N = 912), as detailed in the methodology flowchart (Fig. [2](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Fig2)). The paper proceeds as follows: theoretical framework and hypotheses development, multi-study methodology, empirical results, and discussion of theoretical and practical implications for higher education.
## Theoretical background and hypotheses
### Transformative learning in technology-enhanced higher education
Transformative learning constitutes a fundamental shift in how individuals construct meaning from experiences, involving critical reflection on assumptions and perspective reconstruction that guides future action (Mezirow, [1991](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR28)). This process transcends incremental knowledge acquisition by catalyzing profound changes in learners' frames of reference through disorienting dilemmas that challenge existing mental models (Kim et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR21)). Contemporary applications emphasize cognitive-affective interplay, where learners experience tension between familiar interpretive schemas and emerging understandings demanding perspective transformation (Janssens et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR19)). Authentic transformation requires learners to engage in critical discourse, test new relationships, and integrate revised perspectives through processes that are simultaneously personal and social (Zeivots & Shalavin, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR41)). Recent empirical work demonstrates that transformative learning in professional contexts involves questioning unexamined habits of mind through structured experiences balancing vulnerability with intellectual rigor (Kim et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR21)).
The convergence of transformative learning theory with emerging human-agentic AI partnership perspectives provides a powerful lens for understanding how generative artificial intelligence reshapes educational experiences. Human-AI partnerships transcend instrumental relationships by establishing collaborative dynamics characterized by mutual influence, shared agency, and co-creative knowledge construction (Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR38)). This orientation aligns with transformative learning's emphasis on perspective reconstruction, as learners must reimagine their relationship with knowledge creation when AI systems demonstrate capabilities traditionally reserved for human cognition (Zhang et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR42)). Recognizing AI as an intellectual partner creates the cognitive disruption necessary for transformative learning, forcing students to reconstruct assumptions about learning, intelligence, and cognitive boundaries (Leonardi, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR24)). The bidirectional nature of pedagogical partnerships mirrors transformative learning's requirement for dialectical engagement, where meaning emerges through iterative exchanges challenging and refining understanding (Li et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR25)). When students adopt partnership orientations toward generative AI, they enter transformative spaces where traditional boundaries between human and artificial intelligence dissolve, necessitating fundamental reconsideration of what constitutes learning, knowledge, and intellectual agency (Ramaul et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR32)).
### Human-GenAI pedagogical partnership and dual cognitive pathways
Cognitive vigilance and cognitive offloading represent the two fundamental cognitive responses available to students interacting with generative AI, and their simultaneous activation constitutes the central paradox this study investigates. Cognitive vigilance refers to the systematic process of critically evaluating AI-generated content, encompassing error detection, reasoning verification, source cross-referencing, and the questioning of underlying knowledge claims (Celik, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR4); Pennycook & Rand, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR31)). It extends beyond simple accuracy checking to encompass epistemic vigilance—a deep, critical appraisal of whether AI outputs align with disciplinary standards, logical coherence, and evidential support (Zawacki-Richter et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR40)). Cognitive offloading, grounded in Risko and Gilbert’s ([2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR33)) metacognitive framework, refers to the deliberate use of external tools to alter information-processing requirements so as to reduce internal cognitive demand. In the context of generative AI, this manifests as the strategic delegation of cognitive tasks—such as summarization, synthesis, initial drafting, or data analysis—to the AI system (Gilbert et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR13); Sparrow et al., [2011](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR34)). These two constructs capture the essential tension in AI-mediated education: the concern that delegation erodes learning depth versus the possibility that critical engagement enhances it. By demonstrating that both pathways are simultaneously activated through partnership orientations and that both—paradoxically—contribute positively to transformative learning, this study resolves a fundamental theoretical tension and reconceptualizes student–AI dynamics from zero-sum cognitive competition to synergistic cognitive collaboration.
Partnership-based relationships with generative AI alter students' cognitive engagement by activating dual processing mechanisms reflecting collaborative accountability and strategic resource allocation (Chan & Hu, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR5)). When students perceive AI as an intellectual partner, they experience heightened responsibility for joint outcomes, triggering critical evaluation behaviors analogous to team dynamics where shared ownership necessitates quality control (Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR38)). This partnership framing activates cognitive vigilance through relational accountability mechanisms, as students recognize that collaborative knowledge creation requires verification of AI contributions to maintain intellectual integrity (Celik, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR4)). The mutual exchange in pedagogical partnerships creates dialectical tensions prompting students to question AI-generated content, fostering skeptical inquiry typically reserved for peer collaboration (Li et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR25)). Partnership orientations activate metacognitive monitoring where students must assess their AI collaborator's reliability, leading to systematic scrutiny paralleling quality assurance in professional knowledge work (Pennycook & Rand, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR31)). The collaborative nature of partnerships transforms passive consumption into active verification, as students internalize responsibility for co-created outputs (Zhang et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR42)).
### Hypothesis 1a:
Human-GenAI pedagogical partnership is positively associated with cognitive vigilance.
Paradoxically, the same partnership orientation promoting critical scrutiny simultaneously facilitates strategic delegation of cognitive tasks, as students recognize AI's complementary capabilities for routine intellectual labor. Partnership frameworks enable students to conceptualize task distribution based on comparative advantage, allocating lower-order cognitive operations to AI while preserving human capacity for higher-order synthesis (Gilbert et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR13)). This strategic division mirrors distributed cognition in effective human teams, where partners leverage differential strengths to optimize collective performance (Tang et al., [2023a](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR35), [2023b](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR36)). The mutual goal orientation characteristic of partnerships legitimizes cognitive offloading as collaborative efficiency rather than intellectual abdication, reframing delegation as strategic resource management (Risko & Gilbert, [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR33)). Students viewing AI as partners develop sophisticated offloading strategies preserving cognitive resources for tasks requiring human judgment while exploiting AI’s computational advantages (Sparrow et al., [2011](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR34)). This partnership-enabled offloading represents intentional cognitive design rather than convenience-driven avoidance, as students actively architect intellectual workflows to maximize learning outcomes (Leonardi, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR24)).
### Hypothesis 1b:
Human-GenAI pedagogical partnership is positively associated with cognitive offloading.
### Cognitive mechanisms and transformative learning outcomes
Cognitive vigilance serves as a critical catalyst for transformative learning by creating intellectual friction necessary to challenge mental models and reconstruct understanding. The systematic questioning and verification behaviors characteristic of cognitive vigilance mirror the critical reflection processes central to transformative learning, where learners examine assumption validity (Mezirow, [1991](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR28)). Students actively scrutinizing AI outputs engage in epistemic vigilance extending beyond error detection to fundamental questioning of knowledge claims, creating disorienting dilemmas triggering perspective transformation (Kim et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR21)). This critical evaluation forces students to articulate their understanding while assessing AI-generated content, promoting metacognitive awareness essential for deep learning (Janssens et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR19)). The iterative cycle of AI interaction, skeptical evaluation, and knowledge reconstruction parallels the discourse and testing phases of transformative learning, where new perspectives emerge through dialectical engagement (Dann et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR6)). Cognitive vigilance cultivates intellectual humility by revealing the provisional nature of both human and artificial knowledge, fostering openness to perspective change (Ramaul et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR32)).
### Hypothesis 2a:
Cognitive vigilance is positively associated with transformative learning experience.
Cognitive offloading theoretically diminishes transformative learning by reducing cognitive struggle essential for deep understanding and perspective change. When students delegate critical thinking tasks to AI, they bypass effortful processing creating durable mental representations and conceptual restructuring (Freeman et al., [2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR12)). Outsourcing analytical work to AI systems potentially creates superficial engagement where students become passive recipients rather than active knowledge constructors (Zeivots & Shalavin, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR41)). Transformative learning requires grappling with complexity and ambiguity, yet cognitive offloading offers premature closure through AI-generated solutions circumventing necessary intellectual struggle (Liu et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR26)). Delegating synthesis and evaluation tasks particularly undermines critical reflection processes distinguishing transformative from transactional learning (Küchemann et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR22)). Relying on AI for cognitive heavy lifting may develop learned helplessness atrophying capacity for independent critical thinking and perspective transformation (Dietvorst et al., [2015](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR8)).
### Hypothesis 2b:
Cognitive offloading is negatively associated with transformative learning experience.
### Mediating pathways from partnership to transformation
The relationship between pedagogical partnership and transformative learning operates through cognitive vigilance as a mediating mechanism translating collaborative orientation into deep learning outcomes. Partnership-induced vigilance creates continuous critical engagement where students' scrutiny of AI outputs generates cognitive dissonance necessary for perspective transformation (Zhang et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR42)). This mediating pathway reflects how relational framings shape cognitive processes, with partnership orientations activating verification behaviors subsequently triggering reflection and reconstruction central to transformative learning (Li et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR25)). Vigilance sparked by partnership accountability ensures AI interactions become sites of intellectual challenge rather than passive consumption, creating fertile ground for transformative experiences (Nyberg et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR30)). Students developing vigilant practices through partnership engagement accumulate critical incidents collectively reshaping understanding, illustrating how proximal cognitive behaviors mediate distal learning outcomes (Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR38)). The partnership-vigilance-transformation sequence represents a coherent developmental trajectory where collaborative framings enable critical practices culminating in perspective change (Leonardi, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR24)).
### Hypothesis 3a:
Cognitive vigilance mediates the relationship between Human-GenAI pedagogical partnership and transformative learning experience.
Despite theoretical expectations of negative effects, cognitive offloading may paradoxically mediate a positive pathway from partnership to transformation by creating cognitive space for higher-order reflection. Partnership-based offloading differs qualitatively from convenience-driven delegation by maintaining student agency in strategic task allocation while preserving engagement with complex synthesis (Tang et al., [2023a](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR35), [2023b](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR36)). Cognitive resources liberated through strategic offloading enable students to engage in metacognitive reflection and perspective-taking characterizing transformative learning (Gilbert et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR13)). This mediated pathway suggests partnerships enable sophisticated cognitive management where offloading becomes a tool for transformation rather than impediment (Risko & Gilbert, [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR33)). Students offloading routine tasks within partnership frameworks redirect saved cognitive capacity toward questioning assumptions and reconstructing mental models (Ramaul et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR32)). The partnership context transforms offloading from intellectual outsourcing to cognitive optimization, creating conditions where delegation facilitates rather than hinders deep learning (Liu et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR26)).
### Hypothesis 3b:
Cognitive offloading mediates the relationship between Human-GenAI pedagogical partnership and transformative learning experience.
### Moderating role of efficiency orientation
Efficiency orientation weakens the positive relationship between pedagogical partnership and cognitive vigilance by prioritizing speed over scrutiny in AI interactions. Students driven by efficiency goals view critical evaluation as friction impeding task completion, leading them to suppress vigilant behaviors even within partnership frameworks (Greenbaum et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR14)). The tension between efficiency imperatives and verification requirements creates cognitive dissonance that efficiency-oriented students resolve by minimizing scrutiny practices (Elliot & McGregor, [2001](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR10)). This moderation effect reflects how motivational orientations shape collaborative relationship enactment, with efficiency concerns overriding accountability mechanisms typically activated by partnerships (Ishowo-Oloko et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR18)). Efficiency-oriented students may maintain superficial partnership facades while abandoning critical engagement distinguishing genuine collaboration from instrumental tool use (Hwang & Lee, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR17)). The expedient mindset corrupts partnership dynamics by transforming AI from intellectual peer to productivity accelerator, undermining vigilance cultivation (Logg et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR27)).
### Hypothesis 4a:
Efficiency orientation negatively moderates the relationship between Human-GenAI pedagogical partnership and cognitive vigilance.
Conversely, efficiency orientation amplifies the partnership-offloading relationship by aligning delegation behaviors with students' fundamental motivational drivers. Efficiency-oriented students recognize partnerships as optimal frameworks for maximizing cognitive leverage, enthusiastically embracing offloading opportunities partnerships legitimize (Tang et al., [2023a](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR35), [2023b](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR36)). The synergy between efficiency goals and partnership-enabled delegation creates reinforcing dynamics where students actively seek offloading possibilities to optimize cognitive resource allocation (Gilbert et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR13)). This amplification demonstrates how motivational orientations can strengthen partnership mechanisms when aligned with specific cognitive strategies (Greenbaum et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR14)). Efficiency orientation provides additional impetus for strategic task distribution partnerships enable, accelerating development of sophisticated offloading practices (Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR38)). The partnership framework channels efficiency drives into productive delegation patterns that might otherwise manifest as corner-cutting or superficial engagement (Nyberg et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR30)).
### Hypothesis 4b:
Efficiency orientation positively moderates the relationship between Human-GenAI pedagogical partnership and cognitive offloading.

### Measurement design
This study developed and validated the survey instrument through a systematic, multistage process to ensure robust psychometric properties appropriate for theory testing (Hair et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR16)). The initial item pool for each construct was rigorously adapted from established, high-impact research to ensure theoretical grounding and content validity. Specifically, items for Human-GenAI Pedagogical Partnership were informed by recent conceptualizations of human-AI collaboration and teaming (Dwivedi et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR9); Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR38)). Measures for Cognitive Vigilance drew upon literature on misinformation evaluation and educational technology integration (Celik, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR4); Pennycook & Rand, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR31)). Cognitive Offloading items were adapted from foundational studies on external memory and cognitive delegation (Gilbert et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR13); Risko & Gilbert, [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR33)). The Transformative Learning Experience scale was based on Mezirow’s ([1991](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR28)) seminal theory and its contemporary applications in management education (Janssens et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR19); Kim et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR21)). Finally, Efficiency Orientation was operationalized using well-validated scales from achievement goal and organizational behavior literature (Elliot & McGregor, [2001](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR10); Greenbaum et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR14)).
To further refine the instrument, this study convened an expert panel comprising five academics and doctoral students with expertise in management information systems, educational psychology, and human–computer interaction. Through three iterative rounds of evaluation, the panel scrutinized each item for clarity, relevance, and potential ambiguity, leading to a refined second version of the questionnaire. This version underwent a pre-test with 90 business students, a sample independent from the main study, which confirmed strong internal consistency for all scales (Cronbach’s alpha > 0.700). Feedback from the pre-test led to minor but crucial modifications; for instance, an initial item for Cognitive Vigilance, “I check AI answers,” was refined to the more precise “I verify AI-generated information through independent sources” to better capture the intended construct of critical validation. To ensure conceptual equivalence across the international sample, a rigorous back-translation procedure was employed. Two independent bilingual experts, fluent in both English and Chinese, translated and back-translated the instrument. Discrepancies were resolved through collaborative discussion to guarantee that the semantic and conceptual meaning of constructs remained consistent across cultures.
The combination of items from diverse source literatures into coherent measurement scales was validated through a rigorous, multilayered process. Each construct’s items were not simply transplanted from existing scales; rather, they were carefully adapted through a theory-driven mapping process. For example, items for Cognitive Vigilance were drawn from Pennycook and Rand’s ([2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR31)) work on critical evaluation of information and Celik’s ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR4)) Intelligent-TPACK framework. Although these originate from misinformation psychology and teacher knowledge respectively, both address the same underlying construct—the systematic critical appraisal of information generated by external sources—making their combination theoretically coherent when adapted to the student–GenAI context. Similarly, Cognitive Offloading items integrate insights from Gilbert et al.’s ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR13)) intention offloading paradigm and Risko and Gilbert’s ([2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR33)) metacognitive framework, both addressing the delegation of cognitive tasks to external tools. The expert panel of five academics evaluated each item’s fit with the target construct through three iterative rounds, ensuring that items drawn from different literatures converge on a unified conceptual definition. The pre-test (n = 90) confirmed that combined scales achieved strong internal consistency (all Cronbach’s α > 0.700). The full-sample psychometric analysis (Tables [3](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Tab3) and [4](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Tab4)) provides comprehensive validation: convergent validity was established through indicator loadings (0.727–0.874), composite reliability (0.872–0.912), and AVE values (0.609–0.728); discriminant validity was confirmed through the Fornell-Larcker criterion, cross-loading analysis, and HTMT ratios (all < 0.850, highest: 0.712). These results collectively demonstrate that the measurement instrument is both internally consistent and empirically distinct across constructs, confirming that the combination of items from multiple source literatures successfully captures the intended theoretical constructs. All final items were measured on a seven-point Likert scale, anchored by 1 (Strongly Disagree) and 7 (Strongly Agree). The complete measurement instrument is detailed in Table [1](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Tab1).

### Data collection and sample
To ensure the findings possess both theoretical depth and practical relevance, this study utilized a multistage purposive sampling strategy to recruit participants from higher education business schools across three distinct economic and cultural regions: China, Europe, and the United States. This cross-cultural design enhances the generalizability of the results and allows for a more nuanced understanding of how students interact with generative artificial intelligence in diverse academic environments (Nyberg et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR30)). The primary inclusion criterion required that participants were undergraduate or graduate students who had actively used generative artificial intelligence for their academic tasks, ensuring that all respondents had direct and meaningful experience with the core phenomena under investigation (Ramaul et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR32)). This targeted approach is critical for capturing authentic perceptions and behaviors related to human-AI educational dynamics.
A three-wave, time-lagged survey design was implemented to minimize the potential for common method bias by temporally separating the measurement of predictor and criterion variables (Hair et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR16)). At Time 1, data were collected for the independent variable (Human-GenAI Pedagogical Partnership), the moderator (Efficiency Orientation), and all control variables. Two weeks later, at Time 2, a second survey was administered to the same participants to measure the two mediating variables (Cognitive Vigilance and Cognitive Offloading). Finally, at Time 3, two weeks after the second wave, the dependent variable (Transformative Learning Experience) was assessed. This longitudinal data collection procedure strengthens the basis for making causal inferences. A total of 912 valid and complete responses were obtained for the final analysis. An a priori power analysis conducted using G\\*Power 3.1 indicated that a minimum sample of 85 participants was required to detect medium-sized effects (f2 = 0.15, α = 0.05, power = 0.95), a threshold our final sample size comfortably exceeds. Furthermore, the sample size surpasses the widely recognized ten-times rule heuristic, further affirming the statistical power of the analysis (Hair et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR15)). Table [2](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Tab2) provides a detailed summary of the sample’s demographic characteristics, which reflect a balanced and diverse cohort of business students.

### Analytical technology
Partial least squares structural equation modeling executed in SmartPLS 4.1 provided the variance-based platform needed to estimate the full mediation-moderation architecture while accommodating reflective constructs, non-normal data, cross-regional heterogeneity, and latent nonlinearities (Hair et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR15), [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR16)). Because paradox inquiry requires attention to equifinal and asymmetric mechanisms, fuzzy-set qualitative comparative analysis captured configurational combinations that jointly explain high and low transformative learning outcomes (Fiss, [2011](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR11)). Importance–performance map analysis translated statistical salience into actionable instructional priorities, a step urged by contemporary management education research (Liu et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR26)). Triangulating these quantitative lenses with semi-structured interviews responds to recent calls for mixed-method designs that unpack dynamic agency loops in human–artificial intelligence teaming and illuminate contextual contingencies such as experience levels and knowledge practices (Leonardi, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR24); Nyberg et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR30); Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR38); Zhang et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR42)). This integrated analytic toolkit therefore aligns methodological rigor with the study’s multilevel, sociotechnical focus.
### Common method bias and robustness
Common method bias was mitigated procedurally through three-wave temporal separation, anonymity assurances, and an academic-purpose cover story that discouraged socially desirable responding, practices endorsed in recent measurement primers (Hair et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR16)). Statistically, Harman’s one-factor test showed the first unrotated component accounted for 34.613% of the variance, below the 50% threshold, while a latent marker variable displayed nonsignificant paths to all substantive constructs (|t|≤ 1.213, \*p\* ≥ 0.225), confirming that a single-source inflation threat was negligible. Independent-samples t-tests comparing early and late respondents across demographics and focal variables yielded no significant differences (all \*p\* ≥ 0.306), indicating that non-response did not bias parameter estimates and that the final sample represents the invited population (Hair et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR15)). Variance-inflation factors ranged from 1.649 to 2.677, comfortably below the conservative cut-off of 5.000, demonstrating that multicollinearity among indicators and latent constructs was not a concern and that estimated path coefficients retain interpretive stability (Liu et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR26)).
## Study 1: SEM results
### Measurement model
As detailed in Table [3](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Tab3), the criteria for reliability and convergent validity were fully satisfied (Hair et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR15)). All indicator loadings on their respective latent variables were substantial and statistically significant, ranging from 0.727 to 0.874 and thereby exceeding the recommended 0.700 threshold. Internal consistency reliability was robust, with all composite reliability values (ranging from 0.872 to 0.912) and Cronbach’s alpha values (ranging from 0.871 to 0.907) comfortably surpassing the 0.700 benchmark. Furthermore, convergent validity was firmly established, as the average variance extracted (AVE) for each construct was greater than 0.500, with values ranging from 0.609 to 0.728. This indicates that each construct explained a majority of the variance in its associated indicators.

### Structural model and hypothesis testing
Following the validation of the measurement model, the structural model was assessed to test the hypothesized relationships. The model demonstrates substantial explanatory and predictive power. The coefficient of determination (R2) for the central endogenous construct, Transformative Learning Experience, was 0.475, indicating that the model explains 47.5% of its variance. The R2 values for the mediating constructs of Cognitive Vigilance and Cognitive Offloading were 0.150 and 0.149, respectively. The model fit was deemed excellent, with a Standardized Root Mean Square Residual (SRMR) of 0.031, which is well below the recommended threshold of 0.080 (Hair et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR15)). Furthermore, an analysis of the model's out-of-sample predictive power using the CVPAT metric confirmed that the PLS-SEM model provides significantly lower prediction errors than a naive benchmark, affirming its predictive relevance (Hair et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR16)).
The path analysis, detailed in Fig. [3](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Fig3) and Table [5](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Tab5), provides a nuanced view of the proposed hypotheses. The results offer strong support for the dual-pathway effect of a Human-GenAI Pedagogical Partnership. Specifically, this partnership was positively and significantly associated with both Cognitive Vigilance (β = 0.335, \*p\* < 0.001, f2 = 0.131) and Cognitive Offloading (β = 0.351, \*p\* < 0.001, f2 = 0.144), supporting Hypotheses 1a and 1b. In turn, Cognitive Vigilance demonstrated a strong positive influence on Transformative Learning Experience (β = 0.437, \*p\* < 0.001, f2 = 0.243), supporting Hypothesis [2a](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#FPar3). Contrary to the hypothesized negative relationship, Cognitive Offloading also had a significant positive effect on Transformative Learning Experience (β = 0.333, \*p\* < 0.001, f2 = 0.140), thus not supporting Hypothesis [2b](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#FPar4). Both indirect effects were significant: the mediating path through Cognitive Vigilance (β = 0.147, \*p\* < 0.001) and the path through Cognitive Offloading (β = 0.117, \*p\* < 0.001) were confirmed, lending support to Hypotheses 3a and 3b. These findings highlight that a partnership orientation with generative artificial intelligence concurrently activates both beneficial and potentially risky cognitive strategies, which paradoxically both contribute positively to transformative learning.

### Moderating effect
The analysis of the moderating role of Efficiency Orientation revealed significant interaction effects that further illuminate the complexities of the human-AI learning interface. The results, as shown in Table [5](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Tab5), indicate that Efficiency Orientation significantly moderates the relationship between Human-GenAI Pedagogical Partnership and both cognitive pathways. The interaction term for Cognitive Vigilance was positive and significant (β = 0.176, \*p\* < 0.001, f2 = 0.038). This finding is contrary to Hypothesis 4a, which predicted a negative moderation. A simple slope analysis, plotted in Fig. [4](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Fig4), was performed to deconstruct this interaction (Hair et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR16)). The plot reveals that the positive relationship between Human-GenAI Pedagogical Partnership and Cognitive Vigilance is stronger for students with high Efficiency Orientation (+ 1 SD) and weaker for those with low Efficiency Orientation (− 1 SD). This suggests that, counterintuitively, a stronger drive for efficiency amplifies the tendency to critically scrutinize AI when engaged in a pedagogical partnership.

In support of Hypothesis 4b, the interaction term for Cognitive Offloading was also positive and significant (β = 0.152, \*p\* < 0.01, f2 = 0.029). The corresponding simple slope plot in Fig. [4](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Fig4) illustrates that the positive relationship between Human-GenAI Pedagogical Partnership and Cognitive Offloading is amplified for students with a high Efficiency Orientation. Taken together, these moderation results reveal that a student's motivational orientation toward efficiency acts as a critical catalyst, intensifying both the productive and potentially problematic cognitive responses to a human-AI pedagogical partnership.
### Potential U-shaped dynamics
To probe for more complex relationships beyond the linear associations, this study conducted a post-hoc analysis of potential quadratic effects. This step is crucial for uncovering nuanced, nonlinear dynamics that may better reflect the paradoxical nature of human-technology interactions (Hair et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR16)). The analysis revealed two significant U-shaped relationships, which are visualized in Fig. [5](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Fig5). First, a significant convex relationship was identified between Human-GenAI Pedagogical Partnership and Cognitive Vigilance (β-quadratic = 0.066, \*p\* = 0.039). As depicted in the left panel of Fig. [5](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Fig5), at lower levels of partnership, the relationship is initially flat or slightly negative; however, after passing an inflection point, a stronger partnership is associated with a sharply accelerating increase in Cognitive Vigilance. This suggests that only a deeply established partnership, rather than a superficial one, triggers heightened critical scrutiny of the artificial intelligence.

Second, a significant convex relationship also emerged between Cognitive Offloading and Transformative Learning Experience (β-quadratic = 0.102, \*p\* < 0.001), providing critical nuance to the unexpected positive linear finding for Hypothesis [2b](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#FPar4). The right panel of Fig. [5](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Fig5) shows that while low-to-moderate levels of Cognitive Offloading have a minimal impact, higher levels are associated with a strong, accelerating increase in Transformative Learning Experience. This pattern implies that the strategic delegation of significant cognitive tasks may be necessary to free up mental resources for the higher-order reflection that underpins transformative learning (Mezirow, [1991](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR28); Risko & Gilbert, [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR33)). The other potential quadratic paths were not statistically significant, indicating that their relationships are adequately represented by the linear model. These curvilinear findings underscore that the benefits of human-generative AI collaboration are not monotonic and may only manifest after a certain threshold of engagement is reached.
### Cross-cultural multi-group analysis
A multi-group analysis (MGA) was conducted to examine whether the structural relationships differ significantly across the three regional subsamples (Hair et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR16)). Measurement invariance was first established through the measurement invariance of composite models (MICOM) procedure, which involves three sequential steps: configural invariance (Step 1), compositional invariance (Step 2), and equality of composite mean values and variances (Step 3). Step 1 (configural invariance) was satisfied by design, as identical indicators, data treatment, and algorithm settings were applied across all subsamples. Step 2 (compositional invariance) was established for all pairwise group comparisons: all original correlations were close to 1.000 and all permutation p-values were nonsignificant (\*p\* > 0.05), confirming that the composite scores are formed equivalently across cultures. Step 3 assessed equality of mean values (Step 3a) and variances (Step 3b). The results demonstrate that all original mean differences and variance differences fell within the 2.5–97.5% permutation confidence intervals, with one minor exception: the mean difference for Efficiency Orientation between the China and Europe subsamples reached significance (original difference = 0.177, permutation p = 0.020). However, because all remaining comparisons—including all Step 3b variance tests—were nonsignificant, measurement invariance was deemed established at the partial level, enabling meaningful cross-group path comparisons consistent with established PLS-SEM guidelines (Hair et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR16)). Full MICOM results are reported in Appendix [A](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Sec29).
The MGA results, summarized in Appendix [A](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Sec29), reveal that all six structural paths are directionally consistent and statistically significant (\*p\* < 0.05) across all three regional subsamples, with no path coefficient differences reaching statistical significance at the \*p\* < 0.05 level in permutation tests. This cross-cultural stability provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the dual cognitive pathway model. Descriptive comparisons reveal region-specific intensities. European students report the highest mean levels of both cognitive offloading (M = 4.692 vs. China M = 4.471, U.S. M = 4.626) and cognitive vigilance (M = 4.465 vs. China M = 4.336, U.S. M = 4.434), suggesting that European educational contexts, with their emphasis on critical intellectual traditions and structured learning frameworks, may simultaneously promote both evaluative and strategic delegation behaviors. Chinese students exhibit the strongest partnership-to-offloading path (β = 0.371) and the strongest offloading-to-transformation path (β = 0.372), indicating that cognitive offloading is a particularly productive mechanism in this cultural context. European students demonstrate the strongest vigilance-to-transformation path (β = 0.493), reflecting educational traditions emphasizing critical discourse and Socratic questioning. Consistent with the overall sample results, the efficiency orientation moderation effect is most pronounced in the U.S. subsample for both the vigilance pathway (β = 0.192 vs. China β = 0.166, Europe β = 0.163) and the offloading pathway (β = 0.171 vs. China β = 0.156, Europe β = 0.131), suggesting that the pragmatic risk management mechanism is particularly pronounced in U.S. educational contexts where outcome-oriented assessment cultures prevail. These nuances reflect cultural and institutional contexts while the underlying paradoxical mechanism remains structurally invariant across all three regions. These findings extend beyond demonstrating mere replicability; they reveal that the dual cognitive pathway model captures a universal process whose surface-level expression is culturally modulated but whose deep structure is robust across diverse educational ecosystems.
## Study 2: IPMA results
To translate the statistical findings into actionable pedagogical and managerial priorities, this study conducted an Importance-Performance Map Analysis (IPMA) with Transformative Learning Experience as the target construct. The IPMA extends the standard PLS-SEM results by plotting the total effects (importance) of each predictor construct against their average latent variable scores (performance), which are rescaled to a 0–100 range for comparability (Hair et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR16)). This visualization, presented in Fig. [6](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Fig6), allows for the strategic identification of factors that are not only highly influential but may also be underdeveloped in the current educational context, representing key areas for intervention.

## Study 3: fsQCA results
Fuzzy-set qualitative comparative analysis complements the PLS-SEM findings by revealing configurational pathways to transformative learning experiences. While PLS-SEM assumes linear and symmetric relationships, fsQCA captures equifinality, asymmetry, and conjunctural causation inherent in complex educational phenomena (Fiss, [2011](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR11)). This methodological triangulation provides deeper insights into how different combinations of pedagogical partnership, cognitive strategies, and motivational orientations jointly produce transformative learning outcomes.
Calibration procedures transformed seven-point Likert scale values into fuzzy membership scores ranging from 0.05 (full non-membership) to 0.95 (full membership), with 0.5 as the crossover point. Necessity analysis revealed no single construct achieved consistency above 0.9, confirming that transformative learning emerges from complex configurations rather than individual conditions. Truth table construction identified all possible combinations of causal conditions. For high-degree configurations, the analysis employed a minimum frequency threshold of 10 cases, raw consistency threshold of 0.80, and PRI consistency of 0.45. Nonhigh-degree configurations utilized identical frequency and raw consistency thresholds but required PRI consistency of 0.6.
The intermediate solution, selected for its balance of completeness and parsimony, yielded three distinct pathways to high transformative learning experience and one pathway to its absence. Table [6](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Tab6) reveals that all high-degree configurations demonstrate consistency values exceeding 0.854, indicating robust sufficiency for producing transformative learning. Configuration 1 (raw coverage = 0.678, unique coverage = 0.148) represents the dominant pathway, combining high cognitive vigilance with high cognitive offloading regardless of efficiency orientation. This configuration suggests that simultaneous critical evaluation and strategic delegation create optimal conditions for transformative learning. Configuration 2 (raw coverage = 0.349) features high efficiency orientation and cognitive offloading coupled with absent pedagogical partnership, revealing a paradoxical path where efficiency-driven students achieve transformation through intensive AI delegation without viewing AI as a collaborative partner. Configuration 3 (raw coverage = 0.443) combines pedagogical partnership with cognitive offloading while efficiency orientation is absent, indicating partnership-based transformation occurs when students are not primarily efficiency-focused.

The single nonhigh configuration demonstrates how absence of cognitive vigilance and offloading prevents transformative learning regardless of partnership orientation. The dominance of cognitive offloading across all high-degree configurations underscores its pivotal role, while the varying presence of other conditions highlights the contextual nature of AI-enabled transformative learning.

### Additional qualitative findings
Beyond the core themes presented in Table [7](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#Tab7), the interviews revealed several additional insights that further illuminate the mechanisms underlying the quantitative findings and extend our understanding of the Human-GenAI pedagogical partnership dynamics across diverse student profiles.
Developmental trajectory of partnership orientations. Interviews consistently revealed that students’ relationships with GenAI evolve through distinct phases—from instrumental tool-use to genuine intellectual partnership. One student described this transition vividly: “At first, I just used ChatGPT like a search engine—type a question, get an answer. But when I started working on my strategic management capstone, I began treating it more like a discussion partner. I would present my analysis, ask it to critique my reasoning, and then evaluate whether its counter-arguments had merit. That’s when the real learning happened—not from the AI’s answers, but from the process of questioning them.” (Female, Undergraduate, Business Administration). This developmental pattern aligns with the U-shaped relationship identified in Study 1, suggesting that the partnership threshold required to trigger cognitive vigilance emerges only after sustained, deep engagement rather than casual use.
Discipline-specific cognitive strategy deployment. The interviews revealed notable variation in how students from different business disciplines enact the dual cognitive pathways. Finance and accounting students reported prioritizing numerical verification and factual accuracy checking (vigilance-dominant strategies), reflecting the precision demands of their fields. As one finance student noted: “In finance, a single wrong number can invalidate an entire valuation model. So I check every figure the AI gives me against the source data.” (Male, Master’s, Finance). In contrast, marketing and international business students described engaging in more creative and conceptual offloading, using AI to generate alternative strategic frameworks or brainstorm campaign ideas that they then critically refined: “I use it to generate five different positioning strategies, and then I spend my time evaluating which one actually fits the market data. The AI does the divergent thinking; I do the convergent thinking.” (Female, Undergraduate, Marketing). These discipline-specific patterns suggest that the dual pathways manifest differently depending on the epistemic demands of the academic context.
Pragmatic vigilance among efficiency-oriented students. The counterintuitive positive moderation of efficiency orientation on vigilance (H4a) was richly illuminated by student narratives revealing a “pragmatic risk management” mechanism. Efficiency-oriented students described vigilance not as a deep philosophical quest for truth but as a calculated investment to avoid costly rework. One student explained: “I’m a very efficiency-focused person—I want to get things done fast. But ironically, that makes me more careful with AI, not less. If I submit something with errors because I blindly trusted ChatGPT, I waste more time fixing it than I saved. So being efficient actually means being vigilant.” (Male, Master’s, Finance). This pragmatic framing resolves the apparent paradox: for efficiency-oriented students, verification is not an impediment to speed but a prerequisite for it. Beyond this pragmatic calculus, interviews also revealed affective dimensions of the efficiency–vigilance dynamic that mirror the cognitive-affective interplay central to transformative learning (Kim et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR21)). Several efficiency-oriented students described intellectual excitement when AI outputs challenged their initial assumptions, and conversely, frustration when AI-generated content was superficial or generic. As one student explained: “When ChatGPT pushes back on my analysis with a perspective I hadn't considered, that's actually exciting–-it means I'm getting value from the partnership. But when it gives me a vague, textbook-level answer, I feel frustrated because I've wasted time I could have spent thinking more deeply myself.” (Female, Master's, Marketing). These emotional responses suggest that efficiency-oriented students are not affectively disengaged; rather, their efficiency goals create heightened expectations for AI output quality, generating productive cognitive-affective tension that further catalyzes critical evaluation and perspective reconstruction.
## Discussion
This study reveals that Human-GenAI Pedagogical Partnership simultaneously activates both cognitive vigilance and cognitive offloading, creating a paradoxical dual-pathway mechanism to transformative learning experiences. Unlike prior research suggesting trade-offs between trust and scrutiny in human-AI interactions (Dietvorst et al., [2015](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR8); Zawacki-Richter et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR40)), our findings demonstrate that partnership-based relationships with generative AI foster concurrent critical evaluation and strategic delegation behaviors (Ursavaş et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR37)). Most significantly, cognitive offloading positively influences transformative learning, contradicting theoretical expectations that delegating cognitive tasks diminishes deep learning (Freeman et al., [2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR12); Sparrow et al., [2011](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR34)). The U-shaped relationship between offloading and transformative learning suggests threshold effects where substantial delegation liberates cognitive resources for higher-order reflection, extending cognitive offloading theory beyond simple task substitution to strategic resource reallocation (Gilbert et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR13); Risko & Gilbert, [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR33)). Furthermore, efficiency orientation amplifies rather than diminishes cognitive vigilance when combined with pedagogical partnerships, challenging assumptions about efficiency-driven behaviors leading to uncritical AI acceptance (Ishowo-Oloko et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR18)). These findings reconceptualize human-AI educational dynamics from zero-sum resource competition to synergistic cognitive collaboration.
This research advances theoretical understanding of transformative learning in technology-enhanced higher education by revealing counterintuitive mechanisms through which efficiency orientation reshapes active learning pathways. Building on transformative learning theory (Kim et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR21); Mezirow, [1991](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR28)), this study extends the framework to encompass human-AI partnerships as catalysts for perspective transformation through dual cognitive pathways. Identifying efficiency orientation as an amplifier rather than inhibitor of critical thinking challenges prevailing assumptions about expedient learning behaviors (Greenbaum et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR14)) and contributes to emerging perspectives on AI-enabled knowledge creation (Zhang et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR42)). The configurational analysis reveals equifinal pathways to transformative learning, demonstrating that students achieve deep learning through varied combinations of partnership orientation, cognitive strategies, and motivational dispositions. This advances educational technology scholarship by providing empirical evidence that generative AI integration need not compromise active learning principles when structured as pedagogical partnerships (Liu et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR26); Zeivots & Shalavin, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR41)). The study offers a nuanced framework for understanding how digital transformation can enhance rather than diminish transformative educational experiences in higher education (Nic Giolla Mhichíl et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR29)).
These findings situate within broader debates about AI's role in educational innovation and human development, with implications extending to instructional design, learning analytics, and institutional policy. The paradoxical effects identified align with recent theorizing about agency loops and power dynamics in human-AI relationships (Leonardi, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR24)), suggesting partnership frameworks may resolve tensions between efficiency demands and learning depth across educational settings. Cross-cultural validation through multi-group analysis strengthens claims about mechanism universality: the core dual cognitive pathway model is structurally invariant across China, Europe, and the United States, while acknowledging culturally modulated variations in the intensity of specific pathways—such as higher mean offloading and vigilance levels among European students, a stronger offloading-to-transformation path among Chinese students, and a more pronounced efficiency–vigilance amplification in U.S. contexts—reflecting distinct institutional and pedagogical traditions (Nyberg et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR30)). By demonstrating that cognitive offloading can enable rather than hinder transformative learning, this research challenges fundamental assumptions about active learning requirements and opens avenues for designing AI-enhanced learning environments preserving human agency while leveraging computational capabilities (Ramaul et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR32)). The practical significance extends to policy discussions about AI integration in higher education, providing evidence-based frameworks for fostering critical AI literacy while capitalizing on efficiency gains (An et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR2)). The study contributes to reimagining human-AI collaboration as dynamic interplay of complementary cognitive processes rather than substitutive automation.
## Implications
### Theoretical implications for educational technology research
This study fundamentally challenges binary conceptualizations of human-AI interaction prevalent in educational technology literature by introducing a paradoxical framework where seemingly opposing cognitive processes coexist and mutually reinforce transformative outcomes. Traditional theorizing assumes trade-offs between trust and skepticism (Dietvorst et al., [2015](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR8)), automation and engagement (Freeman et al., [2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR12)), or efficiency and depth (Ishowo-Oloko et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR18)), yet the pedagogical partnership model reveals these dichotomies as false constraints limiting theoretical imagination. Simultaneous activation of cognitive vigilance and offloading through partnership relationships suggests human cognition operates through complementary rather than competitive channels when interfacing with artificial intelligence, necessitating shifts from resource scarcity models to abundance frameworks in theorizing technology-mediated learning (Zhang et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR42)). This reconceptualization profoundly impacts how scholars theorize agency distribution in human-AI systems, moving beyond zero-sum assumptions toward dynamic co-constitution of cognitive capabilities (Leonardi, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR24)).
The discovery of positive relationships between cognitive offloading and transformative learning revolutionizes understanding of how deep learning occurs in digital environments by revealing delegation as strategic cognitive investment rather than intellectual abdication. Classical cognitive offloading theory positioned external memory aids as substitutes for internal processing (Sparrow et al., [2011](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR34)), but the threshold effects identified here suggest offloading operates as cognitive multiplier when sufficiently intensive, freeing mental resources for higher-order synthesis and critique catalyzing perspective transformation (Mezirow, [1991](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR28)). This finding necessitates theoretical refinement distinguishing surface-level task delegation from strategic cognitive partnering, introducing temporal and intensity dimensions previously absent from offloading frameworks (Gilbert et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR13); Risko & Gilbert, [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR33)). The U-shaped relationships imply that transformative learning theories must incorporate nonlinear dynamics and critical mass concepts to accurately model how technology-enhanced learning unfolds through phase transitions rather than gradual accumulation.
Efficiency orientation's role as amplifier rather than inhibitor of critical thinking fundamentally disrupts achievement goal theory's assumptions about expedient learning behaviors undermining depth (Elliot & McGregor, [2001](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR10); Greenbaum et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR14)). The pragmatic vigilance mechanism revealed through interviews suggests instrumental and transformative learning goals can align when mediated through appropriate technological architectures, challenging philosophical separation between technical rationality and critical reflection in educational theory. This finding contributes to emerging posthuman perspectives on learning by demonstrating how human motivations interact with artificial intelligence capabilities to produce emergent cognitive configurations transcending individual limitations (Ramaul et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR32)). Future theorizing must abandon anthropocentric assumptions about learning processes and develop frameworks accounting for hybrid human-AI cognitive systems where efficiency and transformation become mutually constitutive rather than oppositional forces.
### Practical implications for higher education practice and policy
Educational institutions should redesign generative AI integration strategies to explicitly foster pedagogical partnerships through structured activities promoting co-creation and mutual goal-setting between students and AI systems. Rather than introducing AI as mere tool or assistant, educators must scaffold partnership development through collaborative projects where students and AI jointly tackle complex problems, with explicit reflection points for students to articulate their evolving relationship with technology (Zeivots & Shalavin, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR41)). Implementation requires creating partnership protocols guiding students through progressive engagement stages: initial exploration, capability mapping, role negotiation, and collaborative knowledge construction. Institutions should develop assessment rubrics evaluating not just output quality but sophistication of human-AI interaction patterns, rewarding evidence of strategic delegation, critical evaluation, and synergistic knowledge creation (Liu et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR26)). Faculty development workshops must emphasize designing assignments necessitating deep AI engagement rather than superficial use, ensuring activation of transformative mechanisms identified in this research.
Organizations seeking to cultivate cognitive vigilance alongside productive offloading should implement dual-track AI literacy programs simultaneously developing critical evaluation skills and strategic delegation competencies. Practical implementation involves creating AI interrogation labs where learners systematically probe AI outputs across domains, documenting reliability patterns and failure modes while developing personalized verification strategies (Celik, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR4)). Concurrently, cognitive portfolio mapping exercises can help learners identify which cognitive tasks to retain versus delegate based on comparative advantage analysis rather than convenience (Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR38)). These programs should incorporate peer review mechanisms where students evaluate each other's AI interaction strategies, fostering metacognitive awareness about optimal human-AI task distribution. Performance metrics must track both efficiency gains and evidence of deeper understanding, using pre-post assessments capturing perspective shifts rather than mere knowledge acquisition.
Counterintuitively, organizations should leverage rather than suppress efficiency orientation by reframing it as catalyst for transformative learning through productive laziness principles celebrating strategic cognitive conservation. Implementation requires developing efficiency-transformation matrices helping learners visualize how time saved through AI delegation can be reinvested in higher-order thinking activities (Tang et al., [2023a](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR35), [2023b](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR36)). Gamification elements can reward students demonstrating creative reallocation of cognitive resources, with leaderboards tracking both task completion speed and evidence of perspective transformation. Mentorship programs should pair efficiency-oriented learners with those successfully navigating the partnership journey, creating communities of practice around strategic AI collaboration (Nyberg et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00585-x#ref-CR30)). Critically, institutional messaging must shift from warnings about AI shortcuts to celebrating intelligent cognitive resource management, positioning thoughtful offloading as advanced skill rather than academic compromise.
## Conclusion, limitations, and future research directions
This study demonstrates that Human-GenAI pedagogical partnerships fundamentally restructure the cognitive architecture of transformative learning through paradoxical mechanisms defying conventional wisdom about technology-mediated education. The empirical evidence reveals that partnership-based relationships with generative artificial intelligence simultaneously activate cognitive vigilance and offloading, creating complementary rather than competing pathways to deep learning experiences. Most notably, cognitive offloading emerges as enabler rather than inhibitor of transformation, with threshold effects indicating that substantial delegation liberates mental resources for higher-order reflection essential to perspective change. Efficiency orientation functions as pragmatic amplifier strengthening both critical evaluation and strategic delegation when students view AI as collaborative partner, contradicting assumptions that expedient motivations compromise learning depth. These findings extend transformative learning theory into the digital age by establishing that generative AI partnerships can preserve and enhance reflective processes central to adult learning when structured through appropriate pedagogical frameworks. The configurational analysis reveals equifinal pathways, demonstrating that students achieve transformative outcomes through varied combinations of partnership orientation, cognitive strategies, and motivational dispositions, thus providing educational institutions with multiple viable integration strategies rather than prescriptive formulas.
Generalizability is bounded by exclusive reliance on self-reported perceptions from business students engaging primarily with text-based large language models, raising questions about behavioral equivalence in diverse disciplinary contexts and with forthcoming multimodal agents. Furthermore, although the qualitative interviews provide rich process-oriented insights, they were drawn exclusively from the Chinese subsample; future research should extend qualitative data collection to European and American participants to capture culturally nuanced variations in the lived experience of Human-GenAI partnerships. Although the three-wave design mitigates common method variance, longer temporal windows and objective performance indicators are required to trace how partnership routines stabilize and transformative insight endures. Future research should experiment with adaptive scaffolds calibrating delegation intensity, examine cultural moderators beyond the present tri-continental sample, and integrate physiological or neuro-cognitive measures capturing real-time shifts in cognitive load and reflective depth. By extending inquiry to longitudinal field interventions and mixed-reality learning environments, scholars can refine thresholds identified here and build actionable frameworks for cultivating resilient, ethically grounded human-AI learning ecologies in higher education.
