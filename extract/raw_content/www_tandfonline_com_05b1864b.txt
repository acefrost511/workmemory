## ABSTRACT
Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools are becoming integrated into the domains of teaching, learning, assessment and administration of educational institutions. While these advances are seen to provide opportunities, new concerns have arisen about the ethics of AI use. This has precipitated the publication of various AI ethics guidelines, however, the development of AI ethics policies for children and K-12 education has lagged behind. To contribute to the discourse on AI ethics and guide the direction of future curricula for schools, this article presents the findings of a systematic literature review in the ethics of AI within the context of K-12 education. From the analysis of 25 peer-reviewed studies published between 2010 and 2023, the authors map key themes and concerns relating to AI ethics in compulsory schooling. They also identify responses relating to the ethical use of AI, highlight research gaps and provide suggestions for future directions for researching AI ethics in K-12 education.

## Introduction
Artificial Intelligence (AI) has become prevalent in nearly every aspect of our lives and has also penetrated the field of K-12 education through the use of various AI-powered tools. AI-based adaptive learning platforms, for example, can analyse performance data, identify areas of improvement and provide personalised learning experiences by adjusting the pace and content based on individual performance (Demartini et al., [Citation2024](#)). Similarly, Intelligent Tutoring Systems have the potential to provide personalised instruction, tailored content and feedback to students, and support teachers with assessment by providing automated grading and feedback systems (du Boulay, [Citation2019](#)). Furthermore, AI technologies are increasingly being used for education management and delivery. As Miao et al. ([Citation2021](#)) explain, system-facing applications can be adopted to automate various aspects of school administration, such as admissions, timetabling, attendance and homework monitoring. For instance, the use of facial recognition technology can enable attendance management (Selwyn et al., [Citation2023](#)) while AI-powered surveillance systems that use video analytics can be seen to enhance safety and security by monitoring school premises (Tanner & McKelvey, [Citation2019](#)).
As AI use continues to permeate schooling, there is an emerging body of empirical research on how AI is used by teachers and students in K-12 settings. Recent systematic reviews of these studies report that educators use AI to support them with student monitoring, group management, automated grading and data-driven decisions (Crompton & Burke, [Citation2022](#)), as well as for machine learning demonstration and academic performance and behaviour prediction and intervention (Martin et al., [Citation2024](#)). The benefits of AI use for students are seen to relate to the use of AI Tutors for offering academic support similar to a physical educator, as well as the adoption of AI for aiding students in their thinking process and offering personalised learning tailored to their needs (Crompton & Burke, [Citation2022](#)). Furthermore, Martin et al. ([Citation2024](#)) report in their systematic review that students used AI for scientific discovery learning and improving their learning experience.
The use of Emotional AI has also started to become present in classrooms, encompassing ‘technologies that use affective computing and artificial intelligence techniques to sense, learn about and interact with human emotional life’ (McStay, [Citation2020b](#), p. 1). Within an educational context, commercial companies are seen to promote the use of Emotional AI as a means of capturing students’ facial expressions such as boredom, frustration, excitement, to gauge student engagement, regulate and optimise the difficulty levels of content and identify students who may be experiencing emotional distress (see McStay, [Citation2020a](#)).
The public release of generative AI technologies, such as ChatGPT, has also gained significant attention. The application of such Large Language Models (LLM) in educational contexts has sparked interest due to their perceived potential to support teaching and learning. According to Kasneci et al. ([Citation2023](#)) LLMs can play a significant role in fostering the development of reading, writing, math, science and language skills, aiding in research, writing and problem-solving tasks. Similarly, LLMs can potentially equip educators with an extensive array of tools and resources to support adaptive teaching through the production of lesson plans, creating personalised content, customised instruction, inclusive assessment and professional development opportunities (Kasneci et al., [Citation2023](#)).
While AI use continues to gain momentum, the adoption of AI-driven tools and technologies in K-12 education has raised many questions about the evolving role of teachers, students, school curricula and required digital literacies. There is also growing concern over the ethical implications of Artificial Intelligence in Education (AIED) as AI systems and processes start to permeate various aspects of schooling. At this point, it is pertinent to acknowledge the complexity of the ethical issues surrounding AI use in the context of education as well as the complexity of the term ‘ethics’ itself. As Hakimi et al. ([Citation2021](#)) argue, while various well-established schools of ethical thought exist, ‘ethics as a discipline has deep and contested theoretical groundings’ (p. 677). Indeed, the term ‘ethics’ has a complex lineage and the ethics of education, in particular, has been the focus of research and debate for more than 2000 years (Holmes et al., [Citation2021](#)). Providing a comprehensive account of the ethics of education in this short space is not only unrealistic to achieve, it is also not the aim of this systematic literature review (SLR). Instead, we will look in brief at relevant AI ethics policy guidance specific to K-12 education, acknowledging that while these constitute the ‘known unknowns’ of AIED ethics, there are still ‘unknown unknowns’ that ‘have yet to be even identified’ (Holmes et al., [Citation2021](#), p. 521).
The ethical implications of AIED have led to a ‘publication flurry’ of over 300 AI ethics guidelines and policy documents over the past years authored by national and international government agencies, academic consortia and industrial stakeholders (Adams et al., [Citation2023](#), p. 1). These policy documents, national strategies and frameworks represent a valuable source of information for understanding how the benefits and risks of AI use are perceived worldwide. However, as UNESCO recognises, it also essential to consider the evolving role of the teachers and the changing nature of the curriculum in light of AI’s social and ethical implications (Miao et al., [Citation2021](#)). AI ethics policy guidance specific to K-12 education and children appears to have somehow lagged behind, and only recently has this gap started to become addressed. For instance, the Institute for Ethical AI in Education ([Citation2021](#)) has developed The Ethical Framework for AI in Education, highlighting the role of governments in ensuring educators and students have a strong understanding of AI and its ethical implications. Similarly, the European Commission, Directorate-General for Education, Youth, Sport and Culture ([Citation2022](#), p. 6) argues that teachers need to ‘understand the potential AI and big data can have in education – while being aware of the associated risks’. It proposes appropriate ethical guidelines for the use of AI in education. Furthermore, in their review of AIED K-12 ethics policy documents, Adams et al. ([Citation2023](#)) found that the core AI ethical principles of Transparency; Justice and Fairness; Non-maleficence; Responsibility; Privacy; Beneficence; Freedom & Autonomy were present, and sometimes adapted for children. Four new ethical principles unique to K-12 education were also identified, including Pedagogical Appropriateness; Children’s Rights; AI Literacy; and Teacher Well-being (Adams et al., [Citation2023](#)).
While these ethical principles are relevant to inform and guide AIED deployment in K-12 education, there is still minimal research and significant ambiguity regarding how the ethics of AI use are conceptualised and operationalised in school classrooms worldwide by teachers and students alike and what the challenges are. This paper addresses this critical gap in the literature by investigating the key concerns relating to ethical AI use within the context of K-12 education. Providing a more nuanced understanding of the ethics of AI in schools is of particular significance as it can inform and drive future research and implementation of AI ethics curricula. More specifically, the paper reports on the findings of a systematic literature review of 25 peer-reviewed papers, which was guided by the following research questions:
\* What are the primary challenges relating to AI ethics encountered in the context of K-12 education?
\* What are the key responses relating to AI ethics encountered in the context of K-12 education?
## Methodology
The research method for this study is an SLR, an established approach in educational research (Zawacki-Richter et al., [Citation2020](#)). The approach to identifying sources for this SLR was based upon the methodological guidance of the PRISMA 2020 checklist (Page et al., [Citation2021](#)), with adjustments for the context of this research, which can be considered a Mixed Research Synthesis Review (Newman & Gough, [Citation2020](#)) as it is inclusive of all empirical and conceptual approaches. To avoid the potential ethical pitfalls of conducting a systematic review (Suri, [Citation2020](#)), the authors have focused on transparency and verification at all stages of the review process. This section outlines the method in detail, and our data can be accessed via this open-access online dataset (Gouseti et al., [Citation2024](#)).
### Information sources
This SLR only targeted peer-reviewed journal articles to ensure the review was based upon high-quality, credible, current sources that meet standards of academic integrity. This criterion for information sources is essential due to the emergent nature of AI technologies, especially in the field of education (Gašević et al., [Citation2023](#)). We used five subject-specific databases, and three interdisciplinary databases to identify relevant journal articles, as outlined in Table 1. The use of grey literature was excluded as there is an incalculable amount published on AI, much of which is AI-generated and of low quality (Friederich & Symons, [Citation2023](#)). As existing studies have focused on published ethical guidelines (see Adams et al., [Citation2023](#)), the analysis of existing AI frameworks was excluded to help further focus the contribution of this paper.

### Search strategy
Three keywords/phrases were identified for this SLR: ‘artificial intelligence’, ‘ethics’ and ‘K-12’, together defining the topic, focus and context of this review. Synonyms of these keywords and truncated versions of all terms were used to maximise the scope of this review (see Table 2). The search strategy remained identical across all eight databases, with Boolean and search operator adjustments being made to ensure the same search strategy was applied across the different systems. The AI terms were limited to title-search to help identify papers targeted to this topic. A qualified university librarian verified the search strategy. These terms were also used to verify that no existing systematic literature review had been published in this topic area, ensuring the originality of this study that contributes to the ongoing debate in this area.

### Eligibility criteria and selection process
Establishing clear criteria to evaluate the sources identified via searching, was essential to identify a final set of papers for full analysis (Page et al., [Citation2021](#)). To assess eligibility in this review, the research team established a series of inclusion and exclusion criteria (see: Table 3). These criteria reinforced the terms identified in the search strategy, ensuring the papers remained relevant to ethics and K-12 education. Additional criteria were developed to focus on the latest publications (2010 to current), the language the research team know (English), and to remove duplicates. As above, this project includes both conceptual and empirical studies, but the decision was taken to exclude editorials, book reviews and systematic reviews to focus on engagement with original sources as opposed to abstractions of them. While there is justification for retaining systematic reviews in the final paper list (Page et al., [Citation2021](#)), the initial screening showed that no existing reviews had sufficient focus on ethics to be useful.

All four researchers conducted the screening to ensure a robust process, with the full dataset evenly split across the team. Once initial screening was complete, a second phase of masked screening was conducted on all 1383 articles, ensuring each article was screened twice. All members of the research team discussed any disagreements between the first and second reviewers to ensure consensus. A total of 1302 papers were excluded during screening due to a failure to meet the eligibility criteria (see recorded reason for exclusion in Figure 1).
### Synthesis method
Following the double-masked screening process outlined above, a final total of 81 papers were retrieved and assessed for eligibility. This initially involved reading each full paper and assessing it against the inclusion/exclusion criteria (Table 3), leading to the exclusion of a further 22 papers (see Figure 1). The remaining 59 papers were read in full by a member of the research team to identify method, reliability, scale, geography, education phase and key identified ethical concerns. To ensure a consistent approach, the whole team independently analysed four papers and the criteria were further defined to ensure consistency (see Gouseti et al., [Citation2024](#)). While the 59 remaining papers had met the inclusion criteria, the team noted that a considerable number of papers only referenced ethics as a minor component of the article. This could include a single statement acknowledging there were ethical issues, or a short paragraph discussing ethical issues in the context of conducting research. The decision was made to further exclude papers with a nominal (\*n\* = 15) or marginal (\*n\* = 19) focus on ethics, which remained faithful to the established search criteria and the required ethical focus. This left 25 papers for inclusion for full analysis (see Table 4).

All four research team members independently read the final 25 papers selected for this review to identify emergent themes. ### Analysis
The analysis of the final 25 papers was undertaken using both a deductive and inductive approach. The broad deductive approach focused on identifying data and themes relevant to the two main research questions driving this review: i.e. (1) the distinctive primary challenges relating to AI ethics in K-12 education and (2) the key responses relating to the use of AI in K-12 education. This was followed by a more granular, inductive analysis of each article, resulting in a set of emergent themes under each of the above headings. Given the distinctive nature of the empirical and conceptual studies, a decision was made to analyse these separately using the same approach described above.
## Results
The systematic literature review process described in detail above resulted in the identification of 25 academic publications (all journal articles) exploring, to varying degrees, the issues associated with AI ethics in the context of K-12 education, along with some of the strategies and responses adopted by schools and teachers to harness AI as an effective educational tool. Fifteen of these publications were classified as empirical research studies based on primary data usually collected during workshops and practical trials of AI tools or curricula. The remaining 10 publications were classified as conceptual outputs (see Table 4). For purposes of clarity, each is described separately in the following sections.
### Empirical studies
In terms of geographical spread, the empirical studies are highly concentrated, with ten situated in the USA. (7) and China (3) and only four in other countries (Table 5). The paucity of regions and countries included may relate, amongst other reasons, to the novelty of using AI applications and tools in school settings and the reluctance or refusal of certain administrators and educational authorities to implement AI in compulsory school settings.

### Methodological approach
As shown below (Table 6), much of the research reported in the empirical studies was exploratory and small-scale in nature, which was to be expected given how recently many of the studies AI applications have been introduced into the K-12 context. ChatGPT is an excellent example (20). Seven studies utilised a design-based research design, which was often accompanied by a participatory workshop approach involving students, teachers and sometimes both (2,4,5,9,11,15). In some cases, participants were included as co-designers in the research design (4, 5, 11), testing and evaluating different AI applications, and more commonly, innovative curricula resources and courses (5, 7, 9, 11, 24). This type of participatory research design was well matched to the research’s AI subject matter, which was relatively recent in terms of development and application in educational settings. It also enabled the research teams to undertake rapid and iterative development of frameworks and approaches based on emerging data and trends. Accordingly, these studies tended to feature a broad mixture of data collection and analysis methods such as interviews, observations, documentary analysis and the collection of student artefacts produced during the workshops (Table 6). Testing designed to seek and identify correlations between factors that impacted ethical awareness and skills was relatively rare among this sample, although some studies did adopt this approach (11, 23).

### Scale and size
Large-scale studies were identified as having greater than 100 participants, with only three of the fifteen empirical papers meeting this criterion (Table 7). The largest of these was conducted in China, engaging over 3000 primary school teachers who completed an extensive survey to identify the factors associated with AI readiness, one of which was ethical awareness (23). A similar method was used in a study undertaken with middle and secondary school students in Nigeria, which also used a single survey instrument to gauge the impact on students of a single lesson taught about AI (17). Almost half of the studies, however, were much smaller in scale and scope (7/15) and were classified as small (2) or medium (5). In the following sections, we report only the findings from these publications that specifically discuss or relate to ethical issues and challenges associated with using AI. The data emerging from the reviewed papers will be presented thematically, based on our two research questions and the themes below that were identified during our collective readings.
### Challenges associated with AI ethics in the context of K-12 education
#### Students lack awareness and understanding of ethical issues associated with AI
The results from the SLR indicate that one of the greatest barriers preventing teachers from exploiting the educational benefits of AI applications and tools more fully, is the lack of ethical awareness and understanding demonstrated by students. In two of the studies which ran workshops to explore the use and societal issues associated with generative modelling tools, most participants could not differentiate between deep fake images and the real thing (2, 24); in some cases, even after completing various workshop activities. Another study found that middle school students frequently held a range of ‘naive’ or simplistic views about ethical issues linked to AI, such as the belief that AI cannot be biased or that there are no privacy issues associated with AI applications and data collection (10). Nonetheless, one study indicated that students were aware of the ethical issues and dangers associated with AI, but did not necessarily recognise or articulate these as ethical problems (15). One study even suggested ethical issues such as the processes by which AI algorithms are designed, developed and trained on biased datasets may be too complex even for older secondary students after comparing their pre-test and post-test results with comparable undergraduates who were administered the same evaluation instrument (11).
#### Teacher misconceptions and misunderstandings about AI and associated ethical issues
Running parallel with these studies describing students’ lack of awareness and understanding of ethical issues and AI, a range of empirical studies highlight the misconceptions and misunderstandings also held by teachers. These studies reveal how many teachers hold a range of misconceptions about AI that have profound ethical ramifications, such as the belief that AI cannot be biased (10), misunderstanding about how AI algorithms are generated and how they can generate distorted or biased results based on the datasets they are trained on (3, 4). Not surprisingly, perhaps, teachers who had received little or no professional learning in how AI works were most likely to hold such misconceptions and misunderstandings (23) and perhaps more worryingly, at least one study revealed how primary school teachers were also largely unaware of the ethical misconceptions held by their own students, seriously hindering their ability to deliver lessons that are appropriate for their students (15).
#### High levels of concern and anxiety among teachers about the ethical dangers of AI in education
Based on these levels of misconceptions and misunderstanding, it is not surprising that many of the studies in this SLR also highlighted high levels of concern and anxiety among teachers about the ethical challenges and dangers posed by AI and their own ability, or lack of it, to tackle these. Whilst a degree of scepticism and awareness about the ethical dangers posed by AI is critical for educators, a number of the reviewed studies demonstrated how high degrees of anxiety and concern are likely to act as a significant barrier in preventing the further take-up of AI by teachers. Amongst the most commonly espoused fears relates to cheating and plagiarism, particularly around generative tools like ChatGPT, with teachers expressing concerns that such AI tools will make students cognitively lazy and fears about data privacy and security (20, 23).
#### Inadequate professional learning and preparation for teachers to teach about ethical issues
Finally, one of the themes permeating a few of the empirical studies we reviewed (3, 15, 23) was the concern of teachers that they were ill-prepared to understand AI and the ethical issues associated with it. A lack of suitable professional development opportunities was cited by teachers who expressed concern and apprehension about how best to teach about or use AI itself or the ethical issues that stem from it (15). In one study, teachers also felt the problem was exasperated by how the curriculum is developed by outside agencies such as local politicians rather than involving teachers as co-designers, leaving them with a strong sense of disempowerment (15).
### Responses and strategies from schools and teachers for overcoming these challenges
#### Adopt a constructivist pedagogy to increase student’s ethical awareness of AI
By some considerable margin (10 of 15 studies), the adoption of what might be termed ‘constructivist pedagogies’ was identified as the most popular and effective strategy for helping students to understand the ethical challenges posed by AI and, perhaps more significantly, to develop the skills and strategies to enact them in practice. A few studies demonstrated how placing students in the role of AI designers and developers correlated with an increased understanding of the ethical challenges associated with these technologies and how best to approach them (4, 15). In one of these studies situated in a US. middle school, students worked with professional developers and researchers to undertake a range of computational practices such as designing, testing and debugging their own robot to better understand Critical Machine Learning (CML) and how to design ethically sound applications for marginalised and disadvantaged populations (4). In a similar study, researchers and designers adopted constructionist practices and used a Digital Story Writing (DSW) approach to help primary school students develop a deeper awareness of ethical issues such as biased and distorted datasets (7). Another study in the context of primary education reported that involving teachers as co-designers when developing AI Ethics curricula is likely to be more meaningful for students and more feasible for teachers to teach (15).
A theme that appeared several times in these constructivist approaches was the use of real-world scenarios and settings to make the activity more authentic and meaningful for participants (9,11, 24). This type of setting was strongly correlated with a deeper interest amongst students in the ethical aspects of the AI application. Finally, a least one of these studies designed approaches that included elements of gamification. One study in Denmark saw secondary school students using an AI application that had been developed to enable students to train their own datasets to identify potential voting patterns in elections. The study’s authors claimed that gamification elements were a significant factor in engaging young people in deeper levels of understanding around the ethical issues associated with this AI tool, such as disinformation and vote rigging (9).
#### Embed ethical awareness and skills in AI literacy courses and embed these across the curriculum
Whilst there is a growing recognition that AI literacy is a fundamental curriculum requirement for schools, the role and location of ethics within such literacy programmes are less clearly established. Early attempts to teach ethical awareness in isolation from real-world issues and problems, usually in single discipline areas such as computer science or mathematics, appear to have been less than effective (2, 23). The majority of studies identified in this SLR agree that ethical awareness and skills need to be embedded in all AI literacy programmes and are best located across the curriculum rather than in a discrete subject area such as computer studies or mathematics. However, one study appears to contradict these findings, demonstrating that students with higher computational knowledge about how AI works are more likely to have higher ethical awareness and skills (5).
In addition to embedding ethical awareness within AI literacy programmes, it was also noted in two of these studies that ethical issues should be taught explicitly by teachers if it is to be effective. One study that engaged older students in an AI game to better understand voting patterns and behaviours emphasised the need to teach about ethical issues explicitly and openly rather than assuming students would assimilate the ethical implications of using AI tools without further guidance (9). Indeed, the findings from this study proposed that educators should start teaching about AI from the perspective of ethics before moving on to the specific AI aspects of the tool or application. It was also noted that teaching about ethics, ideally through a scenario- based, problem-solving approach, could and should be started at an earlier age, even in the elementary curriculum, to ensure students had time to grasp and understand the implications of technology that already impact upon their lives and are likely to do more so in the future (10).
#### Develop pre-service and in-service CPD courses that focus on ethics and AI
Two of the studies established how teachers are generally well disposed to use and teach about AI in schools but are prevented from doing so due to their perceived lack of understanding (3, 15). They expressed concerns about how AI applications generated datasets and were largely unaware or unconcerned about the potential ethical challenges such as bias and distortion (3). These concerns highlight the importance and value of well-designed pre-service and in-service professional development for teachers focusing on societal and ethical issues associated with AI use rather than purely technical ones (15, 25). At least four of the empirical studies in this SLR emphasised the need for professional development, which explored the opportunities to develop new pedagogical practices, such as assessment (20), and enabled teachers to better conceptualise how to integrate the teaching of ethical issues using real-world problems and scenarios (15, 25). When AI was investigated from the perspective of teacher readiness, several factors, such as cognitive awareness and skills, were foregrounded, but vision was paramount. A strong correlation was identified between possessing a clear vision of what AI could achieve and using and teaching about ethics (23). This reinforces many of the findings in these studies that highlight the importance of a holistic approach to professional development for teachers that includes both technical and societal aspects of AI, such as ethics, and the importance of real-world, problem-solving pedagogical approaches.
### Conceptual studies
In contrast to the empirical studies reported above, the conceptual and philosophical articles focus on more specific ethical issues facing schools, teachers and students including privacy, data security, discrimination, bias and social inequalities associated with the potential use of AI in the classroom, whilst also bemoaning the lack of specific ethical guidance and frameworks (e.g. see paper 1) which was a common motif in the empirical studies.
Privacy and security concerns are the most prevalent issues cited in the conceptual papers, with seven of the ten highlighting these for specific attention (1, 8, 12, 13, 14, 16 and 22). Privacy and data security are frequently cited as major ethical issues that concern teachers and parents, primarily when AI applications are used to collect personal details about students, such as examinations and test performance, which could be used to inform future life predictions about individuals, such as career decisions (1,8). This is likely to intensify as a concern with the increasing use of remote testing and assessment using Artificial Intelligence Proctoring (AIP). AIP is used to scrutinise students to identify cheating (e.g. the presence of third parties) and to capture facial gestures and emotions that may reveal nefarious behaviours (13). This technique, whereby AI applications can capture and analyse facial gestures to reveal inner emotions, raises serious ethical concerns amongst students and teachers about the right to keep emotions private with concerns that such data about emotional well-being and states of happiness could also be commoditised by EdTech companies and others could also commoditise such data about emotional well-being and states of happiness, or could be stolen and exploited at a later date by cyber criminals (8, 12).
Allied to these concerns about privacy and data security is the issue of informed consent, which lies at the heart of most ethical frameworks. The increasing use of AI applications, such as facial recognition software (12), that capture a wide range of data about individuals raises serious concerns about how data might be used without the individual’s explicit consent. In some instances, such as the use of voice-activated platforms or technologies that students often use in the classroom, there are concerns that voice recognition software will be able to identify individual students and that this data could be stored and used for purposes that were not intended or granted permission (22).
Similarly, the issue of discrimination and bias generated by AI algorithms that are trained and developed on unrepresentative, distorted or deliberately biased datasets is highlighted in various papers (1, 13, 14, 22). This issue was raised both directly and indirectly in several conceptual papers with specific examples, such as the use of AIPs to detect cheating and malpractice in high-stakes remote examinations, discriminating against students with different colour skin or religious orientations (e.g. wearing headdress) due to use of algorithms trained on biased or unrepresentative datasets (13).
Further issues included social inequalities that are exaggerated and increased due to lack of technology equipment and internet access amongst lower socio-economic groups, restricting them from accessing the benefits of AI (1, 13, 14, 16). The ethical risks associated with deconstructing the traditional roles of teachers and students when AI is able to replace the teacher was identified in one paper (16) and the fact that AI is currently unable to use empathy as the basis for making ethical choices or decisions as humans can was emphasised in a different paper (22). The latter is especially concerning for school leaders who might be tempted to use AI to analyse large volumes of data and to make decisions based on it (e.g. allocation of scarce resources) without considering any ethical issues related to how AI makes informed decisions (22).
### Responses and strategies proposed in conceptual papers
Similar to the empirical articles reviewed in this paper, the conceptual articles also emphasise the importance of establishing a clear set of ethical principles to inform the design, management and deployment of AI applications and tools in the compulsory school sector. A few relate specifically to the role of school leaders, detailing how they might harness the potential benefits of AI more effectively whilst negating some of the issues and concerns raised previously (see 1, 22). Other issues include the need for accountability and transparency in how AI is used and how it has reached a particular outcome or decision (1, 14). Some other papers highlight the importance of privacy and data security (1, 14, 16) as well as the importance of ensuring the application and use of AI is inclusive and accessible to all (1, 14, 16, 18). Last, establishing that AI is designed and developed in a human-centred fashion that privileges the importance of human characteristics such as empathy and emotion rather than pure logic alone is also seen particularly pertinent to AI use in education (1, 14).
The importance of professional learning programmes to reinforce the effective use of such ethical frameworks is implicit throughout these articles. However, papers 8 and 22 highlight this explicitly, encouraging teachers to learn more about how AI works by collaborating with other educators across contexts and settings. Paper 8 encourages teachers to recognise AI dangers and benefits by asking inquiring questions, imagining what might be and focusing on learning to learn in order to maintain currency with the fast developments in this area. Equally important is the need to develop specific professional learning programmes for school leaders and managers so that AI can be used to ‘support decisioning making rather than replace it’ (22). This should enable schools and their local communities to develop more positive and better-informed responses to the growing use of AI whilst also developing procedures and processes to safeguard against potential ethical transgressions. For example, in using AIPs (13), an appropriate response might be to question if an AIP is essential and, if so, to pare down the scope of data captured (e.g. facial images) to what is absolutely essential. Schools must also ensure parents are better informed about how and why AI is likely to be used and the potential risks are, ensuring they are better informed to provide confirmatory consent about specific uses, rather than general consent which is the current norm (13).
Finally, the broader ramifications of AI in challenging structures and existing paradigms of school education are raised in two articles (16, 18). Whilst not strictly a response to how schools and teachers might respond to the challenges of AI, these articles focus attention on the possibility rather than probability, that AI might transform the dominant current structures and practices of schooling, such as the role of the teacher as expert and knowledge provider, given that AI tools like ChatGPT already herald this shift (16, 18). These articles suggest the place of teachers will still be necessary, but the role will shift significantly to emphasise moral and ethical values that students will need to consider when using the data and solutions offered by the AI tools and applications they take for granted (16).
## Discussion
The above analysis of the reviewed papers has provided evidence-based insights into the challenges underpinning AI ethics in the context of K-12 education and sheds light on the response of some schools so far. This section critically expounds conceptual challenges and their interconnection with international and national imperatives relating to AI use in K-12 education. Furthermore, it examines the complexities that underpin AI ethics implementation and how the roles of teachers and schools will be understood in an increasingly AI-dominated future.
### The complexity of conceptualising AI ethics in the context of K-12 education
While all papers reviewed identify AI ethics as an essential element of K-12 education, there is significant differentiation regarding how the notion of AI ethics is conceptualised and which ethical principles are seen as relevant for this phase of education. This does not come as a surprise, given the overall complexity of the field. As Jobin et al. ([Citation2019](#)) report, there are critical differences and substantive divergences in how ethical principles are interpreted, which principles should be prioritised and how they should be implemented. Indeed, the varying conceptualisations and foci of the reviewed papers appear to be guided at large by different international policy frameworks or literature, with some papers identifying a range of AI ethical principles and highlighting the multifaceted nature of AI ethics (for example, see Adams et al., [Citation2023](#); Davy Tsz Kit et al., [Citation2022](#); Kong et al., [Citation2018](#), [Citation2023](#)) while other papers focused on specific issues such as generative AI (Tlili et al., [Citation2023](#)), machine learning (Arastoopour et al. [Citation2022](#)), deep fakes (Ali et al., [Citation2021](#)) and emotional AI (McStay, [Citation2020a](#)).
Furthermore, the conceptualisation of AI ethics and the implementation of AI curricula are, to some extent, linked to the national context within which the reviewed studies were carried out. The geographical spread of these studies has, thus, potential importance as it may relate to the national AI guidance available as well as the national curricula and broader imperatives relating to AI use for and within K-12 education. While it is not possible to draw concrete or generalisable conclusions, it is relevant to note the dominance in this SLR of empirical studies from the United States (7) and China (3), with only four studies in other countries (South Korea, Denmark, Turkey and Nigeria). This is consistent with the findings of an SLR on AI use in K-12, which reported an increase in research in AI in K-12, with the United States, China and South Korea emerging as the top contributors in terms of number of publications (Martin et al., [Citation2024](#)). Similarly, a UNESCO ([Citation2022](#)) report that looked at K-12 AI curricula also revealed that out of the 193 Member States invited to complete a survey, only 11 countries reported that they had developed and endorsed K-12 AI curricula, and China and South Korea were two out of the four countries which had implemented AI curricula across all levels of K-12 education. The geographical spread of the reviewed publications may also relate to the increased interest of these countries in AI use in education and the top-down imperatives that prevail towards aligning education systems with future workforce demands. For instance, since 2017, China has published several strategic plans relevant to AI education, is actively implementing AI curricula at every level of schooling and is promoting various initiatives to enhance its AI education programme (Peterson et al., [Citation2021](#)).
The findings of our SLR also highlight the complex interplay between a philosophy of AI for education which espouses integration of AI into education for the enhancement of teaching and learning as social goods, and one commensurate with a neoliberal agenda which construes education as for AI, with priority given to ‘an AI-ready workforce and training more AI experts’ (Schiff, [Citation2022](#), p. 527). Accentuating training in AI functions can be seen to align with employability and neoliberal agendas surrounding the augmentation of human capital for future economic prosperity. For instance, according to UNESCO ([Citation2019](#), p. 19) education is perceived as having to play ‘a critical role in efforts to make future workforces AI-ready’. It is, thus, not surprising how some of the reviewed papers appear to be influenced by this rhetoric, claiming that ‘to best prepare students to be AI savvy, there is a need to integrate AI tools, skills, and lessons into the K-12 curriculum’ (Antonenko & Abramowitz, [Citation2023](#), p. 64) or that the AI curricula are implemented to prepare students for ‘applying and evaluating AI in their future careers’ (Kong et al., [Citation2023](#), p. 4704). In contrast, in some other reviewed studies, the implementation of AI curricula is seen as a means of empowering children growing up with AI to navigate the continuously changing sociotechnical landscape (Williams et al., [Citation2023](#)) and fostering pro-activeness in being critical and responsible consumers, creators and stakeholders of technology (Ali et al., [Citation2021](#)). To this end, aligning the goal of creating ‘resilient economies’ (Dignum, [Citation2021](#), p. 88) with those values of education, which emphasise teacher professionalism and the cultivation of critical citizens, remains a challenge and requires some further conceptual adumbration.
### The challenges underpinning AI ethics implementation in K-12 education
As our findings suggest, the main challenges identified relate to students’ lack of awareness and understanding of ethical issues underpinning AI use and teachers’ misconceptions about AI and associated ethical issues. In, addition, there are evident anxieties around potential maleficence, and emerging gaps in teacher preparation and professional learning for teaching about the ethics of AI use. These warrant the development of a distinctive framework for AI ethical literacy for K-12 education, which extends beyond mere technical AI proficiency. While understanding and knowing how to use AI ethically may currently feature in existing AI literacy frameworks (Ng et al., [Citation2021](#)) as well as in political, professional and academic discourses around AI in education (Holmes & Porayska-Pomsta, [Citation2023](#)), our findings highlight the striking disjuncture between these expectations and the realities of everyday schooling. As such, students and teachers at large lag in developing their critical understanding of the ethical issues that underpin AI use.
Furthermore, teachers’ misconceptions and lack of awareness around the ethical issues of AI, reported in the reviewed papers, epitomised their limited understanding of the multifaceted nature of AI use and their insufficient preparedness to support their students with developing AI ethics literacy. This aligns with previous studies, highlighting how teachers’ insufficient preparation can be the primary barrier to fostering students’ digital competencies (Voogt et al., [Citation2013](#)) and critical digital literacies (Gouseti et al., [Citation2023](#)). As with preceding educational technology (ed tech) advancements, although teachers are seen to be at the front lines of implementing new tools and developing their own and their students’ digital skills, relevant strategies and policies supporting teachers with this remain lacking.
It is, therefore, not surprising that many of the studies in this SLR reported high levels of concern and anxiety among teachers about the ethical challenges posed by AI use and their own ability, or rather lack of it, to tackle these. This is consistent with the findings of Henderson and Corry’s ([Citation2021](#)) SLR, which showed how the fast-paced technological changes in educational practices and the changing expectations for teachers can contribute to an increase in anxiety that impacts technology integration in the classroom. There is, therefore, a pressing need to embed ethical awareness and skills in AI literacy courses not only across the K-12 curriculum but also into pre-service teacher training programmes as well as into in-service teachers’ continuous professional development. Indeed, the importance of training programmes to reinforce the effective use of existing K-12 ethical frameworks is implicit throughout the articles reviewed in this SLR. As the findings of Chiu et al. ([Citation2022](#)) attest, such training has the potential to empower teachers and students alike to gain critical perspectives on the multifaceted nature of AI and the profound ethical and other implications that can come with AI use.
### Teachers and schools’ role in an AI-dominated future
While there is emerging research regarding the focus and content of relevant AI literacy courses and curricula for K-12 education (see Casal-Otero et al., [Citation2023](#); Ng et al., [Citation2022](#)), it is not within the scope of this paper to explore these. However, our SLR underscores the importance of the teachers’ role in an AI-dominated future. As our analysis of the various responses and approaches to teaching the ethics of AI suggests, rather than serving as trainers in AI, teachers should embrace a more profound role as pedagogues who remain abreast of ethical dilemmas presented when educating children. To this end, new pedagogical methods and ways of thinking about how best to teach AI ethics in the context of K-12 education are required. Moreover, as previous research in the area of ed tech has shown while technological competences are relevant, educators need to able to design learning experiences grounded in sound pedagogical principles (Glover et al., [Citation2016](#); Starkey, [Citation2020](#)). Furthermore, appreciating where ethical issues are raised requires the ability to identify and remain critical of the human-derived subjectivities that constitute the systems themselves, hence the emphasis in these articles upon explicating stakeholder or agents’ influences. As Akgun and Greenhow ([Citation2022](#)) emphasise, recognition of the value-laden nature of AI tools, regardless of developers’ intent, is pivotal to teaching and learning about the ethical use of AI.
To support students with developing a critical understanding of AI ethics several pedagogical approaches are posited in the reviewed articles, which provided a more detailed analysis of the pedagogy surrounding ethics. First, there is agreement that AI ethics needs to be taught explicitly (Davy Tsz Kit et al., [Citation2022](#); Kaspersen et al., [Citation2022](#)), rather than being a tacit, nebulous construct diffused across a cluster of subjects such as Computer Science and STEM. Indeed, the detachment of the humanistic aspects from functional components, such as the development of AI tools, is a matter of historical, arbitrary boundaries (Arastoopour et al., [Citation2022](#)). This is countermanded by recent calls, discerned across the reviewed articles (Adams et al., [Citation2023](#); Arastoopour et al., [Citation2022](#); Kasperse et al., [Citation2022](#); Sun & Ye, [Citation2023](#)). These articles call for contextualised approaches instead of teaching AI ethics like an add-on, mere afterthought or set of rules. While it is necessary to teach ethics as embedded in human activity networks, with stakeholder interests delineated, that is not to downplay the importance of technical understanding: knowledge and practice of AI precedes students’ optimal understanding of ethics and technical proficiency on the part of teachers facilitates identification of ethical risks (Bae et al., [Citation2022](#)).
The initial barrier concerning teachers’ perception of disconnection from curriculum development is ameliorated through the aforementioned constructivist methods, which enable teachers to demonstrate the analytical processes within AI systems and how decisions are executed (Kong et al., [Citation2023](#)). Moreover, several articles (Ottenbreit-Leftwich et al., [Citation2022](#); Kim et al., [Citation2023](#); Wang et al., [Citation2023](#)) highlight the notion of readiness as far as K-12 learners are concerned since deep learning requires a secure understanding of abstract concepts such as bias, which Kong et al. ([Citation2023](#)) argue can be more challenging for such an age group. This is to be coupled with consideration of which aspects of the black box to avoid unnecessary cognitive overload and direct students’ attention to higher-order issues (Kaspersen et al., [Citation2022](#)). Thus, a minimum working knowledge extends to comprehending the minutiae of the decisioning processes integral to AI systems (McStay, [Citation2020a](#); Zhang et al., [Citation2022](#)). Pertinently, the recognition of algorithms as opinions (Akgun & Greenhow, [Citation2022](#)) represents a fundamental step in enabling K12-aged students to comprehend how algorithms are saturated with human values. In addition, a proffered entry point, which negates the risk of overwhelming learners, involves enabling them to take the perspective of the agent when understanding the decisioning processes to which such agents have contributed (Zhang et al., [Citation2022](#)).
AI’s extension across societal and even affective domains has brought fresh challenges around how a responsible and ethical use of AI can be enacted. This is particularly important given the potentially manipulative capacities of AI algorithms in school environments (Baker & Hawn, [Citation2022](#)). Since issues around child welfare and AI use represent a newly emerging area, a protectionist approach has come to the fore to recapture previously missed considerations around the multifarious ways in which aspects such as privacy can be undermined for vulnerable groups in particular. Rather than schools imposing protectionism, the focus should be on supporting children to develop a critical understanding of how AI works. This must include the potential of AI algorithms to replicate and exacerbate existing biases (Miao et al., [Citation2021](#)) and relevant policies should be developed that focus on facilitating healthy engagement with AI (World Economic Forum, [Citation2019](#)). Indeed, more work needs to be done to recentre students’ intrinsic rights to ‘educational privacy’ (Mita, [Citation2023](#), p. 1548).
There is also a wariness and mistrust stemming from a perception of AI as encroaching into domains considered distinctively human (Hopcan et al., [Citation2023](#)) and its ability to emulate and even surpass human capacities for thinking. Rendering visible just how human thought has shaped AI systems represents one method through which educational organisations can remain abreast of AI’s development and its enmeshment in sociotechnical networks. A resolution to anxieties around the subordination of human values is to maintain transparency of AI’s integral decisioning processes and connections between human-derived inputs, processes and outputs, which thereby facilitates accountability (Dignum, [Citation2021](#)). Although the data inputted by human stakeholders, along with the models for decision-making that they generate and embed into AI systems, can be traceable (Dignum, [Citation2021](#)), Adams et al. ([Citation2023](#)) offer a caution, at a more theoretical level, regarding the potential submersion of AI into sociotechnical networks.
The positive aspects of AI use can be occluded if there is a sole pedagogical focus on challenges such as bias and privacy. These have occupied central territory in teaching ethics in AI, particularly in the wake of AI proctoring and the potential for infringement of students’ rights (Mita, [Citation2023](#)). However, approaches which take AI ethics as embedded in sociotechnical systems indicate the need for a widened remit to encompass an explicit focus on human values and equity issues (Tlili et al., [Citation2023](#)). This includes students’ exploration and critical thinking around issues such as surveillance and autonomy, which become ever more pressing in the face of ‘predictive systems’ (Akgun & Greenhow, [Citation2022](#), p. 437). Beyond its potential harms, AI can have beneficial uses, such as enabling assistive reading (Adams et al., [Citation2023](#)), along with the ability to help combat social inequalities (Schiff, [Citation2022](#)). An emphasis placed upon which aspects can be shared between machines and humans can potentially foster ‘reimagining teaching philosophies’ (Tlili et al., [Citation2023](#), p. 19), whereby chatbots share the load in terms of provision of new content for students’ subsequent critical analysis. This indicates a complementary relationship between the teacher, who can maintain the human relational elements with the student, and AI.
## Conclusion
In this article, we have carried out a systematic literature review of emerging research in the ethics of AI in K-12 education. Our rigorous search of eight databases and the subsequent filtering processes identified 25 peer-reviewed studies that met our inclusion and exclusion criteria. Overall, this review highlighted the complexity of conceptualising and operationalising AI ethics in K-12 education and reported on the responses so far. Our findings revealed that embedding AI ethics courses in compulsory schooling is often informed by national imperatives and, in some instances, forms part of wider AI literacy programmes driven by neoliberal agendas relating to training a future AI-ready workforce.
One of the main challenges this SLR identified was students‘ and teachers’ narrow understanding and misconceptions about ethical concerns underpinning AI use. A significant component relates to teachers’ insufficient preparedness to support their students with developing AI ethics literacy. Given the speed with which AI technologies are becoming part of everyday life and education, this lack of awareness about AI ethics is highly concerning, albeit not surprising. As the long history of ed tech implementation has shown (Cuban, [Citation1986](#)), this is not the first instance when a digital tool has received heightened attention and intense promotion by industry and policy without critical consideration of the digital literacies and pedagogies that are also required for its meaningful and responsible adoption in educational settings. Indeed, the various challenges identified in this article also bring into light the disjuncture between top-down imperatives to embed AI in educational settings and teachers‘ and students’ unfamiliarity with the ethical implications of AI use.
However, in contrast to previous types of ed tech, which could be seen as somehow more benign, many of the issues surrounding the (mis) use of AI in education are intricately linked with broader issues of power, inequalities and exclusion (Verdegem, [Citation2021](#)). Given the complex and potentially troubling ethical ramifications of AI use, this SLR highlights the urgent need to develop relevant and age-appropriate approaches, curricula and pedagogies to support teachers and students with cultivating AI ethics literacy. Our results also indicate that technical knowledge alone is insufficient for grasping the complexity of AI ethical challenges. Instead, a constructivist, multidisciplinary approach where students have practical experience in designing, developing and using AI tools can be more conducive. These insights are vital for global curriculum developers, policymakers and education stakeholders.
While the articles included in our review provided a comprehensive and trustworthy set of data, we recognise there may be some limitations. It is possible that we may not have captured all articles in this area due to our inclusion/exclusion criteria and databases, and because only manuscripts in English were reviewed. Another limitation is that due to the small and emerging nature of the existing body of research in this area, the majority of the empirical studies reviewed were related to secondary schools, and only three studies in the context of primary education were identified. Furthermore, although four researchers were involved in determining the inclusion/exclusion criteria and participated in the double-masked screening process and the analysis process, and took part in multiple team meetings, interrater reliability was not calculated.
Pertinently, this is an emerging field of study; hence, further research is required to gain richer insights into teachers and students’ perceptions of AI ethics across all levels of compulsory education and a more comprehensive range of national contexts. Furthermore, the human and machine inter-relationship represents an area of conceptual incompleteness, thus, further work is warranted that addresses the degree to which responsibility can be the domain of machine, human or shared between both. The utilisation of post-human theoretical tools, which are outlined tentatively across conceptual articles reviewed, would advance understandings around where teachers’ and AI’s moral agency join. This would contribute to the questions that rapid AI advancement raises regarding the teacher’s role and whether teachers can transfer some of the role of guardian of children’s moral development to a non-human.
In summary, while there is a growing body of research regarding the ethics of AI use in Higher Education and broader social contexts, this is the first study providing systematic insights into how K-12 educators and students perceive the ethics of AI and how teaching about AI ethics has so far been conceptualised and operationalised in the context of K-12 education. Our study is particularly timely given current debates by academics, policymakers and politicians globally about the ethical implications of AI use in K-12 education. The reported findings are significant for the development of more explicit AI ethical literacy frameworks regarding K-12 curricula and teachers’ professional learning.
