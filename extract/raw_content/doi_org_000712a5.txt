## Abstract
Artificial Intelligence (AI) technology is now an integral part of our daily life and society. AI offers numerous affordances with potential positive and negative impact on society. K-12 educators are responsible to prepare the future generations to harness the benefits of AI ethically, responsibly, safely, and critically. Teachers need sound knowledge, skills, and dispositions to implement ethics-driven AI literacy education. To achieve this goal, there is a pressing need to identify and operationalize the competencies that teachers need to foster AI literacy. We conducted a larger systematic literature review (SLR), which reviewed empirical studies focused on AI literacy published in the last ten years (2014–2024) to explore and consolidate all competencies for K-12 teachers. This paper reports on teachers’ ethical competencies identified from this SLR. The review of 76 papers included in the final dataset unveils that the ethical AI competencies are interdependent and co-occur in a given context such that teachers may need a combination of knowledge, skills, and dispositions to successfully implement AI literacy education. The findings revealed that teachers need (1) the knowledge competencies of AI ethics issues, limitations of AI, socio-political awareness, and human in the loop, (2) skills of AI transparency and explainability, critical analysis of AI, and culturally responsive and inclusive pedagogy, and (3) the dispositions of AI ethics sensitivity, critical attitude towards AI, and being inclusive and culturally responsive. These ethical competencies can inform teacher preparation and professional development programs for teachers to leverage AI technologies ethically in education.
## Introduction
As artificial intelligence (AI) becomes integral to our daily lives, its societal impact is being increasingly scrutinized (Chaudhry & Kazim, 2022; Chen et al., 2020). Improper use of AI can lead to ethical issues, such as misinformation, hallucinations, bias against underrepresented communities, a growing digital divide, and social inequities (Chaudhry & Kazim, 2022; Chen et al., 2020). Hence, non-AI professionals need to understand the range and scope of AI, its social impact and ethical concerns, while also developing skills to effectively collaborate with AI (Chiu, 2024). In education, AI holds the potential to support teachers and enable learner-centered instruction (Chen et al., 2020; Miao & Cukurova, 2024). However, it also poses risks, including algorithmic bias, privacy violations, and widening the digital divide (Fukuda‐Parr & Gibbons, 2021). Biased data fed into AI systems can perpetuate unfair prejudice and discrimination which may harm students' well-being (Bingham, 2023; Chen et al., 2020; Lindgren, 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023; Prothero, 2023). In addition, more recently, with the proliferation of generative AI (GenAI) tools in education and the widespread use of GenAI by students for their coursework and assignments, there are ethical concerns around academic integrity, over-reliance on GenAI, and the inequitable implementation of GenAI (Eaton, 2025; Nguyen & Truong, 2025). These challenges highlight the urgent need for a paradigm shift in education, where teachers are equipped to address AI's opportunities and risks and ensure equitable and effective integration of AI.
AI can transform education by redefining teaching and learning practices (Ng et al., 2023). Researchers identify three areas of AI integration in education: "AI in Education (AIED)," which enhances teaching through process automation and personalized learning; "AI education," which focuses on teaching AI principles and technical concepts; and "AI literacy education," which promotes a general understanding of AI, its creative use, and critical analysis (Grover, 2024; Ng et al., 2023). AI literacy education includes competencies like collaborating with AI, utilizing it for tasks, and critically evaluating its functions (Long & Magerko, 2020; Wong et al., 2020). While AI education and AI literacy education are often used interchangeably, the key difference between the two is the higher emphasis of AI education on developing technical knowledge and skills in AI (Grover, 2024). AI literacy education, on the other hand, focuses mainly on developing just enough technical know-how to empower the learner to be a critical user of AI. The emphasis on developing AI literacy in K-12 education is growing as seen in the guidelines for AI literacy education which stress on strengthening an ethics-driven AI literacy education (Biagini, 2025; Chiu & Chai, 2020; Lindgren, 2023; Ng et al., 2021; Siau & Wang, 2020; UNESCO, 2021).
An ethics-driven AI literacy education requires the use of teaching approaches that focus on nurturing responsible AI users rather than solely on technology (Williams et al., 2023). Ethics-driven AI literacy education prepares students to make ethical decisions and choices while using AI systems, critically analyze AI output, and be cognizant of the potential harm done by errors and biases of the AI system in addition to having the knowledge and skills needed to interact and collaborate with AI (Miao & Shiohira, 2024). Teachers play a crucial role in recognizing AI's potential biases and ethical implications, using it critically in classrooms, and fostering ethical discussions among students to bridge educational divides (Gouseti et al., 2024; Miao & Cukurova, 2024; Miao & Shiohira, 2024). Thus, teachers should be sufficiently prepared for the implementation of ethics-driven AI literacy education (Miao & Cukurova, 2024). However, research examining teachers' ethical competencies in AI literacy remains limited and highlights that teachers hold misconceptions about AI and its ethical issues (Miao & Cukurova, 2024; Gouseti et al., 2024). Thus, this study systematically analyzes previous research on AI literacy education conducted with teachers to identify ethical competencies that teachers need to successfully implement ethics-driven AI literacy in K-12 education.

- ### [Unleashing human potential: An artificial intelligence competency framework for K–12 education](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X26000172)
2026, Computers and Education Artificial Intelligence
Show abstract
This study explores strategic approaches for integrating artificial intelligence (AI) into K–12 education to prepare learners for the AI era. It addresses two critical questions: 1) What are the key components of AI competency frameworks identified in current research? 2) What framework can guide the effective, responsible integration of AI in K–12 while prioritising human values? We conducted a scoping review of 54 studies, identifying three core student competencies: foundational AI knowledge, practical and cognitive skills of using AI, and ethical awareness. However, existing frameworks place limited emphasis on developing human values. Consequently, we propose a three-component developmental framework—Understanding, Using, and Unleashing—to guide AI integration. The ‘Understanding’ component cultivates a conceptual understanding of AI, while ‘Using’ emphasizes practical and cognitive skills of using AI to enhance learning. Finally, ‘Unleashing’ highlights AI's potential to empower personal growth, and fostering a spiritual self capable of making value-based decisions and do good. This framework aims to prepare learners to contribute meaningfully to future society. Schools must teach students to use AI for empowerment and good, avoiding mere reliance or shortcuts. Through practical activities, education should guide learners to unleash their potential and harness technology for spiritual self-development.
- ### [STEM education: Understanding secondary students’ epistemic cognition in the design process with the support of a personalized multi-agent system](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0360131525002726)
2026, Computers and Education
Show abstract
The engineering design process is a vital component of integrated STEM education, where students' design practices both shape and are shaped by their epistemic cognition (EC). Nonetheless, research on secondary students' EC in STEM contexts is limited, and implementing personalized STEM instruction that accommodates diverse students' interests and needs poses significant challenges. Harnessing a Multi-Agent System (MAS) afforded by Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) offers a promising pathway to address this gap. Grounded in the design thinking model of \_E\_ DIPT (Empathize-Define-Ideate-Prototype-Test), this study developed a \_P\_ ersonalized \_MAS\_, namely EPMAS, to support secondary students' STEM project-based design process. Twenty students, grouped into dyads, participated in a five-day STEM winter camp. We sought to explore students' EC within the EPMAS-supported design process and their perceptions of the system. Data were collected through in-depth group interviews, supplemented with student artifacts and interaction log data, and analyzed using theoretical thematic analysis. Findings revealed students' engagement with epistemic aims, ideals, and reliable processes, reflecting their developed understanding of engineering design. Contrary to common concerns about over-reliance on GAI, students demonstrated high epistemic agency, critically employing EPMAS's stepwise guidance as part of their epistemic repertoire to pursue creative solutions. These results suggest that EPMAS-supported authentic STEM learning holds promise for broader school adoption.
- ### [Co-constructing adaptive lesson plans with GenAI: Pre-service teachers' Intelligent-TPACK and prompt engineering strategies](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0360131525002532)
2026, Computers and Education
Show abstract
Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) technologies present new opportunities for teachers to design adaptive and student-centered instruction. However, the educational value of GenAI depends not only on technical usage but also on teachers' ability to formulate pedagogically meaningful prompts. Prompting strategies are not isolated from teachers′ prior knowledge and skills. Less is known about how pre-service teachers′ AI-related knowledge influences prompt engineering strategies, in turn leading to meaningful adaptive lesson plans. Considering this gap, we design an instructional task for pre-service teachers to generate adaptive lesson plans with the help of GenAI. Prior to the task, we collected data about their AI-related skills, namely AI literacy and Intelligent-TPACK. The prompts were qualitatively analyzed based on the phases of the Knowledge Construction (KC) Framework. Then, we explored the pedagogical value of adaptive lesson plans through a rubric in terms of three indicators: student agency, adaptive strategies, and flexible tools. PLS-SEM analysis revealed that as long as pre-service teachers have AI-specific technological and pedagogical knowledge, they formulate higher phases of prompts based on the KC framework. Our analysis showed that prompts from higher phases generated more adaptive lesson plans in terms of student agency, adaptive strategies, and flexible tools. We also found an indirect effect of Intelligent-TPK on adaptive lesson plans. This study highlights that effective prompt engineering is a pedagogical act shaped by teachers’ knowledge, not merely a technical command. It also underscores the importance of embedding AI-specific pedagogical training in teacher education. By conceptualizing prompts as epistemic moves, we offer new insights into how teachers and GenAI can collaborate to produce responsive and inclusive learning experiences.
