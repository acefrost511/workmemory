Abstract
--------

Providing high-quality feedback on student writing is essential yet increasingly difficult due to rising class sizes and limited instructional capacity. Generative AI (GenAI) offers a promising and scalable alternative, but its effectiveness compared to traditional teacher feedback, particularly across different prompting techniques, remains uncertain. This study employed a quantitative, randomized three-group experimental design with 70 graduate students to compare the effects of teacher feedback and GenAI feedback generated using two prompting techniques: Zero-shot and chain-of-thought (CoT). The study explored how these feedback sources affect feedback quality and students’ uptake during essay revision. It involved a two-stage process in which students first wrote an argumentative essay and then revised it based on the feedback received. Feedback and essay quality were evaluated using a rubric based on Toulmin’s model of argumentation and analysed using inferential statistical methods. Results showed that CoT prompting produced higher quality feedback than both Zero-shot prompting and teacher feedback, suggesting that stepwise reasoning in CoT aligns GenAI outputs more closely with the cognitive demands of argumentative writing. However, this higher feedback quality did not lead to significantly greater improvements in revisions. Teacher feedback, although rated lower in quality, resulted in comparable gains in essay quality. In addition, GenAI feedback quality was significantly associated with students’ initial essay quality, whereas teacher feedback quality showed no such association. These findings indicate that feedback quality alone is insufficient to enhance writing outcomes; rather, students’ engagement with and uptake of feedback play a critical role. Overall, the results highlight the potential of hybrid intelligent feedback systems in which teachers support students in interpreting and applying GenAI feedback to meaningfully improve their writing.

Introduction
------------

The ability to articulate ideas through well-written text is widely recognised as essential for success in both academic and professional settings (Baker et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR8)). In academic contexts, writing often takes the form of assignments such as essays, research papers, reports, and reflections, serving as a means of formative assessment. These tasks allow students to foster critical thinking (Butcher, [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR11)) and enhance essential communication skills (Bora, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR10)). In addition, writing assignments support the development of general and domain-specific learning (Alexander et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR2)). Supporting this view, results from a large-scale, multi-institutional study suggest that effective writing practices are positively associated with deep approaches to learning, as well as with perceived gains in learning and development (Anderson et al., [2015](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR5)).

Among academic writing tasks, argumentative essays are particularly common in higher education (Latifi et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR52)). Such assignments require students to investigate a topic, evaluate evidence, take a position, and construct a coherent, well-reasoned argument (Noroozi et al., [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR63)). However, despite their pedagogical value, many students struggle with writing argumentative essays, often producing texts with weak structure, limited reasoning, and insufficient elaboration (Kellogg & Whiteford, [2009](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR45); Latifi et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR52)). These difficulties may arise from a lack of understanding of what constitutes a strong argumentative essay (Bacha, [2010](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR7)), challenges in applying this knowledge (Noroozi et al., [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR63)), or difficulties in navigating disciplinary conventions (Noroozi et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR64)). Furthermore, argumentative writing is a cognitively demanding, high-level learning activity requiring advanced reasoning and critical evaluation (Conner et al., [2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR20); Fan & Chen, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR26)). Taken together, these challenges highlight the need for structured support to help students strengthen their argumentative writing skills.

Feedback provision is a well-established means of supporting students’ writing development (Henderson et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR37)), helping them bridge the gap between current and desired performance by clarifying expectations and guiding revision (Nicol & Macfarlane‐Dick, [2006](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR60)). Traditionally, teachers and peers have provided such feedback (Wu et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR88)); however, recent advances in generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) now offer immediate, structured, and a more scalable alternatives (Usher, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR76)). GenAI tools can reduce teachers’ workloads (Wu et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR87)) while improving the accessibility and consistency of feedback (Henderson et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR36)). Despite this potential, research on the quality and relevance of GenAI feedback remains inconclusive (Çağlar‐Özhan et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR12)), likely due to differences in language models used (Dai et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR22)) and, more importantly, in the prompts crafted to guide GenAI responses (Wu et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR87)), a process referred to as “prompt engineering” (Lee & Palmer, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR53)).

Although scholars have emphasised the importance of effective prompt design (Çağlar‐Özhan et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR12)) and the need to align prompts with the cognitive demands of the task (Chen et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR16)), limited research has systematically examined how different prompting strategies influence the quality and pedagogical usefulness of GenAI feedback, particularly for cognitively demanding tasks such as argumentative writing. Addressing this gap, the present study investigates how GenAI feedback generated under two prompting conditions, namely *Zero-shot* and *Chain of Thought* (CoT), compares with teacher feedback in terms of (1) feedback quality and (2) usefulness for students’ essay revisions, as reflected in their uptake of feedback. Furthermore, by implementing the study in Persian, a linguistically underrepresented language in large language model (LLM) training, this research extends current understanding of GenAI feedback beyond English-dominant contexts. Together, these efforts aim to demonstrate how GenAI can be effectively used as a feedback source to support students’ argumentative writing in higher education, particularly in underrepresented linguistic settings.

Related work and theoretical framing
------------------------------------

This study has an exploratory nature in which an inductive reasoning approach was adopted to identify the research gap. Rather than conducting an exhaustive review, we synthesised recent studies to highlight key patterns and limitations that shaped the study’s focus. Building on this synthesis, this section reviews three interconnected strands of literature. The first examines feedback as a mechanism to support writing, outlining how high-quality feedback on written assignments is defined in the current study. The second explores GenAI as a feedback source, highlighting its potential while noting persistent challenges related to precision and pedagogical alignment. The third addresses prompt engineering, a critical factor influencing the quality of feedback generated by GenAI.

### Feedback to support writing

Feedback is defined as “information communicated to the learner that is intended to modify his or her thinking or behavior to improve learning” (Shute, [2008](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR71), p. 153), aiming to bridge the gap between students’ current performance and the desired level (Sadler, [1989](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR68)). Extensive research confirms the effectiveness of feedback in enhancing learning outcomes (Morris et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR58)) and improving students’ academic writing (Alvarez et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR4)). Specifically, in the context of argumentative essays, studies showed that feedback can help university students enhance essay quality, acquire domain-specific knowledge, and strengthen peer-learning processes (Latifi et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR52); Noroozi et al., [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR63)).

Despite this potential, not all feedback leads to meaningful improvement. Its effectiveness largely depends on the extent to which it fosters students’ understanding of their performance and how to improve it, thereby increasing the likelihood of implementation (Nelson & Schunn, [2009](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR59); Wu & Schunn, [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR89)). To facilitate this process, high-quality feedback should contain features that make its purpose specific and actionable. Research has demonstrated that localized comments and concrete solutions can strengthen students’ understanding and implementation, whereas overly elaborate explanations may obscure meaning and reduce clarity (Nelson & Schunn, [2009](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR59)). Similarly, focused and substantively oriented comments often lead to higher-quality revisions (Patchan et al., [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR67)), while clear and concise solutions help sustain engagement with feedback (Wu & Schunn, [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR89)). Moreover, suggestive feedback has been found to encourage reflection and foster deeper argumentative improvement (Alvarez et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR4)).

Therefore, effective feedback should identify specific problems in relation to expected standards or learning goals and provide concrete suggestions for improvement (Nicol & Macfarlane‐Dick, [2006](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR60)). However, offering such high-quality and individualized feedback consistently can be challenging in typical educational settings. Teachers remain the primary source of feedback (van der Kleij & Adie, [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR79)), yet factors such as large class sizes, heavy workloads, and modular curricula may limit opportunities for timely and detailed responses (Maringe & Sing, [2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR55); Yan et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR90)). Consequently, teacher feedback may not always be able to provide the depth or frequency needed to fully support students’ ongoing learning and revision processes.

### GenAI as a feedback source

In response to the challenges university teachers face in providing feedback on students’ written assignments, GenAI has been proposed as a scalable and accessible alternative feedback source (Farrokhnia et al., [2025a](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR28); Soleimani et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR73)). A systematic review by Lee and Moore ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR51)) concluded that GenAI enhances feedback timeliness, personalization, and learner motivation while reducing teacher workload. In a multilevel meta-analysis, Fleckenstein et al. ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR29)) reported a moderate effect size for automated feedback on written assignments across 84 samples. Banihashem et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR9)) evaluated a GenAI tool powered by GPT-3.5 and found that it provided more descriptive feedback, particularly on argumentative writing, than peer reviewers. Dai et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR22)) demonstrated that feedback generated by a GPT-4-based system was more readable, structured, and pedagogically aligned than feedback provided by teachers. In domain-specific writing tasks, Jürgensmeier and Skiera ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR44)) reported that GPT-4-generated feedback closely mirrored expert evaluations. Similarly, Usher ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR76)) found that a GPT-3.5-based tool delivered more detailed and structured feedback than peer reviewers.

However, despite these encouraging developments, important limitations of GenAI feedback remain. Comparative studies consistently indicate that such feedback often lacks diagnostic precision and contextual nuance. For instance, Dai et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR22)) found that GPT-3.5 produced feedback with lower alignment to effective pedagogical principles compared to teacher-generated feedback. Similar results have emerged in writing contexts, where GenAI feedback, though descriptively rich, was less effective at identifying specific problems and lacked the critical nuance found in peer or instructor input (Banihashem et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR9)). Usher ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR76)) similarly observed that while feedback generated by GenAI tools often provided more detailed insights and specific suggestions than peer feedback, it occasionally included irrelevant or contradictory elements. In contrast, human feedback tended to be more personalized and context-sensitive. Similarly, Guo et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR33)) reported that a substantial portion of GenAI feedback contained excessive praise or unwarranted inferences, which is consistent with earlier findings by Guo and Wang ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR34)). In the specific context of argumentative writing, Jansen et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR41)) found that only 59% of participants rated feedback generated by a GPT-3.5-powered tool as useful, compared to 88% for feedback from human experts.

These shortcomings have been attributed not only to model limitations but also to how feedback is elicited. While GPT-4 generally outperforms earlier GPT versions in accuracy, consistency, and educational alignment (Dai et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR22); Jürgensmeier & Skiera, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR44)), its effectiveness still largely depends on the quality of the input it receives. For example, Jürgensmeier and Skiera ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR44)) reported that GPT-4’s feedback accuracy significantly improved only when correct exercise solutions were included in the prompt. This is in line with previous findings showing that poorly structured prompts can lead to generic or misleading feedback, underscoring the importance of prompt engineering (Evmenova et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR25); Jukiewicz, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR43)).

### Prompt engineering

Prompt engineering, also referred to as prompt design, prompt programming, or simply prompting, is an emerging discipline within the field of AI, with a particular emphasis on optimizing human–LLM interactions (Oppenlaender et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR65)). It serves as a “steering mechanism” (Cain, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR96), p. 51) through which human users shape AI behavior by carefully crafting and structuring input prompts to elicit accurate, relevant, and contextually appropriate responses (Singh et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR72)). A growing body of research has demonstrated that prompt formulation can significantly influence the quality and content of outputs, even when the same underlying model is used (Chen et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR17); Wei et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR83)). Importantly, this impact extends beyond general applications into educational contexts (Chen et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR16)), influencing outcomes such as automated essay scoring (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR50)) and facilitating complex educational tasks like scientific project planning (Knoth et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR48)).

Prompt engineering strategies can be broadly categorized by their function and the level of guidance provided to the model. A key distinction exists between approaches requiring minimal task-specific training, such as Zero-shot and Few-shot prompting, and those that explicitly model reasoning, such as CoT prompting (Sahoo et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR69)). In Zero-shot prompting, the model receives a simple task description, often with contextual and/or role-based instructions (e.g., “Act as an academic writing teacher and provide feedback based on the uploaded rubric”), but no labeled examples. The model generates a response based solely on its general knowledge (Chen et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR17)). In contrast, Few-shot prompting includes explicit input–output examples to guide the model by demonstrating how similar tasks have been completed. For instance, solved problems precede a new one to help the model infer task structure and expectations. CoT prompting is a more advanced technique that prompts the model to “think through” a problem step-by-step, enhancing logical coherence and accuracy (Wei et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR83)). CoT can be embedded in both Zero-shot and Few-shot contexts and is especially effective for tasks requiring complex, structured reasoning (Kojima et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR49)).

The effectiveness of prompting strategies depends largely on the cognitive demands of the task (Chen et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR16)). For straightforward tasks such as classification or factual retrieval, Zero-shot prompting is often sufficient. In contrast, tasks involving structured reasoning or stepwise evaluation benefit more from CoT prompting, which guides the model to reason explicitly and sequentially (Meincke et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR57)). For instance, CoT has shown clear advantages in educational tasks requiring analytical judgment, improving the accuracy of automated scoring for open-ended science assessments (Cohn et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR19)) and student essays (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR50)). In the latter, CoT prompting combined with contextual item stems and scoring rubrics improved the accuracy of GPT-4’s automated scoring when compared to baseline models lacking these components. Specifically, CoT with context and rubrics improved Zero-shot scoring accuracy by 13.44% compared to Zero-shot without CoT or context, and improved Few-shot accuracy by 3.7% over few-shot without CoT or context. These accuracy rates were calculated based on comparisons to human expert scores, which served as the reference standard.

Despite promising advances, prompting strategies remain underexplored in GenAI feedback research. Most studies, such as Er et al. ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR23)) and Banihashem et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR9)), rely on zero-shot prompts without exemplars or rubric-based walkthroughs. Both studies also emphasise the sensitivity of LLM outputs to prompt formulation, reinforcing the need for more cognitively aligned approaches. This gap is especially evident in feedback on argumentative writing, which follows a structured format and requires careful evaluation of claims, coherence, and supporting evidence, the kind of complex reasoning CoT prompting is designed to elicit. Moreover, existing research has focused mainly on English-written tasks and other languages well represented in LLM training corpora, with English being a prime example (Chimirri et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR18)). This limits the generalizability of findings to underrepresented languages. The issue is critical because, despite advances in multilingual capabilities, LLMs still perform inconsistently across languages. For instance, research on Chilean Spanish found that complex verb forms and sentence structures significantly affected GenAI’s response quality, length, and interpretation (Viveros-Muñoz et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR80)). Similarly, Arora and Srivastava ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR6)) showed that gendered syntax, such as pronouns in English and verb conjugations in Hindi, influenced gender representation in GPT-4o outputs. Together, these findings suggest that GenAI remains highly sensitive to features of the initial draft, including linguistic structure.

Research objective and questions
--------------------------------

This study addresses the critical gap in prompt engineering for educational feedback by systematically comparing Zero-shot and CoT prompting engineering techniques in the generation of feedback on argumentative essays. By shifting the focus from *what* GenAI can produce to *how* we interact with it, we aim to determine whether guiding an LLM’s reasoning process through CoT yields feedback that better aligns with the cognitive and instructional demands of argumentative writing tasks. Our aim is not to position prompt engineering as a general pedagogical solution, but to investigate its role in shaping the quality and usefulness of GenAI feedback within specific educational contexts. In this regard, prompt engineering supports principles of eXplainable AI (XAI), which promotes the use of methods that generate transparent explanations and clear reasoning behind AI decisions (Khosravi et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR47)). Applying XAI in feedback generation helps educators and students understand how outputs are produced, fostering greater trust, interpretability, and alignment with educational goals.

To further extend the relevance and contributions of our findings, this study was conducted in Persian, a language underrepresented in LLM training corpora and largely absent from prior GenAI feedback research. Persian features distinct linguistic characteristics, including flexible word order, rich morphological structure, and the frequent omission of explicit subjects (Megerdoomian, [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR97)). These characteristics pose challenges for natural language processing applications (Moniri et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR98)) and may thereby interfere with GenAI’s ability to generate accurate and contextually appropriate feedback. Conducting the study in this linguistic context thus helps test the generalizability of GenAI feedback beyond English-dominant settings.

To evaluate the effectiveness of these prompting strategies, we compare GenAI feedback with that provided by experienced teacher, offering a pedagogically meaningful benchmark for assessing quality and impact of GenAI outputs. Specifically, we ask:

*RQ1* How does the quality of feedback provided by a teacher differ from that provided by ChatGPT using Zero-shot and CoT prompt techniques?

*RQ2* How do different feedback sources (teacher, ChatGPT with Zero-shot prompt, ChatGPT with CoT prompt) respond to variations in student essay quality?

*RQ3* To what extent does the source of feedback (teacher, ChatGPT with Zero-shot prompt, ChatGPT with CoT prompts) influence the improvement in the quality of argumentative essays written by students?

Method
------

### Participant

This study was conducted during the 2024–2025 academic year at a public university in a Persian-speaking country. Participants were recruited using a combination of purposive and convenience sampling, whereby all students who met the inclusion criteria in programs accessible to the research team were invited to participate. To ensure a homogeneous sample aligned with the study objectives, participants were required to (1) be native Persian speakers, (2) be enrolled in the field of educational sciences, (3) be at the same educational level, and (4) have previously completed the compulsory Academic Writing course, which introduced argumentative essay writing.

Based on these criteria, students enrolled in two compulsory courses, “Educational Psychology” and “Research Methodology,” were targeted. All students enrolled in these courses received an online invitation to participate. Recruitment procedures and instructions were standardized across both courses. Participants were informed that participation was voluntary, responses would remain anonymous, academic standing would not be affected. In addition, one extra course credit was offered as an incentive for participation upon completing all required tasks, regardless of essay quality.

A total of 70 bachelor’s-level students participated in the study. All were in their fifth semester (third year) and were enrolled in closely related programs within educational sciences. Detailed demographic characteristics of the sample are presented in Table [1](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#Tab1). Informed consent was obtained from all participants prior to data collection.

### Study design and procedure

This study adopts a quantitative approach with an exploratory orientation, employing a randomized three-group experimental design and a multi-step data collection procedure. This type of design is widely recommended for educational research because it allows controlled comparison among conditions and supports the identification of potential causal relationships (Creswell & Creswell, [2017](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR21)). It was particularly suitable for distinguishing between traditional teacher feedback and two GenAI prompting strategies, capturing both human–AI and intra-GenAI variations in feedback quality and usefulness.

As shown in Fig. [1](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#Fig1), participants were randomly assigned to one of three experimental conditions. In Condition 1 (*n* = 24), students received written feedback from a teacher with over 10 years teaching experience and expert in the field of argumentative writing. In Conditions 2 (*n* = 24) and 3 (*n* = 22), feedback was generated by ChatGPT using a Zero-shot prompt and a CoT prompt, respectively. All communication throughout the study, including essay submissions and feedback delivery, was conducted via the university’s learning management system, a platform routinely used for instructional purposes at the institution. Considering the linguistic context of the study, all essays were written in Persian.

Study procedure

Data collection began with an introductory session in which the procedures and general purpose of the study were explained. Students were informed that the research focused on how feedback supports argumentative essay writing. To minimize bias, they were not told the primary research objective or the specific source of the feedback they would receive. Following this, students were given one week to compose an argumentative essay on the topic: “*To use or not to use? Generative AI tools in education*.” After submitting their initial drafts, participants received written feedback within two days, according to their assigned condition. This brief delay allowed sufficient time for the teacher to provide feedback on the essays submitted by participants in Condition 1. After receiving the feedback, participants had one week to revise their drafts. This interval aligns with recommended practices in feedback provision, as time for reflection and planning can help students better understand the feedback, thereby increasing the likelihood of effective implementation (Wu & Schunn, [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR89)). Additionally, allowing time to revise written work based on feedback can shift students’ expectations about its purpose, encouraging them to view it not as a judgment of themselves but as an evaluation of their work (Yeatman & Hewitt, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR91)), thereby supporting greater motivation and self-esteem when engaging with feedback (Nicol & Macfarlane‐Dick, [2006](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR60)).

At the conclusion of the study, participants were debriefed to disclose the full purpose of the research and the sources of the feedback they had received, as this information had been intentionally withheld to reduce bias. They were then given the option to withdraw their data from the analysis, but no participants chose to do so.

GPT-4o was selected as the ChatGPT language model for this study due to its demonstrated effectiveness in generating high-quality feedback and its enhanced multilingual capabilities. Compared to earlier models, GPT-4o exhibits significantly improved comprehension and response generation in non-English languages, making it well-suited for multilingual educational contexts such as the present study (Hurst et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR40)). Furthermore, recent research has shown that, at the current stage of AI development, GPT-4 variants outperform both human peers and earlier models in producing accurate, rubric-based assessments (e.g., Dai et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR22); Jürgensmeier & Skiera, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR44)).

The prompts used in both AI-based conditions were informed by established feedback rubrics employed in the assessment of argumentative writing (Latifi et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR52); Noroozi et al., [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR63)). These rubrics are based on Toulmin’s argumentation model (1958), a well-regarded framework for evaluating essays. The model outlines the essential components of high-quality argumentative writing. While the Zero-shot prompt consisted of a direct instruction to generate feedback based on the rubric, the CoT prompt was designed to guide the model through a step-by-step evaluation process with a well-elaborated example provided (see Appendix 1). Following the feedback intervention, students were given one additional week to revise their essays. All participants were instructed to incorporate the feedback into their revisions, resulting in a final draft of the same argumentative essay.

### Measurements

#### Coding scheme to assess the quality of essay writing

To assess the quality of students’ argumentative essays, we used a rubric adopted by previous studies (Latifi et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR52); Valero Haro et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR77)). The rubric was grounded in the Toulmin model of argumentation and included seven key components: introduction, position statement, supporting arguments and related evidence, counterarguments and related evidence, and conclusion. Each component was scored on a three-point scale: a score of 0 was assigned if the component was absent, 1 if it was present but insufficiently developed, and 2 if it was both present and sufficiently elaborated (see Appendix 2).

The cumulative scores across all components were aggregated to generate a total essay quality score, ranging from 0 to 14. To minimize potential bias in scoring, two trained coders with expertise in education independently evaluated the essays, with all submissions pseudonymized and the coders blind to participants’ identities and group assignments. Inter-rater reliability was assessed using Cohen’s Kappa, which indicated a substantial level of agreement between the coders, with 84 percent agreement (*κ* = 0.81, 95 percent CI [0.76, 0.86]; z = 28.12, *p* < .001). This reflects a statistically significant and reliable level of consensus in the scoring process.

#### Coding scheme to assess the quality of feedback

The quality of feedback provided to students was assessed using a rubric adopted from previous studies (Latifi et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR52); Valero Haro et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR77)). The rubric was also grounded in the Toulmin model of argumentation and included seven key components. Each component was scored on a three-point scale: 0 if the component was absent, 1 if it was present but insufficiently developed (for example, only identifying a problem with limited justification), and 2 if it was both present and sufficiently elaborated, including a clear identification of the problem and concrete suggestions for improvement (see Appendix 3). This rubric aligns with the definition of high-quality feedback used in this study, as it is specific, focused on distinct components of an argumentative essay, and sufficiently elaborated to not only highlight issues but also provide actionable guidance for improvement.

The cumulative scores across all components were summed to derive an overall feedback quality score. The same two evaluators who scored the essays also assessed the quality of feedback. Inter-rater reliability was evaluated using Cohen’s Kappa, which indicated a substantial level of agreement, with 77 percent agreement (*κ* = 0.78, 95 percent CI [0.71, 0.85]; z = 21.76, *p* < .001), reflecting a statistically significant and consistent level of consensus between raters.

### Analysis

To ensure the appropriateness of the data for statistical analyses, initial diagnostic tests were conducted. First, Levene’s test assessed the homogeneity of variances across the three feedback conditions, followed by the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test to examine data normality. Results confirmed that the assumptions of homogeneity and normality were met. To address RQ1, investigating differences in feedback quality among teacher-generated feedback, ChatGPT using Zero-shot prompting, and ChatGPT using CoT prompting, a one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with Tukey HSD post-hoc tests was utilized. For RQ2, which explored the extent to which different feedback sources respond to variations in student essay quality, Pearson bivariate correlation analyses were used. Finally, RQ3, which examined how feedback from different sources influenced the improvement of argumentative essay quality from initial drafts to revised versions, was addressed using multiple methods. First, a repeated-measures ANOVA was conducted to assess within-subject changes in essay quality from initial to revised drafts across feedback conditions. Second, a one-way analysis of covariance (ANCOVA) was performed to compare the quality of revised drafts across feedback sources, controlling for the quality of initial drafts. In addition, multiple regression analyses were conducted across all conditions to determine the extent to which feedback quality and initial draft quality predicted the quality of students’ revised drafts.

### Feedback source and revision performance (RQ3)

The descriptive data shown in Table [4](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#Tab4) indicate that, regardless of the source of feedback, there was a notable improvement in the quality of argumentative essays from the initial to the revised drafts. Descriptive statistics indicated that the mean quality of revised essays was highest in the teacher feedback condition (*M* = 10.66, *SD* = 2.31), followed by the Zero-shot prompt condition (*M* = 10.08, *SD* = 2.20), and lowest in the CoT prompt condition (*M* = 9.81, *SD* = 3.00). A one-way ANCOVA was conducted to examine whether the type of feedback significantly affected the quality of revised essays, after controlling for the quality of the initial drafts. After adjusting for initial draft quality, there was no significant effect of feedback condition on revised essay quality, *F*(2, 64) = 1.19, *p* = .30, *η**p*2 = .03, suggesting that the type of feedback did not significantly influence revision outcomes. However, initial essay quality was a significant covariate, *F*(1, 64) = 34.25, *p* < .001, *η**p*2 = .35, indicating a strong and significant relationship between students’ baseline writing ability and their revised performance.

In addition, multiple regression analyses were conducted across all conditions to determine the extent to which feedback quality and initial draft quality in various conditions predicted the quality of students’ revised drafts. In the teacher feedback condition, the overall model was significant, *F*(2, 21) = 9.53, *p* = .001, and explained 47% of the variance in revised draft scores (*R*2 = .47). Both predictors contributed significantly: for every one‐point increase in feedback quality, revised draft quality increased by 0.57 points (B = 0.57, *SE* = 0.17, *β* = .53, *t* = 3.30, *p* = .003), and for every one‐point increase in initial draft quality, revised draft quality increased by 0.38 points (B = 0.38, *SE* = 0.18, *β* = .33, *t* = 2.07, *p* = .05).

In the Zero-shot prompt condition, the regression model again reached significance, *F*(2, 21) = 9.61, *p* = .001, accounting for 48% of the variance (*R*2 = .48). Here, only initial draft quality emerged as a significant predictor: each one‐point increase in draft quality was associated with a 0.51‐point increase in revised draft quality (B = 0.51, *SE* = 0.19, *β* = .49, *t* = 2.64, *p* = .01). The influence of feedback quality, although positive (B = 0.32, *β* = .29), did not reach significance (*t* = 1.56, *p* = .13). And finally, in the CoT prompt condition, the model was significant, *F*(2, 19) = 8.80, *p* = .002, and explained 48% of variance (*R*2 = .48). Again, only initial draft quality significantly predicted revised draft quality: a one‐point increase in initial draft quality predicted a 0.96‐point increase in revised scores (B = 0.96, *SE* = 0.23, *β* = .75, *t* = 4.14, *p* = .001). Feedback quality showed a small, non‐significant negative coefficient (B =  − 0.50, *β* =  − .21, *t* =  − 1.16, *p* = .26).

Discussion
----------

### Feedback quality (RQ1)

The first research question examined the comparative quality of feedback produced by ChatGPT using GPT-4o language model under two prompting conditions, i.e., Zero-shot and CoT, and by an experienced teacher. The findings revealed that feedback generated using CoT prompting significantly surpassed both Zero-shot and teacher-generated feedback in overall quality. Under the CoT condition, the feedback received perfect scores on some components and outperformed teacher feedback across all components of argumentative essays. This finding supports growing evidence in the literature suggesting that CoT prompting enhances the cognitive depth and sequencing of GenAI outputs in educational contexts (Wan & Chen, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR82)). More importantly, it aligns with theoretical perspectives asserting that prompting strategies must be tailored to the cognitive complexity of the task (Chen et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR16)). Providing feedback on argumentative essay writing, which requires structured reasoning and layered critique, clearly fits the profile of complex assessment tasks for which CoT has shown clear advantages over Zero-shot approaches (e.g., Cohn et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR19); Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR50)).

An additional finding was that feedback generated by ChatGPT under the Zero-shot condition was rated on par with that of human teachers. However, this result contrasts with prior studies in the same domain, which have consistently reported the superiority of teacher feedback over that of ChatGPT when providing feedback on argumentative essays (e.g., Jansen et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR41); Steiss et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR75)). There are three plausible explanations for this discrepancy. *First*, earlier studies employed less advanced language models, primarily GPT-3.5, which have repeatedly been shown to underperform in comparison to the more recent GPT-4o model used in the current study (Dai et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR22); Jürgensmeier & Skiera, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR44)). This suggests that, when paired with a more capable model, even minimally prompted GenAI systems can generate feedback that meets the required standards (Meincke et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR57)). This perspective is echoed by Jansen et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR41)), who recommend replicating similar studies using more powerful models such as GPT-4 to better understand potential improvements in feedback quality.

*Second*, differences in prompt design may account for the variation in outcomes. Unlike previous studies that withheld explicit rubrics in their Zero-shot prompts, the present study included a detailed and relevant rubric for providing feedback, even in the Zero-shot condition. This design choice is significant, as it may have enabled the model to provide feedback that explicitly referred to criteria in rubrics or standards, which is a key characteristic of effective feedback (Nicol & Macfarlane‐Dick, [2006](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR60)). The significance of including a rubric is further supported, albeit indirectly, by findings from Steiss et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR75)), who reported that although human feedback generally outperformed GenAI feedback, GenAI exceeded human evaluators in the “criterion-based” category when the quality of feedback was coded. In line with this, Yu and Xie ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR93)) found that when GPT-3.5 was provided with an explicit rubric to guide feedback on students’ language essays, covering aspects such as spelling, grammar, word choice, mechanics, and sentence structure, the feedback it generated was comparable to that of a teacher. This may be attributed to GenAI’s tendency to reference rubric elements explicitly, which is a hallmark of effective feedback, even in cases where the rubric is not directly included in the prompt.

*Third*, differences in how feedback quality was assessed may help explain this discrepancy. In the present study, feedback was evaluated using an objective rubric grounded in Toulmin’s model, which emphasised the structural completeness of argumentative essays. This focus was reinforced through the prompt design, ensuring alignment between the feedback task and the evaluation criteria. In contrast, prior studies often employed more subjective evaluation frameworks. For example, Steiss et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR75)) assessed feedback based on clarity of suggestions, accuracy, and tone, while Jansen et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR41)) relied on user-centered measures such as perceived helpfulness and usefulness. These dimensions, by their nature, may have favored human-generated feedback, which tends to be more personalized and emotionally attuned. Nevertheless, as demonstrated by Yu and Xie ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR93)), when the same objective rubric was applied to GenAI feedback using GPT-3.5, its performance was found to be comparable to that of human teachers. In addition, the capabilities of the language model itself appear to influence the perceived quality of feedback. According to Escalante et al. ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR24)), even when feedback quality was assessed using subjective criteria, responses generated by GPT-4 were rated similarly to those of human evaluators, a finding that is consistent with the results of the present study.

Taken together with the substantial quality gap observed between the Zero-shot and CoT conditions in the current study, these findings underscore that prompt engineering is not merely a technical adjustment but a fundamental pedagogical strategy (Walter, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR81)). The clarity, structure, and scaffolding embedded in a prompt can significantly influence how GenAI functions as a feedback provider. Moreover, the contrast between the results of this study and those of earlier research in comparable contexts suggests that both the evaluation criteria employed and the capabilities of the language model are critical in shaping perceptions of feedback quality. As more advanced models are introduced, the gap between human- and GenAI feedback appears to be narrowing across both objective and subjective aspects of quality.

### Relationship between quality of initial draft and feedback (RQ2)

The second research question examined the relationship between the quality of students’ initial essay and the quality of feedback received across conditions. A key finding was that in both GenAI conditions, Zero-shot and CoT, the quality of feedback was significantly correlated with the quality of the initial essays, with a stronger dependency observed in the CoT condition. This pattern suggests that GenAI feedback tends to scale with input quality: the more the initial draft adheres to the main components of an argumentative essay, the more targeted and specific the feedback becomes.

However, this finding stands in contrast to the results reported by Banihashem et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR9)), who found no significant correlation between the quality of GenAI feedback and the quality of students’ initial argumentative essays. This divergence is likely attributable to several methodological differences between the two studies. In addition to variations in prompt design, Banihashem et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR9)) analysed essays written in English, whereas the present study focused on essays composed in Persian, which is a language with distinct syntactic structures and discourse conventions. The linguistic features of Persian are known to pose unique challenges for natural language processing systems (Moniri et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR98)), which may influence GenAI’s ability to effectively distinguish among varying levels of input quality. For instance, such linguistic differences can affect essay length and content density, factors that have been shown to make language models more vulnerable to conflating verbosity with value (Hu et al., o[2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR38)).

Furthermore, and perhaps more critically, Banihashem et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR9)) employed two distinct evaluation frameworks: one for assessing the quality of students’ argumentative essays and another for evaluating the quality of the feedback provided. While the former aligned closely with the rubric used in the present study, focusing on the structural components of argumentation, the latter incorporated different criteria, including subjective aspects such as emotional tone. This divergence in evaluation focus may have weakened the alignment between input quality and feedback quality, thereby obscuring any potential correlation. In contrast, the methodological coherence of the current study enabled a more direct and objective assessment of how effectively the feedback addressed specific argumentative features in the student texts, thereby enhancing the sensitivity of the analysis to differences in input quality.

In addition to the previous finding, the current study found no correlation between initial essay quality and feedback quality in the teacher feedback condition. This aligns with earlier research showing that essay quality has minimal impact on how human (i.e., peer) feedback is provided (Gao et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR31); Huisman et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR39)). This highlights a key limitation of GenAI in education: while GenAI tends to mirror the quality (or textual features) of the input it receives, human teachers engage in adaptive pedagogical reasoning, adjusting their feedback to support learners regardless of the initial draft’s quality. This adaptability likely stems from teachers’ ability to diagnose deeper learning needs and draw on contextual knowledge about the assessment, course, and students (Henderson et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR36)). As a result, teacher feedback is less constrained by input quality and more attuned to the learner’s cognitive and affective development. This view is supported by the “matching principles” in peer feedback research (Gielen et al., [2010](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR32)), which suggest that feedback is most effective when it matches the recipient’s ability to understand and apply it. From this perspective, teacher-generated feedback may not always be more detailed than GenAI’s, but it is often more pedagogically precise and strategically tailored to support the learner’s next developmental step.

In short, the current study’s findings underscore a fundamental difference between GenAI and human feedback. While GenAI feedback is strongly influenced by the quality and, potentially, surface features of student input, teacher feedback remains pedagogically adaptive and responsive regardless of the initial essay’s quality. This sensitivity to input quality means that GenAI tends to provide more structured and specific recommendations on various components of argumentative writing to higher-performing students, or even to those whose essays are longer or information-dense, even if they do not conform to the criteria for a well-written argumentative essay. This asymmetry risks reinforcing a “rich get richer, poor get poorer” dynamic, commonly referred to as the “Matthew effect” (Stanovich, [2009](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR74)), in which stronger students continue to improve while their peers fall further behind. This concern is further supported by findings from Wetzler et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR85)), who reported that GenAI grading systems (GPT 3.5 and GPT 4o) were more lenient with weaker submissions and more stringent with stronger ones, a pattern of “proportional bias” that, while technically explainable, is pedagogically problematic.

### Feedback source and revision performance (RQ3)

The third research question examined the impact of feedback source on students’ revision performance. Although students in all conditions significantly improved their revised essays, the feedback source, whether teacher, Zero-shot, or CoT condition, did not significantly affect final performance when initial draft quality was controlled. The strongest predictor of revision performance was students’ writing ability, as reflected in the quality of their initial essays, a measure commonly applied in feedback research (e.g., Guo et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR33); Patchan & Schunn, [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR66)). In addition, the findings from the regression analyses revealed a deeper distinction: in the teacher condition, feedback quality significantly predicted revision performance, whereas in both GenAI conditions, it did not.

The lack of a significant effect of feedback source on revision outcomes suggests that students were able to benefit from feedback regardless of whether it originated from a teacher or GenAI. This finding is particularly relevant in educational contexts where scalable and timely feedback solutions are increasingly adopted. However, the similarity in revision outcomes across conditions should not be interpreted as evidence that all feedback functions equally. Instead, it emphasises the importance of how students engage with feedback during revision. This process, often referred to as feedback uptake, plays a central role in shaping revision outcomes (Wichmann et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR86)). Uptake can take various forms, including accepting, elaborating on, modifying, or disregarding feedback, each reflecting a distinct way of integrating feedback into one’s work (Gao, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR30)). These behaviors depend not only on the quality of the feedback but also on learner-related characteristics that influence how feedback is interpreted and acted upon (Aben et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR1)).

In light of this, the finding that students’ writing ability was the strongest predictor of revision performance further underscores the role of individual differences in how feedback is processed and applied. Students with stronger writing skills may possess more developed strategies for identifying which feedback is most relevant, understanding its implications, and translating it into effective revisions. Prior research has shown that writing proficiency supports the development of feedback literacy, which includes the capacity to interpret, evaluate, and apply feedback constructively (Zhou et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR95)). Feedback-literate students are better positioned to recognise the usefulness of feedback and to engage with it in a productive manner (Carless & Boud, [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR14)). These findings highlight the importance of not only providing high-quality feedback but also supporting students’ ability to engage with it effectively, particularly for those with less developed writing skills.

The final and perhaps most nuanced finding is that feedback quality significantly predicted revision outcomes in the teacher condition but not in the GenAI conditions. This suggests that teacher feedback may have been more effectively aligned with students’ needs, thereby supporting more meaningful uptake. Teachers often draw on their knowledge of students’ previous work and learning progress to deliver feedback that is targeted and pedagogically appropriate. Even when teacher feedback does not cover every component of an essay, as reflected by its lower rated quality compared to the GenAI conditions in this study, it is often adapted to address the most pressing issues for each individual. This form of personalized support likely enhanced students’ perception of the feedback’s usefulness, which in turn facilitated deeper engagement during revision (Kerman et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR46)). As Hattie and Timperley ([2007](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR35)) emphasise, the true impact of feedback lies not in what is said, but in how much of it students understand, value, and use to improve their work.

In contrast, GenAI feedback did not significantly predict the quality of revised drafts. A likely explanation is that such feedback tends to follow a standardized structure, addressing all prompted elements uniformly without adapting to the specific context or needs of individual students. While this ensures coverage and consistency, it limits the ability of GenAI systems to prioritize feedback that is most pedagogically relevant. Supporting this interpretation, van den Berg and Papadopoulos ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR78)) argue that teachers are uniquely positioned to personalize their feedback based on an understanding of students’ learning histories and individual challenges. In contrast, GenAI tools, in their current form, lack this contextual sensitivity, which may limit their capacity to support the kind of meaningful engagement that leads to improved revisions.

In sum, the findings suggest that the effectiveness of feedback depends less on its source and more on how well it aligns with learners’ needs, influencing students’ perception of its usefulness and their willingness to act on it. Teacher feedback appeared more attuned to individual differences, offering pedagogically sensitive guidance that likely supported more meaningful revisions. In contrast, GenAI feedback, though consistent and readily available, lacked the contextual understanding needed to tailor support and tended to follow structural cues from the prompt rather than addressing students’ specific challenges.

Limitations and suggestions for future research
-----------------------------------------------

This study offers important insights into the comparative effectiveness of teacher- versus GenAI feedback using Zero-shot and CoT prompting in the context of argumentative essay writing. However, several limitations remain, opening meaningful avenues for future research.

*First*, the sample was limited to graduate students from one Iranian university, mostly female and from educational sciences. As Jayawardena et al. ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR42)) highlight, single-site, homogeneous samples reduce generalizability when comparing AI and human feedback. Similarly, Chan et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR15)) warn that diverse education systems and cultures complicate the transferability of AI-in-education findings. Future work should therefore test these feedback effects with larger, more diverse populations to examine their stability across disciplines, institutions, and student profiles.

*Second,* the writing task in this study required students to produce and revise only a single essay draft in a short-term, controlled setting. This one-shot design limits ecological validity, as academic writing typically evolves through multiple drafts, feedback cycles, and periods of reflection. As such, the study captures only a narrow, “single loop” view of how feedback contributes to learning, focused on immediate surface-level correction. In contrast, Carless’s ([2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR13)) concept of “feedback spirals” emphasises iterative, long-term engagement with feedback that supports deeper learning and self-regulation. Future research should explore how GenAI can be leveraged not only to deliver feedback but also to facilitate these ongoing, developmental processes. For example, GenAI could scaffold students’ work across multiple assignments by prompting reflection (e.g., Wei et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR84)) and supporting self-assessment between tasks (e.g., Ali et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR3)). Investigating which prompt engineering strategies best support such sustained engagement could help reposition GenAI from a reactive evaluator to a formative, pedagogically grounded partner that contributes meaningfully to students’ longer-term writing development.

*Third*, this study used a widely accepted rubric with high inter-rater reliability to assess feedback quality in the context of argumentative writing (e.g., Latifi et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR52); Valero Haro et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR77)). However, the rubric focused exclusively on structural elements, such as the presence of an introduction, supporting evidence, counterarguments, and a conclusion. This limited scope may have overstated the quality of GenAI feedback, as responses that meet structural criteria may still lack the depth, clarity, or motivational tone typical of high-quality human feedback. Future research could replicate this study using alternative feedback quality rubrics that also account for subjective and affective dimensions, such as tone (Steiss et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR75)), perceived usefulness (Jansen et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR41)), and emotional content, including positive or negative sentiment (Banihashem et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR9)), alongside structural indicators. This would help validate and extend the current findings and offer a more nuanced understanding of the relative strengths and limitations of GenAI and human feedback, both generally and in linguistically underrepresented contexts in LLM training, such as Persian.

*Fourth,* the study found that GenAI feedback was moderately sensitive to the quality of the initial essay. This was attributed in part to the task’s language, Persian, unlike prior studies using English essays, which showed no such significant correlation (Banihashem et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR9)). However, attributing this solely to language remains speculative. Future research should test this directly by varying the input language while controlling for other factors. Moreover, although previous studies have provided empirical evidence that GenAI systems are sensitive to surface-level textual features such as length, syntactic complexity, and discourse structure (e.g., Hu et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR38); Viveros-Muñoz et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR80)), this study did not examine such linguistic dimensions, as that was beyond its scope. Future work could analyse these features using established discourse frameworks, such as McNamara et al. ([2010](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR56)), to better understand how they influence GenAI feedback and its educational value, for example, by including them as covariates in predictive models (see Jansen et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR41)). In addition, future research could explore prompting mechanisms that explicitly compensate for lower-quality or shorter submissions to ensure that all students receive feedback that is equally informative and actionable, regardless of their starting point.

*Finally*, although students improved their essays across all feedback conditions, no significant differences in revision performance were found between groups. This lack of between-group effects may mask important differences in how students engaged with feedback, as comparable outcomes may have resulted from different revision processes. To capture these nuances, future research should adopt more fine-grained frameworks for analyzing feedback uptake, such as distinguishing whether feedback is accepted, elaborated, modified, or rejected (Gao, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR30)). Such distinctions could clarify how different feedback sources support or hinder learning and whether GenAI and human feedback promote equally meaningful revisions. In addition, examining students’ emotional responses to feedback, for example through facial expressions (Sahraie et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR70)), may illuminate affective factors influencing feedback processing. Moreover, future studies could explore the role of external supports, such as computer-supported scaffolds (Farrokhnia et al., [2025b](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR27)), in fostering deeper engagement with feedback and more effective integration of revisions into students’ writing (Zhang et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR94)).

Conclusion
----------

This study examined how GenAI feedback, using Zero shot and CoT prompting strategies, compares to teacher feedback in the context of argumentative writing in Persian, a linguistically underrepresented language in LLM training data. The findings demonstrated that with well-designed prompts, particularly CoT, GenAI could produce feedback matching or even surpassing teacher-generated feedback in structural quality. This suggests promising potential for GenAI to support writing instruction across languages that are less represented in LLM development.

At the same time, the study revealed that GenAI feedback, regardless of the prompting technique used, was significantly influenced by the quality of students’ initial drafts, unlike teacher feedback. Higher-quality essays tended to receive more detailed and criterion-aligned feedback, while weaker submissions were given less targeted support. Interestingly, this pattern contrasts with findings from studies conducted in English, suggesting that linguistic features such as Persian’s syntactic structure and discourse conventions may influence how GenAI interprets and responds to student writing. For example, differences in essay organization and content density may cause language models to conflate verbosity with quality, particularly in underrepresented languages. These insights underscore the need to critically examine how GenAI systems perform across diverse linguistic and academic contexts.

Taken together, these findings reinforce the need for a hybrid feedback model that combines the scalability and structural consistency of GenAI with the contextual awareness and pedagogical sensitivity of human educators. GenAI can offer immediate, rubric-aligned feedback efficiently across assignments, while teachers can enrich and adapt this input based on deeper knowledge of students’ learning needs. Recent work by Banihashem et al. ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9#ref-CR99)) has proposed a framework for “*hybrid intelligent feedback*”, identifying five foundational principles: shared agency, complementarity, iterative refinement, dynamic adaptability, and enhanced personalization. Implementing such models will require teacher training in prompt engineering, clear protocols for human and AI collaboration, and ethical safeguards to ensure responsible integration of GenAI into educational settings. Ultimately, the goal is not to replace teachers but to design systems in which GenAI enhances the feedback process, promoting reflection, uptake, and deeper learning, particularly in contexts where linguistic diversity or resource limitations demand flexible and inclusive feedback solutions.

### Appendix 1: The prompts used in condition 2 and 3

### Zero-shot prompt

You are a professor of academic writing at the undergraduate level. Your students have submitted an argumentative essay as an assignment for this course on the role of artificial intelligence in education, which should be no more than 500 words in length. You now need to provide each student with feedback on the quality of their argumentative writing, identifying problems in their essays and suggesting solutions. Your feedback should be given in a paragraph containing 250–300 words and address the following questions:

* Has the student provided an introduction relevant to the topic?
* Has the student presented a clear and definite position regarding the topic?
* Has the student provided reasons to support their position? And have they substantiated these reasons with credible evidence (in the form of examples, personal experiences, statistics, expert opinions, and research evidence)?
* Has the student presented any counterarguments to their position? And have they refuted these counterarguments with credible evidence (in the form of examples, personal experiences, statistics, expert opinions, and research evidence)?
* Has the student concluded their essay effectively?

### CoT prompt

You are a professor of academic writing at the undergraduate level. Your students have submitted an argumentative essay as an assignment for this course on the role of artificial intelligence in education, which should be no more than 500 words in length. You now need to provide each student with feedback on the quality of their argumentative writing, identifying problems in their essays and suggesting solutions. Your feedback should be given in a paragraph containing 250–300 words and address the following questions:

* Has the student provided an introduction relevant to the topic?
* Has the student presented a clear and definite position regarding the topic?
* Has the student provided reasons to support their position? And have they substantiated these reasons with credible evidence (in the form of examples, personal experiences, statistics, expert opinions, and research evidence)?
* Has the student presented any counterarguments to their position? And have they refuted these counterarguments with credible evidence (in the form of examples, personal experiences, statistics, expert opinions, and research evidence)?
* Has the student concluded their essay effectively?

Please use a step-by-step approach to respond to this request.

For example, a student has sent you the following argumentative essay:

“The role of artificial intelligence in education Today, artificial intelligence is used extensively in all fields, especially in education. The use of AI tools in education has benefits such as creating smart educational content, learning in any language, creating tests, etc., but alongside such benefits are challenges and limitations such as lack of quality data, cost, and access. Generally, the benefits of using AI tools outweigh the challenges, and in my opinion, these tools can be used in the classroom to great advantage because firstly, using these tools improves learners’ education—for example, by making many of the abstract contents of courses like physics and chemistry concrete, learning occurs faster and better. Secondly, teachers can receive ideas and help regarding their teaching methods from these tools and clarify educational material for students. For example, teachers for lower grades can use games and entertainments provided by AI tools. Thirdly, with constant access to artificial intelligence, teachers and learners can use it at any time to get more information and enhance their knowledge. Students can easily have essays and any needed texts in class. Teachers can also create 34 creative exercises and smart tests through these tools and even use AI for grading and checking tests. Therefore, artificial intelligence can create a major revolution in the education industry and cause an increase in productivity in various aspects of it”.

Now you want to provide feedback to improve this argumentative text. To prepare this feedback, you should act as follows. First, check whether the student has provided a suitable introduction before stating their own position. If the introduction is weak or incomplete, offer suggestions for its improvement. Next, check whether the student has clearly and explicitly stated their position. If the position is vague, offer suggestions for its improvement. Then, it should be checked whether the student has provided valid evidence (in the form of examples, personal experience, statistics, expert opinion, and research evidence) in support of their position. If the evidence is insufficient or not credible, offer suggestions for its improvement. Next, it should be noted whether the student has raised opposing views to their position and refuted them with valid evidence (in the form of examples, personal experience, statistics, expert opinion, and research evidence). If this part is weak, offer suggestions for its improvement. Finally, check whether the student has provided an extensive summary. If the summary is insufficient, offer suggestions for its improvement. Following these considerations, a sample 250 to 300-word feedback for the argumentative text above could be as follows:

“In the beginning of the text (the first four lines) you mention the advantages and challenges and at the end you also state your position in the form ‘In my opinion, these tools can be used in the classroom to great advantage’. It would be better instead of stating these advantages and disadvantages to start your text with a brief introduction that clarifies the background of the topic and creates an engaging entry into the subject. After this introduction, you should state your position clearly. After stating your position, you have discussed one reason, saying that the use of these tools makes many of the abstract contents of courses concrete and then mentioned several courses like physics and chemistry as examples or evidence, which is excellent. In the second reason, you have stated that teachers can use artificial intelligence in their teaching methods. Then you have mentioned an example or evidence that games and entertainments produced by AI tools can be used. As the third reason, you talk about teachers having access to artificial intelligence at any time which can update their knowledge, but for this reason, you have not mentioned evidence in the form of examples, expert opinions, personal experiences, and statistics. Finally, you have clearly stated your conclusion. However, it would have been better to mention the negative aspects of using AI tools in the classroom as well, after referring to the positive aspects. Providing evidence to refute these negative aspects and then stating conclusions based on that would strengthen your argument, making it easier for the reader to accept”.

Please wait while I send you argumentative essays so that you can provide feedback based on the above instructions.
