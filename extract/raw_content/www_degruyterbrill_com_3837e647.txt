Article
-------

Abstract
--------

With the rapid evolution of artificial intelligence (AI) technologies, their potential to support language learning has captured increasing scholarly attention. This systematic review first maps 105 studies over the past three decades on AI-assisted language learning across all levels of education and then offers an in-depth analysis of 71 studies in higher education (HE) contexts to assess the effectiveness of AI-powered tools. Among the studies in HE settings, 51 reported linguistic outcomes, with 92 % demonstrating positive results. Additionally, 41 studies examined non-linguistic outcomes, revealing consistent findings of enhanced motivation and willingness to communicate, but varied findings on learning attitudes, anxiety, and interest. Despite these positive results, the medium risk of bias across studies due to methodological limitations, such as over-reliance on self-report data, suggested that the findings may not be taken as robust evidence for the effectiveness of AI-assisted language learning. The review provides the following theoretical and practical implications: (1) researchers should adopt more rigorous study designs; (2) learners and teachers should keep utilising AI tools to facilitate personalised and interactive language learning; (3) developers should address the identified problems with AI-assisted language learning tools and promote further technological innovations.

1 Introduction
--------------

Rapid advancements in artificial intelligence (AI) technologies have revolutionised second or foreign language (L2/FL) education ([Chen et al. 2022](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_010)). With the launch of ChatGPT, a generative AI (GenAI) chatbot powered by large language models (LLMs) in late 2022, AI-assisted language education has garnered ever-greater scholarly attention ([Han 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_022)). While numerous empirical studies have documented the effectiveness of AI-assisted language learning, systematic reviews that focus specifically on AI in L2/FL learning remain scarce, especially when compared to broader syntheses in AI in education ([Chen et al. 2020b](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_009), [2022](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_010); [Wang et al. 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_069)). Among the existing reviews of AI-assisted language learning, few have included studies on GenAI and LLMs, which might limit our understanding of how these emerging technologies have transformed language learning. Moreover, extant reviews have rarely focused on specific educational phases such as higher education, although empirical studies have predominantly been conducted among university students. Compared to primary and secondary education, higher education often involves more complex linguistic tasks, where AI’s capabilities to provide immediate and personalised feedback play a more prominent role. Additionally, most previous reviews only provided descriptive mappings of extant literature without critically assessing the methodological rigour of the included studies.

To address these gaps, this review aims to include studies over the past three decades to provide an up-to-date synthesis of research trends regarding the implementation of AI-driven tools in L2/FL learning. It begins with a systematic mapping of studies across all educational phases to outline the characteristics of existing research. However, as the mapping revealed that the majority of studies were conducted in higher education settings, the review followed [Macaro et al.’s (2012)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_045) approach to concentrating on specific educational phases and selected higher education as the focus. Therefore, this study will be the first systematic review focusing on the effectiveness of AI-assisted language learning in higher education specifically and providing an in-depth analysis of the strength of evidence for the effectiveness of AI-assisted language learning. The findings are expected to identify the current state of research and inform future language learning research and practice.


2 Literature review
-------------------

### 2.1 AI in language education (AILEd)

Recent advancements in AI technologies have contributed to their proliferation in diverse domains, including language education ([Chen et al. 2022](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_010); [Hwang and Chang 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_027)). Empirical studies have examined the effectiveness of various types of AI technologies in improving L2/FL skills. For example, automatic speech recognition (ASR) systems and automated writing evaluation (AWE) systems have been substantiated as effective in enhancing L2/FL speaking ([Shafiee Rad 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_058)) and writing skills ([Chang et al. 2021](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_007)). Intelligent tutoring systems (ITSs) have been employed to facilitate vocabulary ([Esit 2011](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_015)) and grammar acquisition [(Chia and Li 2020)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_081). Likewise, chatbots have been shown to improve L2/FL listening ([Kim 2018](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_031)), reading [(Liu et al. 2022)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_082), speaking [(Yuan 2024)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_083), and writing [(Kwon et al. 2023)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_084).

Despite generally positive learning outcomes, learner perceptions of AI-assisted language learning tools remain mixed. For instance, ASR systems sometimes frustrate users due to errors in speech recognition, especially when recognising non-native accents ([McCrocklin 2016](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_047)). Similarly, chatbots occasionally generate irrelevant responses, leading to unsatisfactory user experiences ([Huang et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_026)). Recent breakthroughs in AI technologies have contributed to the introduction of the Transformer algorithm, which is known for its robust text comprehension and generation capabilities ([Vaswani et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_064)). The Transformer algorithm empowers GenAI tools such as ChatGPT, which offers promising solutions to limitations observed in earlier AI-assisted language learning applications. Given these rapid technological advancements, a comprehensive review of AI-powered language learning tools is needed to identify the latest technological developments and research trends. The following section will examine extant review studies to uncover research gaps.


### 2.2 Previous reviews of AILEd

Driven by the increasing application of AI-powered tools in language education, several systematic reviews have been conducted to synthesise research findings. Earlier work (e.g., [Yang and Kyun 2022](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_073)) examined a small set of studies within a narrow timeframe and captured only early applications such as ITSs and automated evaluation systems. Subsequent reviews addressed the temporal limitations but remained constrained by limited database coverage. For example, [Liang et al. (2023)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_041) reviewed 71 AILEd publications from Web of Science (WoS) and identified a notable surge in publications between 1990 and 2020, with particular research interests in ITS for writing, reading, and vocabulary acquisition. More recently, [Zhu and Wang (2025)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_079) analysed 125 studies on AILEd published between 2013 and 2023 and observed an increase in publications on using AWE and ASR systems for writing and speaking development. Nevertheless, their review also suffered from restricted database coverage, raising concerns about comprehensiveness and potential omission of key studies published after 2023.

Taken together, several limitations can be identified from the above-mentioned reviews. First, except for [Zhu and Wang (2025)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_079), most reviews did not cover publications in the past two years, a period which witnessed game-changing advancements in educational AI technologies due to the release of GenAI applications like ChatGPT. This omission would present a limited view of existing studies. Second, a shared limitation of existing reviews is their focus on mapping bibliometric research trends (e.g., publication counts and topic clusters) without providing a quality appraisal of included studies, causing problems in drawing trustworthy findings. [Table 1](#j_jccall-2025-0020_tab_001) summarises these limitations and demonstrates how the present review differs in terms of timeframe, database coverage, and inclusion of quality appraisal. This review aims to synthesise studies published over the past three decades and include the latest publications on GenAI technologies. By collecting data from a broader timeframe and encompassing the recent breakthroughs, this review aims to capture an exhaustive overview of extant AILEd research. Additionally, this review will conduct a formal quality appraisal of individual included studies and a critical assessment of the strength of the overall evidence to assess the trustworthiness of findings on the effectiveness of AI-assisted language learning.

Given the extensive empirical studies on the influence of AI applications in language learning and the potential for enhanced effects with recent advancements in emerging AI technologies, this review first provides an overview of research on AI-assisted language learning and then delves into studies on the effectiveness of AI tools in higher education contexts. Specifically, the following research questions (RQs) will be addressed:

* **RQ1**: What is the current status of research that has been conducted to investigate the effectiveness of AI-assisted tools in L2/FL learning?
* **RQ2**: What is the strength of the evidence of the effectiveness of AI-assisted L2/FL learning in higher education?


To address RQ2, this review synthesises empirical evidence on the effectiveness of AI-assisted learning tools across two aspects: (1) linguistic outcomes, including vocabulary, grammar, pronunciation, listening, speaking, reading, writing, and integrated language skills; (2) non-linguistic outcomes, including attitudes, motivation, willingness to communicate, anxiety, and interest.



3 Methods
---------

### 3.1 Eligibility criteria

Following the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analysis (PRISMA) guidelines ([Moher et al. 2009](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_048)), the inclusion and exclusion criteria were predefined to ensure review transparency (see [Table 2](#j_jccall-2025-0020_tab_002)).

Table 2:

Eligibility criteria.





| Criteria | Inclusion | Exclusion |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Time period | Studies published in or after 1995 | Studies published before 1995 |
| Type of publication | Peer-reviewed literature and doctoral dissertations | Other publications that are not peer-reviewed |
| Participants | Studies on typically developing L2/FL learners | Studies on non-typically developing learners |
| Intervention | Studies that used AI technologies to support L2/FL learning | 1. Studies that adopted AI technologies to develop L1 skills (e.g., literacy) or non-language-related skills; 2. Studies that used AI technologies to support language teaching |
| Outcomes | Studies reporting empirical data on the effectiveness of AI-assisted L2/FL learning (e.g., standardised test scores) | Studies that did not provide empirical data, such as systematic reviews |



### 3.2 Information sources

Informed by recent systematic reviews in education (e.g. [Hamilton et al. 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_021)), the reviewers conducted literature searches in nine databases (see [Table 3](#j_jccall-2025-0020_tab_003)) to ensure a comprehensive dataset of research on AI-assisted language learning.

### 3.3 Search strategy

Search items were generated based on previous systematic reviews (e.g., [Liang et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_041)). The English and Chinese search strings are presented in the [Appendix A.](#j_jccall-2025-0020_app_001) The literature searches were finalised on 31 May 2024.


### 3.4 Data screening and selection process

Following literature searches, all search results (*n* = 3,547) were uploaded to Rayyan ([Ouzzani et al. 2016](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_052)), a web tool for data screening and management in systematic reviews. The reviewers first removed duplicate items (*n* = 675) and then examined the titles and abstracts of the remaining articles (*n* = 2,872). Articles that violated any inclusion criterion were marked as “exclude” (*n* = 2,670), resulting in 202 remaining articles.

Following the initial screening, the author searched for the full texts of the remaining articles. All available full texts (*n* = 196) were then imported to EndNote, a reference management tool, for screening. Following two rounds of screening, 105 articles were included. The screening and selection process is illustrated in [Figure 1](#j_jccall-2025-0020_fig_001).

![]()Figure 1:

PRISMA flow diagram of the data screening and selection process (adapted from [Page et al. 2021](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_054)).



### 3.5 Risk of bias (RoB) assessment

Quality assessment of individual studies was conducted to reduce reporting bias. The 2018 version of the Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool (MMAT; [Hong et al. 2018](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_023)) was used to assess the methodological quality of the included studies. The quality assessment tool was chosen because it is suitable for appraising quantitative, qualitative, and mixed methods studies with different research designs, which facilitates standardised assessment across studies ([Pace et al. 2012](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_053)). Additionally, this appraisal tool has been used in previous systematic reviews in education (e.g., [Hamilton et al. 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_021)). Five quality criteria are provided for assessment, and each criterion can be marked “yes”, “no”, or “can’t tell”.


### 3.6 Data synthesis

Drawing on the data synthesis procedures in [Macaro et al. (2012)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_045), a systematic mapping was conducted to answer RQ1, followed by an in-depth review of studies in tertiary education contexts (*n* = 71) to address RQ2. Specifically, for RQ1, the author generated figures to present overall research characteristics. For RQ2, the author followed [Petticrew and Roberts’ (2006)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_055) framework to present a narrative synthesis of the data. The three steps consisted of (1) grouping studies into two logical categories corresponding to the two sub-questions; (2) analysing findings within each category; and (3) summarising findings across studies.



4 Results
---------

### 4.1 Mapping of included study characteristics across all levels of education

To answer RQ1, mapping the 105 included studies revealed the following characteristics:

1. Publication trends: The number of studies on AI-assisted language learning increased over the past three decades, particularly between 2020 and 2024 (see [Figure 2](#j_jccall-2025-0020_fig_002)).
2. Participant characteristics: The participants were primarily tertiary-level students studying English as L2/FL (see [Figures 3](#j_jccall-2025-0020_fig_003) and [4](#j_jccall-2025-0020_fig_004)).
3. Geographical distribution: Studies were predominantly conducted in Asian countries, such as China and South Korea (see [Figure 5](#j_jccall-2025-0020_fig_005)).
4. Adopted AI technologies: The most frequently investigated AI technologies were chatbots (see [Figure 6](#j_jccall-2025-0020_fig_006)).
5. Studied language skills: Integrated and writing skills attracted the researchers’ most attention (see [Figure 7](#j_jccall-2025-0020_fig_007)).
6. Theoretical backgrounds: A large portion of studies did not delineate their theoretical backgrounds, revealing a need to base studies on theoretical frameworks (see [Figure 8](#j_jccall-2025-0020_fig_008)).

The RoB for individual studies (see [Supplementary Material](#j_jccall-2025-0020_s_007)) was assessed using the MMAT. Three studies were rated as having a “high” risk of bias, 78 as “moderate”, and 24 as “low”. The primary source of bias was confounding variables (*n* = 54), which affected the overall robustness of conclusions.


### 4.2 In-depth analysis of studies in higher education

The in-depth analysis reported 71 studies in higher education settings, since they accounted for the largest proportion of all eligible studies.

To answer RQ2 on the extent of evidence of AI-assisted language learning outcomes, the following analysis groups studies by the language skills they addressed. Substantive learning outcomes of three types of linguistic knowledge (i.e., vocabulary, grammar, and pronunciation) will be analysed first, followed by a synthesis of learning outcomes of four language skills (i.e., listening, speaking, reading, and writing) and integrated language skills.

#### 4.2.1 Evidence of AI-assisted vocabulary learning outcomes

Four studies adopted a pretest-posttest design to examine the impact of different AI-assisted tools on vocabulary learning, including chatbots ([Chen et al. 2020a](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_008); [Qasem et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_056)), AI-based interactive learning systems ([Huang and Wang 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_025)), and AI-powered vocabulary memorisation applications ([Agnes and Srinivasan 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_001)). For instance, [Qasem and colleagues (2023)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_056) randomly assigned participants to a control group (CG), who received traditional instruction through textbook exercises and teacher explanations, and an experimental group (EG), who received the same instruction supplemented with chatbot assistance for checking vocabulary meaning. Comparisons of pretest and post-test scores between groups showed that the EG outperformed the CG. Similar findings were observed across the remaining studies, all of which reported greater vocabulary gains in the EGs.


#### 4.2.2 Evidence of AI-assisted grammar learning outcomes

Two studies examined the effectiveness of AI technologies in grammar acquisition. [Nagata (1995)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_049) investigated a self-developed language instruction system for learning Japanese sentence constructions. The researcher compared the system that detected grammatical errors and provided explanations (EG) with traditional tutoring systems that solely identified errors (CG). Similarly, [Nghi et al. (2019)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_050) examined the use of chatbots as supplementary tools in classroom instruction. The EG received English preposition instructions alongside interactive feedback from chatbots, while the CG only received classroom instructions. Both studies indicated substantial improvements in grammatical knowledge for the CG and EG, with the EG showing more significant gains.


#### 4.2.3 Evidence of AI-assisted pronunciation learning outcomes

One study focused on improving L2/FL pronunciation. [El Shazly et al. (2023)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_014) conducted a pretest-posttest control group study among 49 non-native Arabic-speaking university students in Egypt to examine the effectiveness of an AI-driven storytelling tool. Both CG and EG received classroom instruction on Arabic emphatic consonant pronunciation over six weeks, with the EG assisted by additional practice and personalised feedback from the AI tool. Results showed that the EG achieved significantly better pronunciation skills test scores than the CG. However, the study involved a potential confounding factor, since the EG’s better score may be partially attributed to the additional time on pronunciation practice rather than the AI-driven approach itself. The study was, therefore, rated “moderate” RoB.


#### 4.2.4 Evidence of AI-assisted listening learning outcomes

Three studies examined the role of AI-assisted tools in developing L2/FL listening skills, reporting mixed findings. [Liao (2023)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_042) studied the effect of an intelligent teaching platform on 93 Chinese college students’ listening skills. The participants received pre-course vocabulary preview, in-course intensive listening exercises, and post-course automated feedback on the platform for 18 weeks. Results showed that the students, regardless of language proficiency, significantly improved their listening proficiency from the pre-test to the post-test. However, due to the lack of a CG, it should be cautioned that the effectiveness might simply be attributed to natural learning progressions after continuous instruction rather than the AI intervention.

[Vu et al. (2022)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_065) investigated the impact of an AI-powered language learning application featuring movie caption transcription exercises on EFL listening comprehension in a 12-week RCT among 53 Vietnamese university students. The EG used the application for listening transcription tasks, corrective feedback, and follow-up personalised practices, while the CG completed traditional listening course book exercises. Results indicated that both groups enhanced listening comprehension in the post-test, but only the EG showed significant gains. However, the study was rated “high” RoB, due to insufficient consideration for the homogeneity of participants’ pre-intervention English levels and lack of assessor blinding.

By contrast, the study by [Li and Peng (2021)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_040) found no evidence for the efficacy of AI interventions. The researchers employed a non-randomised comparison design, dividing 59 Chinese freshmen into an EG receiving personalised instruction on an AI-assisted English listening platform and a CG receiving traditional teacher-led face-to-face instruction. The study revealed lower post-test scores in both groups, with no evidence of statistically significant differences between them. Overall, the effectiveness of AI-assisted tools in developing listening skills remains inconclusive.


#### 4.2.5 Evidence of AI-assisted speaking learning outcomes

Thirteen speaking studies reported findings on the effectiveness of AI-assisted speaking tools. Nine of the 13 studies focused on chatbots designed for speaking practice, which can be categorised into three strands. The first strand adopted a one-group pretest-posttest design in different EFL settings, which consistently established the effectiveness of chatbots in enhancing speaking performance after the intervention. For example, [Kim et al. (2021a)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_036) assessed 49 South Korean university students with different language proficiencies. Results showed that both low and intermediate-level groups improved significantly in pronunciation, intonation, and stress from the pre-test to the post-test. However, the absence of CG posed challenges in controlling confounders such as the natural progression of language learning, leading to “moderate” RoB in these studies.

The second strand consists of four studies ([Fathi et al. 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_016); [Hsu et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_024); [Kemelbekova et al. 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_030); [Kim et al. 2021b](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_037)) comparing chatbot-assisted groups with CGs learning without AI assistance. Employing a pretest-posttest design, all these studies indicated improvements in speaking skills from pretests to post-tests within groups, with the EGs exhibiting more significant gains than the CGs in the post-tests. The third strand features one study ([Lin and Mubarok 2021](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_043)), in which participants utilised chatbots with different functionalities. The study investigated the effect of integrating mind maps into AI chatbots in a five-week intervention. Findings showed that the EG achieved significantly higher post-test scores than the CG, reflecting the positive impact of mind maps.

The remaining studies using language learning applications in speaking differed in their interventions. Three of the four studies involved a CG receiving traditional teacher-led instruction and an EG receiving AI-assisted learning on Speeko ([Shafiee Rad 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_058)), FIF oral English Platform ([Wu 2022](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_072)), or Duolingo ([Qiao and Zhao 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_057)). All three studies showed notably greater improvements in the EG. Another study ([Zou et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_080)) targeted the influence of interactive activities, such as peer evaluation, in chatbot-assisted speaking practice. Both groups using chatbots with or without interactive activities attained better speaking scores following 5-week practice, with participants engaging in interactive activities scoring significantly higher.

Collectively, speaking studies highlighted the potential usefulness of chatbots to improve speaking skills. However, caution should be applied when interpreting the findings, since the studies adopted various speaking measures. While several studies focused on sub-measures of speaking, such as fluency, accuracy, and pronunciation (e.g., [Kim et al. 2021a](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_036)), other studies only reported an overall score without explaining the marking descriptors. Future investigations may adopt complexity, accuracy, and fluency as indicators of speaking performance. These indicators, which have been widely used for measuring language learning outcomes in applied linguistics, can enhance the comparability of findings across studies.


#### 4.2.6 Evidence of AI-assisted reading learning outcomes

One reading study reported learning outcomes. [Kim and Cha (2023)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_034) used a pretest-posttest research design to examine the role of AI translators in reading comprehension among 113 South Korean university students over one semester. The participants were divided into three groups: one receiving teacher-led instructions to analyse reading texts, another using AI translators along with teacher-led instructions, and the third receiving revised AI translations alongside teacher-led instructions. All three groups significantly improved their reading comprehension scores; yet, the authors reported no evidence of significant differences between groups. They argued that the observed improvement across groups might be attributed to factors other than AI interventions, such as the natural progression of learning. Calculating effect sizes could have provided a more comprehensive understanding of the magnitude of the effect.


#### 4.2.7 Evidence of AI-assisted writing learning outcomes

Nineteen studies investigated L2/FL writing learning outcomes, of which 7 focused on chatbots and 12 on AI-assisted writing and evaluation tools, all reporting positive outcomes. Five of the seven chatbot studies examined the use of ChatGPT, a chatbot currently receiving considerable scholarly attention, though they slightly differed in the intervention for the CG. Four studies ([Boudouaia et al. 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_005); [Ghafouri et al. 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_019); [Mahapatra 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_046); [Song and Song 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_060)) compared EG receiving ChatGPT feedback with CG receiving teacher feedback. These studies consistently showed improved writing scores in both groups, with the EG gaining significantly higher post-test scores than the CG. In the remaining study ([Wiboolyasarin et al. 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_071)), the EG received personalised and timely ChatGPT-generated writing feedback, while the CG simply completed the tasks without any feedback. Results revealed a significant gain in writing proficiency only in the EG. However, due to the lack of feedback in the CG, caution should be applied when attributing the writing improvement to the use of AI or the provision of feedback more generally.

Two other chatbot studies by [Zhang et al. (2023a](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_075), [2023b)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_076) investigated the impact of chatbots on EFL argumentative writing through one-group pretest-posttest studies. Although the participants scored higher in post-tests, the lack of a CG made it unclear whether the observed improvements were due to the AI intervention or natural progression. The limitation in study designs resulted in “moderate” RoB in these studies.

Of the 12 studies on AI-assisted writing and evaluation tools, six studies (e.g., [Shafiee Rad et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_059)) adopted similar research designs to some of the above-mentioned chatbot studies, involving EGs receiving automated writing feedback from the AI systems and CGs receiving traditional feedback from teachers or peers. Consistent with findings in those chatbot studies, the EG’s writing test scores were significantly improved in the post-test.

Four other studies ([Kostikova and Miasoiedova 2019](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_038); [Sun 2022](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_061); [Wang 2022](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_067); [Zhang 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_074)) adopted one-group pretest-posttest designs, all demonstrating a notable gain in writing test scores after the intervention. Similar to the limitations pointed out in [Zhang et al.’s (2023a](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_075), [2023b)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_076) studies, the absence of CG leads to problems with attributing the improvements to AI interventions.

Despite the general consistency in pretest-posttest design and support for effectiveness, the writing studies varied in terms of specific evaluation rubrics for writing performance, which resembles the limitations highlighted in the speaking studies. For example, several studies assessed writing performance according to different sub-measures (e.g., [Song and Song 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_060)), whereas the remaining studies reported overall writing scores (e.g., [Shafiee Rad et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_059)). The inconsistency in marking descriptors may limit the comparability of findings across studies.


#### 4.2.8 Evidence of AI-assisted integrated language skills learning outcomes

Eight studies examined the effectiveness of AI tools in enhancing integrated language skills, where two or more language skills were developed simultaneously. Five of them compared AI-mediated instruction (EG) with traditional teacher-led instruction (CG), which covered various types of AI technologies, including AI-assisted language learning applications or platforms ([Azamatova et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_003); [Kim 2022a](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_032); [Wei 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_070)), chatbots ([Nozhovnik et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_051)), and ITSs ([Liu and Ardakani 2022](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_044)). All studies, except [Liu and Ardakani (2022)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_044), showed that both groups improved their test scores from the pretest to the post-test, with the EG exhibiting more gains. In [Liu and Ardakani (2022)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_044), the researchers observed that their system failed to exert a statistically significant influence on participants’ language skills.

Two studies report intervention with a single group. These cohort studies also demonstrated a positive effect of AI-assisted language learning platforms ([Kim 2022b](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_033); [Zhou 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_078)). However, similar to the above-mentioned studies without CG, the study findings should be interpreted with caution, since the improvements might be ascribed to participants’ natural progression in language proficiency.

Overall, 51 studies reported findings on AI-assisted language learning outcomes in higher education settings, which mostly substantiated the positive role of AI technologies as either alternatives or supplements to traditional instruction. However, the RoB in studies may limit the trustworthiness of findings.



### 4.3 Evidence of non-linguistic outcomes

Following the synthesis of L2/FL acquisition outcomes, this section turns to an in-depth analysis of findings on learners’ non-linguistic learning outcomes, which were mainly collected from questionnaires, interviews, and focus group data. The findings can be categorised into the following five main themes.

#### 4.3.1 General attitudes

Forty-one studies examined participants’ general attitudes towards the use of AI tools in language learning, identifying both affordances and challenges.

In terms of the benefits, most studies reported participants’ perceived effectiveness and perceived ease of use of different types of AI technologies. Only [Zhang et al. (2023a](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_075), [2023b)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_076) found from questionnaire and interview data that participants perceived chatbot-based learning as less effective for improving argumentative writing than they had expected. The result may be explained by the relatively short duration of the intervention (5 weeks), which might be insufficient for participants to notice writing improvements and internalise the learning outcomes.

Problematic issues with AIEd tools have also been identified. First, some learners reported that these tools did not provide authentic and reliable interactions. For example, [Tegos et al. (2014)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_062) examined a conversational agent designed to facilitate classroom communication and observed that some learners found its computer-generated voice unnatural. Similarly, studies on ChatGPT (e.g., [Karataş et al. 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_029)) revealed participants’ concerns about the reliability of ChatGPT-generated responses. Since these studies focused on ChatGPT-3.5, future research is needed to examine whether the use of ChatGPT-5 would generate different findings.

Secondly, studies reported personalisation issues. As [Chen et al. (2020a)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_008) noted, some participants complained that the chatbot in this study failed to tailor responses to their needs. The repetitive practice questions provided by the chatbot also led to boredom among the participants.

Finally, some participants expressed privacy concerns about the AIEd tools regarding data collection and storage ([Jamshed et al. 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_028)). Overall, general attitudes towards AI-assisted language learning tools were mixed.


#### 4.3.2 Motivation

Eleven studies examined the effect of AI technologies on learning motivation, all reporting positive findings. Among studies comparing AI interventions (EG) against traditional controls (CG), the EG consistently exhibited higher motivation levels than the CG in post-intervention questionnaires and interviews. For example, [Wang and Feng (2023)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_068) studied the application of ChatGPT for reading assistance. The EG was assisted by ChatGPT for story background interpretation, vocabulary explanation, and sentence translation, while the CG employed electronic dictionaries to read traditional paper books. Questionnaire data showed that the EG reported higher reading speed, comprehension rate, and motivation levels. Studies that involved only one cohort group reported similar findings on enhanced motivation for language learning. For instance, [Chew and Chua (2020)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_011) examined the application of a humanoid robot for basic Chinese vocabulary learning. Interviews with six university students revealed that learning with the robot enhanced their motivation. The self-report data could be more robust if further substantiated by quantitative measures.

All these studies, except for [Liao (2023)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_042), collected self-report motivation data from questionnaires and interviews only once after the intervention. This approach may have potentially led to an overestimation of the effect of AIEd tools on motivation, since participants might report improved motivation shortly after using the tool due to novelty effects, but their enhanced motivation might not be sustained over time. To reduce these biases, future studies could combine self-report data with objective measures and collect data multiple times.


#### 4.3.3 Willingness to communicate (WTC)

Five speaking studies investigated the effect of AI tools on WTC in L2/FL, all observing an improved WTC after the intervention. Among them, four studies compared EGs who practised speaking with chatbots ([Fathi et al. 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_016); [Kim and Su 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_035)) or AI-assisted language learning applications ([Shafiee Rad 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_058); [Zhang et al. 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_077)) against CGs who interacted with peers or teachers. These studies adopted validated questionnaires consisting of Likert-scale items to gauge participants’ perceived WTC. The results consistently revealed that the EGs exhibited significantly higher WTC levels than the CGs. However, since both CGs and EGs in these studies received regular teacher-led speaking instruction, it is difficult to isolate the potential effect of traditional teaching activities on WTC.

The remaining study, [Ayedoun et al. (2019)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_002), examined a chatbot equipped with communicative strategies (e.g., requests for clarification) and affective backchannels (e.g., verbal and non-verbal cues such as nodding). Self-report survey data showed that combining communicative strategies with affective backchannels was most effective for improving participants’ WTC.

Overall, studies demonstrated the benefits of AI tools in enhancing WTC, but potential biases from confounding variables and selection bias necessitate cautious interpretation of the findings. Studies with more rigorous controls are needed to assess the effect of AI-assisted applications on L2/FL WTC in different countries.


#### 4.3.4 Learning anxiety

Five studies used questionnaires and interviews to examine the effect of AI tools on learning anxiety, yielding inconsistent results. Among them, three studies compared AI-assisted EGs with CGs receiving traditional teacher-led instruction. [Kim and Cha (2023)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_034) investigated the influence of AI translators on reading comprehension and identified from open-ended questionnaires that nearly 20 % of the EG regarded AI translators as anxiety-relievers. Similarly, [Kim and Su (2024)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_035) and [Zhang et al. (2024)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_077) established the effectiveness of chatbots in relieving speaking anxiety from Likert-scale questionnaires. A shared limitation of these studies was the lack of inferential statistics and effect size reporting, which rendered the conclusions less convincing.

By contrast, the remaining two studies ([Çakmak 2022](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_006); [El Shazly 2021](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_013)) reported increased speaking anxiety from scale questionnaires. The result might arise from problems with the chatbot in recognising certain sounds in students’ oral output and the ensuing communication breakdowns that frustrated the students. While communication breakdowns can bring opportunities for input modifications, they might increase anxiety when students struggle to be understood by the AI tool.


#### 4.3.5 Learning interest

Four studies focused on the effect of chatbots on language learning interests, generating mixed results. In [Chew and Chua (2020)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_011) and [Wang and Feng (2023)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_068), participants reported enhanced interest in learning after the intervention. However, since these studies either did not specify the duration of the intervention or lasted for a relatively short time period, it should be cautioned that the positive effect on learning interest might arise from the novelty effect of newly introduced technologies.

Two speaking studies by [Fryer et al. (2017](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_017), [2019)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_018) examined the effect of chatbots on long-term learning interest to further explore whether the novelty effect could be sustained over time. [Fryer et al. (2017)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_017) compared chatbot against human task partners to investigate their effect on 122 Japanese EFL university students’ speaking task interest. Quantitative questionnaire data showed a significant drop in the chatbot group’s task interest over time, while no significant decline in the human partner group was observed, suggesting that perhaps the novelty effect did not occur in the human partner group. Another study by [Fryer et al. (2019)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_018) also compared the chatbot intervention against a human partner control. Quantitative survey data revealed that participants’ interest in chatbot conversation decreased because of the diminishing novelty effect, but it showed a notable rebound after five months. The results suggested the chatbot’s potential to stimulate and maintain interest in the long term, despite an initial decline.

Altogether, 59 of the 71 studies in tertiary education settings reported findings on non-linguistic outcomes of AI-assisted language learning, yielding mixed results. Problems with study designs, such as limited intervention durations and the introduction of confounding variables, warrant caution regarding the reliability of findings.




5 Discussion
------------

### 5.1 RQ1: what is the current status of research that has been conducted?

Given the burgeoning use of AI technologies to empower educational practices, the recent growth in the number of studies is unsurprising. The publication trend aligned with findings from earlier systematic reviews on AILEd ([Liang et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_041); [Zhu and Wang 2025](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_079)). Driven by ongoing technological breakthroughs in the Transformer algorithms ([Law 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_039)), research output in AI-assisted language learning is expected to grow in the coming years.

In terms of educational levels, the review was consistent with previous studies ([Liang et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_041); [Yang and Kyun 2022](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_073); [Zhu and Wang 2025](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_079)) in identifying higher education as the primary research context. This focus might be attributed to the convenience of accessing tertiary-level students who were cognitively mature enough to harness advanced AI technologies and complete relevant tasks.

Regarding the research contexts, the finding that most studies were conducted in Asian contexts diverged from [Liang et al. (2023)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_041), who identified the US as the major country of contribution. This discrepancy might be due to the recent rapid integration of technology into classrooms in Asian regions ([UNESCO 2022](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_063)). Additionally, consistent with previous review findings ([Yang and Kyun 2022](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_073); [Zhu and Wang 2025](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_079)), most studies focused on learning English as an FL, with only sporadic studies conducted in other language learning contexts. This trend underscores a need for studies focusing on the acquisition of languages other than English (LOTE) to provide a comprehensive understanding of the effectiveness of AI-assisted language learning across various research contexts.

In terms of studied language skills, this review reported that writing received particular research attention, which supported what was found in [Yang and Kyun (2022)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_073) as well as [Zhu and Wang (2025)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_079). Additionally, speaking and vocabulary studies were prominent in the number of publications, which slightly differed from [Liang et al. (2023)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_041), who found that writing, reading, and vocabulary were the top three most-researched language skills. The increasing research interest in speaking might be attributed to the recent innovations and developments in chatbots, especially the iterative GPT series ([Gillani et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_020)). The rapid advancements in chatbots have also been manifested in the largest proportion of chatbot studies in this review and the recent surge in the number of publications, driven by the emergence of LLM-powered chatbots such as ChatGPT.

The vast majority of studies did not situate their study within a theoretical framework, which aligns with the findings of [Chen et al. (2020b)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_009) and [Wang et al. (2024)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_069). Among the remaining studies, the most frequently used theory was [Vygotsky’s (1978)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_066) Sociocultural Theory. A core concept of the theory is the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD), which refers to the tasks that can be performed under guidance but cannot be performed independently. AI applications function to provide such scaffolded support and meet personalisation needs ([Qiao and Zhao 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_057)). Another prominent perspective was the cognitivist approach, including the Input Hypothesis, the Interaction Hypothesis, and the Noticing Hypothesis. Specifically, traditional EFL classrooms are necessarily limited in terms of their input quantity and individualisation. AILEd technologies can address this problem by offering personalised comprehensible input and encouraging learners to engage in interactive learning activities. Altogether, the cognitivist and sociocultural perspectives on second language acquisition both lend support for the use of AI in language learning. However, the overall scarcity of theoretically informed studies is concerning. For the field to mature, future research should engage more explicitly with established theories of language learning or contribute to theory-building.


### 5.2 RQ2: what is the extent of empirical evidence for the effectiveness of AI-assisted language learning?

#### 5.2.1 The extent of findings on linguistic outcomes of AI-assisted L2/FL learning

A total of 51 studies reported findings on substantive learning outcomes. [Figure 9](#j_jccall-2025-0020_fig_009) illustrates AI-assisted language learning outcomes across different language skills. It shows that studies with rigorous study designs (i.e., rated “low” RoB) reported positive learning outcomes in all grammar studies and some of the studies on vocabulary, speaking, writing, and integrated skills. However, more speaking and writing studies, represented by larger bubbles, produced positive results but received “moderate” RoB ratings, suggesting that their findings should be interpreted with caution. Additionally, the robustness of positive results in pronunciation and listening was affected by moderate or high RoB. Taken together, while 47 studies showed positive learning outcomes after AI interventions, the cumulative weight of evidence was limited due to RoB in individual studies. Overall, although most studies claimed that AI-assisted language learning was effective, robust evidence for positive learning outcomes was limited.

#### 5.2.2 The extent of findings on non-linguistic outcomes of AI-assisted L2/FL learning

Regarding non-linguistic outcomes, the considerable number of publications (*n* = 59) reflected the research trend identified by [Yang and Kyun (2022)](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_073) that learning attitudes have garnered increasing scholarly attention. More notably, a number of studies have examined the role of emotional factors such as motivation and anxiety in AI-assisted language learning. This aligns with the growing recognition in applied linguistics that emotions are worthy of investigation in second language acquisition ([Dewaele and Li 2020](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_012)). Findings on learning perceptions indicated both opportunities and challenges of AI technologies, conforming to what has been summarised in previous systematic reviews ([Liang et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_041)).

Opportunities included general positive attitudes towards AI-supported pedagogies, stronger motivation for language learning, and enhanced willingness to communicate, which have been reported across studies on all five types of AI tools. Challenges encompassed technical and ethical issues. The technical issues, primarily presented in chatbot studies, included the inaccuracies in speech recognition and the unreliability of speech output, which caused anxiety and loss of interest among language learners. These problems, consistent with previous findings ([McCrocklin 2016](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_047)), demonstrated a need to train chatbots on non-native speakers’ oral input, despite the recent technological breakthroughs in ASR and chatbots ([Han 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_022); [Hwang and Chang 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_027)). Ethical issues, such as privacy concerns, echoed findings from prior studies ([Bond et al. 2024](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_004); [Gillani et al. 2023](#j_jccall-2025-0020_ref_020)). Altogether, the mixed learning perception findings suggested the need for future research to examine how AI-assisted language learning environments could shape learners’ emotional trajectories.




6 Conclusions
-------------

### 6.1 Summary of findings

This study systematically reviewed publications on the effectiveness of AI-assisted tools in L2/FL learning. To explore the current research status (RQ1), the systematic review searched 9 electronic databases and identified 105 eligible studies, involving 10 different L2s/FLs, 23 locations of study, 5 types of AI technologies, 8 language skills, and participants from diverse gender, age, and proficiency groups. The review revealed research trends in implementing chatbots to develop writing skills among tertiary-level students in Asian countries, such as China and South Korea. A common problem with the retrieved studies, however, was a lack of theoretical backgrounds.

Due to the dominance of research in higher education contexts, RQ2 was narrowed down to delve into higher education settings and report evidence of linguistic and non-linguistic learning outcomes. The synthesis of 71 studies in tertiary contexts found that 51 reported findings on linguistic learning effectiveness and 59 included findings on non-linguistic outcomes. Positive learning outcomes of AI technologies were generally reported across studies on different language skills, except for four studies. However, the moderate RoB across studies limited the robustness of conclusions. Additionally, studies reporting non-linguistic outcomes identified generally consistent results on learning motivation and willingness to communicate, yet mixed findings on learning anxiety and learning interest. Given the opportunities and challenges of AI-assisted language learning, the subsequent sections present the implications of the findings and critically discuss the limitations of this review.


### 6.2 Theoretical and practical implications

The review findings have implications for language learners, teachers, researchers, and AI developers. Practically, given the effectiveness of AI-assisted language learning tools, language learners and teachers can integrate the novel technology into the learning and teaching process to enhance learning outcomes. At the same time, developers are guided to address the problems with existing AI tools, such as technical inaccuracies.

Theoretically, the fact that most of the studies were not framed in clear theoretical frameworks makes it difficult to draw firm conclusions about how AI-assisted language learning aligns with or supports specific second language acquisition (SLA) theories. Future research may benefit from being explicitly grounded in relevant theoretical frameworks – not only from SLA but also from educational technology theories (e.g. Technology Acceptance Model). Looking ahead, emotional theories such as Control Value Theory can be integrated to examine how positive emotions (e.g., enjoyment and motivation) and negative emotions (e.g., anxiety) influence learners’ AI-assisted language learning experience.


### 6.3 Limitations and future research

A major limitation of this review concerns its selection bias, since it only conducted an in-depth review of studies in higher education contexts. Future systematic reviews should synthesise studies in all educational phases to provide a more comprehensive picture of research trends.

Another limitation is that, to maintain a manageable amount of data, the review excluded studies on the use of AI educational tools by teachers, for example, to support them in lesson planning and generating materials. Future empirical studies and systematic reviews can explore the similarities and differences in the perceptions of using AI tools in language learning between learners and teachers.

Finally, while most studies claimed the effectiveness of AI-assisted language learning, robust evidence remained limited. Future studies should employ more rigorous research designs to enhance confidence in attributing positive language learning outcomes and perceptions to AI interventions. With the rapid developments of AI technologies such as GenAI, future research can also examine how language learners use these emerging tools to improve their learning outcomes and how their emotions influence their engagement with GenAI-assisted learning environments.
