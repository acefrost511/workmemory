## Abstract
The integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) into K-12 education holds significant promise for transforming teaching and learning processes. Central to this transformation are teachers’ perspectives, which play a key role in shaping the adoption and effective use of AI technologies in classrooms. Existing literature has not systematically synthesized these perspectives through the lens of well-established theoretical frameworks, nor developed structured models to guide evaluation efforts. To address these gaps, this study applies constructs from the UTAUT and TPACK frameworks to guide a thematic synthesis of findings, with the aim of identifying enabling and constraining factors influencing AI integration in K-12 education from the standpoint of educators. The review process followed PRISMA guidelines to ensure rigorous and systematic literature selection and analysis. Findings suggest that teachers generally exhibit a blend of optimism and cautious concern regarding the adoption of AI in K-12 education. We identified three critical factors as particularly influential: teachers’ Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge (TPACK), teacher agency, and teacher affective orientations. In response to the complexities of implementation, we propose a novel preliminary assessment model, guided by the evaluative principles of UNESCO’s \*Global Education Monitoring Report 2023\*. The proposed model offers a practice-grounded application of global policy dimensions: \*Equity, Sustainability, Appropriateness, and Scalability\*, linked with synthesized classroom-level insights. It further delineates ten essential subthemes, providing a structured approach for evaluating the effectiveness of AI integration in educational settings.

## 1. Introduction
Artificial Intelligence (AI) is increasingly recognized as a game-changing technology in education that can improve student learning outcomes and teaching methods. In K-12 settings, AI is being leveraged to facilitate the explanation of complex concepts, support classroom management, and streamline instructional planning (Yim & Su, [Citation2024](#)). However, despite these promising developments, the integration of AI into classrooms is accompanied by a range of challenges. These include technical development, concerns about data privacy, and the need for professional development (Ahmed, [Citation2023](#)). As the gatekeepers in educational reform, teachers play a central role in mediating the adoption of AI, making their insights and experiences crucial for understanding the feasibility and direction of AI integration in K-12 education.
The integration of AI in education extends beyond the adoption of advanced technologies. It demands an informed understanding of how these tools can support teaching and learning. For educators to harness the full potential of AI, they must develop a deeper awareness of its pedagogical affordances, the challenges associated with its use, and its practical applications within the classroom contexts (Celik, [Citation2023](#)). However, many teachers report feeling unprepared to implement AI effectively, largely due to insufficient training and a lack of sustained professional support (Mamit & Maksetbay, [Citation2023](#)). This disconnect between the potential of AI and its classroom application is further compounded by concerns related to data privacy, the perceived threat to established teaching practices, and the broader uncertainties about AI’s role in preserving the human-centered nature of education (Ahmed, [Citation2023](#)).
Despite the growing body of research on the impact of AI on student learning, the perspectives of teachers, who are key agents in the implementation of this technology, remain largely underexplored. Teachers’ acceptance, understanding, and confidence in using AI are crucial to its successful integration in classroom settings. Accordingly, it is imperative to systematically examine their attitudes to inform effective implementation in K-12 education. Moreover, prior reviews have not systematically synthesized teachers’ perspectives using established theoretical frameworks, nor have they proposed structured models that can guide evaluation efforts. This article addresses this gap by reviewing existing studies on teachers’ perceptions of AI in K-12 contexts, with particular attention to the factors that facilitate or impede its adoption. Building on this review, we propose a structured framework for understanding the multifaceted relationships among cognitive, emotional, and action-oriented factors that shape teachers’ engagement with AI in K-12 education. Based on these insights, this study introduces a preliminary assessment model designed to evaluate the effectiveness of AI integration, providing practical guidance for educators, administrators, and policymakers who seek to identify implementation gaps and make evidence-based decisions to support the adoption, scaling, and refinement of AI technologies in alignment with educational goals.
## 2. Literature review
The perceptions of K-12 educators regarding the use of AI in the educational realm encompass their perspectives, beliefs, and understanding of its incorporation and implementation within classrooms (Şenel, [Citation2024](#)). These perceptions significantly contribute to the efficacy of AI integration and are shaped by various determinants, including educators’ familiarity with AI technologies, prior experience with educational technologies, and anticipations about AI’s potential benefits and challenges (Kim & Kim, [Citation2022](#)). Through their daily engagement with classroom dynamics, teachers develop valuable insights into how AI can be adapted to support pedagogical goals, facilitate differentiated instruction, and enhance teaching effectiveness. These insights are essential for the design and development of AI tools that are responsive to the practical needs of educators and grounded in real-world teaching contexts (Chounta et al., [Citation2022](#)). In addition to these generalized perceptions, K-12 teachers offer more situated insights into how AI can be meaningfully integrated into classroom practice. As facilitators of students’ formative learning experiences, teachers serve as key contributors in shaping their future learning paths (Bledsoe et al., [Citation2004](#)). Their perspective often emphasize how AI assists in classroom management and tailoring instructional strategies to address the diverse needs of learners (Nazaretsky et al., [Citation2022](#)). Educators who hold favorable views toward AI are more likely to adopt and integrate it into their pedagogical practices (Kim & Kim, [Citation2022](#)). They tend to regard AI as a tool that enhances individualized instruction, simplifies complex content, and enables real-time feedback on student performance (Slamet, [Citation2024](#)). Studies have reported that educators value its utility in lesson planning, differentiated instruction, and the creation of engaging, student-centered learning environments (Lin & Van Brummelen, [Citation2021](#)), they also report a degree of skepticism, particularly regarding the impersonal nature of AI, the risk of algorithmic bias, and the persistent need for professional development and training (Aghaziarati et al., [Citation2023](#); Tatar et al., [Citation2025](#)).
Research demonstrates that the adoption and integration of AI technologies in K-12 classrooms are significantly influenced by the teachers’ interpretations and applications of these tools (Lee et al., [Citation2024](#)). Therefore, understanding teachers’ perspectives is essential for designing professional development programs that build their confidence in using AI and equip them with the necessary skills to incorporate it effectively within their teaching methodologies. Such initiatives support classroom implementation (Garofalo & Farenga, [Citation2025](#); Kim & Kim, [Citation2022](#)) and ensure that AI tools are developed to meet the specific needs of K-12 education, namely that they are user-friendly, aligned with curricular goals, and adaptable to diverse student needs (Kizilcec, [Citation2023](#)). Furthermore, eliciting teachers’ opinions on AI can provide educational stakeholders and policymakers with grounded insights into classroom realities and the specific factors that shape effective implementation (Aghaziarati et al., [Citation2023](#)). Recognizing these insights can inform the formulation of targeted implementation strategies and context-aware policies (Yau et al., [Citation2023](#)). Beyond practical implementation, educators’ perspectives also highlight the importance of addressing ethical implications in its deployment (Nazaretsky et al., [Citation2021](#)). Actively involving educators in the design and evaluation of AI tools fosters transparency, builds trust, and helps ensure that these technologies are consistent with and supportive of educational objectives (Akgun & Greenhow, [Citation2022](#)). While existing literature highlights the potential advantages of AI in education, substantial shortcomings and critical gaps remain that warrant further attention. Firstly, although a substantial body of research addresses the general application of AI in educational contexts (Zawacki-Richter et al., [Citation2019](#)), limited attention has been given to the perspective of teachers on the integration of AI in education (Lee et al., [Citation2024](#)). In particular, research focusing on the use of AI in K-12 classrooms is still scarce (Yue et al., [Citation2022](#)). This study seeks to fill these gaps by conducting a systematic review of the literature on teachers’ views, with particular attention to issues such as teaching effectiveness, ethical considerations, and the importance of professional development. Understanding these dimensions helps address concerns and ensure the appropriate and responsible use of AI techniques in K-12 settings. By synthesizing current research and identifying directions for future inquiry, this review aims to guide AI implementation in K-12 education and inform educators, policymakers, and researchers about the opportunities and challenges associated with it.
## 3. Theoretical framework
This study employs the theoretical underpinnings of the following models:
### 3.1. Unified theory of acceptance and use of technology (UTAUT)
The UTAUT framework (Venkatesh et al., [Citation2003](#)) provides a comprehensive model for understanding how individuals adopt and use technology. The model outlines four core constructs that shape behavioral intention and technology use: Performance expectancy, Effort expectancy, Social influence, and Facilitating conditions. These constructs position UTAUT as a comprehensive model for analyzing the factors that influence technology integration and user engagement across various contexts.
### 3.2. Technological pedagogical content Knowledge (TPACK)
The TPACK framework (Mishra & Koehler, [Citation2006](#)) provides a model for understanding how educators integrate technology into their teaching. Expanding on the concept of Pedagogical Content Knowledge (PCK) (Shulman, [Citation1986](#)), the TPACK framework incorporates Technological Knowledge (TK) as a fundamental dimension that integrates with pedagogy and content knowledge, emphasizing their interdependence. The framework emphasizes that educators must understand how technology enhances pedagogical strategies and subject content to improve learning and teaching. TPACK consists of seven fundamental concepts: Content Knowledge (CK), Pedagogical Knowledge (PK), Technological Knowledge (TK), Pedagogical Content Knowledge (PCK), Technological Content Knowledge (TCK), Technological Pedagogical Knowledge (TPK), and Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge (TPACK).
This study employs UTAUT’s constructs, Performance Expectancy, Effort Expectancy, and Social Influence, alongside TPACK’s TCK, PCK, and TPACK, to analyze the factors influencing AI adoption in K-12 education. Given UTAUT’s flexibility and comprehensive scope, numerous researchers have adopted this model to explore various contexts. For instance, a study applied UTAUT to study student teachers’ use of interactive whiteboards (Wong et al., [Citation2013](#)), while another study used UTAUT to explore students’ experiences with university reference databases (Avdic & Eklund, [Citation2010](#)). Similarly, this study applies Performance Expectancy to understand how educators perceive AI’s potential to enhance instructional effectiveness and student learning outcomes. Effort Expectancy provides a lens to assess how the perceived ease of AI integration influences educators’ willingness to adopt AI-based tools. Social Influence offers a framework to analyze how peer adoption, institutional expectations, and administrative support shape educators’ attitudes toward AI usage in classrooms, whereas previous studies have leveraged TPACK to explore various aspects of technology adoption in education. For instance, TPACK has been applied to support the teaching of 21st-century skills such as critical thinking and collaboration (Valtonen et al., [Citation2017](#)) and it has also been employed to examine teachers’ knowledge of using web technologies (Lee & Tsai, [Citation2010](#)). In a similar way, this study also applies TPACK constructs such as PCK, which offers a lens to interpret how AI supports instructional decision-making and ensures pedagogical alignment. Moreover, TCK provides a structured perspective on how AI tools support subject matter delivery. TPACK offers a structured perspective to guide the analysis of AI’s seamless integration into teaching, emphasizing its interplay with pedagogy and content.
Furthermore, drawing on the guiding principles outlined in UNESCO’s \*Global Education Monitoring (GEM) Report 2023\* (Antoninis et al., [Citation2023](#)), this study proposes a preliminary assessment model centered on four core considerations: equity, sustainability, appropriateness, and scalability. Anchored in teachers’ perspectives, the proposed model offers a structured framework for the impact of AI in classroom settings, ensuring that technological innovations are aligned with pedagogical objectives and the broader aims of quality education. Building upon these interconnected foundations, the current study explores the following research questions:
RQ1:
How do teachers perceive the factors that facilitate or impede the integration of AI into K–12 education?
RQ2:
How can the effective integration of AI in K–12 education be assessed from a pedagogical standpoint?
## 4. Method
### 4.1. Search strategy
We conducted this systematic review following the guidelines set forth by the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) (PRISMA Group, 2010). We select articles from multiple well-regarded databases, including Scopus, Web of Science, and Education Resources Information Center (ERIC), as they have global recognition, extensive coverage of the education domain, and a robust source of scholarly resources. Our final search query was ((Perception\\* OR View\\* OR Outlook) AND (Teach\\* OR Instruct\\*) AND (AI OR “Artificial Intelligence”) AND (Primary OR Preparatory OR Secondary OR “Middle School” OR “High School” OR Elementary OR “K-12”)). This query yielded 306 articles on Scopus, 148 on Web of Science, and 74 results on ERIC. Further, we used a snowball method to gather relevant and comprehensive research material. A total of 627 articles were found.
### 4.2. Initial screening stage
The initial screening stage involved a preliminary evaluation based on titles and abstracts to have a general idea of the research environment. An Excel spreadsheet was used to efficiently organize and arrange the data, which served as the foundation for further review. The articles were screened based on the inclusion and exclusion criteria given below.
### 4.3. Inclusion and exclusion criteria
Studies were considered if they (a) exclusively focused on examining teachers’ perspectives, (b) specifically discussed AI’s impact and its associated effects, (c) were confined to the K-12 educational context, (d) presented robust evidence or findings regarding teachers’ perceptions of AI use in K-12 settings, (e) maintained rigorous methodologies and clarity in their presentation, (f) were published in reliable peer-reviewed journals, and (g) were available exclusively in English to maintain consistency in the analysis.
Studies were excluded if they (a) involved pre-service teachers, (b) focused on teacher populations with specific demographic characteristics that limited generalizability, (c) included teachers addressing subjects outside the traditional K-12 curriculum, such as vocational training or specialized courses, or (d) were review articles or non-empirical studies that did not contribute original research findings. Furthermore, the PRISMA chart demonstrates the flow of the screening process as shown in Figure 1. This chart detailed each step of the review process, from identification and screening to eligibility and inclusion. Overall, it offered a clear and ordered overview of the article selection process, which ensured transparency and accuracy.

### 4.4. Full text review and final selection
Then, we performed a careful rechecking process to ensure consistency and accuracy just to increase the reliability of the screening results. The selection process adhered strictly to the established norms, with abstracts and conclusions of qualified papers carefully looked over. After the screening, which resulted in the tentative inclusion of 147 papers, a full examination was performed. Then, after some more scrutiny, 31 papers that met all the inclusion and exclusion rules were chosen to be the core material for this investigation. These studies were reviewed for their quality and usefulness, with a focus on themes such as teacher views and the influence of AI on educational results. A detailed summary of the key features of the included studies, including their (a) author, (b) discipline, (c) AI tool used (s), (d) type of study, (e) population, (f) study design, (g) inference from the study, and (h) country of publication, is provided in ([Supplementary Data Table S1](https://doi.org/https://doi.org/10.1080/07380569.2025.2602507)). To ensure coherence across insights, recurring themes were systematically organized, allowing for a structured synthesis of the primary factors shaping teachers’ perspectives on AI adoption.
### 4.5. Thematic analysis approach
To analyze the 31 selected studies, we employed a thematic synthesis approach. This synthesis was guided by selected constructs from the UTAUT and TPACK frameworks as interpretive lenses. These constructs served as sensitizing concepts to help organize and derive novel thematic and sub-thematic patterns reflecting teachers’ perceived enablers and barriers to AI integration in K-12 education.
The key component of the UTAUT model, performance expectancy, is the teacher’s belief that technology will improve teaching outcomes (Venkatesh et al., [Citation2003](#)). This is a determinant of adoption of technology and a driver of intentional action. When teachers leverage these conditions to adapt tools, co-design lessons, and assert autonomy in shaping their professional practice and student learning experiences, this is what we have conceptualized as Teacher Agency. Furthermore, within UTAUT, effort expectancy and social influence extend into the affective domain by shaping teachers’ emotional orientations toward technology (Venkatesh et al., [Citation2003](#)). Their enthusiasm, skepticism, anxiety, or ethical concerns emerge from whether AI feels easy to use, trustworthy, and supported by professional communities. These emotional responses influence confidence and readiness to adopt AI, which we translate as Teacher Affection, that guide willingness to engage with AI. Lastly, the TPACK framework emphasizes that effective technology integration requires teachers’ deep understanding of how technological knowledge interacts with pedagogy and content (Mishra & Koehler, [Citation2006](#)). When educators demonstrate the cognitive ability to align AI tools with subject matter goals, adapt instructional methods, and resolve technical challenges, this reflects their Teacher TPACK, the knowledge-based foundation for meaningful AI integration.
Therefore, Teacher Agency, Teacher TPACK and Teacher Affection, correspond to the agentic, cognitive and affective dimensions of teacher engagement respectively, which play a pivotal role in adopting and effectively utilizing AI tools. Based on these three themes, we draw out several sub-themes from our review, categorized under our three fundamental themes to discuss in detail their potential to foster or hinder the AI integration process.
### 4.6. Development of the preliminary assessment model
After completing the thematic synthesis, we adopted the four guiding questions from UNESCO’s Global Education Monitoring (GEM) Report 2023 (Equity, Sustainability, Appropriateness, and Scalability) as overarching evaluative categories. These dimensions served as a policy-aligned lens through which we reexamined classroom-level findings from the included studies. This synthesis led to subthemes that highlighted practical considerations for evaluating AI integration under each dimension. On this basis, we developed a practice-informed preliminary model for assessing the contextual effectiveness of AI integration in K-12 education.
## 5. Results and discussion
Despite a growing body of work on AI in education, existing literature lacks a synthesis of teachers’ perspectives grounded in well-established theoretical frameworks and offers no structured models to evaluate classroom-level implementation. Addressing these gaps, our review, guided by selected constructs from the UTAUT and TPACK frameworks, identifies three critical factors: Teacher TPACK, Teacher Agency, and Teacher Affection, along with eight sub-themes (see Figure 2) that collectively shape the integration of AI into K-12 education.

### 5.1. Teacher TPACK
This theme was reflected in the shortlisted articles through two sub-themes: (1) innovation pedagogy with AI, and (2) technical AI competency. Innovation pedagogy with AI reflects how AI supports creative, student-centered teaching (Celik, [Citation2023](#)), while technical AI competency captures teachers’ ability to adapt AI tools in alignment with pedagogy and subject matter (Yim & Su, [Citation2024](#)).
#### 5.1.1. Innovation pedagogy with AI
AI-driven innovation in K-12 pedagogy is reshaping classroom practices by helping teachers to deliver adaptive learning and timely feedback. Analytics platforms such as Human-Tutor Coaching Technology (HTCT) and the FACT system provide real-time insights into student progress, which support immediate instructional adjustments and facilitate the early correction of misconceptions (Booth et al., [Citation2024](#); Holstein et al., [Citation2019](#); Marcus et al., [Citation2011](#); Shamsuddinova et al., [Citation2024](#)). These adaptive feedback mechanisms encourage a transition from didactic teaching to student-centered, higher-order thinking and creativity in classrooms.
In addition to fostering cognitive engagement, educators use AI tools to promote more inclusive learning environments by implementing interventions tailored to diverse student needs (Alibakhshi et al., [Citation2023](#); Cheng & Wang, [Citation2023](#); Tatar, [Citation2023](#); Vargas, [Citation2023](#)). For instance, the NAO humanoid robot combined with ChatGPT has been effective in engaging learners on the autism spectrum (Sacco et al., [Citation2023](#)). These findings suggest that well-designed AI applications can support differentiated instruction, thereby ensuring that all students have equitable opportunities to succeed (Alenezi, [Citation2023](#)). Overall, these developments underscore that, as structured by the TPACK framework, robust teacher cognitive engagement, particularly in the domains of TCK and PCK, remains foundational for effective AI integration. Persistent gaps in these constructs continue to limit meaningful adoption (Celik et al., [Citation2022](#)).
#### 5.1.2. Technical AI competency
Technical AI competency entails the purposeful application of AI-related concepts, principles, and tools to support instruction in educational settings (Chiu et al., [Citation2024](#); Voulgari et al., [Citation2021](#)). Its development depends not only on conceptual understanding but also the practical ability to select appropriate tools, resolve technical challenges, and maintain pedagogical oversight (Lin et al., [Citation2022](#); Roshan et al., [Citation2024](#)). Empirical evidence shows that teachers with stronger technical AI competency are better able to meet these demands (Chou et al., [Citation2022](#)). However, such competency is unevenly distributed across grade levels: while elementary teachers more often report intermediate or confident proficiency, most high school and middle school teachers self-identify as beginners (Lee et al., [Citation2024](#)).
This variation in competency levels is accompanied by challenges such as limited clarity on AI principles, insufficient subject-specific content knowledge, and minimal hands-on support. Together, these factors collectively contribute to uncertainty about how to effectively embed AI tools into daily instruction (Li et al., [Citation2024](#); Ottenbreit-Leftwich et al., [Citation2023](#); Slamet, [Citation2024](#)). Within our review, technical AI competency is best understood through the TPACK construct, which integrates technological, pedagogical, and content knowledge. Teachers with well-developed TPACK are better positioned to align AI tools with instructional goals, adapt their use to diverse student contexts, and support effective integration into teaching practices. In contrast, teacher with limited TPACK presents significant barriers to the incorporation of AI in the classroom, often resulting in the underutilization of tools and diminished instructional effectiveness.
This pattern of uneven technical competency and rapidly shifting toolsets suggests that teachers’ knowledge needs are not static. Rather, effective AI integration increasingly requires teachers to recalibrate their pedagogical and technological reasoning in real time. In light of this, we suggest the idea of an “Adoptive TPACK,” which foregrounds flexibility and adaptive recalibration within TPACK as prerequisites for effective technology integration in rapidly evolving educational environments. This is not intended as a redefinition of the original model but rather as a perspective that highlights the importance of flexibility within TPACK domains for navigating continuous technological change in AI-integrated classrooms. Future studies may also consider whether such adaptive configurations hold relevance for other forms of educational technology.
### 5.2. Teacher agency
This theme was reflected through: (1) collaborative lesson planning, and (2) professional development. Collaborative lesson planning highlights how peer-driven co-design strengthens teacher ownership and autonomy (Lin & Van Brummelen, [Citation2021](#)), while professional development opportunities capture how sustained training builds confidence and agency in AI integration (Mamit & Maksetbay, [Citation2023](#)).
#### 5.2.1. Experience of collaborative lesson planning
Research consistently demonstrates that when teachers are actively involved in the co-design of AI platforms and curricula, their enthusiasm and ownership increase, enabling alignment with local classroom needs (Cheng & Wang, [Citation2023](#); Chiu et al., [Citation2022](#); Garofalo & Farenga, [Citation2025](#); Lin & Van Brummelen, [Citation2021](#); Slamet, [Citation2024](#); Tatar et al., [Citation2025](#); Xie et al., [Citation2024](#); Yau et al., [Citation2023](#)). Teacher-led action research further invigorates the iterative planning and refining of AI-supported pedagogies. Importantly, both Lawrence’s prior (Lawrence et al., [Citation2023](#)) and recent study (Lawrence et al., [Citation2024](#)) highlight that teachers prefer to retain control over AI-supported classroom orchestration while leveraging AI suggestions for informed decision-making. Our review finds that such peer-driven, participatory practices are central to fostering teacher agency, as they cultivate shared goals, professional confidence, and practical autonomy in AI integration. The synthesis further indicates that teacher agency operates along two intersecting axes: individual and collective. These dimensions often interact in several contexts, where collaboration appeared to reinforce teacher confidence and innovation, especially in the face of limited structural support. The collaborative nature underlying these initiatives aligns with the ‘\*social influence\*’ construct of the UTAUT, with peer interactions and shared goals positively influencing adoption and sustained use. However, the absence of effective collaboration, manifested in limited peer support or unclear objectives, can hinder the integration process by undermining users confidence and diminishing the possible impact of technology implementation.
#### 5.2.2. Professional development opportunities
The rapid evolution of AI technologies often outpaces the preparedness of educational systems, resulting in a notable technological proficiency gap (Berendt et al., [Citation2020](#); Celik et al., [Citation2022](#); Chiu & Chai, [Citation2020](#); Kilhoffer et al., [Citation2023](#); Sanusi et al., [Citation2024](#); Yue et al., [Citation2024](#)). In this context, professional development becomes not only desirable but essential for building both technical competence and pedagogical confidence among educators (Slamet, [Citation2024](#)). However, the extent to which teachers can access and benefit from effective professional development varies substantially across contexts, contributing directly to uneven levels of preparedness for AI integration. Our cross-national review documents this variation: studies from the Global South highlight barriers such as limited infrastructure and foundational training (Al-Qawabah, [Citation2024](#); Fundi et al., [Citation2024](#)), while findings from the Global East are mixed. South Korean teachers express satisfaction with national training, in contrast to Saudi Arabian teachers who stress the need for improved support (Alkhuzaim et al., [Citation2024](#); Lee et al., [Citation2024](#)). Global North adds further complexity, with Sweden and Estonia reporting persistent misconceptions and gaps in teacher preparation (Chounta et al., [Citation2022](#); Velander et al., [Citation2024](#)). While countries from the Global West appear in author affiliations, none of the studies in the current dataset were conducted with teacher populations from those regions, leaving questions about regional dynamics in professional preparation open for future investigation. These cross-national findings converge on a central insight: the quality and sustained organization of professional development, rather than broad regional classification, is the primary driver of teacher preparedness.
When professional development is sustained and robust, it cultivates teacher agency, empowering educators to make informed and autonomous decisions about AI use in their classroom (Ahmad & Shah, [Citation2022](#); Velander et al., [Citation2024](#)). However, its impact is contingent on sustained training and mentorship, for without continued support, educators can only develop a superficial understanding of AI, limiting their ability to exploit its full pedagogical potential (Ottenbreit-Leftwich et al., [Citation2023](#)). Thus, well-structured professional development does more than build competence; it enables practical autonomy by equipping teachers to critically evaluate AI tools, adapt them to their classrooms, and assert professional judgment, rather than passively adopting prescribed solutions. This capacity for autonomous and confident use aligns with the \*Performance Expectancy\* construct of the UTAUT, which posits that the perceived usefulness of a technology significantly influences its adoption and integration. High-quality, ongoing professional development can enhance these perceptions, promoting engagement and more effective integration of AI in education. Conversely, when educators perceive limited benefits, motivation to engage with these technologies declines, ultimately hindering successful implementation.
Adoption decisions in K-12 AI integration are shaped not only by perceived usefulness and social influence but also by a dynamic interplay between professional autonomy, collaborative co-design, and iterative, context-specific adaptation. This interplay suggests that agency in technology adoption operates as a collective and evolving process, rather than a static individual trait. To capture this nuance, we tentatively suggest the notion of a complementary construct, “Adoption Agency,” to describe this context-responsive, collaborative form of agency observed across several cases in our review. We offer this construct as a conceptual starting point and invite future research to further examine its relevance and utility in diverse implementation settings.
### 5.3. Teacher affection
This theme was reflected through four sub-themes: (1) perceived efficiency of AI, (2) attitude toward AI, (3) ethical concerns about AI, and (4) bias of AI. Perceived efficiency and attitude shape teachers’ enthusiasm or hesitation (Aghaziarati et al., [Citation2023](#); Yim & Su, [Citation2024](#)), while ethical concerns and bias highlight apprehensions about fairness, trust, and responsible use of AI (Akgun & Greenhow, [Citation2022](#); Nazaretsky et al., [Citation2021](#)).
#### 5.3.1. Perceived efficiency of AI
Teachers’ perceptions of AI as an efficient tool for streamlining daily tasks and supporting pedagogical innovation strongly influence their willingness to adopt these technologies in classroom practice (Alkhuzaim et al., [Citation2024](#); Al-Qawabah, [Citation2024](#); Shamsuddinova et al., [Citation2024](#); Wardat et al., [Citation2024](#)). For example, AI-powered platforms that automate lesson planning, grading, and attendance reduce teachers’ administrative workload and allow them to devote more time to student learning (Garofalo & Farenga, [Citation2025](#); Monteiro et al., [Citation2024](#)).
However, educators have also raised concerns about technical constraints and pedagogical limitations, which may hinder AI integration in K-12. These limitations reflect concerns that AI struggles to support diverse teaching practices and systematic assessment procedures due to its emphasis on automation over pedagogical nuance (Henriksen et al., [Citation2016](#)). Similarly, challenges in error prevention and procedural adaptability could undermine AI’s reliability, increasing frustration and resistance among educators (Chounta et al., [Citation2022](#)). These findings suggest that when AI tools are perceived as overly complex or unreliable, they tend to discourage adoption. This indicates that teachers’ willingness to adopt AI in classrooms depends largely on how straightforward or challenging they find these tools to integrate into their daily practices, a principle of \*Effort Expectancy\*.
#### 5.3.2. Attitude toward AI
Openness and readiness to integrate AI into K-12 settings primarily reflect a focus on the practical and pedagogical benefits of AI technologies. Our review highlights that most studies (Al-Qawabah, [Citation2024](#); An et al., [Citation2023](#); Ayanwale & Sanusi, [Citation2023](#); Chounta et al., [Citation2022](#); Fundi et al., [Citation2024](#); Lee et al., [Citation2024](#)) uncover two distinct trends within teachers’ perceptions, one marked by optimism about AI’s transformative potential, albeit with marginal concerns. While another trend is shaped by caution, as evidenced in works (Garofalo & Farenga, [Citation2025](#); Li et al., [Citation2024](#); Slamet, [Citation2024](#); Vargas, [Citation2023](#)), which recognize the benefits of AI in K-12 education but also raise more pronounced concerns regarding its pedagogical and ethical implications. Our findings suggest that teachers’ attitudes toward AI are strongly tied to whether they perceive it as tangibly enhancing instructional outcomes, a principle closely tied to \*Performance Expectancy\*. For instance, when teachers perceive AI as enhancing pedagogical innovation and supporting effective teaching practices, their openness and readiness increase (Fundi et al., [Citation2024](#)). Conversely, when teachers perceive AI as misaligned with their professional goals or as introducing new challenges, their hesitation and lack of confidence grow (Monteiro et al., [Citation2024](#)). Findings from several studies (Şenel, [Citation2024](#); Seo et al., [Citation2025](#); Uygun, [Citation2024](#)) validate similar perspectives, underscoring the need for a balanced and thoughtful approach to integrating AI, given the coexistence of both positive and cautious views (Al-Zahrani, [Citation2024](#)). While teachers recognize AI as a catalyst for educational evolution, their concerns necessitate the development of actionable strategies (Jong, [Citation2022](#)).
A closer examination of our included studies illustrates how methodological choices influence the insights gained about teachers’ attitudes toward AI. Quantitative studies have employed closed-ended surveys to measure general teacher optimism, with one study reporting that 63.7% of participants perceived benefits from AI tools in the classroom (Slamet, [Citation2024](#)), while another reported a similar finding of 61.1% (Al-Qawabah, [Citation2024](#)). However, these studies did not probe the reasons for support or hesitation, which limited their findings to broad trends. On the other hand, qualitative research has relied on open-ended interviews to allow teachers to articulate context-specific anxieties, such as resistance to change and fears of misuse (Shamsuddinova et al., [Citation2024](#)). This perspective also reveals teachers’ emotional ambivalence toward AI integration, reflecting a complex blend of hope, excitement, and concern (Vargas, [Citation2023](#)). While these findings provide rich detail, they reflect the experiences of a small group and may not be generalizable. Mixed-methods studies demonstrate how combining quantitative surveys with qualitative inquiry can reveal both broad attitudinal trends and nuanced contextual insights. For instance, one study identified cautious optimism alongside dilemmas such as plagiarism and challenges in providing emotional support, while another highlighted generally favorable attitude tempered by critiques of theory-heavy teacher training and calls for more practice-oriented professional development. Taken together, these studies illustrate that, even within a limited sample, methodological triangulation, when carefully aligned with research aims, can enable a more comprehensive understanding of teachers’ confidence, readiness, and the contextual dilemmas shaping their attitudes toward AI.
#### 5.3.3. Ethical concerns about AI
Our analyses indicate that while teachers acknowledge AI’s transformative potential, many remain alarmed by ethical concerns, making ethics a decisive factor in AI integration. As shown in studies (Garofalo & Farenga, [Citation2025](#); Monteiro et al., [Citation2024](#); Shamsuddinova et al., [Citation2024](#); Vargas, [Citation2023](#)), educators adopt a cautious stance toward using AI due to concerns about plagiarism, cheating, and AI’s impact on academic integrity. They fear that excessive reliance on AI-generated content may weaken students’ critical thinking and authentic learning skills. Similarly, several studies (Chounta et al., [Citation2022](#); Li et al., [Citation2024](#); Shamsuddinova et al., [Citation2024](#)) highlight growing unease over data privacy and potential misuse of student information. These dilemmas fuel hesitation and distrust, as educators fear losing autonomy or compromising fairness. Prior research (Akgun & Greenhow, [Citation2022](#); Berendt et al., [Citation2020](#); Borenstein & Howard, [Citation2021](#); Celik et al., [Citation2022](#)) further underscores the need to address these challenges to enable AI adoption in classrooms. Evidence suggests that targeted interventions, such as involving teachers in AI curriculum co-creation, can alleviate ethical concerns by enhancing teachers’ understanding of AI ethics (Chiu, [Citation2021](#)) and fostering confidence to teach AI effectively (Chiu & Chai, [Citation2020](#)). Additional findings further emphasize that integrating ethical considerations into curriculum development significantly boosts teachers’ readiness to adopt AI (Borenstein & Howard, [Citation2021](#)). Moreover, ongoing professional learning can support teachers in navigating AI-related ethical complexities, fostering trust and comfort in responsible AI use (Du et al., [Citation2024](#); Fundi et al., [Citation2024](#)).
Despite these efforts, national and international bodies have yet to establish comprehensive ethical frameworks for AI in education (UNESCO & C., 2022). Additionally, research indicates that when teachers perceive AI ethics policies as unreliable or insufficiently defined, they may hesitate to implement AI into their classrooms (Du et al., [Citation2024](#)). As a result, ethical concerns surrounding AI adoption persist. This highlights the need for well-structured ethical frameworks that can help reduce uncertainty and build trust by providing greater clarity and safeguards, potentially increasing teachers’ willingness to adopt AI. The balance between confidence and hesitation in teachers’ responses, shaped by ethical apprehensions, closely aligns with the principle of \*Effort Expectancy\*, as it significantly influences their perceived ease of using AI.
#### 5.3.4. Bias of AI
Our analyses further indicate teachers’ emotional responses toward the bias of AI as another critical factor that can either hinder its integration in K-12 education if ignored or facilitate its adoption when proactively managed. Teachers worry that AI-driven decision-making, when based on skewed or limited datasets, may worsen educational inequalities by reinforcing biases, particularly disadvantaging marginalized student groups (Cheng & Wang, [Citation2023](#); Li et al., [Citation2024](#)). Empirical findings show that imbalanced training data produces disproportionate accuracy in automated assessments, causing AI tools to favor overrepresented groups and reinforce disparities in student evaluation (Chounta et al., [Citation2022](#)). This apprehension is particularly pronounced in underfunded schools, where the scarcity of resources and support exacerbates biases and complicates efforts to mitigate these disparities, ultimately leaving these institutions further behind (Li et al., [Citation2024](#)).
To mitigate these issues, prior studies (Cheng & Wang, [Citation2023](#); Chounta et al., [Citation2022](#); Li et al., [Citation2024](#)) suggest that systematic audits of AI-driven tools, incorporating diverse perspectives during AI tool design, and providing adequate training and support for educators may contribute to enhancing teachers’ confidence in identifying and addressing biases in AI-generated outputs. While large-scale empirical validation in K-12 education remains limited, existing literature suggests that such interventions could support teachers in making informed pedagogical adjustments, thereby fostering more equitable learning environments. As educators gain exposure to these practices, their trust in AI as a fair, transparent, and reliable tool may improve, strengthening their Performance Expectancy and encouraging AI adoption. However, if bias-related concerns persist, teachers may begin to perceive AI as unreliable or ineffective, reducing their \*Performance Expectancy\* and increasing hesitancy toward AI adoption.
### 5.4. Assessing the effectiveness of AI integration in K-12 classrooms: a preliminary assessment model
Despite the increasing integration of AI in education, systematic methods for assessing its effectiveness remain underexplored (Meylani, [Citation2024](#)). To address this gap, this study repurposes \*UNESCO’s GEM Report 2023s\* Four Questions (Antoninis et al., [Citation2023](#)) as policy-aligned evaluative dimensions for constructing a preliminary assessment model. As depicted in Figure 3, classroom-level insights were organized under these four categories: equity, sustainability, appropriateness, and scalability. This structure offers a practice-informed basis for evaluating the effectiveness of AI Integration in K-12 education. It also provides a foundation for future empirical validation and contextual adaptation across diverse educational settings.

#### 5.4.1. Equity
Technology can help address equity and inclusion, but it can also be used in ways that leave the most disadvantaged behind (Gottschalk & Weise, [Citation2023](#)). Similarly when it comes to AI tools, it is essential to evaluate how they promote inclusivity (Tatar, [Citation2023](#)), ensure accessibility (Cheng & Wang, [Citation2023](#)), and uphold educational fairness (Li et al., [Citation2024](#)). Therefore, promoting inclusivity, ensuring accessibility, and upholding educational fairness are suggested as key factors to be assessed for equity.
##### 5.4.1.1. Accessibility
\* Evaluate the extent to which AI tools promote access across diverse socio-economic and geographic contexts.
\* Assess how the features of AI tools support students with disabilities.
##### 5.4.1.2. Inclusivity
\* Assess the extent to which interventions promote engagement among diverse learners, including marginalized communities.
##### 5.4.1.3. Educational fairness
\* Evaluate the effectiveness of interventions in ensuring unbiased learning outcomes across student groups.
#### 5.4.2. Sustainability
Technology is developing quickly, and education systems are struggling to adapt (Soskil, [Citation2018](#)). The costs of using digital technology are often underestimated, and resources spent on technology instead of classrooms and teachers can hinder progress toward the global education goal (Cuban, [Citation2001](#)). This is particularly relevant for AI tools, which require sustained professional growth (Cheng & Wang, [Citation2023](#); Fundi et al., [Citation2024](#); Tatar et al., [Citation2025](#)), curriculum alignment (Chiu & Chai, [Citation2020](#)) and leadership (Chiu & Chai, [Citation2020](#); Shamsuddinova et al., [Citation2024](#); Tatar, [Citation2023](#); Vargas, [Citation2023](#)) to determine their impact on educational systems. Therefore, professional growth, curriculum alignment, and leadership are suggested as key factors to be assessed for sustainability.
##### 5.4.2.1. Professional growth
\* Assess the extent to which continuous skill development opportunities are provided for educators.
\* Evaluate how effectively teachers are equipped to integrate AI tools into pedagogical practices.
##### 5.4.2.2. Curriculum alignment
\* Determine the alignment of AI tools with long-term educational goals and standards.
##### 5.4.2.3. Leadership
\* Evaluate the effectiveness of leadership strategies in resource allocation, ethical guidance, and maintaining AI relevance.
#### 5.4.3. Appropriateness
Technology can improve learning in some contexts, but it can also be detrimental if used in excess or to replace a qualified teacher (Linden, [Citation2008](#)). This is particularly relevant for AI-powered intervention, which necessitates evaluating their alignment with Evidence-Based Instructional Practices (EBIPs) (Slamet, [Citation2024](#)) and their adherence to ethical standards, including data privacy, transparency, and accountability (Ottenbreit-Leftwich et al., [Citation2023](#)). Therefore, alignment with EBIPs and ethical compliance are suggested as key factors to be assessed for appropriateness.
##### 5.4.3.1. Alignment with EBIPs
\* Assess whether AI-powered interventions align with Evidence-Based Instructional Practices (EBIPs) to enhance learning.
\* Evaluate whether AI functionalities align with educational objectives while preserving the teacher’s role.
##### 5.4.3.2. Ethical standards
\* Verify adherence to ethical guidelines, including data privacy, transparency, and accountability.
#### 5.4.4. Scalability
There is a lack of robust evidence on the added value of digital technology in education (Butler, [Citation2019](#)), particularly concerning the effectiveness of AI tools in diverse educational contexts (Chounta et al., [Citation2024](#)). Most of the evidence comes from rich countries (Escueta et al., [Citation2020](#)), which limits our understanding of how digital technologies can scale effectively in more diverse and underrepresented educational settings. This gap underscores the need to specifically examine the scalability of these tools across varied socio-economic and infrastructural conditions. For that reason, the roles of Strategic Partnerships (Cheng & Wang, [Citation2023](#); Lee et al., [Citation2024](#); Shamsuddinova et al., [Citation2024](#)) and collaborations (Shamsuddinova et al., [Citation2024](#); Wardat et al., [Citation2024](#)) in pooling resources, sharing expertise, and bridging gaps in access to AI-driven tools, particularly in underrepresented regions, should be carefully assessed.
##### 5.4.4.1. Strategic partnerships
\* Assess whether multi-stakeholder collaboration for resource-sharing, technology adoption, and capacity-buildingenhancesscalability.
\* Evaluate the role of strategic alliances in bridging infrastructure gaps and promoting sustainable growth.
##### 5.4.4.2. Collaboration
\* Evaluate whether cross-functional teamwork contributes to effective communication and problem-solving.
\* Assess the effectiveness of peer support networks in enhancing adaptability and fostering continuous improvement.
## 6. Conclusion
This review identifies a recurring pattern in teachers’ perspectives on AI integration in K-12 education. While many educators express optimistic intentions toward AI adoption, their views often include mild reservations. Others, though initially positive, display growing concern over time. Furthermore, our analysis proposes a thematic framework for understanding AI integration in K-12 education from teachers’ perspective, structured around three core dimensions and eight subthemes. The cognitive dimension, represented by \*Teacher TPACK\*, reflects the role of technical AI competencies and innovative pedagogy in teachers’ instructional practices. The practical dimension, referred to as \*Teacher Agency\*, reflects teachers’ autonomy in collaborative lesson planning and their engagement in professional development. The emotional dimension, termed \*Teacher Affection\*, captures educators’ emotional responses to AI, including their attitudes, perceptions of its efficiency and concerns related to ethics and bias.
Complementing this framework, our preliminary assessment model, guided by the UNESCO framework, identifies four main dimensions for evaluating effective AI integration in K-12 education. First, \*\*equity\*\* evaluates whether AI initiatives promote accessibility and inclusivity while ensuring educational fairness. Second, \*\*sustainability\*\*examines AI’s alignment with curriculum standards, its role in ongoing teacher development and is supported by strong and effective leadership. Third, \*\*appropriateness\*\* assesses whether AI tools are aligned with EBIPs and adhere to ethical standards. Finally, \*\*scalability\*\* examines the extent to which AI technologies can be adapted and supported collaboratively across diverse educational settings.
This study should be considered within the context of its limitations. Its scope is confined to teachers’ perspectives, which, while valuable, do not capture the broader ecosystem of AI integration in K-12 settings. Additionally, given the rapid pace of technological advancement, some of the most recent developments in AI may not be fully addressed in this review. Future research should broaden its scope to include the perceptions of students, parents, and school administrators to gain a more comprehensive understanding of AI’s impact. Moreover, exploring the role of emerging technologies, such as machine learning, augmented reality, and virtual reality, in shaping educational experiences may provide deeper insights into how these tools can enhance learning environments, and will offer valuable insights for creating more effective and adaptive educational frameworks.
