## Abstract
As digital distraction has become a critical issue in secondary education, it is important to explore the heterogeneous patterns linking individual digital distraction to academic performance. We analyzed data from 15-year-old students across 30 countries in the Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA) in 2022. The main method we used included K-means for Ensemble Clustering, XGBoost and SHAP analyses for classification and interpretation. Results identified four distinct student profiles reflecting their susceptibility to digital distraction and mathematics performance patterns. These profiles are predominantly represented across East Asian, European, Latin American, Eastern European, and Middle Eastern education systems. The first two clusters (optimal and susceptible profiles) confirm the expected negative relationship between distraction and performance, while two unconventional profiles (resilient and invisible at-risk) demonstrate exceptions to this trend. Key factors distinguishing these profiles include environmental factors (peer influence and classroom discipline), internal factors (self-efficacy, ICT usage habits, and emotions), and demographic characteristics. Based on these results, we propose targeted interventions for educators, institutions, and policymakers.
## Introduction
With the widespread availability of internet connectivity, digital devices such as laptops, tablets, and smartphones have become indispensable tools for educational contexts ranging from higher education to K-12 classrooms (Flanigan et al., 2023; Park et al., 2024). These devices offer substantial benefits, such as promoting student motivation, increasing participation, and supporting learning tasks (Rashid & Asghar, 2016; Tang et al., 2022; Wijaya et al., 2022). However, this connectivity introduces a pervasive global challenge: digital distraction, which is defined as the diversion of attention from academic tasks to non-academic activities due to the use of digital devices (Flanigan et al., 2022). Although the detrimental impact of digital distraction has been widely emphasized, a considerable number of students continue to engage in off-task digital activities during class (Liao & Wu, 2022; Flanigan et al., 2022). Importantly, research indicates that not all non-academic digital use is detrimental. For instance, brief digital engagement can help regulate attention by providing short cognitive breaks; it may also support creative thinking by interrupting rigid thought patterns (Kim et al., 2019; Ritter & Dijksterhuis, 2014). At the same time, academic performance may also reflect differences in digital behavior patterns. For instance, high-achieving students often demonstrate stronger self-regulatory behaviors, allowing them to maintain task focus (Bandura, 2001; Zimmerman, 2002; Gaudreau et al., 2013). In contrast, lower-achieving students may experience heightened academic boredom or lack of motivation, with a higher likelihood of engaging with digital devices for non-academic purposes during class (Park et al., 2024; Pekrun et al., 2010).
Despite the growing body of literature, some critical gaps remain. First, existing findings are primarily situated in higher education, and relevant research in K-12 education remains insufficient (Park et al., 2024; Zhou & Deng, 2023). Given the developmental differences, secondary school students are likely to exhibit less mature self-regulation and greater sensitivity to environmental stimuli compared to college students (Crone & Dahl, 2012). Therefore, greater scholarly attention to digital distraction in K-12 contexts is warranted. Second, there is a lack of a comprehensive framework to explain the explanatory processes linking digital distraction to academic performance. Traditional variable-centered approaches (e.g., examining whether distraction predicts poorer performance) often rely on linear associations and average effects, which may overlook the heterogeneity and configurational patterns underlying students’ digital distraction behaviors and academic outcomes.
Therefore, this study focuses on secondary school students’ digital distraction in the classroom and its association with academic performance, conceptualized as distinct profiles of distraction and performance. Rather than assuming a uniform linear relationship between distraction and achievement, this study hypothesizes meaningful heterogeneity in students’ digital distraction-performance profiles, based on the integration of previous literature. Furthermore, guided by the Social Cognitive Theory, we develop an integrative framework to organize the factors associated with these profiles into three dimensions: internal, external, and demographic. Methodologically, this study utilizes data from 30 countries and regions participating in the 2022 Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA), where mathematics literacy served as the main assessment domain (OECD, 2023). To capture complex and potentially non-linear relationships, this study adopts a person-centered analytical approach. We initially utilize data-driven clustering techniques to identify student profiles based on their individual susceptibility to digital distraction and mathematics performance. We then used supervised classification models to examine the key factors associated with profile membership. We aimed to identify distinct profiles linking students’ digital distraction and academic performance, interpret them through the lens of Social Cognitive Theory, and ultimately develop targeted interventions for secondary school students.
