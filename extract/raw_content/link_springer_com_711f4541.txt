Abstract
--------

The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) in education is prompting a reevaluation of personalized learning terminology and its impact on teaching practices and learner engagement. Personalized learning (PL) involves various instructional strategies tailored to individual student needs and interests, utilizing data and technology to boost engagement and success. The evolving landscape requires a clear understanding of how AI can support personalized learning, distinguishing it from traditional methods. The variability in PL terminology reflects diverse interpretations of AI technologies in education, necessitating a common framework to clarify definitions and practices. This document presents an overview of the latest research literature on personalized learning, highlighting how technology is transforming the framework and effectiveness of individualized learning experiences. By analyzing reputable articles from 6 databases, the review seeks to provide insights into how AI can redefine personalized learning, enabling more precise definitions. The findings emphasize the use of PL terms in technological contexts and call for a unified term to enhance clarity and effectiveness in educational technology practices. Ultimately, the review aims to inform educators and policymakers about precise terms defining personalized learning in the AI context.

Introduction
------------

Personalized learning (PL) is a learner-centered approach that tailors instruction to learners’ needs, goals, abilities, motivations, and interests (Schmid et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR50)), enabling differentiated learning pathways that support academic development (Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62)). With advances in digital technologies, PL has increasingly been implemented through data-driven and adaptive systems. However, the expansion of PL has also produced conceptual ambiguity, as related terms such as adaptive learning, individualized instruction, and customized learning are often used interchangeably. Shemshack and Spector’s ([2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR51)) systematic review highlighted this lack of definitional clarity and mapped the overlapping terminology used across the literature.

Since that review, artificial intelligence (AI) has become deeply embedded in educational technologies, enabling real-time feedback, adaptive sequencing, intelligent tutoring, and dynamic learning pathways that further reshape how personalization is designed and enacted (Castro et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR10); Katiyar et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR28)). These developments suggest that both the meaning and operationalization of personalized learning have evolved. Accordingly, the present review extends Shemshack and Spector’s ([2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR51)) work by examining recent definitions of PL and related terms in AI-driven educational contexts, with the aim of clarifying how personalized learning is being redefined in the AI era.

### Research gaps and rationale for redefinition

Recent advances in artificial intelligence have transformed personalized learning from static, one-size-fits-all instruction into adaptive, data-driven, and learner-centered approaches that support real-time feedback, individualized content, and dynamic learning pathways, thereby enhancing engagement, motivation, and academic outcomes (Castro et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR10); Katiyar et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR28)). Emerging technologies such as generative AI, intelligent tutoring systems, and learning analytics are increasingly regarded as transformative, with the potential to expand access and promote educational equity (Kanta, [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR27); Taşkın, [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR53)).

Despite these developments, substantial gaps persist in theory and research. Current frameworks often lag behind technological innovation, and critical concerns—including data privacy, algorithmic bias, ethical issues, teacher preparation, and human–AI collaboration—remain insufficiently addressed (Castro et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR10); Laak & Aru, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR31)). Furthermore, there is a lack of longitudinal, inclusive, and methodologically rigorous research, particularly in underrepresented populations and diverse educational contexts (Bayly-Castaneda et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR5); Halkiopoulos & Gkintoni, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR18); Laak & Aru, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR31)). Although some reviews examine specific AI applications, few systematically analyze how AI is reshaping the conceptual foundations of personalized learning or map future research directions (Bayly-Castaneda et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR5); Castro et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR10)).

This gap is most evident in the absence of updated syntheses that reconsider definitions and terminology in light of recent AI developments. While studies increasingly show that AI has redefined personalized learning through adaptive, data-driven, and individualized environments (Castro et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR10); Deckker et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR13); Katiyar et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR28)), conceptual clarity, inclusivity, and empirical validation remain limited (Bhutoria, [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR9); Laak & Aru, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR31)). Consequently, there is a pressing need to clarify how overlapping concepts are defined, operationalized, and theoretically integrated within AI-supported educational contexts.

Guided by this gap, the present systematic review addresses the central research question:

### What are the similarities and differences among the terms used to describe personalized learning approaches in the era of artificial intelligence?

To systematically examine this question and establish a coherent theoretical foundation, the study further investigates the following sub-questions:

1. 1.
   
   How is personalized learning defined in recent literature, and what are its core components?
2. 2.
   
   How has adaptive learning been used, and how does it relate to AI-driven personalized learning?
3. 3.
   
   How has individualized instruction been conceptualized, and how does it connect to AI-assisted personalized learning?
4. 4.
   
   How is customized learning related to personalized learning in AI-supported environments?
5. 5.
   
   How is personalized adaptive learning connected to broader conceptions of personalized learning?
6. 6.
   
   How is intelligent tutoring positioned in relation to personalized learning frameworks?
7. 7.
   
   What roles do teachers and AI systems play in the design and implementation of personalized learning?

By addressing these questions, this review seeks not only to map conceptual similarities and differences among commonly used terms, but also *to develop a unifying definition of personalized learning* that integrates its essential components within contemporary AI-driven educational contexts.

### Personalized learning

Personalized learning (PL) is a learner-centered approach that tailors instruction to learners’ needs, goals, abilities, motivations, and interests (Schmid et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR50)), enabling differentiated learning pathways that support academic development (Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62)). Although advances in digital technologies have increasingly supported PL through data-driven and adaptive systems, the field remains conceptually ambiguous, with related terms such as adaptive learning, individualized instruction, and customized learning often used interchangeably; Shemshack and Spector’s ([2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR51)) systematic review highlighted this lack of definitional clarity and mapped overlapping terminology in the literature. Since that review, artificial intelligence (AI) has become deeply embedded in educational technologies, enabling real-time feedback, adaptive sequencing, intelligent tutoring, and dynamic learning pathways (Castro et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR10); Katiyar et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR28)), suggesting that the meaning and operationalization of personalized learning have evolved. Accordingly, the present review extends Shemshack and Spector’s ([2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR51)) work by examining recent definitions of PL and related terms in AI-driven educational contexts to clarify how personalized learning is being redefined in the AI era.

### Adaptive learning

Adaptive learning and personalized learning are often used interchangeably, yet they represent distinct, overlapping concepts within technology-enhanced education (Shemshack & Spector, [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR51); Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR61); Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62)). Adaptive learning is typically system-centered, using AI, machine learning, and computational models to algorithmically adjust difficulty, pacing, and content based on learner performance (Mirata et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR43); Shemshack & Spector, [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR51); Wang et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR59); Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62)), whereas personalized learning constitutes a broader, learner-centered paradigm that incorporates learners’ goals, interests, abilities, and self-regulatory processes (Ipinnaiye & Risquez, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR24); Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62)). Although the boundary between the two is often blurred (Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62)), the literature generally characterizes adaptive learning as a technology-driven subset of personalized learning, as AI-enhanced systems primarily optimize instructional sequences without fully integrating broader learner attributes, even in large-scale contexts such as MOOCs (Hooshyar et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR22); Kaiss et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR26); Mejeh & Rehm, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR42); Shemshack & Spector, [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR51); Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR61); Yu & Chauhan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR67); Zhao, [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR71)).

### Individualized instruction

Individualized instruction tailors pacing, methods, and content to learners’ abilities, interests, and goals to enhance relevance, motivation, and engagement (Ackermans et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR1); Zhao, [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR71); Zou et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR74)), requiring both teacher digital pedagogy and learner digital literacy in technology-enhanced environments (Li & Wong, [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR36); Xu et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR65)). Advances in artificial intelligence have expanded individualized instruction through AI-assisted personalization, enabling real-time data analysis, adaptive content, targeted feedback, immersive and accessible learning tools, and individualized plans for diverse learners (Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62); Yu & Chauhan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR67); Zhao, [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR71)). While individualized instruction primarily adapts the pace of progress through predetermined curricula (Schmid et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR50)), personalized learning entails broader customization of objectives, content, methods, and sequencing, emphasizing learner “choice and voice” (Schmid & Petko, [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR49); Schmid et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR50); Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62); Zou et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR74)), with AI systems further operationalizing individualized pathways based on learner profiles and competencies (Bendahmane et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR6)).

### Customized learning

In AI-enhanced educational contexts, customized learning is conceptualized as a core component and practical operationalization of personalized learning, which tailors instruction to learners’ needs, preferences, and progress within an overarching learner-centered framework (Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62); Yu & Chauhan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR67); Zhao, [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR71); El-Sabagh, n.d.). Customization provides the technological and pedagogical mechanisms—such as AI-driven content delivery, individualized pacing, adaptive feedback, and real-time adjustment—through which personalization is enacted, transforming digital environments into dynamic systems responsive to learner diversity (Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62); Yu & Chauhan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR67); Zhao, [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR71) Kaouni et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR75)). Empirical studies indicate that AI-based customization enhances engagement, critical thinking, learning effectiveness, and learner autonomy by aligning learning paths, resources, and interfaces with individual profiles (Almuhanna, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR3); Hooshyar et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR22); Kolekar et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR29); Nguyen et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR47)).

### Intelligent tutoring

Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) are a major technological application within personalized learning, using artificial intelligence, machine learning, and computational algorithms to deliver adaptive, individualized instruction and real-time feedback based on learner data, behaviors, and preferences (Hibbi et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR20); Mousavinasab et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR44); Yu & Chauhan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR67)). Recent advances in NLP, deep learning, and big-data analytics have strengthened ITS personalization through sophisticated learner modeling, tailored recommendations, and large-scale instructional support, including recommender-based and hybrid human-AI systems that enhance engagement, performance, and teacher support (Beyyoudh et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR8); Hobert & Berens, [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR21); Huiji, [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR23); Troussas et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR54); Zayet et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR69); Zhao, [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR71)). While ITS effectively operationalize personalization, they remain a specific technological instantiation within the broader pedagogical framework of personalized learning, which extends beyond ITS alone (Hibbi et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR20); Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62); Yu & Chauhan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR67)).

### Personalized adaptive learning

Personalized and adaptive learning is a widely recognized and extensively studied concept in contemporary education. Personalized learning is increasingly conceptualized as an AI-driven, dynamically adaptive process that customizes content and feedback based on individual learner performance and motivational states (Liu, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR37)). The sustained volume of academic publications and growing institutional interest confirm its prominence in research and practice (Mirata et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR43); Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62)). However, despite the conceptual overlap, the compound term *“personalized adaptive learning”* remains rarely used in educational research, with studies more commonly referring to the constructs separately as either *“adaptive learning”* or *“personalized learning.”*

### Context-sensitive personalized learning in the AI era

Personalized learning is a core benefit of Artificial Intelligence in Education (AIED), defined as a learner-centered approach that adapts content, pacing, and instructional methods to individual needs (Mustafa et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR46)). However, its effectiveness is highly context-dependent, varying according to learners’ disciplinary backgrounds and stages of study. Evidence indicates that different student groups exhibit distinct learning trajectories and respond differently to personalized interventions, underscoring the need for context-aware rather than uniform personalization strategies (Gao et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR16)).

Comparative analyses of Computer Science (CS) and Mathematics students in introductory programming courses reveal contrasting patterns of engagement and performance. CS students tend to develop more efficient learning strategies over time, showing reduced surface engagement alongside increased productivity and conceptual mastery (Gao et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR16)). In contrast, Mathematics students often display high engagement without corresponding achievement, characterized by complex but inefficient work and greater susceptibility to learning avoidance—performing adequately on formative tasks yet underperforming in summative assessments (Gao et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR16)). These differences are linked to goal congruity: CS students typically perceive programming as central to their academic and professional trajectories, whereas Mathematics students may regard it as less relevant, resulting in more superficial engagement (Gao et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR16)).

These findings suggest that effective AI-driven personalized learning must account for learners’ academic backgrounds, motivations, and disciplinary contexts. For example, personalization for CS students may emphasize increased challenge and skill refinement, whereas for Mathematics students it should address low initial engagement and learning avoidance through structured scaffolding and domain-relevant applications (Gao et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR16)). Overall, personalized learning in the AI era requires a nuanced, context-sensitive approach that aligns instructional strategies with the specific needs, goals, and learning profiles of diverse learner populations (Gao et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR16); Mustafa et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR46)).

### Core ethical challenges

AI-driven personalized learning systems require large amounts of student data, making data privacy and security a primary concern. Risks include unauthorized data access, misuse, and lack of student control over personal information. Robust data protection, anonymization, and clear consent protocols are essential to safeguard privacy (García-López & Trujillo-Liñán, [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR17); Xu, [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR63); Yu & Guo, [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR68)). Algorithmic fairness and bias are equally critical. AI models can unintentionally perpetuate or amplify existing inequalities if trained on biased data, leading to unfair educational outcomes for marginalized groups. Regular bias audits, diverse datasets, and fairness-aware algorithms are recommended to mitigate these risks (Chinta et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR11); García-López & Trujillo-Liñán, [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR17); Yu & Guo, [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR68)).

Furthermore, transparency and explainability are necessary for trust and accountability in personalized learning processes. Many AI systems operate as "black boxes," making it difficult for educators and students to understand or challenge decisions. Explainable AI models and transparent data practices are needed to ensure accountability and foster trust (García-López & Trujillo-Liñán, [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR17); Chinta et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR11); Lepri et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR76)). However, ethical considerations—especially data privacy, algorithmic fairness, and transparency—are central to the responsible use of AI in personalized learning. Addressing these challenges requires technical safeguards, robust governance, and a human-centered approach to ensure educational innovation benefits all learners equitably.

Research methodology
--------------------

For this review, the guidelines published by Okoli ([2015](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR48)) for conducting systematic literature reviews in Information Systems Research were applied. Okoli’s framework offers a comprehensive approach to conducting such reviews, with a strong foundation in information technology. Given that this review is similarly grounded in the field of information technology, Okoli’s methodology was considered a suitable model for guiding the process. The researchers discussed the previously studied terms: personalized learning, adaptive learning, customized learning, and individualized instruction.

As suggested by the authors Shemshack and Spector ([2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR51)), personalized adaptive learning was added to the terms in addition to the term intelligent tutoring, as it's frequently used in today’s research on personalized learning. These terms overlap significantly, all emphasizing the use of technology to support personalization in education for individual learners. The variety and interchangeable use of these terms highlight the lack of a single, unified definition for personalized learning, which can be an obstacle to research progress in the field. Therefore, this review aims to clarify these definitions and their interrelationships, providing a more coherent framework to guide future research and practice in personalized learning. Review design
-------------

This study utilized a systematic review methodology to identify, evaluate, and synthesize peer-reviewed literature on the evolving terminology and conceptualization of AI-driven personalized learning. The review adhered to the PRISMA 2020 (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) guidelines (Page et al., 2021), which are designed to ensure methodological transparency, rigor, and reproducibility.

Sources of literature
---------------------

To address the research question, the researchers selected several well-established and reputable databases to serve as the foundation for this systematic review. These include Scopus, ScienceDirect, EBSCOhost, IEEE Xplore, JSTOR, and Web of Science, ensuring comprehensive coverage of journals relevant to the field. The most pertinent journals for the systematic review were consistently selected from these databases. Additionally, the h5-index for the “Educational Technology” category on Google Scholar was used as an initial reference point, as this category directly relates to studies on personalized learning. Supplementary procedures
------------------------

Relevant papers were first located through conventional searches of online databases and academic journals. These papers were then examined to assess their relevance and suitability for inclusion in the study. To keep the scope of the review both focused and manageable, the top nine journals from the “Educational Technology” category on Google Scholar’s h5-index were chosen. This approach aimed to balance breadth and depth, ensuring enough high-quality studies were included to answer the research question effectively. Subsequently, most journals in the field of educational technology were indexed, and the SCImago Journal Rank (SJR) was used to assess their impact. Although the impact factor rankings did not always align perfectly with Google Scholar’s h5-index, the selected journals represent the most influential publications in educational technology. For instance, although the ‘Computer Assisted Language Learning’ journal appeared as highly ranked in Google Scholar, it was excluded from the review due to the absence of qualifying articles based on the review’s selection criteria.

Only peer-reviewed articles from online academic journals were included, as these sources are recognized for their credibility and reliability. Such journals provide verifiable, data-rich content, enhancing the reliability and depth of the literature review. A clearly defined method was followed for journal selection to maintain methodological rigor and scientific consistency. The researchers review the main databases for educational technology to ensure all relevant journals of the field are included. This review is solely focused on journals to keep the scope of the review manageable and present reviewed data to create a resource for future studies. Table [2](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#Tab2) lists the journals included in this review.

Search query
------------

A suitable search query was developed to identify relevant studies on personalized learning. The search query was as follows: (Publication Title: (“journal name”)) AND (“term”) and the journals listed in the table were searched for each of following terms: “personalized learning”, “adaptive learning”, “individualized instruction”, “customized learning”, “intelligent tutoring”, and “personalized adaptive learning”.

Inclusion/exclusion criteria
----------------------------

To determine the final set of papers for review, a four-phase selection process was established based on specific inclusion criteria.

In Phase One, an initial search was conducted using key terms such as “personalized learning”, “adaptive learning”, “individualized instruction”, “customized learning”, “intelligent tutoring”, and “personalized adaptive learning”. The search was limited to publications from 2019 to 2025 to focus on recent developments in personalized learning, a topic of increasing interest among researchers and policymakers. Only English-language articles were considered to avoid delays related to translation. Further filters included papers that addressed technology integration and were categorized as research articles published in peer-reviewed scientific journals. This helped maintain a manageable review scope.

Phase Two involved screening by title, abstract, and keywords. Researchers examined each result from the initial search and selected studies that appeared relevant to the topic.

Phase three represented the full-text screening phase, during which the entire content of each article was reviewed to determine whether it offered definitions or discussed the terminology associated with personalized learning. Only papers accessible via open-access sources or the researchers’ institutional library were included.

Phase Four entailed downloading and then uploading the eligible papers into the Rayyan application, where they were organized into subfolders based on their respective journal databases. Each full article was read thoroughly to identify definitions and key components of personalized learning. Papers that met all inclusion criteria were starred and reread to confirm their relevance to the review.

An initial search on Google Scholar using the term *“personalized learning”* revealed a steady increase in the number of publications over the years, with a notable spike around 2024 (see Fig. [1](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#Fig1)). The review focused on articles from 2019 onwards, aligning with the rising interest in personalized learning as technology became more prominent in education and artificial intelligence evolved.

Results
-------

This section presents the findings of the systematic review on how key terms related to personalized learning have been conceptualized and used in educational technology literature between 2019 and 2025. Following a multi-stage screening process, 55 studies were included in the final corpus (see Fig. [2](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#Fig2) and Table [1](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#Tab1) for journal distribution and term usage). Nine journals were searched using the terms *personalized learning*, *adaptive learning*, *individualized instruction*, *customized learning*, *intelligent tutoring*, and *personalized adaptive learning*. After screening 861 papers across four phases, 55 high-quality studies met the inclusion criteria and are marked with an asterisk in the reference list.

**Research Question 1: How is Personalized Learning Defined and What Are Its Core Components?**

Across the 28 studies using personalized learning as the primary term, definitions converged on tailoring content, pacing, and support to individual learner needs, characteristics, and goals, consistently framing PL as learner-centered, goal-oriented, and flexible rather than uniform (Bernacki et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR7); Shemshack & Spector, [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR51)). Many studies emphasized the role of digital environments in collecting and analyzing learner data to guide content sequencing, difficulty, feedback, and modality, positioning personalization as a data-driven process enabled by analytics and algorithmic decision-making (Murad et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR45); Wang et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR60); Zheng et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR72)). Several works further introduced Digital Personalized Learning (DPL), distinguishing between integrated and standalone tools in technology-rich contexts, with integrated approaches more closely aligned with institutional strategies (Van Schoors et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR55); Vanbecelaere et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR56)). Overall, personalized learning was portrayed as a flexible, technology-mediated, and data-informed approach that optimizes individual learning while remaining aligned with curricular and institutional constraints.

**Research Question 2: How Is Adaptive Learning Used and How Does It Relate to AI-Driven Personalized Learning?**

Adaptive learning appeared in 13 studies and was consistently described as the primary mechanism through which personalization is operationalized, dynamically adjusting task difficulty, learning sequences, feedback, and pacing based on learner data (Barbosa et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR4); El-Sabagh, [2021](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR15); Ipinnaiye & Risquez, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR24)). Many studies adopted the three-component model (learner, content, and instructional models), in which algorithms translate performance, interaction, self-report, and motivational data into instructional adaptations (Li, [2023a](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR34), [2023b](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR35); Liu, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR37); Martin et al., [2020a](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR40), [2020b](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR41); Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR61); Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62)). Most applications were in higher education—particularly computer science, language learning, and mathematics—with positive effects on achievement, engagement, and efficiency reported across quasi-experimental and design-based studies (Hooshyar et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR22); Wang et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR59)). Overall, the literature positions adaptive learning as the algorithmic “engine” of personalized learning, providing the technical means for continuous, data-driven tailoring.

**Research Question 3: How Is Individualized Instruction Related to AI-Assisted Personalized Learning?**

Only one study used *individualized instruction* as its primary term, indicating that it has largely receded in recent AI-focused literature; where it appeared, it referred to teacher-designed variation in content and pacing based on professional judgment rather than automated analytics. In contrast, AI-assisted personalized learning relies on learner models, algorithmic recommendations, and data-driven adaptation (Ackermans et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR1); Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR61); Zhang et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR70)), suggesting that individualized instruction functions as a pedagogical predecessor to AI-based personalization. While both emphasize responsiveness to learner needs, they differ in scale, automation, and data use, with emerging hybrid models combining teacher expertise and system-generated recommendations (Lee et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR32); Schmid et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR50)).

**Research Question 4: How Is Customized Learning Connected to Personalized Learning in AI Contexts?**

Two studies used *customized learning* as a primary term. Customized learning generally referred to tailoring materials, tasks, or pathways based on learner preferences or choices, often supported by digital tools for content selection (Almuhanna, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR3); Wang et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR60)).

In AI-supported environments, customization was typically implemented through recommender systems that matched resources to learner profiles using performance data, inferred interests, or learning styles (Murad et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR45); Wan & Yu, [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR58); Zayet et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR69)). Unlike fully adaptive systems, customization was often episodic or user-initiated rather than continuously adaptive. Conceptually, customized learning represents a dimension of personalized learning—particularly content personalization—rather than a distinct paradigm.

**Research Question 5: How Is Personalized Adaptive Learning Related to Personalized Learning?**

Two studies explicitly addressed *personalized adaptive learning*, describing systems that integrate learner profiles with real-time adaptation based on performance and engagement data (Luo et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR39); Zheng et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR72)). These systems employed advanced learning-path algorithms, knowledge tracing, and predictive models to recommend instructional sequences. Findings indicated improvements in learning efficiency and performance compared to static or teacher-paced approaches. Personalized adaptive learning thus represents a highly integrated operationalization of personalized learning in AI-enhanced environments, aligning with calls for systems that combine learner modeling, adaptivity, and human oversight (Hashim et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR19); Mustafa et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR46)).

**Research Question 6: How Is Intelligent Tutoring Connected to Personalized Learning?**

Nine studies examined Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS), defining them as computer-based environments that emulate human tutoring through step-by-step guidance, adaptive feedback, and individualized practice (Mousavinasab et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR44); Xu et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR64)). These systems employed AI techniques such as rule-based reasoning, Bayesian networks, recommender algorithms, and natural language processing (Beyyoudh et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR8); Hibbi et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR20); Hobert & Berens, [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR21)). Most studies reported positive learning outcomes, particularly in programming, mathematics, and STEM, with recent work also addressing motivational and affective dimensions through emotion recognition, gamification, and conversational agents (Li, [2023a](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR34), [2023b](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR35); Liu, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR37); Yu & Chauhan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR67)). Overall, ITS represent one of the most mature and technologically sophisticated implementations of AI-driven personalized learning.

**Research Question 7: What Roles Do Teachers and AI Systems Play in Personalized Learning?**

Findings across studies indicate that personalized learning functions as a collaborative human–AI model in which teachers and AI systems play complementary roles. Teachers lead design, implementation, and evaluation by interpreting learner data, aligning system recommendations with curricular goals, providing socio-emotional support, and intervening when automated pathways conflict with learner needs (Hobert & Berens, [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR21); Lee et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR32)). AI systems contribute through learner modeling, real-time adaptation, and continuous analytics, generating learner profiles, predicting performance, personalizing content, and monitoring engagement (Barbosa et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR4); Luo et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR39)). Together, these findings suggest that personalized learning is most effective when AI provides adaptive precision while teachers ensure pedagogical coherence, ethical oversight, and human involvement.

Moreover, AI systems in personalized learning contribute primarily through data-driven modeling, real-time adaptation, and continuous analytics. In the design phase, they generate learner profiles, predict performance, and propose content sequences using machine learning, recommender systems, and cognitive modeling (Bendahmane et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR6); Murad et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR45); Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR61); Zine et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR73)). During implementation, AI personalizes learning by adjusting difficulty, pacing, modality, and feedback, with intelligent tutoring systems providing immediate corrective feedback and identifying misconceptions (Barbosa et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR4); Beyyoudh et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR8); El-Sabagh, [2021](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR15); Hibbi et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR20)). In evaluation, AI analytics monitor progress, engagement, and risk patterns to identify learners requiring support (Huiji, [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR23); Ipinnaiye & Risquez, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR24); Li, [2023a](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR34), [2023b](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR35); Luo et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR39)). Overall, AI enhances scalability and precision, while teachers ensure pedagogical integrity, ethical oversight, and human connection, making personalized learning most effective when both function as interdependent partners.

Synthesizing findings across terms and implementations (e.g., Bernacki et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR7); Hashim et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR19); Martin et al., [2020a](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR40), [2020b](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR41); Shemshack & Spector, [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR51)), eight core components emerged as essential to a comprehensive definition of AI-supported personalized learning: (1) learner profile, (2) data inputs, (3) content model, (4) instructional model, (5) adaptive engine, (6) interface and user controls, (7) personalization goals, and (8) evaluation and governance. These components highlight that personalized learning in AI-rich contexts is best understood as a socio-technical system integrating pedagogical design, algorithmic processes, and ethical governance. Although multiple terms are used in policy and practice, research usage is increasingly concentrated around *personalized learning*, *adaptive learning*, and *intelligent tutoring*.

The concept of *Digital Personalized Learning (DPL)* (Vanbecelaere et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR56)) offers a promising unifying framework, encompassing the cognitive, emotional, motivational, and efficiency dimensions of learning in technology-mediated environments. DPL emphasizes both integrated and standalone implementations, with integrated use being more prevalent.

Existing and emerging trends
----------------------------

Research in e-learning increasingly emphasizes personalization and adaptivity to address individual differences in learners’ needs, abilities, and learning patterns. A major trend is the development of personalized recommender systems (PRS), particularly in K–12 education, which aim to enhance engagement and performance by integrating student profiling, content processing, and recommendation mechanisms (Martin et al., [2020a](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR40), [2020b](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR41); Zayet et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR69)). Student profiling is becoming more sophisticated, incorporating not only performance data but also learning characteristics and psychological factors to create holistic learner models.

Another prominent trend is adaptive learning, which dynamically adjusts content, feedback, and navigation based on learner interactions. Research has expanded from focusing solely on learner models to integrating content and instructional models, forming comprehensive adaptive systems (Martin et al., [2020a](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR40), [2020b](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR41)). Although most studies have been conducted in higher education—especially in computer science—there is a recognized need for research in more diverse contexts, including K–12 and informal learning (Zayet et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR69)).

Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) represent a mature strand of personalized learning, using AI techniques such as rule-based reasoning, data mining, and Bayesian networks to deliver individualized feedback and instruction (Mousavinasab et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR44)). Emerging developments include emotionally intelligent systems, conversational agents, emotion recognition technologies, serious games for assessment, and a shift toward mobile-based platforms to enhance accessibility and learner engagement (Beyyoudh et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR8); Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62); Yu & Chauhan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR67)).

Overall, the field is moving from static, one-size-fits-all e-learning toward complex, data-driven, and holistic personalized systems that integrate cognitive, behavioral, and affective dimensions of learning. Future research is expected to validate these approaches across diverse educational levels, refine learner modeling techniques, and evaluate the effectiveness of adaptive strategies in supporting individualized learning and growth.

Discussion
----------

Personalized learning is an educational approach that adapts instruction to learners’ needs, preferences, and pacing. Although rooted in traditional practices such as tutoring and apprenticeships, it has been fundamentally reshaped by digital technologies and, more recently, artificial intelligence (AI). In contemporary technology-enhanced learning, personalized learning encompasses related concepts such as adaptive learning, individualized instruction, customized learning, and intelligent tutoring, which are often grouped under “personalized TEL” despite important differences in focus and implementation (Hooshyar et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR22)). A core principle of personalized learning is achieving alignment among ICT, learners, educators, and instructional content to sustain motivation and learning effectiveness (Romi, [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR77)). AI-driven personalization enhances engagement, inclusivity, and learner well-being, particularly for students with special needs, and supports innovations such as conversational tutors and smart learning environments (Li, [2023a](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR34), [2023b](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR35); Walter, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR57); Yu & Chauhan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR67)).

Despite its potential, research indicates that educational institutions underutilize digital technologies for personalized learning, revealing a persistent gap between technological capability and classroom practice (Schmid et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR50)). Addressing this gap requires stronger professional development, improved integration of technology, and pedagogical frameworks that incorporate interactivity, gamification, and individualized assessment (Alé-Ruiz et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR2); Leshchenko et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR33)). Personalized learning further aims to ensure coherence across learning pathways by aligning knowledge units and prerequisites to reduce cognitive overload and enhance learner progression (Zheng et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR72)).

Adaptive technologies constitute a major operational dimension of personalized learning, offering data-driven recommendations and continuous adjustment of instruction to learners’ real-time needs (Smyrnova-Trybulska et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR52); Wang et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR59)). Learning management systems such as Moodle exemplify this approach by tailoring content, pacing, and assessment based on learner performance, while still requiring meaningful human mentoring to guide instructional design (Barbosa et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR4); Smyrnova-Trybulska et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR52)). Although adaptive learning, intelligent tutoring, and personalized learning are frequently used interchangeably in academic discourse, adaptive learning remains the most prevalent term following personalized learning, whereas intelligent tutoring and customized learning are more commonly associated with specific digital systems (Elmaadaway & Abouelenein, [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR14)).

Recent reviews indicate that while the fundamental goal of personalized learning—tailoring education to individual learners—remains stable, its terminology and conceptual foundations have become increasingly data-driven and precise in the AI era. Earlier literature noted significant conceptual overlap and ambiguity among personalization-related terms (Bernacki et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR7); Hashim et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR19); Shemshack & Spector, [2020](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR51)), whereas contemporary work emphasizes AI-enabled learner profiling, adaptive content delivery, and automated feedback as defining features of modern personalized learning frameworks (Castro et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR10)).

Conclusion & implications
-------------------------

The field of personalized learning is rapidly evolving in response to technological advances and deeper insights into human–machine interaction. While this evolution presents significant opportunities, it also underscores the need for clearer conceptual frameworks and coordinated adaptation to emerging technologies. At present, students, educators, researchers, and policymakers face persistent challenges related to conceptual ambiguity, implementation barriers, and methodological limitations. A central issue is the absence of a universally accepted definition of personalized learning, which continues to function as a broad, multi-layered umbrella term encompassing diverse student-centered approaches (Ackermans et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR1); Schmid & Petko, [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR49)). Moreover, existing research disproportionately focuses on older learners, leaving substantial gaps in understanding the long-term effects of personalized learning on younger, elementary-aged students (Schmid & Petko, [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR49)).

From a policy and practice perspective, effective school-wide implementation is often constrained by insufficient technological infrastructure and inconsistent integration strategies. Schools frequently adopt personalized learning without coherent institutional frameworks, resulting in fragmented and teacher-dependent use of digital tools (Schmid et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR50); Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR62)). Methodologically, much of the literature relies on cross-sectional designs and self-report data, limiting causal inference and increasing the risk of bias (Schmid et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR50)). Although personalized learning increasingly leverages data mining and adaptive technologies to enhance engagement and outcomes (Yang & Alabool, [2022](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR66)), the complexity of educational environments complicates efforts to isolate the effects of specific interventions (Ackermans et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR1)). Additionally, disruptions during the COVID-19 pandemic—particularly declines in student motivation and self-regulation—further challenge the interpretation of recent findings and reinforce the need for more rigorous, longitudinal research, especially with younger learners (Ackermans et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR1)).

Overall, advancing personalized learning requires the development of clearer conceptual definitions, the removal of infrastructural and organizational barriers, and the adoption of more robust empirical methodologies. Future research should extend to underrepresented learner populations and explicitly address cognitive and motivational processes such as metacognition, rather than assuming they will emerge from technological implementation alone (Ackermans et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR1)). Although recent evidence confirms that adaptive and personalized learning has become central within educational technology, the field continues to face significant gaps in theory, inclusivity, and empirical validation. Consequently, new reviews must articulate their unique contributions, address unresolved research gaps, and advance more equitable and effective AI-enhanced educational practices.

Limitations
-----------

This study faced several limitations related to the scope and management of the literature review. The large volume of publications on the topic created the possibility that some relevant studies were unintentionally omitted, a common challenge in extensive reviews. Although multiple reference management tools were used to organize and streamline the process, the review was restricted to English-language, peer-reviewed articles from nine high-impact journals, potentially excluding valuable findings from other ranked journals, non-English sources, and alternative scholarly formats such as conference papers, dissertations, and reports.

Future research
---------------

The findings of this review emphasize the need for greater conceptual clarity in defining personalized learning in AI-driven educational contexts, encouraging the use of more precise terminology—such as digital personalized learning (DPL) or personalized adaptive learning—to better reflect the roles of AI and system-level adaptation. Advancing this agenda also requires closer collaboration between AI developers and educators to ensure that emerging tools are pedagogically sound, user-friendly, and responsive to evolving educational needs (Yu & Chauhan, [2024](/article/10.1186/s40561-026-00440-6#ref-CR67)).

The review further highlights the importance of integrating technology-based diagnostic assessment to address both cognitive and emotional dimensions of learning, with greater emphasis on higher-order thinking and the responsible integration of emerging technologies while considering ethical and privacy implications. Finally, it calls for systematic, comparative evaluations of personalized adaptive learning platforms to assess effectiveness across learner profiles, instructional contexts, and educational goals, supporting more informed adoption of personalized learning technologies.
