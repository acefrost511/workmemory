## Abstract
Students’ use of generative artificial intelligence (GAI) to avoid engaging in generative processing can undermine the validity of higher education. In contrast, Flipped-Interaction Intelligent Tutoring Systems (FIITSs) may promote active engagement by leading a personalised dialogue. The underutilisation of FIITS may stem from the lack of a framework to guide prompt creation and from a dearth of published FIITS prompt examples. This article presents the Flipped-Interaction Prompt (FIP) Framework, abstracted from two validated prompts. To achieve this validation, 26 preservice science education students at a South African university engaged with either prompt in a free GAI five times over ten weeks. The resulting 114 engagements, each involving at least 10 flipped-interaction dialogue exchanges, were analysed for implementation fidelity and for students’ engagement in generative processing. Findings were triangulated against questionnaire and group interview responses, as well as written reflections. The technical implementation was closely aligned with the prompt instruction, with minor deviations noted for not providing answers outright. Additionally, students demonstrated moderate to high levels of generative processing. Findings support the efficacy of the abstracted FIP Framework in guiding the creation of FIITS prompts. Investigating instantiations for additional subject domains would further strengthen confidence in this framework.

## 1. Introduction
The underutilisation of free generative artificial intelligence (GAI) as a Flipped-Interaction Intelligent Tutoring System (FIITS) may be due to a lack of a prompt framework and exemplars. In a flipped-interaction (FI), GAI asks, rather than answers, questions ([White et al., 2023](#B24-education-16-00573)). An FI that is customised to the user’s needs is an intelligent tutoring system (ITS) ([Zheng et al., 2025](#B28-education-16-00573)). Underutilisation of free GAI to lead, rather than respond to, a dialogical exchange, is evident in its absence in reports on students’ GAI use, for example, by South African preservice teachers ([Ramnarain et al., 2024](#B19-education-16-00573)) and biology students across nine universities in the United States of America ([Coelho et al., 2025](#B5-education-16-00573)). Other information and communication technologies (ICTs), such as search engines and websites, can provide information. Therefore, using GAI to answer users’ questions involves substitution or augmentation relative to these other ICTs ([Franco, 2019](#B10-education-16-00573)). In contrast, whereas search engines and websites cannot, GAI can ask users questions and respond with personalised feedback. Therefore, prompting GAI as a FIITS can be seen as potentially redefining ICT-mediated learning by providing functionality absent in other ICTs.
Consistent with the argument that GAI’s unique capabilities are underutilised in education, a meta-analysis of GAI-related research revealed that GAI continues to perpetuate, rather than redefine, education paradigms ([Lee et al., 2025](#B16-education-16-00573)). Despite calls to pioneer innovative integration of free GAI into education ([Zhai, 2024](#B27-education-16-00573)), few concrete guidelines, such as template-like frameworks and illustrative examples, have been published for FIITS. Possibly, the paucity of prompt templates and examples of this kind results from a preference for more user-friendly chatbots and custom-built intelligent tutoring systems over lengthy prompts that need to be copied from a repository and pasted into GAI. However, these more user-friendly alternatives are prohibitively expensive for many higher institutions, particularly in the developing world ([Avcı et al., 2025](#B1-education-16-00573)). Also, obtaining licences for such alternatives may be difficult due to institutional bureaucracy.
Prompt frameworks and examples do, however, abound in the literature for the more common form of using GAI, i.e., to provide or evaluate information. These include frameworks for prompting GAI to formatively assess scientific argumentation ([F. M. Watts et al., 2025](#B22-education-16-00573)), pedagogical content knowledge ([Blonder et al., 2024](#B3-education-16-00573)), and pedagogical reflection ([Hofmann et al., 2025](#B11-education-16-00573)). This article extends the literature on prompt examples and frameworks into the domain of the flipped-interaction intelligent tutor, as it answers the overarching question:
> How can free generative artificial intelligence be prompted to serve as a flipped-interaction intelligent tutor?
To answer this question, the technical and pedagogical efficacy of two FIITS prompts is first investigated, guided by the following research questions:
(1)
: To what extent do free generative artificial intelligence (GAI) platforms execute the prompts with technical fidelity?
(2)
: To what extent do the Flipped-Interaction Intelligent Tutoring Systems (FIITSs) resulting from these prompts encourage student generative processing?
Positive responses to these questions provide evidence for abstracting an easily customisable generic framework from these two investigated prompts. This framework is called the Flipped-Interaction Prompt (FIP) Framework. The FIP Framework could guide educators in creating free FIITS tailored to their specifications, without bureaucratic oversight.
## 2. Theoretical Frameworks
Generative processing serves as a theoretical framework for interpreting students’ engagement with GAI. Cognitive Load Theory, in conjunction with the Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning, holds that generative processing is necessary for meaningful learning to occur ([Mayer, 2024](#B18-education-16-00573)). Generative processing involves selecting relevant information, forming connections between prior and new knowledge, and reflecting on feedback to construct a deeper understanding ([Fiorella & Mayer, 2021](#B9-education-16-00573)). Generative processing occurs within the limited space of working memory, which can be expanded through skilful collaboration with peers, instructors, or even artificial tools serving these purposes ([Kirschner et al., 2018](#B14-education-16-00573)). When used pedagogically appropriately, GAI enhances the working memory space and facilitates and stimulates students’ generative processing ([Katsenou et al., 2025](#B13-education-16-00573)). When used pedagogically inappropriately, however, GAI promotes gaming the system ([K. J. Watts, 2025](#B23-education-16-00573)), i.e., circumventing generative processing. Concerns about GAI misuse in higher education arise from GAI’s ability to game traditional assignments by generating human-like answers while requiring little generative processing from students ([Hughes et al., 2025](#B12-education-16-00573)). This concern is irrelevant to a FIITS, given its role of asking students questions.
Against this background, the FIITS prompt examples and abstracted framework presented in this article were pedagogically informed by [Laurillard](#B15-education-16-00573)’s ([2013](#B15-education-16-00573)) Conversational Framework. Dialogue likely to promote generative processing is conceptualised as students expressing their current conception, receiving feedback that challenges or extends that conception, applying revised ideas in practice, and reflecting on the outcomes.
While the questioning dialogue of a FIITS is more likely to promote generative processing than is more passive information access ([Katsenou et al., 2025](#B13-education-16-00573)), it is not immune to attempts to game the system. Research into integrating ICTs into education over several decades has revealed various methods for detecting attempts to game the system ([Baker et al., 2013](#B2-education-16-00573)). These include avoidance of more difficult questions, over-reliance on hints, and guessing. Guessing may be evidenced by high error rates and repeated selection of a single multiple-choice option. In contrast, behaviour that suggests generative processing includes active selection of relevant information, connection-building, and concept modification ([Fiorella & Mayer, 2021](#B9-education-16-00573)).
## 3. Materials and Methods
### 3.1. Research Design
This is a pragmatically conducted mixed-methods case study ([Creswell & Cresswell, 2003](#B6-education-16-00573)) aimed at evaluating the technical and pedagogical efficacy of two example FIITS prompts to validate a generic FIT framework. The two prompts and their creation are first described, followed by an explanation of the data collection and analysis process.
### 3.2. The Prompts, Their Creation, and Output Examples
The two investigated prompts are available at . When pasted into free GAI, they result in FIITS aimed at developing subject-matter knowledge among preservice high school natural or physical science teachers. These two FIITS prompts were created through a series of iterative interactions with ChatGPT Plus (version 4). These initial prompts were refined through approximately five iterations of testing, guided by the pedagogical intuition of two experienced educators. Each iteration included testing the prompts on three free GAI platforms (ChatGPT, Gemini, and CoPilot). During these tests, the user experience, consistency, and fidelity across the interactions and simulations were assessed, and improvements were implemented by adding content, pedagogical elements, student control, and administrative details as the need for these became evident. The prompts were then input into ChatGPT Plus (version 4) and the free version of Gemini to run 50 simulated dialogues, each consisting of ten original question-answer exchanges. The simulators were prompted to answer some questions incorrectly, resulting in additional follow-up question-answer exchanges. The simulated dialogues were then analysed by ChatGPT Plus (version 4). This analysis suggested that the GAI platforms implemented the prompts with high fidelity, and, therefore, the prompts were finalised, enabling initiation of the implementation and investigation stages of this study.
[Table 1](#table\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-t001) illustrates the setup sequence that results when the physical science prompt is pasted into Gemini. The FIITS provides choices for topic, grade, sub-topic, and difficulty level. Although the grade level refers to school-level physical science, the prompts were used with university students preparing to teach school physical science.

### 3.3. Implementation, Data Collection, Sample, and Ethics
The two FIITS prompts were implemented as part of the Bachelor of Education degree at a South African University. Using population sampling, data were collected from 11 third- and 15 fourth-year BEd students who engaged with the physical science and natural science prompts, respectively, every other week during a 10-week intervention. The students were all Black South Africans (16 male and 10 female) under the age of 25, who had been schooled in South Africa and were majoring in middle and high school natural and physical sciences and mathematics education at the university where this research was conducted. The students copied the prompt from their module’s learning management system, pasted it into a free GAI of their choice, selected the topic, grade, and difficulty level, and interacted with the resulting FIITS. This meant that ideally, each of the 26 students submitted five engagements, yielding a total of 130. However, a few students failed to submit all expected engagements, resulting in a data corpus of 114 engagements. Each engagement consists of the 10 questions that the FIITS was prompted to pose, along with any follow-up questions when the student provided an incorrect answer. Additionally, the students completed a reflective questionnaire immediately after each engagement, wrote a reflective essay, and participated in an audio-recorded group interview at the end of the 10-week intervention. Ethical clearance for the study was obtained from the relevant regulatory body (reference number deleted for review). The students were provided with written informed consent prior to the intervention. In this process, they were informed of the study’s purpose, their right for their responses not to be used within the study without any penalty.
Given the use of prompt-based interactions, particular care was taken to ensure that no personally identifiable information was included in the data. Data were stored securely and reported in aggregate or anonymised form to protect student confidentiality.
### 3.4. Data Analysis
The FIITS student–engagements formed the primary data source. In correspondence to the research questions, this data was analysed for: (1) the fidelity with which the resulting FIITS implemented the prompt, by calculating the percentage of engagements that abided by the prompt instructions, per prompt aspect; (2) evidence for and against generative cognitive processing during the interaction. The engagements were analysed quantitatively for evidence of gaming the system, and the students’ post-activity reflections were analysed qualitatively for evidence of generative processing. Qualitative detection of generative processing was guided by the purpose-built rubric given in [Table 3](#table\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-t003). Generative processing is operationalised across three dimensions: E (engagement), B (building on feedback), and C (constructing conceptual understanding). Each student’s written reflection was coded on a 0–4 scale for each dimension. Analysis was supported by ChatGPT Plus through an iterative process of exploratory prompting, rubric-guided manual evaluation, and consistency checking, which enhanced analytic efficiency, reliability and transparency. Quantitative summaries were generated using Microsoft Excel. To strengthen validity, findings from the reflection analysis were triangulated against group interview data, allowing for the identification of convergent and divergent patterns across data sources. The group interviews were also used to member check emergent themes.

## 4. Results
Findings reveal that when the prompts were pasted into free GAI, the resulting FIITS demonstrated high technical fidelity. Also, students employed moderate to high levels of generative processing as they engaged with the FIITS.
### 4.1. High Fidelity
[Table 4](#table\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-t004) shows the overall fidelity during the implementation of the FIITS prompt, indicating a high to perfect success rate. Three of the five structural elements analysed showed perfect enactment of the prompt, while the remaining two showed minor implementation fidelity errors. These errors are indicated in [Table 4](#table\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-t004) as structural errors (not staying within the chosen difficulty level) and answer violations (providing answers outright after an incorrect answer).
\*\*Table 4.\*\*
Implementation fidelity.
![](https://pub.mdpi-res.com/img/table.png)
[Table 5](#table\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-t005) shows that fidelity errors were unevenly distributed across platforms. The most popular platform, ChatGPT, accounted for the two structural errors and 74 of the 76 answer violations, with Gemini accounting for the remaining 2. Regarding the structural errors, the FIITS prompted progression to a higher difficulty level without explicitly offering the option to remain at the previously chosen level. Although the students could have instructed the FIITS to remain at their current difficulty level, the prompt’s suggestive nature appeared to advance the students by default. These two cases of structural error, therefore, reflect reduced interface clarity rather than a breakdown in prompt logic.

### 4.2. Moderate to High Levels of Generative Processing
In general, moderate levels, with some evidence of high levels of generative processing, were deduced. This deduction is based on the observations that the students: voluntarily chose medium to harder difficulty levels for a considerable fraction of their engagement; displayed a reasonable correct-incorrect response rate; only occasionally avoided follow-up questions; did not noticeably resort to random or repetitive button pressing activity; and scored moderately to highly in their written reflection using a rubric ([Table 3](#table\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-t003)) designed to detect self-reported engagement in generative processing, as previously explained.
#### 4.2.1. Difficulty Levels
[Figure 1](#fig\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-f001) indicates the number of students who chose each difficulty level within the FIITS for each of the five fortnightly engagements. This choice was voluntary. Although students were verbally encouraged to progress to more difficult levels over time, this was not incentivised. The 11 cases where students chose the easy option after week 1 could be evidence of avoidance of generative processing, as suggested by a student’s confession in a group interview: “There were times that it was hard to focus because maybe the thing was too easy for me, or maybe sometimes I was just rushing to finish the questions to submit”. In contrast, some students reported intentionally progressing through the levels, for example, “Choosing difficulty levels was fun too—I started with Easy questions to understand the basics, then moved up to Hard and Challenging, which made me apply my knowledge to solve more complex problems. This was a great learning experience for me.” Consistent with this student’s statement, [Figure 2](#fig\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-f002) shows migration towards the hard level in the fifth and ninth weeks, and to a lesser extent, towards the challenging level in the seventh week. Inconsistent with this student’s statement, however, the challenging level was largely avoided, and it appears that some students regressed from hard to medium between weeks 5 and 7.

#### 4.2.2. Correct–Incorrect Response Rates
[Figure 2](#fig\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-f002) displays the frequency with which each possible score was attained across the sets of engagement. Since each engagement set consisted of 10 questions, the maximum score per set was 10, and the maximum total score across the five weeks was 50. [Figure 2](#fig\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-f002) shows a high frequency of perfect scores. Similarly, a high mean score (35/50) was calculated. It is possible that some of this success arose from gaming the system by using an external GAI platform to obtain correct answers. During the focus group discussions, one student admitted to doing this when pressed for time. However, these high success rates appeared more largely attributable to genuine engagement in generative processing, as evidenced by the following statement:
> Using ChatGPT as an intelligent tutor made me feel as though I always had a personal advisor available. Whether I was stuck on a perplexing concept or simply wanted to explore an idea further, ChatGPT offered straightforward explanations, relatable examples, and immediate feedback. It adapted to my questions and learning pace, making studying feel less formal and more conversational. For example, if I didn’t understand a mathematical formula or the context of a historical event, I could ask clarifying questions without feeling judged or rushed. This created a learning process that felt more natural and less intimidating, especially for topics I typically struggled with.
>
> (Student 6—Reflections)
Additional explanations for the high scores are the revisionary nature of the FIITS and students’ misunderstanding of the incentive system. Since the FIITS were aligned to a school-level curriculum, these university students may have experienced them as a revision of previously mastered work. Moreover, the focus group discussion revealed that some students had misunderstood the assessment expectations: “I thought we would lose marks if we got answers wrong, so I redid it until I got full marks” (Focus group 3).
#### 4.2.3. Avoiding Follow-Up Questions
[Table 8](#table\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-t008) illustrates a student avoiding engagement with the FIITS’s optional follow-up questions. Since answering these follow-up questions was not incentivised by mark allocation, a student who nevertheless answered them was likely self-directed, suggesting generative processing. In contrast, a student who avoided these follow-up questions may have been attempting to minimise generative processing by doing only the minimum required to secure a mark. Even if this avoidance of follow-up questions indicates an attempt to game the system, its occurrence was rare, affecting only one or two students each week.

#### 4.2.4. Button-Pressing Possibilities
Random or repetitive button pressing is a possible way to game the system, which may be encouraged if students are aware that marking does not incentivise giving the correct answer. Possibly conducive to random button-pressing behaviour, several students said they prioritised completing the activity quickly rather than answering carefully, particularly under time constraints. However, the pattern displayed in [Figure 3](#fig\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-f003) does not suggest widespread random responding or an overreliance on any single option. Instead, the spread of A–C responses appears relatively balanced. Fewer D responses are evident, corresponding to the observation that the FIITS rarely positioned the correct option at D.
![](/education/education-16-00573/article\_deploy/html/images/education-16-00573-g003-550.jpg)

The number of times each student chose each multiple-choice option.
[Figure 3](#fig\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-f003) shows variations in total responses across the students. Totals under 50 resulted from students failing to complete all engagements (e.g., Student 6) or engaging in more challenging levels, which deviated from the multiple-choice format (e.g., Student 13). Totals over 50 resulted from the student providing incorrect responses to one or more of the 10 initial questions per set, prompting the FIITS to ask follow-up questions to guide them to the correct answers.
#### 4.2.5. Generative Processing
[Figure 4](#fig\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-f004) represents the analysis of the reflection-based generative processing scores using the 0–4 scale rubric for each of the dimensions of engagement, building, and constructing (see [Table 3](#table\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-t003)). Engagement scores were strongly concentrated at the upper end of the scale, with all but one student’s reflection demonstrating rich (n = 15) or clear (n = 8) engagement, i.e., engagement at levels 4 and 3, respectively. No students scored below level 2 on this dimension, indicating consistently high levels of reported interaction, motivation, and sustained participation when using the intelligent tutor.
![](/education/education-16-00573/article\_deploy/html/images/education-16-00573-g004-550.jpg)
\*\*Figure 4.\*\*
Reflective-based generative processing scores.
A similar but more pronounced pattern was observed for building. Most students (n = 23) achieved the maximum score of 4, with only one student scoring 3 and no scores recorded below level 3. This indicates widespread evidence of feedback-driven adjustment, including the use of hints, explanations, and corrective guidance to refine understanding.
In contrast, Constructing scores showed greater spread and moderation. While most students scored at level 3 (n = 15), fewer reached level 4 (n = 1), and a notable number remained at level 2 (n = 8). This suggests that although students frequently reported improved understanding, fewer articulated fully reconstructed or transferable conceptual reasoning in their reflections.
Student 18 is an example of a student who demonstrated sustained engagement, feedback-driven adjustment, and conceptual construction when engaging with the FIITS. Their written reflection was evaluated at the maximum level (4) for each aspect of generative processing. Below is an extract from this reflection:
> When I struggled to determine whether a species is reduced or oxidised, the tutor broke the redox process into less intimidating steps: determining oxidation numbers, determining electron transfer, and writing half-reactions. It immediately corrected me if I had gotten it wrong and explained why the solution was wrong.
This extract evidences B—building through iterative feedback and adjustment, while the explicit articulation of redox mechanisms and half-reactions reflects C—constructing at a high level. The student not only reports improvement but also explains how conceptual understanding was reorganised, aligning with their maximum C score.
In contrast, Student 17 (assigned the scores: engagement = 3, building = 4, and construction = 2) reflected strong engagement and reliance on feedback, but with more limited conceptual articulation:
> Having to have immediate feedback, made the learning process more enacting as If I was unclear about something, the interactive AI would give me the correct answer. Unlike the typical system of having to search or wait to come in contact with a teacher to get clarification. The AI gave it immediately and even went further on justifying its answers where I was correct. Where the answer was wrong it also gave me correct answer immediately and even asked if I needed further explanation.
The above quote emphasises interaction, immediacy, and clarification, with little reference to the student providing further explanation; it focuses only on correct answers. Thus, the student primarily frames learning as receiving confirmation of correct answers. This pattern supports the lower constructing score, as conceptual reconstruction appears to be externally driven by feedback rather than internally articulated by the student.
Across reflections, a consistent pattern emerged: high engagement and responsiveness to feedback do not automatically translate into articulated conceptual construction. Students who scored highest on C explicitly described disciplinary mechanisms, causal relationships, or conceptual reorganisation. By contrast, students with moderate C scores emphasised immediacy, correctness, and reassurance, without detailing how knowledge structures changed.
This pattern helps explain the quantitative distribution observed in the findings, where E and B scores cluster at the upper end, while C scores show greater spread and moderation. The reflections suggest that the FIITS supported engagement and feedback-driven learning, but either deeper generative processing was limited, or the prompt’s emphasis on multiple-choice questions, for ease of answering, obscured the verbalisation of underlying conceptual change.
## 5. Discussion
The findings demonstrate high to perfect technical implementation fidelity and moderate to high levels of student generative processing for the two FIITSs investigated. These findings contribute to a growing body of evidence that student–GAI dialogue can result in deep learning through generative processing for the same reasons that student–lecturer dialogue can ([Hofmann et al., 2025](#B11-education-16-00573); [Katsenou et al., 2025](#B13-education-16-00573)). Dialogue enables the sharing of cognitive resources, thereby decreasing cognitive load ([Kirschner et al., 2018](#B14-education-16-00573)). Additionally, pedagogically skilful dialogue can direct attention towards relevant items for selection, prompt concept linkage, and provide formative feedback to guide concept construction ([Fiorella, 2023](#B8-education-16-00573)). The finding that even free GAI can be prompted to engage in such pedagogically skilled dialogue is consistent with findings from other studies ([Ling & Jan, 2025](#B17-education-16-00573); [Yan et al., 2024](#B25-education-16-00573); [Zeng, 2025](#B26-education-16-00573)).
The positive findings reported above justified our use of the two investigated prompts as a starting point, from which we abstracted a generic prompt framework. We present the Flipped-Interaction Prompt (FIP) Framework in [Figure 5](#fig\_body\_display\_education-16-00573-f005). The FIP Framework is a template for creating customised prompts for GAI to behave as a FIITS. Square brackets indicate placeholders to guide educators in applying their pedagogical and curricular expertise, without requiring technical expertise such as coding, to create a suitable FIITS prompt for their context. The FIP Framework has contextual and pedagogical framing sections, which are described and justified below.
![](/education/education-16-00573/article\_deploy/html/images/education-16-00573-g005-550.jpg)
\*\*Figure 5.\*\*
The Flipped-Interaction Prompt Framework (FIP Framework).
The context framing sections of the prompt require customised completion of the relevant square brackets to specify the following: subject domain (e.g., history, nursing, or economics); level of instruction (e.g., Grade 12, first-year undergraduate, or postgraduate); mode of interaction (e.g., multiple-choice simulation and dialogue, case-based); list of topics to choose from; and difficulty levels to choose from. Providing difficulty level choices enables differentiation and cognitive load management ([Fiorella & Mayer, 2021](#B9-education-16-00573)).
The pedagogical framing sections of the prompt are guided by [Laurillard](#B15-education-16-00573)’s ([2013](#B15-education-16-00573)) Conversational Framework, which aims to facilitate dialogue that promotes articulation, feedback, practice, and conceptual refinement. Articulation is promoted by asking questions. The students’ answers to these questions are followed by personalised feedback. Practice is provided through multiple questions asked on each chosen topic. Conceptual refinement is encouraged when a student chooses an incorrect answer, and a Socratic dialogue follows to guide them to analyse alternative answers. Socratic dialogue may help students revise their misconceptions and develop metacognition ([Elder & Paul, 2019](#B7-education-16-00573)), as well as refine their understanding iteratively ([Shute, 2008](#B20-education-16-00573)). The framework emphasises conceptual understanding over memorisation since this is more durable and promotes higher-order thinking skills ([Bransford et al., 2000](#B4-education-16-00573)).
The FIP Framework aligns with [UNESCO](#B21-education-16-00573)’s ([2024](#B21-education-16-00573)) AI competency framework for teachers. The flipped, interactive nature of the FIITS generated using this framework encourages active learning. This human-centric, ethical use of GAI to promote learning contrasts with students’ passive, unethical dependence on GAI to complete tasks with minimal generative processing. The FIP Framework’s guidance on using free GAI platforms enhances equitable access to AI for pedagogical and professional development. Furthermore, the FIP Framework is intended to enhance the prompt proficiency of students, lecturers, and teachers, thereby advancing their competence in using GAI in pedagogically powerful, ethical, and human-centric ways.
## 6. Conclusions
This article presented the Flipped-Interaction Prompt (FIP) Framework to address this underutilisation. This framework was abstracted from two prompts that were found to be implemented with high to perfect fidelity when pasted into free GAI and to prompt moderate to high generative processing, engaging students. Lecturers and students could benefit from entering their module details into the FIP Framework template. The resulting prompt can be pasted into free GAI to turn it into a FIITS or application in various contexts and disciplines. In this way, the FIP Framework could expand educators’ GAI pedagogical repertoires and enhance students’ generative processing. Future research could replicate this study in other contexts and with larger samples to evaluate the influence of these variables on limiting the generalisability of this study. Additionally, the technical and pedagogical efficacy of instantiations across other discipline domains and study levels, and with various GAI platforms, could be investigated. Other areas of interest include evaluating how students’ engagement in FIITS can be assessed for university credit, as well as the role of frameworks, such as this one, in developing prompt literacy among students and educators. The FIP Framework is offered as a tool to increase the uptake of free GAI prompting as Flipped-Interaction Intelligent Tutoring Systems (FIITSs). Such an uptake of FIITS prompting should promote student thinking through GAI use at a time when fears that GAI undermines higher education are abundant.
