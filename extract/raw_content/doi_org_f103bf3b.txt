Abstract
--------

Educators in nursing education have been exploring and applying various approaches to developing students’ nursing skills. These approaches include lecturing, live demonstrations, role-playing, human standardized patients, virtual reality (VR) simulations, etc. However, the impact of integrating the recently emerging generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) technology into VR simulations on the development of students’ nursing skills has not been fully explored due to its novelty and the constrained timeframe available for comprehensive research. This study examines nursing students’ perceptions of the effectiveness of and learning experiences during a GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter simulation in developing clinical skills. A total of 23 undergraduate nursing students participated in the study. Each of them had an individual interview with the researchers. Students reported positive learning experiences during the simulation. They believed that this type of simulated practice was effective. However, they also identified limitations in the current system, including technical issues and difficulty with empathy building. Implications of these findings are discussed.

Introduction
------------

Nursing education contributes a vital and specialized role to higher education, focusing on preparing a highly skilled workforce for high-stakes clinical environments. The training of nursing students in patient encounters is a critical component of this educational process, as it equips them with essential skills for effective communication, information extraction, and patient interaction (Sims-Koenig et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR100); Vessey & Huss, [2002](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR113)). These encounters form the backbone of clinical training, providing students with the opportunity to develop the confidence and competence necessary to engage with patients in real-world settings (Ross [2013a](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR86)). Traditionally, this training has taken place in two primary forms: (1) Peer-to-peer practice and (2) interactions with human-standardized patients (HSPs) (Talbot et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR106)). In peer-to-peer practice, one student assumes the role of the nurse, while another acts as the patient, allowing for a controlled yet somewhat limited simulation of real-life scenarios. Although this method is useful for basic skill development, it lacks the complexity and authenticity that come with interactions involving HSPs, who are trained actors playing the roles of patients with specific medical histories and emotional states. In a patient encounter practice involving an HSP, these actors can provide a more realistic and dynamic environment, helping students practice inquiry skills, build rapport, and gather key information from the patient, while also challenging them to think critically and respond empathetically under pressure (Talbot et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR106)).

Traditionally, nursing students’ patient encounter practices take place in an in-person form (Ross [2013b](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR87)). However, reliance on in-person interactions with HSPs presents several challenges. The availability of actors, actor recruitment and training costs, and the logistical constraints of organizing these encounters can limit the frequency and quality of these training sessions (Vessey & Huss, [2002](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR113)). Moreover, drawbacks such as the lack of communication skills training for specific patient groups may compromise the quality of training and limit students’ adaptability to various patients (Chant et al., [2002](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR19)). These challenges have prompted educators and researchers to explore alternative methods that can supplement traditional in-person patient encounter practices. One promising avenue is to use virtual reality (VR) as a training tool.

Educational researchers and practitioners have long recognized the potential of VR environments as valuable learning tools, particularly when they are effectively implemented within appropriate educational contexts (Dickey, [2003](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR30)). In the context of nursing education, VR provides a controlled and repeatable environment where students can practice patient encounters without the logistical issues associated with HSPs (Reger et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR84)). These systems traditionally employ pre-programmed, keyword- or rule-based dialogue systems (Maicher et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR71)), which allow students to interact with virtual patients in a linear fashion. For example, Li et al. ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR65)) developed a desktop VR simulation in OpenSimulator that used a pre-programmed conversation system to enhance nursing students’ communication skills. However, these systems often fall short in replicating the unpredictability and depth of human conversation, as the virtual agents are limited in their ability to respond to a wide range of questions and scenarios. The linear nature of these interactions can hinder the development of critical thinking and adaptability, which are essential skills for nursing professionals.

To address these limitations, recent advancements in generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) offer new possibilities for enhancing VR-based patient encounter training. Though still in their early stage and being continuously iterated, GenAI models, such as GPT-4 by OpenAI or Claude3 by Anthropic, have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in generating human-like text, understanding context, and responding to a wide array of conversational prompts. By integrating GenAI into VR training environments, we can create more dynamic and realistic patient simulations that better prepare nursing students for the complexities of real-world patient interactions.

In this qualitative study, we explored the use of a GenAI-enhanced virtual reality learning environment (VRLE) to enhance nursing students’ patient encounter skills. Specifically, we investigated how students perceived the effectiveness of the GenAI-enhanced VR practice, their learning experiences, and the empathy-building aspects of the practice. The following research questions guided our study:

* How did students perceive the effectiveness of the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice in preparing them for real-world, in-person patient encounters?
* How did students perceive their learning experiences during the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice?
* How did students perceive their empathy with virtual patients during the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice?

Through this investigation, we aim to understand how GenAI can enhance nursing education by creating more effective, scalable, and accessible training tools. The study will provide valuable insights and practical recommendations for instructors, educators, researchers, and administrators on leveraging emerging technologies, such as GenAI, to nurture students’ skills more effectively.

Literature review
-----------------

### Introduction to patient encounter training

Patient encounter training (PET) is a crucial component of nursing education that emphasizes the development of clinical skills, diagnostic reasoning, and communication skills through simulated patient interactions (Jeffries, [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR56)). It refers to the practical exercises and simulations that students participate in to develop their skills in communicating and caring for patients (Pluhar et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR82)). This type of training is designed to bridge the gap between theoretical knowledge and practical application, ensuring that learners can apply their understanding in real-world scenarios (Jeffries, [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR56)).

Traditional PET methods often involve lectures and demonstrations, where students observe and then practice skills in a controlled environment, such as on a simulated patient’s bed (Ahmady et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR1)). Some relatively newer PET methods include role-playing, standardized patient exercises, and supervised clinical experiences, often supplemented by debriefing sessions for reflection and feedback (Taylor et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR107)). These activities enhance nursing students’ empathy, clinical judgment, and understanding of patient needs (Taylor et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR107)).

Regarding the advantages and disadvantages associated with these methods, Ahmady and colleagues ([2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR1)) noted that lectures and demonstrations deliver knowledge and skills efficiently and systematically; however, they cannot replicate the real clinical environment, and students may remain passive learners in the process. Additionally, because these methods are taught collectively, they may not address students’ individual learning needs. Comparatively, role-playing may provide better clinical experiences (Ahmady et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR1)). However, effective role-playing requires delicate instructional design and a great amount of investment, including time, funds, and human resources (Ertmer et al., [2010](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR32)).

In real patient encounters, nursing and medical students often face multiple challenges, one of which is handling problematic patient behaviors (Pluhar et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR82)). Therefore, nursing educators use HSPs to prepare nursing students for real patient encounters (Talbot et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR106)). Bokken and colleagues ([2008](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR10)) categorized HSPs into four types: (1) Real patients as educational resources, (2) simulated patients as educational resources, (3) real patients as instructors, and (4) simulated patients as instructors. Types 1 and 2 are passive roles that only provide medical or symptom information for training purposes, whereas Types 3 and 4 actively provide instructions and guidance to students in addition to what Types 1 and 2 may offer. For each type, a significant investment is required to ensure that patients, whether real or simulated, meet the standards for nursing or medical training and provide consistent training effectiveness. To reduce the costs, previous researchers, such as Keiser and Turkelson ([2017](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR59)), used healthcare students (rather than paid actors) as HSPs and partially achieved the goal.

### Virtual reality and its application in nursing education

Combining multiple digital technologies, VR creates immersive and interactive 3D environments to simulate real-world or fictional scenarios (Mikropoulos & Natsis, [2011](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR75)). VR offers unique advantages and has attracted increasing attention from the educational research community. Hedberg and Alexander ([1994](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR45)) pointed out that VR can serve as an educational technology, utilizing multimedia to capture students’ interest in learning. Other researchers have also found that VR enhances conceptual understanding and improves learning performance (Lee & Wong, [2008](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR64); Salzman et al., [1999](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR91)).

In addition to the above benefits, VR improves student engagement by allowing them to actively interact with the learning content and thus leads to a deeper understanding of the subject matter (Jantjies et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR55)). VR’s ability to deliver authenticity and realism makes it a powerful instructional medium, offering enhanced learning experiences (Kasapakis & Dzardanova, [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR58)) that closely mimic real-world scenarios without the limitations of safety or distance (Tepe et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR108)). Moreover, the interactions and instant feedback in VR foster active learning by enabling students to manipulate components and receive immediate responses (Ma et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR70)).

VR has evolved significantly, starting with text-based systems and advancing to fully immersive environments (Loeffler & Anderson, [1994](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR68); Moore, [1995](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR76)). Early text-based VR was applied in fields such as language learning (Schwienhorst, [2002](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR93)). Semi-immersive desktop-based VR, available in both 2D and 3D, continues to be used, with studies showing its impact on cognitive load and engagement (Bueno-Vesga et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR16)). Sensory-immersive VR, often seen in entertainment contexts like 4D theaters (Huang et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR51)), has grown in popularity but remains costly and less accessible to the public (Halarnkar et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR38); He et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR44); Muñoz-Saavedra et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR77)). More affordable head-mounted display (HMD) VR has gained traction in recent years. This fully immersive experience allows users to engage with a 3D virtual world through a head-mounted device and has been widely adopted in higher education to boost motivation and develop competencies (Radianti et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR83)).

In nursing education, VR and its effectiveness have been widely studied. Rizzo and Talbot ([2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR85)) discussed how VR technology evolved to enable virtual simulations in nursing education and proposed migrating these simulations to mobile platforms for greater accessibility despite usability concerns like speech recognition. Shin et al. ([2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR97)) identified key general and virtual-specific characteristics of virtual simulations in nursing education, urging educators to incorporate these features to enhance learner engagement and performance. Vogelsang et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR114)) identified five major aspects that VR promotes in nursing education: Self-efficacy, skill acquisition, learning outcomes, attitude development, and engagement. Nursing instructors were satisfied with the cost-effectiveness of VR simulations (Shorey & Ng, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR98)) and the scalability compared with non-VR simulations (Kiegaldie & Shaw, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR61)). Students found VR to be an engaging and effective learning tool (Saab et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR89)), which provides authentic learning environments, keeps learning fun and interactive, improves knowledge mastery, and develops nursing skills (Chen et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR20); Hilty et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR47); Kiegaldie & Shaw, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR61); Liu et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR67)).

### Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) in education

Artificial intelligence, or AI, has been studied for more than half a century (Zawacki-Richter et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR118)). It involves integrating technologies such as machine learning, algorithms, natural language processing, neuroscience, and cognitive science, designed to replicate human intelligence and the functioning of the human brain (Akgun & Greenhow, [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR2)). AI in education (AIEd) refers to using AI to support and improve educational processes (Chiu et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR22)). Among various types of AI, generative AI, or GenAI, refers to AI systems that create new content such as text, images, audio, or videos (Banh & Strobel, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR6)).

The recent breakthrough in GenAI, exemplified by the public release of ChatGPT and tools such as DALL-E, MusicLM, and Codex, has gathered global attention and prompted educators and researchers to explore new applications in education. GenAI offers new opportunities for personalized learning, intelligent tutoring, and interactive environments (Bahroun et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR3); Baidoo-anu & Ansah, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR4); Yu & Guo, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR117)). Specific to VR learning, GenAI supports creating virtual scenarios, 3D objects, avatars or non-player characters (NPCs), and conversations (Lv, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR69)). In addition to content generation, GenAI can also improve visual realism and enhance learner engagement in VR environments (Hashim et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR43)). However, challenges such as misinformation, bias, and privacy issues have also emerged (Baidoo-anu & Ansah, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR4)). Ethical concerns, including plagiarism and content fabrication, highlight the need for critical evaluation and responsible use of AI-generated materials (Cooper, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR24)).

While most current research focuses on theoretical discussions of GenAI’s educational potential, some studies have explored its practical impact. Sun et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR105)) found that ChatGPT improved programming education by enhancing student performance and user experiences. Chheang et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR21)) reported higher knowledge-based task performance using GenAI-supported avatar-based assistants in anatomy learning. Dai and collaborators ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR26)) adopted GenAI technology in teacher education and found that GenAI agents’ humorous responses during conversations helped facilitate pre-service teachers’ situational and social teaching practice. Song and colleagues ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR102)) developed an immersive GenAI-VR platform, enabling oral interaction with NPCs, and found that it increased engagement, motivation, and knowledge retention across various subjects through personalized learning interactions. In more nursing education-relevant studies, Benfatah et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR8)) utilized a web-based GenAI tool—ChatGPT—as a virtual patient and had nursing students communicate with it. The study reported that students recognized ChatGPT’s value in training their interaction skills; however, their performance was impacted by multiple factors from the GenAI tool, including the clarity and comprehensibility of responses, the relevance of responses, and the ability to provide useful information by ChatGPT (Benfatah et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR8)). Fung et al. ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR34)) conducted a crossover randomized controlled trial with 44 global nursing undergraduates to compare a GenAI patient simulation with a 360° VR video-based simulation. The study assigned participants to either an initial GenAI-mediated dialogue followed by 360° VR video observation (Group A) or the inverse sequence (Group B) to facilitate autonomous and dynamic medical history-taking. Results showed that both simulations significantly enhanced clinical competence, cultural awareness, and AI readiness in the two groups. Notably, GenAI displayed a stronger initial effect on clinical outcomes and readiness when administered first, and participants particularly valued the individualized feedback provided by GenAI. Liaw et al. ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR66)) implemented a desktop AI-enabled VR simulation to enhance students’ clinical readiness in identifying and addressing critical patient deterioration, alongside evaluating the factors governing its pedagogical integration and systemic uptake within the curriculum. Results showed significant within-group knowledge improvements in managing clinical deterioration and communication. While between-group differences were non-significant, students found the tool highly appropriate for clinical readiness. However, technical issues and scripted interactions were identified as barriers, leading to recommendations for future GenAI integration.

### Concerns and challenges in existing GenAI + VR nursing simulation

The technological advancement in VR and GenAI has received attention in nursing education. However, there are still concerns and challenges in this field.

First, simulation authenticity in nursing education should no longer be viewed solely as a matter of physical fidelity (the degree to which the VR looks like a clinic) (Bland et al., [2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR9)). Traditionally, higher physical fidelity was equated with greater authenticity within a VR environment (Slater, [2003](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR101)). Recent scholarship, however, emphasizes functional task alignment and psychological authenticity, which focus on whether the simulation triggers appropriate interaction with the VR environment and characters and, correspondingly, the same cognitive processes and professional behaviors as real-world practice (Hamstra et al., [2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR40)). Authenticity is thus a multidimensional construct that involves the alignment of the environment, the task, and the social interaction, ensuring that learners are not just observing but are situated within a credible clinical reality (Liu et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR67)).

Second, engagement with affective learning remains a critical challenge in technology-mediated professional training (Saab et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR89)). Affective learning involves the development of values, attitudes, and empathy that are inherently social and emotional (Shephard, [2008](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR96)). In the context of GenAI + VR, theorists argue that for deep affective learning to occur, the technology must facilitate social presence and emotional resonance (Makransky & Petersen, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR72)). This resonance is essential for students to transition from theoretical understanding to genuine emotional involvement within the virtual clinical space (Dyer et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR31)). However, the use of AI agents raises questions about whether a machine can truly scaffold the nuanced emotional labor required in nursing, or if it merely encourages the performance of superficial, procedural empathy (Castonguay et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR17); Stokes & Palmer, [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR104)).

Third, the increasing automation of healthcare training through GenAI introduces significant ethical and pedagogical dilemmas, particularly concerning the socio-emotional dimensions (Chiu et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR22)) within the context of nursing education. While GenAI offers unprecedented scalability, critical scholarship warns against the dehumanization of nursing education, where teacher-student and patient-student relationships are redefined by algorithms (Hou et al., [2026](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR50)). Educators must remain vigilant to ensure that AI-driven simulations serve as a supplement to, rather than a replacement for, authentic human interaction to maintain the human touch that is central to the nursing profession (Tlili et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR109)).

### Research gaps

The above literature review provides an in-depth discussion of the definitions, evolutions, and applications of PET, VR, and GenAI in education. While PET, VR, and GenAI have each been studied independently, there is limited research on the integration of VR and GenAI for enhancing PET in nursing education. Specifically, the extant literature lacks comprehensive studies on how GenAI-enhanced fully immersive VR can address the challenges of traditional PET methods, such as passive learning and resource constraints, by offering low-cost, immersive training experiences. Existing studies that applied GenAI and VR technologies in nursing education have typically relied on discrete learning environments. These environments include either standalone web-based dialogue interfaces, such as the ChatGPT website (Benfatah et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR8)) or proprietary online systems (Fung et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR34)), or semi-immersive VR modalities, such as screen-based 360° videos (Fung et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR34)) or desktop VR simulations (Liaw et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR66)). However, few studies have deeply integrated GenAI into a fully immersive VRLE. Furthermore, there remains a notable scarcity of empirical research evaluating the capacity of GenAI and VR technologies to foster affective learning or sustain the humanization of nursing care. Future inquiries should investigate the efficacy of GenAI in synthesizing authentic, diverse patient encounters to enhance communicative competence and clinical decision-making within virtual simulations and ensure that technological advancement aligns with the humanistic core of the nursing profession.

Methodology
-----------

### Theoretical framework

We adopted two theories as theoretical frameworks to guide our research design. The first theory is Richard Mayer’s ([2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR74)) Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML). This theory, as described by Rudolph ([2017](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR88)), is a theoretical framework that explains the processes involved when individuals learn in environments utilizing multiple media. It is built on three foundational assumptions: (1) the dual-channel assumption, which posits that learners process information using both visual and auditory pathways; (2) the limited-capacity assumption, which suggests that each of these pathways can handle only a certain amount of information at one time; and (3) the active processing assumption, which emphasizes that effective learning involves engaging cognitive processes in a structured manner. According to Mayer ([2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR74)), the objectives of CTML are threefold: to minimize unnecessary cognitive load (extraneous processing), to regulate essential cognitive processing required for constructing mental models of the learning content, and to enhance cognitive processing aimed at deep comprehension of the material. CTML has been extensively employed in educational settings, particularly in conjunction with VR technology, as evidenced by studies such as those by de Back et al. ([2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR28)), Huang et al. ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR52)), and Parong and Mayer ([2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR80)). In our research, we implemented a GenAI-enhanced VR system for patient interactions that aligned with CTML’s principles and assumptions to enhance students’ educational experiences and outcomes.

The second theory, Situated Learning Theory (SLT), introduced by Lave and Wenger ([1991](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR63)), posits that learning is inherently tied to the context and culture in which it occurs. Unlike traditional theories that view learning as the transmission of abstract knowledge from teacher to student, SLT emphasizes the importance of social interaction and real-world experiences in the learning process. Central to this theory is the concept of “legitimate peripheral participation” (Lave & Wenger, [1991](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR63), p. 91), where learners start on the periphery of a community of practice and gradually move towards full participation as they gain skills and knowledge. This process is not just about acquiring information, but it involves becoming an active member of the community, where learning is a dynamic and social experience. In addition, the theory also highlights the importance of authentic tasks, where learners engage in activities directly relevant to the skills they are trying to develop. These tasks allow learners to connect theoretical knowledge with practical application, enhancing the depth and retention of learning.

Previous research has widely applied SLT across various fields. The theory has been studied in virtual reality (e.g., Dawley & Dede, [2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR27)) and nursing education (e.g., Onda, [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR79)). For instance, in nursing education, Henderson et al. ([2006](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR46)) utilized SLT to design clinical simulations that mimic real-world nursing challenges, providing students with immersive experiences that closely resemble actual clinical practice. Similarly, Wyrostok et al. ([2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR116)) developed a culturally sensitive simulation of a patient-centered end-of-life care program using SLT as the theoretical framework. The study found that this approach significantly improved students’ self-awareness, self-efficacy, and ability to connect theory with practice in a realistic context. In another study, Chang and colleagues ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR18)) developed a virtual reality-based situated learning system to nurture nursing students’ clinical skills. They found that the VRLE, following the principles of SLT, improved students’ learning achievements, fostered their problem-solving ability, and enhanced their learning engagement.

### Research design

In this qualitative study, our goal was to gain a nuanced and in-depth understanding of how nursing students perceive the effectiveness and challenges of the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice system. After developing such a system and facilitating student engagement with it, we conducted semi-structured interviews to capture their impressions, experiences, and difficulties. This qualitative approach was specifically chosen because the novel integration of GenAI and VR in nursing education creates complex, multi-dimensional interactions. A qualitative lens allows for the exploration of these subjective meaning-making processes and pedagogical affordances in a way that standardized quantitative measures might overlook (Creswell & Poth, [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR25)), prioritizing depth of insight into this emerging technology over statistical breadth.

### System design and development

Following SLT, which emphasizes learning through real-world tasks in authentic contexts, we created a VR patient encounter practice system in Unity3D (Unity Technologies, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR111)). The system consisted of three core modules: The environment module, the virtual agent module, and the learning module (see Fig. [1](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#Fig1)).

**Fig. 1**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9/figures/1)

The three modules of the VR patient encounter practice system (Acronyms: LLM = Large Language Model, STT = Speech-to-Text, TTS = Text-to-Speech)

The environment module is the entire 3D scenario of the virtual world, including all 3D models, objects, and characters. We created the environment in Unity3D. The environment was designed to be a replica of a standard clinic room, which included a chair, a desk, a computer, a water sink, a bottle of disinfecting wipes, some tissues, several cabinets, three virtual patients (see Fig. [2](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#Fig2)), and a menu that allowed participants to choose from the three patients (see Fig. [2](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#Fig2)b).

An overview of the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice systemFootnote

The virtual agent module integrated a Large Language Model (LLM) into the virtual characters, enabling them to “talk.” We incorporated Inworld ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR53)), a conversational AI agent platform, into our environment and linked it to the virtual patients through the platform’s API and three distinct character IDs. Inworld provides pre-trained models (based on the GPT-4 model) for natural language understanding and generation, as well as speech-to-text (STT) and text-to-speech (TTS) engines, allowing users to interact with AI agents through microphones. In addition, developers can customize agents’ personalities and moods (see Fig. [3](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#Fig3)), which impact the agents’ responses and behaviors. Following the Introvert-Ambivert-Extrovert spectrum (Petric, [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR81)) and the course instructors’ guidance, we configured three distinct virtual patient personalities that represented typical real-world patients’ characters: The first patient presents a neutral, ambivert demeanor, demonstrating a balanced approach to interaction. The second patient exhibits extroverted traits, being open, talkative, and readily sharing emotions. This patient may spontaneously offer details beyond the scope of direct questions. Conversely, the third patient displays a more introverted nature, with responses being reserved and concise, providing only essential information and requiring specific prompts for further elaboration. These comprehensive settings, combined with the ability for oral communication with participants, allowed the virtual patients to vary their responses per students’ questions and enhanced the “situated” nature of the learning activity.

The learning module encompasses all aspects related to students’ information gathering and skill development. We first established the following profiles for the three virtual patients:

* Demographic background: They shared different names, genders, ages, appearances, and family lives; however, they had the same career—a nurse assistant in a local clinic; they started the job less than three months and were on probation; their manager was not happy with their leave; they hoped to get a solution so that they could return to work as soon as possible.
* Clinical background: They had the same issue—lower back pain radiating to the right ankle; the pain started at 6:00 A.M. that day when they were carrying a heavy patient in their workplaces; the pain level was around eight out of 10, with 10 being the worst; they attempted two pills of Advil, which did not help; they had no previous or chronic back pain issue.

During the practice, a nursing student would talk to all three types of patients. However, they were allowed to freely choose any sequence to speak to them from the patient selection menu. Notably, each virtual patient had a unique appearance and voice. We also implemented subtitles displaying both participant and patient conversation transcripts above the patients’ heads (see Fig. [2](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#Fig2)c), so that students could see or refer to these texts while communicating with the virtual patients audibly. Such a design is aligned with the dual-channel assumption of the CTML. Meanwhile, to better scaffold the students, we placed a cheat sheet on the floor, which displayed only some keywords rather than complete questions (see Fig. [2](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#Fig2)d) to help participants remember what to ask, reflecting CTML’s limited-capacity assumption. Participants were not required to follow the sequence of the keywords on the cheat sheet; instead, they had the flexibility to adapt the order of their questions based on the flow of their conversations with the virtual patients. This design encouraged participants to think and formulate their own questions, echoing CTML’s active processing assumption.

Once the design, development, and integration of the three modules were completed, we exported a build of the VR system from Unity3D and deployed it in Meta Quest 2, the headset device used by participants to experience the practice. Figure [4](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#Fig4) illustrates the interactions between participants and virtual patients within the VR practice system. First, each participant greeted a virtual patient and asked the patient a question orally. Once the virtual patient received the question, the STT engine in the virtual agent would convert the speech to text, which was then prompted to the LLM to understand. The LLM would respond to the prompt in text, and the text response would be converted to speech by the TTS engine. After that, the speech response would be transmitted back to the virtual patient, who would speak the response out. Finally, the participant would understand the response and gather information from it. After all these steps, the work process would repeat until the participant believed that they had gathered enough information from all three virtual patients and could actively exit the conversation.

### Interview protocol

We designed an interview protocol to guide our data collection. We aimed to understand how participants perceived the effectiveness of the GenAI-enhanced VR practice system, the challenges they encountered while using it, and their learning experiences during its use. Specifically, the interview included a series of questions regarding their overall impression of the GenAI-enhanced VR practice, perceptions of the impact on learning, perceived immersion in the VR, difficulties or challenges encountered during the practice, and suggestions for improvement. We finalized these questions through multiple rounds of discussions and agreements among the research group, as well as with three instructors who actually taught our target student participants to ensure the validity of the questions. Sample questions included:

* What was your overall experience of the VR patient encounter practice?
* To what extent do you think the VR practice helped you learn?
* How confident are you in applying the knowledge you gained from the VR practice to the real, in-person patient encounter?
* How much do you feel like you were in a real patient encounter clinic room during the VR practice?
* What frustrations or challenges did you encounter during the VR practice?
* What do you suggest for improving the current VR practice?

These questions gave us a comprehensive understanding of students’ perceptions of the learning experiences in the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice.

### Participants

Our participants included 23 undergraduate nursing students from a Midwest R1 land-grant university in the United States. Specifically, two male and 21 female students voluntarily participated in the VR practice. Nine of them were 18 to 19 years old, while the rest were between 20 and 23. Most of them (21 out of 23) were white students, with one African American and one Asian student. Two students were in their first year, five were in their second year, and the rest were in their third year of study. None of the 23 students had any previous VR learning experiences using an immersive VR headset; however, 11 of them had prior experience using VR for gaming or recreational purposes.

### Data collection

We collected data in the spring semester of 2024. All participants were recruited through purposive sampling from a nursing course to ensure they all had appropriate knowledge and skills for the VR patient encounter practice. This approach allowed for an in-depth exploration of the VR practice, which is the primary goal of our study. The study adhered to ethical research design, as indicated by the IRB number 2085123. The participants were individually given a consent form by researchers before the data collection. They read the form to learn details about the purposes, goals, methods, data protection, and their rights during the study, and signed it to indicate their voluntary participation.

The data collection for each student took about one hour. As shown in Fig. [5](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#Fig5), at the beginning, each participant read and signed the consent form. After that, they had 30 min to practice their conversation and information-gathering skills with the three virtual patients (with a maximum of 10 min for each patient) in the VR practice system. Each participant directly spoke with the virtual patients through the built-in microphone in Meta Quest 2. The cheat sheet in the VR environment helped them, when necessary, remember and formulate what to ask the virtual patients. After the practice, each participant completed an individual interview with the researchers. Each interview was around 10 to 15 min and was audio recorded for data analysis purposes.

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9/figures/5)

The data collection process

The data collection was ceased after 23 interviews as the research team determined that data saturation (Guest et al., [2006](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR37)) had been reached at the 20th interview. All subsequent interviews yielded redundant information.

### Data analysis

Before conducting the data analysis, we transcribed the interview recordings into text using both an automated transcription tool and a manual review to balance the efficiency and accuracy in the transcription process. Then, we adopted the reflexive thematic analysis (RTA) approach as outlined by Braun and Clarke ([2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR13), [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR14)). In accordance with the epistemological underpinnings of RTA, we did not employ multiple independent coders or calculate inter-rater reliability (IRR) measures. Within this framework, coding is viewed as an organic, subjective, and deeply interpretive process, where the researcher serves as an active instrument in knowledge production (Braun & Clarke, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR14)). Consequently, the pursuit of statistical consensus (e.g., interrater reliability, or IRR) was prioritized less than the depth of interpretation and the reflexive engagement with the data. To ensure the rigor and trustworthiness of the findings, a transparent audit trail was maintained, and the evolution of themes was subject to continuous reflexive scrutiny.

Specifically, we analyzed the transcripts following Braun and Clarke’s ([2006](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR12)) six-step thematic analysis process in ATLAS.ti 24.1.1. First of all, we immersed ourselves in the transcripts to become thoroughly familiar with the data. Second, one researcher generated initial codes based on their preliminary understanding of the data. Sample codes include “Like,” “Dislike,” “Confident,” “Immersive,” “Engaging,” “Neat,” “Interactive,” “Robotic,” “Unnatural,” “Keep talking,” etc. Third, the researcher generated candidate themes based on these codes. Sample themes include “Positive Experience,” “Improved Communication Skills,” “Technical Limitations,” “Improve Interaction,” etc. Then, we collaboratively refined these codes and themes by reviewing and discussing them within the research team to ensure the validity and reliability and improve the depth of interpretation of the codes and themes. Through this iterative process of refinement, a total of 10 distinct themes were identified. Each theme was subsequently defined, named, and mapped to the research questions to ensure a comprehensive representation of the data. Finally, we documented these themes in the paper, incorporating our interpretations and supplementing them with quotations from the transcripts.

Results
-------

The interview results are presented around five topics: (1) Overall impression, (2) perceived impact on learning, (3) perceived immersion, (4) issues and challenges, and (5) suggestions for improvement. Under these topics, 10 themes emerged. Table [1](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#Tab1) shows the topics and themes. ### Overall impression

*Theme 1:* Positive Experience. Participants consistently reported that the GenAI-enhanced practice functioned as a pedagogical bridge between theoretical coursework and the high-stakes reality of clinical environments. For the majority of participants (18 out of 23), the simulation’s value lay in its ability to provide a “*natural* (P12)” and “*realistic* (P04)” interaction without the immediate consequences of real-world errors. By characterizing the experience as a “*low-pressure environment* (P11),” students revealed that the simulation lowered their affective filters, enabling them to focus on exploring their professional roles and mastering conversational nuances. Some participants (e.g., P23) even advocated for permanently integrating GenAI into the nursing curriculum, suggesting that the fun and interesting nature of the AI interaction significantly boosted their intrinsic motivation to practice.

### Perceived impact on learning

In addition to feeling positive, participants acknowledged that the VR practice benefited their learning outcomes. They improved their communication skills with patients, increased their confidence, enhanced their critical thinking, and learned how to handle unexpected responses.

*Theme 2:* Improved Communication Skills. Sixteen (16) out of 23 participants highlighted that the integration of unique patient personalities forced students to transition from rote checklist-following to strategic social navigation. The simulation required students to not only ask the correct medical questions but also to actively manage the flow of the conversation, such as when dealing with a talkative patient who frequently veered off-topic or another who might speak much less. For example, P03 mentioned that “*the second patient kept talking a lot about the Smoky Mountains*”[Footnote 3](#Fn3) and found it “*difficult stopping him*.” In such situations, it was essential for participants to know when and how to encourage patients to share more information and when and how to stop them from doing so excessively. As a result, participants learned not only how to ask the right questions in an appropriate order to gather information but also how to bring the conversation back to the main point.

*Theme 3:* Enhanced Confidence. A majority of participants (13 out of 23) explicitly reported feeling more confident after the VR practice, particularly in their readiness to apply what they learned to real patient encounters. While the remaining ten participants did not spontaneously identify confidence as a primary outcome, these 13 participants emphasized that the VR practice not only helped them become familiar with the patient encounter process but also relieved their nerves, as P06 shared, “*I think I could go out and do it on a real patient now without feeling as nervous*.”

*Theme 4:* Nurtured Critical Thinking. Eight (8) participants described a sophisticated process of active assessment that was triggered whenever the virtual patients provided non-linear or unexpected responses. Unlike traditional simulations with fixed paths, GenAI’s ability to deviate from the expected script compelled students to “*think critically* (P23)” and “*formulate alternative questions* (P23)” in real-time. This unpredictability forced participants to move beyond passive information gathering and instead engage in active clinical reasoning, as they had to constantly “*assess what the patient was saying and respond accordingly* (P07).” Despite these eight participants’ positive perceptions, however, it is noteworthy that the critical thinking skill was not explicitly reported by the remaining majority, which suggests that while the system has the potential to nurture critical thinking, the impact may depend on individual student readiness or the specific trajectory of their AI-driven conversation.

### Perceived immersion

*Theme 5:* Visual Immersion. The VR headset and the Unity-created virtual clinic room provided a strong sense of spatial presence for 10 participants, who found the environment’s replica-like qualities deeply engaging. By incorporating realistic models of standard clinic items, such as water sinks, disinfecting wipes, and cabinets, the simulation successfully induced a state of immersion where students felt they were in a physical room. This environmental fidelity was crucial for participants. As P05 noted, the visual cues were so compelling that “*I sometimes forgot it wasn’t a real room*,” demonstrating how high visual realism can effectively ground a learner within a professional context.

*Theme 6:* Conversational Immersion. Beyond environmental aesthetics, seven (7) participants identified the dynamic and genuine nature of the GenAI conversation as another primary driver of their sense of presence. The integration of the LLM allowed virtual patients to understand diverse phrasing and generate adaptive responses, transforming them from static computer agents into responsive social actors. When virtual patients shared “*unexpected answers* (P06)” or “*personal stories* (P16),” participants perceived the encounter as being “*very close to a real-life conversation* (P06),” indicating that conversational fluidity is as essential for immersion as visual fidelity.

### Issues and challenges

Despite the positive and immersive learning experience, participants spotted some issues and challenges during the VR practice. The issues and challenges included communication delays, control and navigation difficulties, and a lack of empathy and realism.

*Theme 7:* Technical Limitations. Technical disruptions (reported by 12 participants) were not merely functional failures but acted as significant “presence-breakers” that hindered the learning process. For instance, communication lags caused by Wi-Fi signals created a disconnect between the participants’ oral input and the patients’ reaction, leading to confusion and a loss of focus. Furthermore, the learning curve required to master VR controls and navigation indicates that when the interface is not intuitive, learners must divert limited cognitive resources away from the medical task to troubleshoot the technology itself.

*Theme 8:* Failure to Build Empathy. Even though some participants considered the conversation content with virtual patients realistic, eight (8) participants struggled to establish an emotional bond with virtual patients due to the robotic and synthetic nature of the AI-generated voices. Analysis reveals that the absence of human-like emotional inflections and natural pitches made it difficult for students to perceive the virtual patients as real humans. This auditory disconnect was a major barrier to empathy; students felt they were “*talking to a machine* (P08)” and “*difficult to connect emotionally* (P07),” which suggests that for clinical training, emotional resonance is heavily dependent on the authenticity of the voice and the non-verbal cues present in human interaction.

### Suggestions for improvement

Finally, participants proposed suggestions for improving the current version of the VR practice. Most suggestions were based on issues and challenges they encountered during the practice. These suggestions can be categorized into two major groups:

*Theme 9:* Improvement of Interaction. Eight (8) participants suggested incorporating non-verbal cues, such as facial expressions, body gestures, and emotional speech, which reflect a desire for a more holistic communication experience. Participants recognized that clinical meaning is often communicated through physical affect and emotional tone, and that the current life-like limit of the avatars hindered their training. They specifically called for more “*natural voices* (P12)” and for patients to “*show more emotions* (P14)” when describing pain, which would better simulate the sensory-rich nature of real patient encounters.

*Theme 10:* Enhancement of Control. Five (5) participants requested increased agency over the simulation’s pace, suggesting that “*a pause or stop button* (P10)” and “*a speak button* (P23)” would enhance their metacognitive control during the practice. These requests imply that students want to better calibrate the simulation to their own cognitive processing speeds, allowing them to stop the patient’s speech to formulate questions or take a moment for reflection. This need for control highlights the importance of user-friendliness in VR systems to ensure that the technology serves the learner rather than dictating the rhythm of the learning.

Discussion
----------

### Effectiveness of the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice

Overall, despite the reported technical challenges, there was a robust consensus among participants regarding the effectiveness of the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice. While high-level cognitive skills, such as critical thinking (Theme 4), were explicitly recognized by a subset of students, the foundational benefits, such as improved conversational abilities and information-gathering skills, were more widely acknowledged. These perceptions suggest a hierarchical benefit structure: the system consistently delivers core clinical skills, and meanwhile offers a “ceiling” for advanced cognitive development for those who engage more deeply with the AI’s unpredictability.

On the one hand, participants reported improvement in communication skills (Theme 2) and confidence (Theme 3), echoing previous research studies that highlighted VR’s effectiveness in developing nursing or medical students’ communication skills (Hoek et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR48)) and boosting confidence (Hannans et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR41); Siah et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR99)). Specifically, our GenAI-enhanced VR practice helped students learn which questions to ask, how and when to ask, and how to refine the order and delivery of their inquiries. These findings align with earlier research that nursing students adjust their verbal expressions and language use to communicate effectively with patients (Harder et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR42); Kettunen et al., [2001](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR60)). Unlike the findings of Liaw et al. ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR66)), where learners reported frustration with scripted interactions that required exact wording to progress, our system leveraged the adaptive capabilities of LLMs to support diverse phrasing and responses. This flexibility is critical for fostering strategic social navigation, a process where students move beyond rote checklists to actively manage clinical conversations. The pedagogical value of this interactive depth has been corroborated by Fung et al. ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR34)), whose quantitative data revealed that such adaptive simulations yield significantly higher initial gains in clinical competence compared to static, non-interactive modalities.

On the other hand, although earlier research acknowledged the value of irrelevant information in understanding patients’ emotions and needs (van der Linde-van den Bor et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR119)), few studies have examined how to train nursing students to manage such information in a VRLE. By incorporating irrelevant information in GenAI-supported conversations, our study demonstrates that GenAI can create dynamic interactions where virtual patients answer questions correctly or deviate from the expected responses, challenging students to think actively and adapt their questioning strategically, resonating with findings in nursing education that VR enhances critical thinking (Jans et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR54); Sims-Koenig et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR100)). This process helps develop students’ patience and their ability to handle real-world patient interactions.

The observed improvements in communication skills and critical thinking directly exemplify the active processing assumption of CTML (Mayer, [2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR74)). Unlike traditional linear VR simulations using either non-VR simulations or rule-based or recurrent/convoluted neural network (RNN/CNN)-based agents (Maicher et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR71)), our LLM-based GenAI-enhanced VR practice presented more variability and flexibility, including unexpected responses and irrelevant information. These features forced students into high-level cognitive engagement, as they had to actively select relevant (or filter irrelevant) information, organize it into clinical mental models, and strategically adapt their questioning in real-time. This process mirrors the concept of legitimate peripheral participation within SLT (Lave & Wenger, [1991](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR63)). By managing the social complexities of diverse patient personalities, such as redirecting talkative patients or prompting less articulate ones, students operated on the periphery of actual nursing practice. The VRLE provided a safe container for this participation, allowing students to build the professional confidence and decision-making schemas necessary to eventually transition to full participation in real-world clinical communities. Consequently, our GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice could be, as concluded by previous researchers (e.g., Sardesai et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR92)), an effective preparation for real-world tasks.

While discussing the effectiveness of our system, it is important to consider whether the participants’ positive perceptions were influenced by the novelty effect (Clark, [1983](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR23)), a phenomenon where the introduction of a new medium temporarily boosts students’ motivation, engagement, and even performance, which is typical of emerging technologies in education. While the students expressed high levels of enthusiasm, their feedback suggests a nuanced, comparative judgment rather than a superficial attraction to novelty. Participants specifically highlighted that the GenAI-enhanced VRLE offered a level of interactivity and clinical realism that surpassed their previous experiences with linear, rule-based VR simulations. Moreover, the fact that students remained highly critical of the system’s shortcomings, such as communication delays and the robotic nature of AI voices, indicates that they maintained a critical stance throughout the practice. This ability to identify both the effectiveness and the limitations of the system provides evidence of credible, reflective engagement, suggesting that their positive responses were rooted in the perceived educational value of the simulation.

### Learning experiences in the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice

The interview results showed that our participants enjoyed the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice. They viewed the immersive and intelligent VR practice as a fun, interesting, and, to some extent, challenging learning process. We believe that several factors, which we label as the “Realistic-3Cs” (see Fig. [6](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#Fig6))—a realistic *context*, realistic *characters*, and realistic *conversations*, contributed to their positive learning experiences.

**Fig. 6**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9/figures/6)

The realistic-3Cs framework

The Realistic-3Cs framework serves as more than a design heuristic; it aligns with the core tenets of situated cognition, embodied learning, and dialogic learning. First, the *realistic context* (in our case, the virtual clinic) involved two components: a visually realistic environment and authentic tasks. On the one hand, a realistic environment anchors learning in situatedness (Brown et al., [1989](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR15)), where the immersive VRLE engages learners well during the VR experience and brings them a strong sense of immersion and presence (Bowman & McMahan, [2007](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR11); Newman et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR78)). On the other hand, authentic tasks provided learners with real-world training experience and improved their professional skills, which conforms to SLT (Lave & Wenger, [1991](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR63)). Altogether, the high-fidelity context provides the necessary sensory cues and cognitive practices for professional identity transition from a student to a professional or expert in the workplace. Second, realistic characters facilitate a form of embodied learning (Johnson-Glenberg et al., [2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR57); Shapiro, [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR95)). Although the interaction is virtual, the learners’ sensory and cognitive immersion with a life-like avatar or agent triggers professional training and emotional responses that are central to practice. In our study, the three uniquely designed virtual patients, with their authentic appearances and personalities, further enhanced participants’ learning experiences and outcomes. They had to adjust their strategies dynamically—sometimes prompting the patients, sometimes controlling the conversations—to maintain effective and efficient communication. Finally, realistic conversations move beyond linear scripts and keyword matching (e.g., Liaw et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR66)) into the realm of dialogic learning (Bakhtin et al., [1981](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR5)), which features two important concepts: dialogism and polyphony (or multi-voicedness). On the one hand, our GenAI-enhanced VR system reflects the dialogism concept by allowing participants and the virtual patients to engage in a dynamic negotiation of meaning, where the unpredictability of AI responses forced participants to move beyond rote questioning and actively interpret, react, and co-construct the clinical inquiries with the conversational AI agents. On the other hand, by confronting students with a diverse array of patient voices and personalities—each with unique temperaments, narratives, and emotional states—our VR practice mimics the socio-discursive complexity of real-world patient encounters. In this sense, the current system operationalizes polyphony by requiring nursing students to recognize and adapt to the unique subjectivity of each patient, thereby fostering the communicative agility necessary for professional practice. In summary, by synthesizing the principles of situatedness, embodiment, and dialogic learning, the Realistic-3Cs framework offers a robust, theoretically grounded heuristic for the design and development of GenAI-enhanced VR simulations. This integrated approach optimizes high-quality learning experiences by ensuring that the convergence of immersion, authenticity, and feedback aligns with established research on the essential components of effective virtual training (Salovaara-Hiltunen et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR90)).

Of course, a sound learning experience in a VRLE is not only about the realism of the environment or characters, but can also be related to other factors, such as the smoothness of interaction and the ease of use of the VR system. Even 30 years ago, researchers had found that pictorial realism induces less presence than interactivity and timely feedback do in a virtual environment (Welch et al., [1996](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR115)). More recently, researchers (e.g., van Gisbergen et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR112)) have pointed out that realism in VR may not be achieved by improving the level of realism of only one element (e.g., visual) but rather by enhancing that of a combination of elements. We would like to expand on this conclusion by stating that the level of high-quality *learning experience* in a VRLE does not rely on a single element, but rather on multiple elements. In addition to the Realistic-3Cs stated above, other elements may include, but are not limited to, the human-like speech voice, high-quality system control, and excellent user-friendliness. For example, incorporating non-verbal cues (e.g., capturing body gestures and facial expressions from HSPs) into virtual patients may boost the learning experience in a VRLE (Kasapakis & Dzardanova, [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR58)).

### Empathy in the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice

From our implementation and interviews, several issues emerged in the current version of the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice. Aside from technical issues, such as communication delays or visual freezing, the most common concern was that participants found it difficult to share empathy (Theme 8) with virtual patients, which does not conform to previous findings (e.g., Sengul & Sarıköse, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR94)). The findings can be deeply interpreted through the voice principle of CTML (Mayer, [2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR74)). According to CTML, when learners perceive a non-human voice, they are less likely to engage in the social processing required for a deep emotional connection. While the environment and characters successfully engaged the visual channel with high-fidelity clinic replicas, the robotic and synthetic auditory input likely inhibited social agency. In other words, the absence of human-like speech/voice reduced the realism of the VR practice, which is a problem identified by previous researchers (e.g., Liaw et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR66); Shorey & Ng, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR98)). Furthermore, from the perspective of SLT (Lave & Wenger, [1991](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR63)), learning is an inherently social and dynamic experience. Although the task in our VR practice was real-world-oriented, the lack of human-like emotional inflections in the patients’ speech/voice made it difficult for participants to view them as genuine social participants within a community of practice. This mismatch between visual realism and auditory input created a barrier to socially situated engagement, thereby diminishing participants’ overall experience in this VRLE, and reducing a potentially rich patient encounter to a transactional data-gathering exercise rather than a compassionate clinical interaction.

Hoorn et al. ([2003](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR49)) argued that VR environments should prioritize emotional relevance over visual fidelity to enhance user experience. Our findings on participants’ visual immersion and engagement corroborated Hoorn and colleagues’ proposal. However, we found that our participants’ emotional engagement was also closely tied to their auditory experience, connecting to whether virtual patients had human-like voices or not. If “empathizing with virtual patients” is a learning objective in a GenAI-enhanced VR training, then seeing visually realistic virtual patients and hearing human-like voices in their narrations simultaneously may help learners build emotional connections with virtual patients more easily, as such learning conforms to the visual/multimedia principle and the voice principle of Mayer’s ([2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR74)) CTML, which assumes that people have both the visual and auditory channels to receive information, and each channel has limited capacity for information processing. To prevent cognitive overload in a single channel, it is advisable to split the information burden across both channels to receive better learning outcomes than relying on one channel (Mayer & Pilegard, [2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR74)). Therefore, we agree with Hoorn and colleagues’ argument, but we want to complement that while visually realistic VR environments and characters are now easily achievable with modern game engines, auditory realism in conversational AI agents is a critical, yet underexplored, factor that may significantly impact learner experience and their ability to empathize with virtual patients in nursing or medical VR simulations. We call for more research regarding the impact of auditory realism on learning experiences and outcomes.

### The paradox of empathy in GenAI-enhanced conversation simulation

Beyond technical limitations, the reported difficulty in empathy-building invites a deeper interrogation of the ontological boundary (Turkle, [2011](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR110)). This concept refers to the fundamental existential divide between human beings, who are characterized by consciousness, biological vulnerability, and subjective lived experience, and artificial intelligence, which functions through simulated algorithms and digital processing (Turkle, [2011](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR110)). In this study, when students perceived the virtual patient as a machine rather than a human, they might lack the affective resonance (Decety & Meyer, [2008](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR29); Fuchs, [2017](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR33)) necessary for genuine emotional engagement. From a nursing philosophy perspective, empathy is not merely a communication skill but a share vulnerability (Gadow, [1990](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR35)) that a non-living AI agent cannot inhabit. Consequently, interactions across this ontological gap risk devolving into empathy induced with rules or mechanisms of a system (Belman & Flanagan, [2010](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR7)), where students perform surface acting (Larson & Yao, [2005](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR62); Stepien & Baernstein, [2006](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR103)) to satisfy a checklist in the system rather than truly willing to empathize and experience the underlying emotional connection and genuine compassion. This suggests that the empathy deficit reported by students may not be merely a technical flaw to be fixed with better audio, but a reflection of the inherent limits of AI-mediated human connection. While GenAI is an exceptional tool for cognitive and procedural rehearsal, it cannot fully replace the nuanced emotional labor (Larson & Yao, [2005](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR62)) learned through interaction with human standardized patients or real-life clinical encounters.

### Implications

Our study provides more recent evidence to prior research (e.g., Benfatah et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR8)) by demonstrating the effectiveness of GenAI tools in enhancing the clinical learning of nursing students. Furthermore, our findings reinforce the notion that GenAI-enhanced VR practice holds considerable promise in preparing students for real-world tasks. This approach improves their professional skills, boosts confidence, and fosters critical thinking, making it a valuable tool for education in a variety of disciplines and subjects. We encourage more educators and researchers to further explore this approach.

As articulated earlier, among the many factors that influence the learning experience in the GenAI-enhanced VR practice, the Realistic-3Cs play a core role. The three integral components—a realistic context, realistic characters, and realistic conversations—are foundational to designing and developing high-fidelity, conversation-based VRLEs in the context of professional training. The central thesis is that the perceived authenticity of these three elements directly influences the quality of the learners’ experiences. Hence, the Realistic-3Cs have the potential to be conceptualized as a theoretical framework and serve as an evidence-based heuristic for instructional designers and educators. By prioritizing these dimensions, practitioners can systematically design and implement more immersive and effective LLM-based GenAI-enhanced VR simulations across various disciplines, including, but not limited to, nursing education. We would like to see more empirical research studies exploring the application of the Realistic-3Cs in the future.

In terms of the practical implications, unlike traditional patient encounter methods using HSPs (Talbot et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR106)), VR offers a risk-free and cost-effective alternative (Shorey & Ng, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR98)). Mistakes in a virtual setting have no real-life consequences (Saab et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR89)), so students can experiment with different strategies, test their critical thinking, and learn from their errors in a safe environment, which enhances their clinical readiness. Overall, while the emergence of high-order cognitive gains, such as critical thinking, exhibited individual variability across the cohort, the simulation consistently provided a robust foundation for core clinical competencies. The fact that foundational benefits like communication fluency and professional confidence were widely recognized validates the pedagogical investment in this technology, even if the most sophisticated cognitive shifts were explicitly articulated by only a subset of learners during the VR practices. Meanwhile, traditional clinical training often demands substantial resources, including instructors, trained HSPs, and equipment, which are often limited in their availability (Keiser & Turkelson, [2017](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR59)). GenAI-enhanced VR simulations can reduce these costs by providing a scalable solution (Kiegaldie & Shaw, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR61)), where large groups of students can be trained simultaneously, even remotely, with virtual patients responding to students’ questions of various phrasing. These advantages make the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice highly suitable for higher institutions that offer nursing programs with limited funding, such as small public universities, teaching-oriented universities, community colleges, or private universities with modest resources.

Another thing to note, while VR can help students develop conceptual knowledge, procedural skills, and interaction strategies, the current GenAI-enhanced VR training system may fall short in fostering empathy with virtual patients. As noted by Hall and Walmsley ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR39)), combining multiple simulation modalities, such as VR/MR and manikin simulations, can enhance the sense of “clinical realism” (Good, [2003](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR36), p. 19) necessary for effective training. Given the current limitations (e.g., high cost, computing resource demand, etc.) in creating human-like AI voices and guided by the Realistic-3Cs, we suggest training GenAI models using human patients’ voices, tones, and responses from real-world patient encounters and then integrating the models into virtual patients in a VR/MR environment. This method may maintain visual realism while improving auditory realism, particularly when existing AI-generated voices sound robotic or unnatural.

### Limitations and future work

Although we aimed to design a well-structured study, several limitations remain. First, the virtual patients lacked nonverbal cues, such as body language, facial expressions, and emotional inflections in speech. These nonverbal elements could have provided participants with a more realistic interaction and helped them establish stronger empathy with the patients. Unfortunately, due to technological limitations, we were unable to incorporate these features into the current version of the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice.

Second, the current version of the GenAI-enhanced VR patient encounter practice does not allow students to adjust the virtual patients’ personality traits—only researchers can do so. This limitation hinders the VR practice from offering more personalized learning experiences.

Furthermore, while we consider it unlikely, it is still possible that the participants’ high levels of engagement and positive perceptions were influenced by the novelty effect (Clark, [1983](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00587-9#ref-CR23)). Since this study involved a single-session intervention, longitudinal studies on the long-term sustainability of these learning experiences, attitudes, and gains are needed as the GenAI technology becomes more commonplace.

In future work, we plan to make virtual patients more human-like and explore ways to incorporate nonverbal communication under the guidance of the Realistic-3Cs. We will also create a visual control panel within the VR environment that allows students to customize patients’ personalities. Additionally, we expect to expand participants’ ethnic diversity and include nursing students from different institutions to broaden the generalizability of the findings and measure students’ long-term retention of skills and sustained improvement in empathy after the VR practice. We believe these enhancements will significantly improve the learning experience and yield more interesting findings, and therefore, we encourage more researchers and educators in nursing education to explore this approach.

Conclusion
----------

This study demonstrates that a GenAI-enhanced VR simulation serves as a transformative, cost-effective alternative to traditional patient encounter training in nursing education by offering a dynamic, interactive, and low-risk clinical environment and realistic, interactive, and customizable learning experiences. It effectively improves students’ communication skills, critical thinking, and confidence, particularly through the incorporation of non-linear and irrelevant information in patient responses to foster students’ adaptability in diverse patient scenarios. However, iteration faces challenges regarding technical stability and the difficulty of establishing genuine empathy with virtual agents, highlighting a critical gap between cognitive training and affective resonance.

To effectively integrate this approach into nursing curricula, several actionable steps are recommended. First, curriculum designers should move beyond one-off interventions and scaffold these simulations across different levels of complexity, aligning them with clinical rotation objectives. Second, faculty training must focus on the debriefing process; instructors need to be trained not only to manage the technology but to help students navigate the cognitive dissonance caused by AI’s unpredictable responses. Finally, regarding assessment, GenAI-generated dialogue logs can be used as formative assessment tools to track the evolution of a student’s clinical reasoning and therapeutic communication patterns over time.

Future advancements must focus on enhancing voice realism and incorporating non-verbal cues to overcome existing barriers to empathy-building in virtual environments. Furthermore, while the methods are applicable to any domain requiring critical or sensitive communication, it is necessary to note the potential risk that over-reliance on AI agents might lead to a dehumanized pedagogical experience, where the nuanced art of human care could be reduced to algorithmic processing. To prevent technological immersion from eclipsing interpersonal connection, educators and researchers should strategically position automated scenarios as auxiliary tools that enhance, but do not supersede, genuine face-to-face engagement. A balanced approach is imperative to ensure that technological efficiency does not compromise the essential human touch and ethical maturity central to professional care.
