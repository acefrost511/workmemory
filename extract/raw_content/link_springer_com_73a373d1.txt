Abstract
--------

This study explores the use of generative AI, exemplified by ChatGPT, for self-directed professional development (SDPD) among K-12 schoolteachers. Specifically, it examines how ChatGPT assists teachers in their instructional practices and teaching methods, supports their SDPD, and explores teachers' perceptions of its role in this process. Based on Garrison’s (Garrison, Adult Education Quarterly 48:18–33, 1997) self-directed learning framework, we utilized a mixed-method approach to collect and analyze 298 completed survey responses, five individual semi-structured interviews, and documents (e.g., ChatGPT chat history regarding SDPD usage). Our research found that teachers primarily used ChatGPT to get inspiration and structure for classroom pedagogies, class activities, and lesson plans. Notably, while schoolteachers’ motivation for SDPD was generally passive due to teaching pressure; ChatGPT, in fact, played an important role in motivating teachers to advance their SDPD. Teachers demonstrated high self-management and self-monitoring capabilities in using ChatGPT for self-directed professional development (SDPD), including the ability to apply a variety of learning strategies and track time and goals while using ChatGPT for SDPD. In addition, teachers generally felt confident when interacting with ChatGPT for SDPD purposes. The findings of this research suggest strategies for teachers to enhance their teaching performance through the integration of ChatGPT. These findings may also be beneficial to policymakers and educators seeking to understand the potential of ChatGPT in supporting SDPD and inform future explorations of its role in informal learning contexts.

Introduction
------------

Professional development (PD) is crucial for teachers to improve their content knowledge and move toward a more professional stance both in their contributions as well as understanding as future educators (Sutherland et al., [2010](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR48)). It often occurs in various formats, formally and informally (Richter et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR40); Yurkofsky et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR52)). PD activities are traditionally organized as lectures, workshops, training sessions, seminars, and conferences, in which teachers may play a passive role. Typically, such activities are not situated in the specific workplace (Van Veen et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR50)). PD can also take place through collaborations with colleagues, mentoring, and participation in a network where teachers play an active role in deciding the PD content and resolving issues in their teaching practice (Van Veen et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR50)). In recent years, emerging technologies and systems, particularly artificial intelligence, have enabled teachers to have more personalized PD experiences that better align with their interests and needs (Ayanwale et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR3); Dede et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR15)). This study explores the use of generative AI, exemplified by ChatGPT, for self-directed professional development (SDPD) among in-service schoolteachers.

Literature Review
-----------------

### Self-Directed Learning

Self-directed learning (SDL) is a widely recognized concept that explores how learners take initiative in their own learning, especially in adult and online learning contexts (Doo & Zhu, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR17); Knowles, [1975](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR27); Song & Hill, [2007](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR47)). Garrison ([1997](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR21)) developed a three-dimensional model that includes self-management, self-monitoring, and motivation. Self-management refers to a learner’s ability to manage their own learning process, including planning, goal setting, and managing resources such as time and other learning resources (Candy, [1991](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR8); Garrison, [1997](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR21)). Garrison ([1997](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR21)) explained self-monitoring as a learner’s capability of reflecting on and assessing their learning strategies, learning processes, and learning outcomes. As part of this self-monitoring, learners engage in cognitive and metacognitive processes that allow themselves to identify whether and when they need to make appropriate adjustments to their learning strategies or seek additional support to achieve their intended learning objectives. Motivation in SDL can be both initial and sustaining, enabling the learner to begin and maintain their learning efforts (Garrison, [1997](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR21)).

Importantly, SDL is not a linear or solitary journey; it is iterative, dynamic, and situated (Knowles, [1975](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR27); Morris, [2019](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR35)). Learners continuously reorient their approaches based on evolving self-assessments, contextual demands, and learning objectives (Jossberger et al., [2010](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR25); Schweder et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR44)). In this way, SDL serves as a valuable lens for understanding how individuals navigate emergent technologies like generative AI to pursue self-initiated development (Canaran, [2025](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR7)). The flexibility of GenAI tools aligns well with SDL’s emphasis on learner agency and responsiveness, yet this flexibility also raises questions about the scaffolding, legitimacy, and depth of such learning.

### Self-Directed Professional Development (SDPD)

Building on the principles of SDL, self-directed professional development (SDPD) refers specifically to teachers’ autonomous efforts to enhance their professional competencies. Traditionally, professional development has been institutionally organized, credentialed, and top-down, mostly centered on externally mandated content. Yet a growing body of research documents a shift toward more bottom-up, internally motivated, and teacher-initiated forms of PD that reflect educators’ own goals, experiences, and evolving professional identities (Sancar et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR43); Eekelen et al., [2006](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR49); Mushayikwa & Lubben, [2009](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR36)).

This shift positions SDPD as a kind of informal professional development often unrecognized by institutions but deeply situated in teachers’ actual classroom practices and challenges. In this sense, SDPD is a specific application of SDL within the context of teacher professional growth. As Alshaikhi ([2020](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR1)) found in his study of Saudi EFL teachers, many prefer self-initiated PD precisely because it is embedded in their local contexts and responsive to the changing nature of their work. Teachers engage in reflection, collaboration, and informal networking as part of their development without necessarily adhering to institutionally regulated models. Similarly, Lan ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR28)) highlights how SDPD involves teachers’ self-assessment and intentional skill-building to address context-specific challenges. It is a process that enhances motivation, dedication, and accountability by reinforcing self-directed autonomy and internalized commitment to growth.

Lopes and Cunha ([2017](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR32)) emphasize that effective SDPD requires more than motivation; it must also be supported by appropriate tools, processes, and structural conditions. Without clear pathways, teachers may struggle to convert intent into meaningful learning. This concern is echoed in Mushayikwa and Lubben’s ([2009](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR36)) study of Zimbabwean A-level science and mathematics teachers who demonstrated seven motivating factors that drive SDPD, i.e., (1) their perceived professional identity, (2) their need for career development, (3) their need for networking, (4) their need to improve subject content knowledge, (5) the need to adapt and integrate materials to teach for understanding, (6) the need to acquire more practical knowledge and skills for the subject discipline, and (7) the perceived benefits which they derive from satisfying these needs. These seven factors were ultimately categorized into two core dimensions: professional efficacy and classroom efficacy, underscoring that SDPD is not merely about individual development but also involves responding to instructional demands and systemic pressures.

While SDPD is inherently flexible, this flexibility presents its own risks. Informal PD lacks external validation, making it difficult for teachers to gain recognition or institutional support. It also places the burden of identifying needs, curating resources, and sustaining motivation solely on the individual, the tasks of which can be overwhelming in already taxing work environments. As Morris ([2019](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR35)) notes, although SDPD gives educators greater control over their learning experiences, it also entrusts them with the responsibility of designing solutions to complex instructional problems, often with minimal formal support. Furthermore, it also demands a high level of emotional resilience (Mushayikwa & Lubben, [2009](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR36)). SDPD requires teachers to overcome negative emotions (e.g., frustration and disappointment) when there is a temporary clash between their refreshed teaching and students’ responses (Zhang, [2020a](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR54), [2020b](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR55)).

Despite these challenges, SDPD holds transformative potential when supported appropriately. SDPD is a complicated process that requires teachers to critically and continually examine their ongoing or past practices and update them accordingly (Shurr et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR45)). Nevertheless, teachers’ SDPD does not mean that teachers initiate their learning totally in isolation; they can also look for external support, such as collegial assistance that may help clarify and resolve issues regarding their teaching (Shurr et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR45)) or locate and select appropriate teaching resources that could be freely accessed (Zhang, [2020a](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR54), [2020b](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR55)) as well as online communities to sustain their learning. In this way, SDPD is not only a process of internal reflection but also one of relational and resourceful engagement, shaped by both individual agency and social interaction.

### Online Professional Development

With the advancement of new technologies and platforms, teachers have more access to online professional development (OPD) opportunities (Çelen & Seferoglu, [2020](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR9); Kabilan, [2004](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR26); Lay et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR29)). OPD can benefit teachers by providing high-quality, low-cost, and flexible PD training and materials at anytime and anywhere that breaks geographical constraints (Dede, [2006](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR14); Fishman et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR20); Mushayikwa & Lubben, [2009](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR36); O’Dwyer et al., [2007](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR39)). Not surprisingly, the COVID-19 pandemic increased the need and necessity for OPD (Bragg et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR6); Hartshorne et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR22)). Bragg et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR6)) conducted a systematic review of 11 articles to examine what practices and design elements of OPD programs improve teaching. They reported that content knowledge, pedagogical content knowledge, self-efficacy, beliefs about teaching, and instructional practices are crucial elements that lead to successful OPD. Other studies also suggested that OPD has a positive impact on changing teachers'professional identity, classroom culture, and connections with colleagues and online communities (e.g., Beach, [2017](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR4); Hou, [2015](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR23); Yurkofsky et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR52)).

Dille and Røkenes ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR16)) emphasize that teachers’ internal motivations and attitudes play a pivotal role in shaping their engagement with formally organized online professional development (OPD) programs. While structured OPD often relies on institutional scaffolding and facilitator guidance, self-directed approaches place greater responsibility on teachers to navigate resources and sustain motivation on their own. In this context, generative AI tools such as ChatGPT have emerged as potential facilitators of informal professional learning. These tools can generate instructional materials, offer real-time suggestions, provide formative feedback, and model pedagogical strategies (Atlas, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR2); Moorhouse & Kohnke, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR34); Li et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR31)). These functions parallel some of the scaffolding typically embedded in formal PD settings. When used purposefully, GenAI can support just-in-time learning and experimentation, making it a valuable companion in self-directed professional development (SDPD).

### Gen AI and SDPD

Yet the integration of GenAI into SDPD also creates new tensions. On one hand, GenAI offers unprecedented access to information and feedback, enabling just-in-time learning and experimentation (Samala et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR42); Yusuf et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR53)). On the other, it may reinforce surface-level engagement, obscure the origins of pedagogical knowledge, and exacerbate inequities if access and digital literacy are uneven (Farahani & Ghasemi, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR19); Lutz, [2019](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR33)). As such, the question is not merely whether GenAI supports SDPD, but how it reshapes the very nature of informal professional learning—its rhythms, risks, and possibilities. Thus far, research on teachers'practice regarding the utilization of generative AI for their PD and teaching improvement has been limited in scope and depth (Bonos, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR5)).

### Purpose of the Study

This study explores the use of ChatGPT, a popular generative AI tool, for SDPD among schoolteachers, including those in contexts with limited PD opportunities. Specifically, it addresses the following research questions:

1. (1)
   
   How do teachers use ChatGPT to support and develop their instructional practices and pedagogical approaches?
2. (2)
   
   How do teachers engage with ChatGPT as part of their self-directed professional development process?
3. (3)
   
   What are teachers'perceptions of ChatGPT's functionality in supporting SDPD?

Method
------

An explanatory mixed-method approach (Creswell & Creswell, [2017](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR13)) was employed in this study. First, we collected 298 completed responses from K-12 schoolteachers based in the United States. After that, we conducted five individual semi-structured interviews and analyzed relevant documents (e.g., ChatGPT chat history regarding SDPD usage).

### Quantitative Phase

The research started with quantitative data collection in May and June 2024. Participants were recruited through Prolific, an online research participant recruitment platform, and an embedded online questionnaire on Qualtrics. The Prolific filter enabled us to pre-screen individuals who met recruitment criteria, which included the following three items. Specifically, the participants had to: (1) currently hold a teacher status in a K-12 setting, (2) live in the United States, and (3) possess experience using ChatGPT for teaching and professional development purposes. Importantly, the questionnaire was purposely developed to address the key elements in Garrison’s ([1997](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR21)) self-directed learning theory.

### Survey Instrument Design (Garrison’s SDL Framework)

The survey consisted of three demographic questions, four inquiries on general ChatGPT usage in education, and a set of three Likert scale questions with 27 items tailored to assess learners'SDPD based on Garrison’s ([1997](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR21)) three-dimensional SDL model.

### Survey Participants

A total of 298 K-12 schoolteachers completed the survey. As displayed in Table [1](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#Tab1), very few of these schoolteachers (i.e., 0.67%) had less than one year of teaching experience, and slightly more than 7.0% of them had one to two years of teaching experience. In addition, nearly one in five (i.e., 19.80%) had three to five years of teaching experience, whereas roughly one-third (i.e., 33.56%) had six to ten years of teaching experience, while 18.46% had 11 to 15 years, and 20.47% had more than 15 years of teaching experience (see Table [1](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#Tab1)).

### Data Analysis

All quantitative data were analyzed through descriptive statistics that were described without the intention of drawing generalized conclusions (Nasir & Sukmawati, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR37)). The quantitative data were primarily used in this study to summarize information on a single variable by frequency counts and percentages (Rogers & Goodrick, [2010](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR41)). Researchers converted the categorical responses into numeric levels (“Not at all” = 1, “A little” = 2, “Some” = 3, “Quite a bit” = 4, “Very much” = 5) for analysis. While means and standard deviations are presented for these scales in the findings, the ordinal nature of the data means that the intervals between categories are not necessarily equal. Thus, these means should be interpreted as indicators of central tendency. We used stacked bar charts to visually display the distribution of responses for each item. This approach more appropriately reflects the ordinal nature of the data and improves the interpretability of item-level differences.

The qualitative data were analyzed by thematic analysis (Clarke & Braun, [2017](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR10)). All the interviews were recorded and automatically transcribed by Kaltura, followed by researchers'manual corrections in a verbatim manner (Paulus et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR38)) and first-level member checking. One of the researchers read through two interview transcripts, suggesting initial codes, categories, and themes. Two researchers then openly discussed the initial codebook together, resolved any disagreements, and eventually finalized the coding. Table [4](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#Tab4) demonstrates the coding themes, codes, and examples of excerpts.

Finding
-------

### How Does ChatGPT Support Instructional Practices and Pedagogical Approaches?

As indicated in Table [5](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#Tab5), our quantitative data found that nearly 20 percent of the participating teachers (N = 167, 19.49%) primarily used ChatGPT to get inspiration for classroom pedagogies, class activities, and lesson plans (e.g., learning materials, PowerPoint slides, etc.). In addition, 17.97% of these teachers used ChatGPT for generating practice questions or quizzes, 15.75% for creating customized educational content (e.g., learning materials, PowerPoint slides, etc.), and 12.37% for providing explanations or clarification on various subjects. As shown in Table [5](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#Tab5), a smaller percentage used ChatGPT to attempt to foster critical thinking and problem solving, support special student needs, obtain writing assistance or feedback, engage in language-related learning and translation, foster or enhance class discussions, and various other activities.

**Table 5 Distribution of types of educational activities that teachers engage in with ChatGPT**

[Full size table](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8/tables/5)

Besides the various major activities and tasks for ChatGPT indicated by the survey responses, interviewees also indicated that they used ChatGPT to provide a structure to initiate their course planning. For instance, Eden is a high school teacher in social science who has six to ten years of teaching experience. He claimed that,

> [ChatGPT] helps me form a structure like an outline, and I will fill it in with what I know. So it's really good to help the foundation for what you're going to do with your lesson plans or a lesson plan.

Dan also discussed ways that generative AI can be beneficial in the classroom. In fact, he had just developed and taught new lessons in an anthropology class for the first time. Notably, he used generative AI, both ChatGPT and Google Gemini, to support him in building his anthropology curriculum. Dan showed us an example of how he prompted a task and what answers the generative AI provided. In utilizing Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#Fig1), he asked “Give me an 18-week pacing guide for Anthropology a semester course in high school. I only want the broad topics covered each week, not activities or anything else.” This interaction showed us how participants might use ChatGPT to provide a structure or a starting point for course planning.

### How Does ChatGPT Support Self-Directed Professional Development?

### Motivation

Figure [2](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#Fig2) illustrates the distributions of motivational factors to use ChatGPT for SDPD. A substantial portion of teachers reported strong agreement or agreement with being "I have the motivation to use ChatGPT to improve my teaching practice"(M = 3.87, SD = 1.07),"I want to learn new ideas related to teaching brought up by ChatGPT"(M = 3.94, SD = 1.07) and “I enjoy learning new information related to teaching through ChatGPT"(M = 3.96, SD = 1.07) indicating an intrinsic drive for professional growth and a positive attitude towards acquiring new knowledge. Similarly, many teachers expressed a desire to"learn from mistakes"(M = 3.23, SD = 1.14) suggesting a reflective and improvement-oriented approach. While still largely positive, agreement on"want to understand ChatGPT's feedback"(M = 3.23, SD = 1.27) and “share ChatGPT teaching experiences” (M = 3.34, SD = 1.22) showed slightly more variability, indicating diverse practices in utilizing feedback mechanisms or collaborative sharing."Need to use ChatGPT for improvement"also showed a strong positive trend, suggesting a perceived utility of the tool for enhancing their skills.

[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8/figures/2)

Through in-depth interviews, we found that K-12 schoolteachers’ motivation for SDPD was generally passive due to teaching pressure given by state policy and local conditions; however, the introduction of generative AI tools appeared to change the dynamics of engagement by reducing the initial effort required to participate in SDPD. For instance, Chloé was a high school teacher who had over 15 years of teaching experience. Chloé mentioned that she could not make an SDPD goal because “getting ahead in public school education is really difficult.” She further explained that:

> The only thing you really can do [sic]outside of being a classroom teacher is to go ahead and get a specific degree that allows you to be a principal… This is not my first career, but…it's the only career I've ever had where I feel like there's a ceiling.

While her SDPD motivation was constricted due to the limited room for further advancement in her career, she found the generative AI tool was particularly useful. Chloé started using ChatGPT out of curiosity and later was motivated to use it for PD and teaching practice. She observed, “I think it's really helped me gather information a lot quicker, a lot more effectively weed out the non-reputable sites and spammy sites and blogs and things like that. So that's been really helpful.”

Similarly, Beth, who taught elementary-level reading courses in a home-based educational setting, had difficulty entering the SDPD stage because it was deemed challenging to initiate anything, but ChatGPT provided a starting point for her to think about her teaching and preparing for classes. Beth claimed that:

> I am not self-motivated. I will lay [around], and I know I have my class to do. I need to prepare for my class. My problem is, I cannot get started. I know all I need to do is get a pencil piece, a pencil, and a piece of paper, and I'll write [that] I want to talk about this. If I do that one sentence, I'll be planning my class. I cannot do that. ChatGPT helps me because I can lay on the floor and just say on my phone I need art that does da da da, and it'll spit out a whole paragraph and some details. And I'll be like, oh, okay. And I started looking at it.

Apparently, though various external pressures and individual personality traits may encourage schoolteachers to assume a passive mode for SDPD, ChatGPT with its significant advantage of being convenient and efficient can motivate teachers to participate in the SDPD process.

### Self-Management

Figure [3](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#Fig3) illustrates teachers’ self-management in using ChatGPT for SDPD, A significant majority of teachers reported strongly agreeing or agreeing with organizing activities (M = 4.81, SD = 1.05), managing time (M = 4.70, SD = 1.05), and applying a variety of learning (M = 4.65, SD = 1.07). Such findings suggested participants’ strong practical application and organizational skills. While most teachers also indicated positive engagement with setting PD goals (M = 3.73, SD = 1.26), seeking help (M = 4.95, SD = 1.35), following PD plans (M = 3.94, SD = 1.28), and having high expectations when learning with ChatGPT (M = 4.42, SD = 1.19), there was a slightly broader spread of responses for these items. Such results indicated noticeable variability in responses.

**Fig. 3**

[![]()](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8/figures/3)

Distribution of teachers’ responses to items measuring self-management activities while using ChatGPT to support self-directed professional development

[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8/figures/3)

Interview data reinforced the quantitative findings regarding time management and organization, highlighting ChatGPT's role in improving efficiency. In fact, such time savings may become a key influence or motivator for engaging in SDPD with generative AI. For instance, during her interview, Chloé claimed that ChatGPT can provide more specific answers than other search engines; therefore, she saved many hours in planning. As she observed, “It's really speeding up our thought processes as we plan for the teaching, and so saving time in that respect.” Dan also shared his thoughts on how ChatGPT facilitated SDPD by providing better time management. He explained,

> I think it's a time saver like the biggest thing for me as a teacher. My time is limited, and you know as much as I would love more time to plan my stuff. I find that at the end of the day, I become stressed. I'm exhausted from all the work in the day. So for me, having the AI assist me with building something, or giving me ideas, allows me not to constantly search the Internet for something, but to have something specific, that kind of fits, the use case I need. In other words, so for me, the biggest selling point is, that it saves me time, and it comes up with ideas that you traditionally won't find anywhere else unless you're really really looking for them.

Eden also confirmed that time management advantage when she mentioned,

> [ChatGPT] helped me better utilize my time, so I can take my time and use it on more things in the classroom, because as a teacher preparing your lessons, creating lesson plans is about half the time you have to be in the classroom is a lot… and by using something like ChatGPT, it cuts back on that time so I can work on other projects… So it does free up some time to be able to do other responsibilities and tasks I have in the classroom.

While survey responses for"I set up my personal professional development goals when using ChatGPT for teaching-related activities"showed noticeable variation, with some participants being less proactive, interviews revealed a range of goal-setting behaviors. Some interviewees were not motivated to engage in SDPD due to teaching pressure, and, therefore, they had limited, if any, degree of goal-setting; others participants were more motivated to improve teaching, and, therefore, established goals for SDPD. For example, Eden reflected on her PD goals and processes, and stated that,

> I'm always looking to better be able to write better lesson plans to create better engaging lessons, to teach my students the proper way to use technology, how to grasp critical thinking skills. I'm always looking for opportunities in which I can develop the things I do, and do it better, or learn new skills. I think whenever you're working, wherever field you're going into, if you're always learning, I think you're always staying up, you know, staying above and staying current. Whenever you get to the point where you stop trying to learn or stop trying to understand what you're doing, that's when you start falling behind again, stagnating, and you don't grow.

### Self-Monitoring

As indicated in Fig. [4](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#Fig4), a strong majority of teachers reported strongly agreeing or agreeing with the item, “I am responsible for my learning with ChatGPT for improving teaching” (M = 4.19, SD = 0.89), suggesting that survey participants generally feel a strong sense of ownership for their SDPD when using ChatGPT. There was also a largely positive attitude towards the item “I have high learning standards when I use ChatGPT to improve teaching” (M = 4.04, SD = 1.04), and"I can direct my own learning progress while using ChatGPT to improve teaching"(M = 3.83, SD = 0.96), indicating that participants generally believe they are capable to guide their own PD activities with ChatGPT. Though many teachers indicated agreement with"I review the online teaching materials provided by ChatGPT based on my needs"(M = 3.82, SD = 1.18), there was slightly more spread in responses, which reflected that some participants were more selective and focused on their specific needs than other people."I am not distracted by other online tools and activities (e.g., WhatsApp, Instagram, Facebook, etc.) while using ChatGPT for teaching-related activities"(M = 3.52, SD = 1.38) had a big variation in responses. It suggests that some participants managed to stay focused when using ChatGPT, but other participants struggled with distractions from social media and other online learning tools.

**Fig. 4**

[![]()](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8/figures/4)

Distribution of self-monitoring behaviors during ChatGPT-supported self-directed professional development activities

Interview data further illuminated teachers'active self-monitoring, particularly their continuous reflection and self-assessment of teaching improvements resulting from ChatGPT use. For instance, Beth shared that she struggled to break down a large amount of information into smaller, more manageable parts. However, ChatGPT helped by providing a list of the topics she covered and suggesting an effective way to organize the information. She recollected:

> [ChatGPT] has helped me a lot. I talk a lot, and I don't breathe because I believe that breathing is for amateurs. ChatGPT helps me to break my ideas down into smaller pieces. So I'll talk, for I'll just have this great big, mushy brain dumping paragraph, and ChatGPT will look at it and say,"okay, you talked for 5 min. Here are the 10 things that you talked about,” and I go, “oh”, and so then I can expound on those 10 things. And I can see that [improvement] because I'm [getting] more succinct with the pieces.

Many interviewees also mentioned that they revisited the information provided by ChatGPT and went through a cognitive process to critically review and adapt the ideas to fit their teaching needs and prior experience. Dan, for example, described how he used ChatGPT to prepare materials for his anthology class. He would ask ChatGPT to generate an activity plan and rubric, then adjust them to align with his preferred teaching style and the specific needs of his students. He observed that,

> I refined [the teaching material generated by ChatGPT] based on the parameters I wanted. Like I was specific about like a generated 100 point rubric. And then I had to tweak the rubric. So for me, I use as a multi-step process that if it gives me something and I don't like it, I'll go back in and I'll edit it to. I refine it to what I am exactly looking for, [or at least] close to it.

These findings suggested that teachers generally held a sense of responsibility for their SDPD activities when using ChatGPT; meanwhile, they maintained a selective and need-focused approach when they reviewed the materials provided by generative AI, integrating their prior teaching experience and knowledge of their specific classroom context.

### What are Teachers'Perceptions of ChatGPT's Functionality in Supporting SDPD?

As illustrated in Fig. [5](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#Fig5), participants largely agreed that they critically evaluated the instructional ideas provided by ChatGPT (M = 4.17, SD = 0.95). The results suggested a strong tendency for these teachers to go through some level of metacognitive processing to assess the ideas presented by generative AI. In addition, participant responses revealed agreement that they felt confident when interacting with ChatGPT (M = 4.04, SD = 1.00). Such results suggest that they had a positive attitude towards using the tool with a moderate level of consistency in confidence among survey participants. Survey answers also showed that participants exhibited moderate confidence in the accuracy of the information provided by ChatGPT for educational purposes (M = 3.46, SD = 1.03). The responses to this question had the largest standard deviation indicating that confidence levels were somewhat varied. While some participants trusted the information more than others, participants disclosed a degree of skepticism or caution when engaging ChatGPT in their practice.

**Fig. 5**

[![]()](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8/figures/5)

Participants'perceptions of ChatGPT’s effectiveness in supporting SDPD

Interviewees also indicated that they mostly approached ChatGPT with a critical mindset, assessing the instructional ideas provided by generative AI and feeling confident in their ability to interact with the tool. However, they emphasized that ChatGPT cannot replace human intelligence and placed a higher level of trust in their teaching knowledge accumulated over years. For instance, Beth mentioned,

> While AI is good, you should always depend more on your expertise. AI know[s] a lot, [but] you know more. Never underestimate what you know from experience in learning and your own intuition as a human being and as a teacher. People trump machines every day. Always edit slash, rewrite whatever ChatGPT gives you in your own words. Your inflection, the way you say things are more natural and more understandable to your student. ChatGPT is still a machine and so you have to humanize what it gives you [to] your words. With or without ChatGPT, you're a good teacher. And remember that your teaching is more about your students than anything… it's always a tool. And you are the trained expert.

As indicated, teachers in this study felt there was not enough training and PD support on using AI for PD as well as integrating AI in classrooms. For example, Chloé stated that,

> Professionally, I don't think I've learned to use it to its fullest extent. I still feel a little hesitant, hesitant to use it to just type in, for example, write me a lesson plan on… I want [the training] to be more specific, more high level, not how to log in, and that you can write a prompt, I think… [current training on generative AI] just didn't go as deep as I wanted it to.

In fact, due to the insufficient formal training provided for teachers to use AI for both teaching and learning, alternatively, many teachers had to seek other online resources or consult colleagues for assistance. What the accumulation of these findings illuminates is that school leaders and policymakers could play a significant role in the coming decade in addressing this perceived gap between the potential of generative AI and the training provided to take advantage of it.

Discussion
----------

### Motivation and Affordances in GAI Supported SDPD

Across the various findings detailed above, the present study makes salient that K-12 teachers are highly motivated to use ChatGPT for professional development due to its affordances for providing new teaching ideas that enhance their instructional practices. They effectively managed time, organized their professional development activities, and applied various learning strategies. This proficiency in self-management may be the result of the transferability of self-management skills, which have been practiced and developed through the teachers’ prior experiences with navigating various digital tools for educational purposes. At the same time, the teachers participating in this study also utilized ChatGPT to help structure their courses and modules and provide bases for their lesson planning, which in turn helped them manage their professional tasks more efficiently. Self-monitoring is evident as teachers critically evaluate the instructional ideas provided by ChatGPT and reflect on their learning processes.

GenAI-mediated SDPD offers teachers a more personalized, flexible, and context-sensitive learning experience. As shown in our findings, teachers used ChatGPT not only to generate teaching materials and ideas, but also to scaffold their planning processes, manage their time more effectively, and refine their pedagogical decisions through iterative prompting and reflection. These activities mirror many of the core supports provided in formal OPD such as expert modeling and curated resources (e.g., Bragg et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR6); Dille & Røkenes, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR16)); nevertheless, they are initiated and customized by the teachers themselves. ChatGPT enables just-in-time support that aligns closely with teachers’ immediate instructional needs, serving as informal facilitators for SDPD. Several teachers in our study, such as Dan and Eden, emphasized how GenAI accelerated their planning and reduced cognitive load, the benefits of which were more time-consuming and difficult to access through existing PD structures. Moreover, teachers in our study indicated that generative AI helped lower the entry threshold into SDPD by making it easier to begin tasks that would otherwise be demotivating. This finding is particularly significant when contrasted with conventional PD formats that may feel detached from day-to-day teaching realities. As Alshaikhi ([2020](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR1)) and Lan ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR28)) noted, SDPD is most impactful when it is embedded in practice and motivated by teachers’ own reflections and instructional needs, which is, notably, an immediate condition that AI appears to uniquely fulfill.

Apart from the cognitive benefits of making use of the affordances of ChatGPT for SDPD such as accelerating the thinking process, the findings of the study also revealed affective benefits among participants. Such positive outcomes can be seen in some participants'practical reasons for using ChatGPT for facilitating pedagogical activities, as they experienced burnout in their daily instructional activities and needed mental and emotional space for other responsibilities and tasks needed for their profession. Sims et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR46)) emphasize the importance of motivation in effective PD, where teachers need to be motivated to engage with new practices and integrate them into their teaching. As shown in this study, ChatGPT can serve as a motivational tool by encouraging teachers to experiment with new methods, fostering a more engaged and proactive approach to professional development. These findings may also offer policymakers and teacher training providers valuable insights for incorporating ChatGPT into effective training programs.

### Tensions and Critical Points in GenAI Supported SDPD

While ChatGPT facilitates a high degree of flexibility and access to information, teachers should complement the use with critical reflection. The participants in the current study demonstrated a high level of confidence and comfort in this regard, likely because the majority of them were experienced teachers with extensive knowledge and expertise pertaining to their teaching fields and contexts. However, novice teachers may encounter myriad challenges and perplexities and may be in need of additional support to effectively validate, evaluate, and utilize the outputs from generative AI. This support can be facilitated through continuous learning and collaboration with colleagues (Cochran-Smith et al., [2008](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR12)) to fully leverage the benefits of self-directed professional development. The current study also showed that participants were less inclined to share their generative AI-assisted PD experiences with other colleagues and peers. Such hesitations limited opportunities for enhancing their SDPD through social interactions within their immediate teaching and learning communities. On the one hand, their reluctance may stem from certain negative connotations surrounding the use of generative AI in academic and educational contexts, leading participants to be cautious and reserved about disclosing their use of AI in work (Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR51)). On the other hand, this tendency not to share their uses of generative AI may also have to do with institutional policies and cultures regarding generative AI, which can negatively influence teachers’ willingness to openly share as well as their motivations to form learning communities around generative AI-facilitated PD practices. Institutional contexts also affect teachers’ professional identities and their willingness to collaborate with colleagues on SDPD (Cohen, [2008](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR11); Lefstein et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR30)). Thus, the study emphasizes the importance of institutional support in helping teachers make the most of ChatGPT’s capabilities, aligning their self-directed learning with broader institutional education goals. Furthermore, shared learning experiences regarding SDPD also offer teachers a collaborative space to refine their respective instructional practices over time, thereby providing sustainable and evolving SDPD.

Our findings also expand current concerns raised in the literature about the risks and complexities of integrating GenAI into SDPD. While generative AI tools offer rapid access to pedagogical resources and encourage experimentation, they may also foster surface-level engagement. For instance, although many participants demonstrated strong self-monitoring, some reported challenges with goal-setting or maintaining focus, suggesting that the ease and immediacy of GenAI can sometimes bypass deeper cognitive engagement. Additionally, uneven levels of digital and prompt literacy among teachers may further disadvantage those with limited technological confidence or institutional support. The tendency among participants to rely on ChatGPT for structure and planning also raises important epistemological questions: Where is pedagogical authority located when knowledge is co-constructed with AI systems trained on opaque data sources? These tensions suggest that GenAI not only supports but also reshapes the rhythms and risks of informal professional learning. The future of GenAI-mediated SDPD, therefore, depends not only on access to tools but also on cultivating teachers’ critical AI literacy, enabling them to question, adapt, and responsibly integrate AI outputs into their pedagogical knowledge base.

In the end, this initial study suggests that ChatGPT can support teachers’ SDPD by offering personalized and relevant resources that enable teachers to make meaning of their professional growth and autonomy (Jamal, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR24)), thereby, aligning with the principles of SDL. For optimal use of ChatGPT in SDPD, it is crucial for teachers to develop critical AI literacy and strategies to mitigate distractions and digital burnouts (Duan & Zhao, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11528-025-01123-8#ref-CR18)) and ensure that their engagement with AI tools remains productive, focused, critical, and ethical. Taken together, our findings suggest that GenAI offers distinct added value to teacher learning, such as personalization, efficiency, and embeddedness in context; yet, it also raises new questions about the equity, depth, and long-term development of such self-directed engagement.

### Implications

Findings of this research suggest that generative AI expands the boundaries of SDL by embedding learning within practice that fosters personalized experiences and real-time reflections. As such, these findings blur the line between doing and learning. On the one hand, it brings a new model of SDPD that is more embedded and iterative learning that is shaped by immediate instructional needs and individual motivation. On the other hand, it forms new responsibilities on teachers to cultivate critical AI literacy, reflective mindsets, and collaborative cultures to avoid shallow engagement and isolation. As GenAI continues to embed into educational ecosystems, SDL and SDPD may increasingly emphasize co-agency with AI systems, raising profound questions about autonomy, authenticity, and equity in professional knowledge-building. Therefore, institutions should not only support access to AI tools but should also reimagine their existing structures to scaffold ethical, sustained, and collectively informed teacher learning in an AI-augmented era.

### Limitations and Future Research

As with any study, there are several improvements that can be made in future research. Firstly, a limitation is that, as with many large-scale surveys, participants may interpret Likert items differently depending on their prior experience, context, or familiarity with the tool (i.e., ChatGPT). These variations in interpretation may influence how respondents perceive and rate their engagement. Secondly, the qualitative data was relatively small due to the dropout of participants which restricted the breadth of perspectives captured. Though the depth of the interviews still provided valuable contextual information to explain the quantitative data, future research may benefit from alternative recruitment strategies to stimulate motivation to participate. Secondly, though we collected and analyzed some document data, future studies could employ experimental designs to examine how participants use standardized prompts to make information inquiries related to teaching and then attempt to evidence the follow-up engagement of teachers. Future research may also record the actual teaching practice and impact on students after engaging generative AI for SDPD.

Conclusion
----------

To sum up, ChatGPT supports SDPD in aspects such as pedagogical techniques, ideas, and materials that support teachers in managing the particularities of their teaching context by personalizing the PD experience. Furthermore, such generative AI platform also promotes teachers’ PD by helping them mitigate the workload and emotional labor. The findings of the study suggest that by creating room for flexibility, fostering motivation, alleviating cognitive and emotional burdens, and encouraging critical reflection, ChatGPT facilitates SDPD. However, the teachers in the present study also faced challenges with regard to contextual and institutional constraints. Given the findings of this study, it seems plausible that providing institutional support and encouraging collaboration would allow teachers to more effectively use ChatGPT to enhance their professional growth and instructional practices. It is certain is that generative AI tools and platforms will continue to evolve, and, as they do, so too will previously untested and unexplored avenues for teachers to self-direct their learning of vital skills and competencies in this increasingly online and open teaching and learning age.
