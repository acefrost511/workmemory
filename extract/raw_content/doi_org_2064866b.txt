## Abstract
Peer feedback is crucial for promoting deeper thinking and interaction. However, various challenges exist in conducting high-quality peer feedback in collaborative argumentation learning. Generative AI (GenAI) has shown potential for supporting peer feedback, but how GenAI can be efficiently applied to support peer feedback needs to be further explored. In addition, the effect of GenAI-supported peer feedback on argumentation performance and feedback quality remains unclear. To address these gaps, this study compared three peer feedback conditions: peer feedback (PF), peer feedback with GenAI (GenAI-PF), and peer feedback with GenAI supported by prompt scaffolding (PS-GenAI-PF). Forty-five student teachers were assigned to 12 groups of 3–4 students. All groups were randomly assigned to three peer feedback conditions, each including four groups. Kruskal-Wallis H test results showed that the GenAI-supported peer feedback groups performed better in argumentation performance. In particular, the PS-GenAI PF group performed better on “Rebuttal data and warrant” and “Addressing the opposing view”. For feedback quality, the GenAI-supported peer feedback group showed more explanation, suggestions, as well as neutral and negative feedback. The epistemic network analysis revealed that the PS-GenAI PF group had strong associations with negative emotions and higher-order feedback content, such as identification-suggestion and explanation-suggestion. Implications for the instructional design and implementation of GenAI-supported peer feedback in higher education are discussed.

## Introduction
Peer feedback is a promising student-centered instructional method to enhance learning (Banihashem et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR4)). Based on formative assessment, collaborative learning is promoted by encouraging students to analyze tasks, plan, monitor, and reflect on their behaviors, emotions, and motivations. Students act as both feedback providers and receivers, transitioning from passive participation to active learning (Lahza et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR47)). Peer feedback requires students to clarify their learning goals, think critically about their peers’ ideas, understand the assessment criteria, evaluate others’ perspectives, and provide constructive suggestions. In this process, feedback providers can develop their critical evaluative judgment and ability to make decisions about their own and others’ learning performance.
Collaborative argumentation learning is a process in which students actively share different ideas, negotiate the meaning of opinions and evidence, and revise, synthesize, or reject initial opinions (Noroozi et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR63), [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR64)). It aims to help students learn deeply through critical discussion and reflection. Integrating peer feedback into collaborative argumentation learning can support students in critically examining diverse ideas, understanding and expanding their knowledge and skills from different perspectives, and promoting interaction and reflection among students. By helping students identify and address their cognitive blind spots, the quality of argumentation and critical thinking skills can be enhanced (Chen et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR12); Ma et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR56)).
Although peer feedback can help promote collaborative argumentation learning, there are also a series of challenges in providing high-quality feedback to peers, such as the complexity of argumentation knowledge and structure (Han et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR35); Noroozi et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR65)), the high-order cognitive demands of evaluation and feedback (Dawson et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR18)), and students’ lack of self-regulation skills (Narciss et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR59); Tam, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR75)). The rise and development of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) has sparked interest in its potential applications in education, and peer feedback supported by GenAI is a particular focus (Noroozi et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR62); Ba et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR3); Usher, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR84)). The conversational interaction between students and GenAI introduces new perspectives to peer feedback. However, when applying GenAI tools, students show cognitive offloading phenomenon of relying on GenAI feedback content. This further emphasizes the need for effective scaffolding to facilitate GenAI-supported peer feedback (Lahza et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR47)).
Scaffolding is widely recognized in the literature in the form of structured instructions for peer feedback (Kerman et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR43)). Such scaffolding provides learners with guidance and support that help them navigate complex tasks and promote learning performance (Reiser, [2004](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR71); Zheng et al., [2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR109)). Prompts serve as a critical bridge for effective interaction between students and GenAI (Lee & Palmer, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR48)). The quality of students’ prompts largely determines the effectiveness of peer feedback (Noroozi et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR62)). In this regard, prompt scaffolding can be used to mediate learners’ interaction with generative AI to improve peer feedback quality. Therefore, the introduction of a prompt scaffolding that supports students’ dialogue with GenAI can be considered to help students engage in cognitive and metacognitive processes in peer feedback. However, there is currently limited empirical research on GenAI-supported peer feedback in collaborative argumentation learning. It is still unclear how to fully utilize GenAI to support peer feedback. Therefore, how to help students use GenAI to support peer feedback learning and improve feedback quality and argumentation performance requires more in-depth exploration.
## Literature review
### Collaborative argumentation learning
Argumentation is an important aspect of scientific thinking and reasoning (Kuhn et al., [2008](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR46)). The theory of dialogue emphasizes that argumentation is a key step in the dialogue process, where supporters and opponents engage in persuasion, inquiry, negotiation, and information seeking (Walton, [2000](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR87), [2006](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR88)). Argumentation, therefore, aims to resolve differences of opinion and issues rationally through critical dialogue (Jonassen & Kim, [2010](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR42)). In educational research, argumentation learning is further conceptualized as a structured reasoning practice in which learners construct and evaluate knowledge claims through the coordinated use of claims, evidence, and warrants, while actively engaging with alternative or opposing viewpoints (Erduran et al., [2004](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR22); Berland & McNeill, [2010](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR6)). Collaborative argumentation learning emphasizes students’ engagement in argument exchange, reasoning, and meaning negotiation, through which they collaboratively construct knowledge by sharing and interpreting perspectives across a sequence of discourse moves (Noroozi et al., [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR65), [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR63), [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR64); Han et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR35)). Previous studies have shown that Collaborative argumentation learning effectively promotes knowledge construction (Noroozi et al., [2013](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR66); Han et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR35)) and domain-specific knowledge learning (Wecker & Fischer, [2014](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR90)), enhances argumentative logic and structure, and fosters critical thinking ability for tackling complex problems and generating creative solutions (Chen et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR11)).
According to Toulmin’s Argument Pattern, an argument is composed of several key elements, including a claim that states a position or conclusion, data (or evidence) that support the claim, and a warrant that explains the logical connection between the data and the claim. In more elaborated arguments, additional components such as backing, which provides further justification for the warrant, and rebuttals, which address potential counterarguments or conditions under which the claim may not hold, are also included (Toulmin, [1958](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR82); Erduran et al., [2004](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR22)). Prior studies emphasize that high-quality argumentation involves not only producing claims supported by evidence, but also articulating warrants that justify the connection between evidence and claims, as well as generating rebuttals that respond to counterarguments (Mühlen et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR86); Turós et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR83)). Notably, the presence and nature of rebuttals have been widely recognized as a critical indicator of argumentation quality, as they reflect learners’ ability to understand opposing views and respond to them through reasoned justification, representing a more advanced level of argumentative ability (Erduran et al., [2004](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR22); Rapanta & Macagno, [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR69)).
However, students often have difficulty producing high-quality arguments containing essential elements such as claims, evidence, and reasoning. Students often have difficulty expressing different opinions on a discussion topic and often avoid offering a rebuttal to their peers’ arguments. Students may view criticism and rebuttals as personal attacks rather than constructive feedback, and tend to support their ideas rather than present counterarguments to opposing views. Furthermore, students rarely respond to others’ ideas and tend to repeat ideas that others have already constructed. To address the aforementioned difficulties and challenges in collaborative argumentation learning, it is essential to utilize artificial technology to enhance instruction design, thereby promoting collaborative argumentation learning. Peer feedback involves social interaction among peers and goes beyond simple information sharing to critically evaluate collaborative sensemaking and learning performance (Nicol, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR61)). An exploratory classroom study found that groups with greater improvement after peer feedback tended to give and receive feedback that emphasized specific solutions and overall argument-level issues, which in turn was associated with enhanced argumentation performance following peer feedback activities (Chen et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR11)). Therefore, incorporating peer feedback into collaborative argumentation learning may be beneficial for improving the argumentation performance.
### Peer feedback
Feedback is acknowledged as one of the most important tools for facilitating learning (Banihashem et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR4)). A wide range of studies have shown that feedback has a positive impact on various aspects of the student learning (Butler & Winne, [1995](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR7); Callender et al., [2016](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR8); Zhang & Hyland, [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR107); Amiryousefi & Geld, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR2); Gan et al., [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR27); Banihashem et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR4)). Feedback is information provided by an agent (e.g., teachers, peers, self, experience) about aspects of one’s performance or understanding (Hattie & Timplerely, [2007](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR36)). Feedback can be provided not only by educators but also by student peers (Day et al., [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR67)). Viberg et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR85)) found that peer feedback has a positive effect on the perception, acquisition, and social interaction of knowledge skills compared to teacher feedback. Peer-feedback broadens assessment practices by requiring learners to reflect on and critique both their own and peers’ work, engaging them in metacognitive evaluation based on predetermined criteria (Tan & Chen, [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR76)).
Peer feedback is often recognised as an important collaborative learning process with the potential to influence learning (Hattie & Timperley, [2007](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR36); Kollar & Fischer, [2010](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR44); Chen et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR11)). Peer feedback provides various advantages when students take on the roles of both feedback receivers and providers (Gao et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR28); Noroozi et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR62)). Feedback from peers can promote self-regulation (Fernández-Ferrer et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR24)) and critical thinking (Filius et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR25); Zhan, [2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR101); Jiang et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR41)), facilitate improvement in conceptual knowledge (Tan et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR77)), and improve writing performance (Liu et al., [2018](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR53); Guo, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR31)). Providing feedback by evaluating the artifacts of peers can stimulate students to reflect and express their ideas (Van Popta et al., [2017](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR110); Tan et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR77)). As the quality of peer feedback improves, students may perceive peer feedback as increasingly positive (Huisman et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR38)). The process of peer evaluation and feedback can facilitate deeper thinking and interaction through critical thinking, planning, monitoring, and regulation (Liu et al., [2001](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR52); Filius et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR25)).
Extensive studies have demonstrated that peer feedback can significantly enhance the quality of feedback comments (Guo et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR32)), which in turn influences students’ learning performance (Huang et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR37); Ba et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR3)). Prior research has identified peer feedback quality through various multidimensional typologies. In a comprehensive review by Gao et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR28)), peer feedback in higher education is typically categorized into three types: cognitive, affective, and metacognitive content. Specifically, cognitive feedback pertains to knowledge-related content, highlighting problems, explanations, and suggestions (Lu & Law, [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR55)). Affective feedback (e.g., in the form of text messages) conveys emotions through positive or negative appraisals (Chen et al., [2015](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR10)). Other scholars have proposed alternative frameworks. For instance, Chien et al. ([2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR13)) identified four feedback types: praise, criticism, opinion, and irrelevance. Guo ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR31)) evaluated feedback quality across five specific dimensions: affect, description, identification, justification, and recommendation. Specifically, affect refers to evaluative comments that convey the reviewer’s emotional stance. Description involves summarizing textual content to ensure interpretive alignment. Identification focuses on pinpointing the specific location of strengths or weaknesses. Justification provides the evidentiary or logical rationale for evaluations to enhance credibility. Finally, recommendation offers concrete strategies or actionable guidance for future revisions. Notably, justification and recommendation are often regarded as core indicators of high-quality, constructive feedback (Kerman et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR43); Guo et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR32)). Collectively, these dimensions constitute a comprehensive framework for objectively evaluating the quality and constructive nature of peer feedback.
Peer feedback is complex or potentially informative, involving the interaction of multiple elements such as feedback sources, feedback content, and student characteristics (Gao et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR28)). Producing high-quality peer feedback in collaborative argumentation faces significant challenges. For complex tasks of collaborative argumentation, students are unable to provide high-quality peer feedback when they do not have argumentation-specific knowledge, cannot provide peer feedback effectively, or have expert argumentation knowledge (Panadero & Lipnevich, [2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR68)). Additionally, students may not take tasks critically or fully engage in peer feedback (McConlogue, [2015](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR57)). Students are more likely to question peer feedback than feedback provided by the teacher. And interestingly, it is precisely because the feedback is provided by peers that it appears to stimulate deep reflection and critical thinking (Filius et al., [2019](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR25)). Existing studies have supported student peer feedback through training, example demonstrations, and other methods. However, most of the training is brief and insufficiently detailed, and exhaustive and intensive peer feedback training can be overwhelming for students. In addition, students receive little immediate and timely support when engaging in peer feedback activities (Guo et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR32)).
Intelligent technology plays an important role in supporting peer feedback. GenAI can provide timely, specific, comprehensive, and constructive insights to enhance the learning experience by expanding personalized support, enriching learning materials, enabling timely feedback, and innovative assessment methods (Yan et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR96); Rawas, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR70); Zhang et al., [2026](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR103)). AI assessment and monitoring will serve as a valuable resource to motivate students to share and discuss their judgements, providing them with ongoing opportunities to improve their ability to make informed decisions (Guo et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR32); Zhan & Yan, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR102)). GenAI can provide feedback of higher quality than that offered by peers by delivering concrete, detailed, and actionable improvement suggestions. However, such feedback may occasionally include irrelevant or internally inconsistent information that requires students’ critical judgement (Usher, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR84)). Consequently, students need to engage interactively with GenAI to construct “internal feedback” that actively empowers their learning processes (Tam, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR75)).
Peer feedback is not a one-sided process that includes only student-to-student interactions, but rather involves a dynamic interplay of various sources of feedback. A combination of peer feedback and other feedback can improve feedback quality and enhance learning experience (Noroozi et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR62); Gao et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR28)). Students engage in collaborative and self-reflective processes that are enhanced by technology to enrich the learning experience and improve the quality of peer feedback provided and applied (Gao et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR28); Gu et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR30)). However, fewer studies explore how GenAI can scaffold peer feedback processes to enhance feedback quality and optimize collaborative argumentation learning.
### Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI)
Artificial intelligence can provide real-time, personalized feedback and guidance that can promote positive attitudes and increase students’ interest and engagement. This trend is resonating in the education field. Topping et al. ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR81)) conducted a scoping review of AI-enhanced peer evaluations and found a positive impact of AI in enhancing individual evaluations and optimising peer evaluations. GenAI tools such as ChatGPT, Bing Chat, or ERNIE Bot offer a supportive, low-pressure environment that enables students to receive and engage with feedback without fear of negative judgment (Ait Baha et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR1); Corbin et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR16)). GenAI has contextual understanding, text processing capabilities, and shows potential in providing immediate feedback to enhance learning (Fan et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR23); Dai et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR17)). It has been found that ChatGPT provides more detailed feedback on descriptive problem identification compared to peer feedback. ChatGPT-generated affective feedback showed a positive correlation with argumentation writing quality (Banihashem et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR4)). GenAI-enabled solutions can be effective in providing feedback, and both GenAI feedback and teacher feedback are positively perceived by students (Er et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR21)). AI-enabled feedback and feed-forward methods can significantly improve collaborative knowledge building, co-regulated behavior, and group performance (Zheng et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR108)). Minh ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR58)) further found that the application of GenAI not only increased the depth of students’ arguments but also improved the structure and complexity of their argumentation.
Student interaction with CenAI promotes deeper engagement, perspective generation, and critical thinking development. The process of student dialogue with the GPT enables students to analyze and evaluate information, identify valid arguments, and examine information with a critical perspective (Suriano et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR73)). Topping et al. ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR81)) emphasized the need for students to combine their assessment judgment with feedback from the GenAI. Hybrid intelligence highlighted combining the strengths of humans and machines through a co-evolutionary process to collaborate, learn, and enhance each other (Järvelä et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR40)). Gao et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR28)) proposed that the combination of AI and human feedback can further improve the quality of peer feedback. Therefore, incorporating GenAI into peer feedback activities minimizes the likelihood of low-quality peer feedback and increases the validity and credibility of peer assessment and feedback (Guo, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR31)).
However, passive use of ChatGPT may displace thought processes by fostering a cognitive dependency (Wu et al. [2025a](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR91), [b](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR92); Zhang and Tur [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR105); Lee and Wu [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR49)). Individuals who use ChatGPT may be inclined not to actively participate in cognitive processing (Suriano et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR73)). A study by Krupp et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR45)) showed that many students accept inaccurate answers and simply copy and paste without critically evaluating the information. This highlights the importance of empowering students to use AI responsibly. In addition, despite GenAI’s powerful content generation and response capabilities, students are often confused when asking GenAI questions and do not know how to organize their language and describe the question to ensure that their intentions are accurately understood by the large language model (Zhan & Yan, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR102)). Students’ questioning skills and the precision of the prompts are key factors that affect the quality of students’ interactions with GenAI (Tlili et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR80)). Teachers should therefore consider how they can support students to actively apply GenAI (Fan et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR23)), improve the quality of peer feedback, and collaborative argumentative learning.
Structured prompts can transform AI from answer provider to learning facilitator (Lee & Wu, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR49)). The study by Xu et al. ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR95)) found that metacognitive cues can enhance students’ self-regulation abilities and optimize their learning experience. Script theory has proposed that an external script can be defined as a form of representation that triggers internal script that facilitates dialogue between peers, students, and experts or GenAI (Fischer et al., [2007](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR26)). The use of external scripts is crucial in peer feedback (Kollar & Fischer, [2010](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR44)). And it has been suggested that more instruction needs to be considered to support students in providing peer feedback (Yu & Schunn, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR100)). In particular, in GenAI-supported peer feedback learning, students lacked the knowledge and experience to interact efficiently with GenAI to provide high-quality argumentation feedback.
In the context of GenAI-supported peer feedback learning, prompt scaffolding can help students with cognitive and metacognitive monitoring and regulation while interacting with GenAI to support peer feedback. Prompts are an effective strategy for supporting students to engage in high-quality questioning and dialogue with GenAI (Bearman et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR5); Zhan & Yan, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR102)), such as the ICIO framework (Giray, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR29)), the CLEAR framework (Lo, [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR54)), and the CO-STAR framework. As well as Taylor ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR78)) proposed five pillars of prompt design, including examples, directions, parameters, format, and links. Comprehensively, the prompt scaffolding should include elements such as context, objective, role and rule, example, etc., which also provides a basis for the design of the peer feedback prompt scaffolding in collaborative argumentation learning. However, empirical evidence remains scarce regarding GenAI-supported peer feedback. There is a lack of in-depth understanding of how GenAI-supported peer feedback improves the quality of peer feedback and the effectiveness of collaborative argumentation learning.
### The present study
Building on prior research highlighting the importance of peer feedback in collaborative argumentation learning (Chen et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR12), [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR11)), there remains limited empirical evidence on how generative AI (GenAI) can be systematically leveraged to support peer feedback, particularly through scaffolded human–AI dialogue. Despite the potential of GenAI to provide immediate and personalized support through prompt–response interactions, few studies have examined whether and how prompt scaffolding can guide students’ effective use of GenAI to enhance feedback quality and argumentation performance. To address these gaps, we designed and implemented four rounds of collaborative argumentation learning activities in higher education across four distinct argumentation topics to explore the feedback quality and argumentation performance under varying peer feedback conditions. The research framework of this study is shown in Fig. [1](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#Fig1).

The research framework of this study
In this research framework, the effectiveness of peer feedback in collaborative argumentation learning was examined by considering the presence of generative AI (GenAI) support and prompt scaffolding. Accordingly, three peer feedback conditions were designed: peer feedback (PF), peer feedback with GenAI (GenAI-PF), and peer feedback with GenAI supported by prompt scaffolding (PS-GenAI-PF). To capture the effect of these different peer feedback conditions, this study focused on two key aspects: argumentation performance and feedback quality.
First, argumentation performance was analyzed from two complementary dimensions: argument structure and argument quality. Argument structure refers to the extent to which students’ collaborative argumentation artifacts contained essential structural components of a well-formed argument. Argument quality concerns the overall effectiveness of the argumentation, focusing on how well arguments were organized, sufficiently developed, clear, elaborated, relevant, persuasive, and whether opposing views were adequately addressed. Second, feedback quality was examined from the perspectives of feedback elements and feedback affective. Feedback elements describe the functional components of peer feedback, including descriptive comments, identification of problems, explanations of underlying issues, and suggestions for improvement. This dimension reflects the cognitive depth and usefulness of peer feedback for supporting argument revision. Feedback affective capture the emotional tone conveyed in peer feedback, including positive, negative, and neutral expressions, which indicate the interpersonal and motivational aspects of the feedback process. By integrating these dimensions, the present study aimed to systematically examine how different conditions of peer feedback influence both students’ collaborative argumentation performance and the feedback quality. Accordingly, the following research questions were proposed:
1. 1.
How does collaborative argumentation performance differ across the PF, GenAI-PF, and PS-GenAI-PF?
2. 2.
How does feedback quality differ across the PF, GenAI-PF, and PS-GenAI-PF?
## Methods
### Research context and participants
The study was conducted in a 16-week teacher education course called ‘Information Technology and Curriculum Integration’ at a university in central China. The course focused on theories, methods, technology, and practical examples of integrating information technology with the curriculum. The aim is to improve pre-service teachers’ information technology teaching literacy and competence, and to learn how to integrate technology into future education. During the curriculum, students were asked to engage in collaborative argumentation discussions around cutting-edge topics in information technology education and to submit an argumentation report on their learning outcomes as required by the assignment.
Forty-five students participated in the study, including 16 male and 29 female students. The participants were third-year student teachers majoring in Educational Technology. All students had previously completed foundational coursework on learning and teaching theories, and had applied information technology tools for collaborative learning during their first and second years of study. The GenAI tool adopted in this study (ERNIE Bot) was accessible to all students. Prior to the study, students had gained experience using GenAI tools to support learning tasks in earlier professional courses. Therefore, all students were familiar with the application of GenAI tools.
### Collaborative environment and learning tools
A total of three online collaboration platforms and tools were used in this study. One of them was the social media platform Tencent QQ, and the other two were the Tencent meeting platform and the Boardmix collaboration platform. QQ provided functions such as organization and management of collaborative argumentation activities, information sharing and exchange, literature sharing and storage, and task planning and scheduling. QQ served as a supportive environment for group collaborative argumentation, assisting students in all phases of collaborative argumentation learning. In this study, a total of 12 Tencent QQ groups were established, and the QQ group interface of the fourth group was shown in Fig. [2](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#Fig2).

In addition, the GenAI-supported peer feedback group (GenAI-PF groups and PS-GenAI-PF groups) used the ERNIE Bot tool () to assist with intergroup peer feedback on the collaborative argumentation artifacts. The GenAI served as a conversational partner that enriched the feedback discourse, expanded students’ reasoning space, and prompted them to reflect on the quality of their peers’ collaborative argumentation artifacts. In this study, students interacted with the ERNIE Bot tool primarily by posing questions, requesting alternative claims, seeking elaborations, and asking for evaluative comments on peers’ argumentation artifacts. Through this dialogic interaction, the ERNIE Bot tool played the role of a cognitive and evaluative scaffold that supported students in identifying argument weaknesses, deepening conceptual understanding, examining opposing claims, and refining their feedback ideas.
Specifically, the ERNIE Bot tool assisted students in (a) evaluating the structure and coherence of the argument, (b) analyzing the sufficiency and relevance of evidence, (c) identifying potential counterarguments or missing warrants, and (d) generating constructive suggestions for improvement. Students typically initiated their interaction by inputting summaries or excerpts of peers’ arguments into the ERNIE Bot tool and then posing prompts such as “evaluate the argument from X perspective,” “identify missing evidence,” or “suggest improvements.” The ERNIE Bot tool’s responses provided elaborated comments that students subsequently reviewed, filtered, and integrated into the final feedback report with their own critical judgement. ### Research design
The study included three different peer feedback conditions: peer feedback (PF group), peer feedback with GenAI (GenAI-PF group), and peer feedback with GenAI supported by prompt scaffolding (PS-GenAI PF group). Participants were randomly assigned to three peer feedback conditions, each including four groups. The groups engaged in four rounds of collaborative argumentation learning based on the four topics, with different peer feedback conditions occurring in both the intergroup evaluation and feedback and intragroup improvement phases. All groups used the 5-point assessment rubric to evaluate collaborative argumentation artifacts, which included different aspects of argumentation quality: organisation, sufficiency, clarity, elaboration, relevance, persuasiveness, and addressing the opposing view (Guo et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR34)) (see Appendix [A](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#Sec22)).
The PF group discussed the collaborative argumentation artifacts of the mutual evaluation group based on the Tencent meeting during the intergroup evaluation and feedback phase. During the group discussion, students scored and wrote feedback reports based on the assessment rubric. In the group improvement phase, the Tencent meeting was used to discuss and revise the group’s collaborative argumentation artifact according to the feedback reports received. Compared to the PF group, the GenAI-PF group and the PS-GenAI PF group were allowed to dialogue with the ERNIE Bot tool during the peer feedback to facilitate the completion of the feedback report. One week before the collaborative argumentation activity, students in both groups participated in a brief online training on the usage guidelines of the GenAI tool, lasting about 10 min. During the training, the instructor introduced the role and functions of GenAI in peer feedback, demonstrated cases of GenAI-supported feedback, and reminded students not to rely exclusively on GenAI-generated content. No explicit instruction on prompt construction or prompt engineering was provided to the GenAI-PF group. While the GenAI-PF group generated their own prompts for interaction, the PS-GenAI-PF group received prompt scaffolding to guide the formulation of queries and evaluations, which further shaped the nature of their interaction with the GenAI tool.
Prompts are the language used by the students in dialogue with the GenAI to guide the GenAI in generating a specific output or performing a specific task. Combined with the task of peer feedback in collaborative argumentation learning, the study designed the prompt scaffolding for peer feedback based on the elements of context, objective, and role in the prompt model (see Appendix [B](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#Sec23)). Specifically, the context included argument content, background knowledge, and situations that require GenAI-assisted evaluation. Objective referred to the tasks expected to be performed by GenAI, including evaluation of the quality of support arguments, support arguments, rebuttal arguments, and counterarguments, and suggestions for modification (Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR89)). For example, ‘Please evaluate how well the above argumentation supports the arguments in favour of a particular topic, and what suggestions do you have to better provide reasons and evidence in support of the position’. Alternatively, roles (e.g., identity, approvers, detractors) could be added to allow the large language model to conduct thought experiments. For example, set roles for GenAI, ‘You are the student that supports the positive side of the argument’, ‘You work as an education policy maker’, and ‘You work as a teacher who advocates for online education’. Example of students’ interaction with GenAI is shown in Appendix [B](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#Sec23). To ensure effective implementation of the GenAI tool to support peer feedback, the study required GenAI-PF group and PS-GenAI PF group to apply the ERNIE Bot tool at least three times during the collaborative discussion process and to record videos of the student intergroup evaluations and feedback and the intragroup improvement phase of the collaborative argumentation learning.
### Research procedure
As shown in Fig. [5](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#Fig5), participants were required to complete the following phases of learning: pre-test, training, phase 1-collaborative ideation, phase 2-Inter-group evaluation and feedback, phase 3-Intra-group improvement and Interview. Before collaborative argumentation learning, students were asked to complete a test of a priori experience with argumentation and task motivation. Two teaching assistants calculated the pre-test scores and grouped the students, with each group including one high-level student, one or two intermediate-level students, and one low-level student. The aim was to ensure that the levels of argumentative prior experience and task motivation were comparable between the groups and to achieve heterogeneity within and homogeneity between groups. All students were divided into 12 groups of 3–4 students each. The researcher randomly assigned the 12 groups to three peer feedback conditions, including four PF groups, four GenAI-PF groups, and four PS-GenAI PF groups.
\*\*Fig. 5\*\*
![](//media.springernature.com/lw685/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs41239-026-00586-w/MediaObjects/41239\_2026\_586\_Fig5\_HTML.png)
[Full size image](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w/figures/5)
Experimental procedure of this study
The teaching assistants created QQ chatrooms for each group individually, introduced the tasks and schedule of collaborative argumentation learning, provided training on collaborative argumentation case materials, tools, and feedback rubric. And all students participated in the training. Teachers designed four argumentation topics based on the course content: Topic 1 was ‘Can online education replace traditional face-to-face teaching?’; Topic 2 was ‘Are digital educational resources narrowing the education gap or widening the education divide?’; Topic 3 was ‘Can MOOCs replace traditional university education in university education?’; Topic 4 was ‘Is the flipped classroom model suitable for teaching all subjects (basic education)?’. Each argumentation topic lasted for a two-week period and was conducted in the form of collaborative argumentation discussions rather than confrontational debates.
The collaborative argumentation discussions consisted of three stages, namely, collaborative ideation, inter-group evaluation and feedback, and intra-group improvement. Firstly, in the collaborative ideation phase, group members shared ideas, discussed and consolidated all the different ideas, integrated high-quality group ideas, and collaborated on the artifacts based on the Boardmix collaboration platform. In the intergroup evaluation and feedback stage, students in each group based on the Tencent meeting platform jointly exchanged and discussed the argumentation content of the mutual evaluation group, expressed their opinions, and evaluated the collaborative argumentation artifacts of other groups. Students scored other groups’ collaborative argumentation artifacts based on the argumentation assessment rubric and wrote an intergroup evaluation feedback report. In the intra-group improvement stage, each group engaged in argumentation discussions based on the feedback reports received, reflecting together on the strengths and weaknesses of the group’s arguments, rationale and evidence, and revising and improving the group’s collaborative argumentation artifacts.
After the collaborative argumentation learning, the researcher conducted a semi-structured interview to collect their feedback about the perceptions of GenAI-supported peer feedback and how they leverage the GenAI to supported peer feedback in their collaborative argumentation learning context.

Figure [6](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#Fig6) illustrates the process of data collection and analysis. To address research question 1, the study collected 96 collaborative argumentation artifacts in two versions: before intergroup evaluation and feedback, and after intragroup improvement.

![](//media.springernature.com/lw685/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs41239-026-00586-w/MediaObjects/41239\_2026\_586\_Fig6\_HTML.png)
[Full size image](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w/figures/6)
The analytical framework
The groups’ collaborative argumentation artifacts were analyzed in terms of argument structure and argument quality, respectively (see Appendix [C](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#Sec25)). For the argumentation structure, we referenced the coding framework proposed by Chuang and Yan ([2022](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR15)) and Guo et al. ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR34)) based on the Toulmin model. The analytical framework consisted of Claim, Data and warrant, Rebuttal claim, and Rebuttal data and warrant, which captures key structural components of argumentation and has been widely applied to examine the quality of students’ argumentation artifacts. The units of analysis were excerpts that express a complete idea or independent content, and were analyzed for argumentation structure by calculating frequency scores for the elements of the argument. For example, the claim scores represented the number of claims in the group’s argumentation artifact. For argumentation quality analysis, we used the argumentation quality rating scale proposed by el Majidi et al. ([2021](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR19)) and Guo et al. ([2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR34)), which included seven dimensions: organisation, sufficiency, clarity, elaboration, relevance, persuasiveness, and addressing the opposing view, with each dimension being a 5-point rating scale.
To ensure coding reliability, two researchers scored 24 argumentation artifacts (25% of the data). The two coders were members of the research team who had been involved in the design and implementation of the collaborative argumentation activities and were familiar with the argumentation tasks and learning context. Prior to formal coding, both coders received joint training on the Argumentation performance analytical framework and reached a shared understanding of the coding criteria and categories. The artifacts were selected using stratified random sampling, with eight artifacts selected from each peer feedback condition, with two artifacts drawn from different groups within each condition and covering two randomly selected topics. To reduce potential bias, the coders conducted the analysis independently using a back-to-back coding procedure and were blinded to the peer feedback conditions during coding. Any disagreements were resolved through discussion until consensus was reached.
For the argumentation structure dimension, the consistency coefficient was calculated using the Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) to reach 0.899, with \*p\* = 0.000 < 0.05, which was a good consistency. The ICC is between 0.7 and 0.9, which can be considered to have a high level of consistency. To ensure inter-rater reliability for the argumentation quality dimension, reliability scores were calculated using Cronbach’s alpha, which was 0.890. It is generally accepted that a Cronbach’s alpha greater than 0.7 indicates good internal consistency. Then, inconsistencies were discussed to reach consensus. Each of the two researchers independently scored one-half of the remaining argumentation artifacts. Based on the results of the analysis of the two dimensions of structural complexity and argumentation quality, the gains of collaborative argumentation artifacts before and after the intergroup evaluation and feedback activities was calculated to evaluate the argumentation performance of each group, which was calculated by the following formula.
$$\text{S}\text{C}= \left(\sum\limits\\_{i=1}^{4}F2-\sum\limits\\_{i=1}^{4}F1 \right)/\sum\limits\\_{i=1}^{4}F1$$
$$\text{A}\text{Q}=\left(\sum\limits\\_{i=1}^{7}R2-\sum\limits\\_{i=1}^{7}R1 \right)/\sum\limits\\_{i=1}^{7}R1$$
The above formula quantifies argumentation performance through relative rather than absolute change. First, SC denotes structural complexity, and F1 and F2 represent the frequency of individual dimensions of structural complexity before and after peer feedback. The numerator is the difference between the sum of the frequencies of individual dimensions of the structural complexity after and before peer feedback. The denominator is the sum of the frequencies of the individual dimensions of the structural complexity before peer feedback. Second, AQ represents argument quality, and R1 and R2 represent the scores of individual dimensions of argument quality before and after peer feedback. The numerator is the difference between the sum of the scores of individual dimensions of the argument quality after and before peer feedback. The denominator is the sum of the scores of individual dimensions of the argument quality before peer feedback.
Firstly, the Shapiro-Wilk test was used to test the normal distribution of the data, and the results showed that the dimensions of argumentation performance were less than 0.05, and the assumption of normal distribution was rejected. Therefore, the study employed the Kruskal-Wallis H test for non-parametric comparison and analysis. The Kruskal-Wallis H test was then performed using SPSS to calculate whether there were statistically significant differences in all dimensions of argumentation performance among the PF groups, GenAI-PF groups, and PS-GenAI PF groups.
To address research question 2, the study collected feedback reports during the intergroup evaluation and feedback phase, for a total of 48 feedback reports. Referring to the feedback quality analysis framework adopted in the studies of Wu and Schunn ([2020](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR93)) and Banihashem et al. ([2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR4)), this study analyzed the quality of feedback reports in terms of the dimensions of feedback elements and affective. Among them, feedback elements included description, identification, justification, and suggestion; feedback affective included positive, negative, and neutral. And the detailed coding framework is shown in Table [1](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#Tab1).
\*\*Table 1 The coding framework for feedback quality\*\*
[Full size table](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w/tables/1)
Each feedback report was analyzed by coding complete meaning units as the unit of analysis, resulting in a total of 756 meaning units. Of these, 155 meaning units were in the PFgroup group, 277 meaning units in the GenAI-PF group, and 324 meaning units in the PS-GenAI PF group. To ensure coding reliability, two researchers coded 189 meaning units from 16 feedback reports, representing 25% of the total data. Cohen’s kappa was then used to calculate the consistency coefficients for each dimension of feedback quality separately. The consistency scores for each dimension were as follows: the consistency coefficient for the feedback elements was 0.848, and the consistency coefficient for the feedback affective was 0.900. Discussions were held to reach consensus in response to inconsistencies. Next, two researchers independently coded one-half of the remaining feedback reports. Finally, epistemic network analysis (ENA) was used to compare the structural characteristics and variability of the feedback quality between groups in the three experimental conditions.
The research collected the interview data to examine students’ ways of using GenAI to support peer feedback and their perceptions of this experience. We conducted a semi-structured interview with four questions (see Appendix [D](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#Sec26)) to gain deeper insights into how students perceived and enacted GenAI-supported peer feedback in practice. Two students were randomly selected from each group, resulting in a total of eight students from each condition participating in the semi-structured interviews. Individual one-on-one interviews were conducted, with each interview lasting approximately 20 min. All interviews were video-recorded and subsequently transferred to text format. The interview transcripts were then analyzed using a thematic analysis, with all interview data systematically coded and managed using the qualitative data analysis software \*ATLAS.ti\*.
## Results
### Argumentation performance
As shown in Table [2](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#Tab2), for the structural complexity, the results showed a significant difference between the three groups (H = 9.66, \*p\* = 0.008 < 0.05, ε2 = 0.17). The PS-GenAI PF group had the highest score on the structural complexity (Mean = 0.20, SD = 0.16), the GenAI-PF group had the second highest (Mean = 0.15, SD = 0.26), and the PF group had the lowest (Mean = 0.13, SD = 0.37). Pairwise comparisons showed that PS-GenAI PF group was significantly higher than PF group, \*p\* = 0.006 < 0.05. In terms of the elements of structural complexity, there was a significant difference between the scores of the three groups on the rebuttal data and warrant (H = 8.41, \*p\* = 0.015 < 0.05, ε2 = 0.14), with the PS-GenAI PF group (Mean = 0.49, SD = 0.80) and the GenAI-PF group (Mean = 0.49, SD = 1.38) scored higher than the PF group (Mean = 0.21, SD = 0.53). Pairwise comparisons showed that the PS-GenAI PF group scored significantly higher than the PF group for the rebuttal data and warrant, \*p\* = 0.016 < 0.05. However, the three groups did not differ significantly in the other three elements of structural complexity: claim (H = 1.04, \*p\* = 0.596, ε2 =-0.02), data and warrant (H = 5.74, \*p\* = 0.057, ε2 = 0.08), and rebuttal claim (H = 4.20, \*p\* = 0.123, ε2 = 0.05).
For argument quality, there was a significant difference between the three groups (H = 9.69, \*p\* = 0.008 < 0.05, ε2 = 0.17), with the PS-GenAI PF group scored the highest for argument quality (Mean = 0.16, SD = 0.10), the GenAI-PF group the next highest (Mean = 0.12, SD = 0.16), and the PF group had the lowest (Mean = 0.07, SD = 0.09). Pairwise comparisons showed that the PS-GenAI PF group had significantly higher argument quality than the PF group, \*p\* = 0.007 < 0.05. There were significant differences between the three groups on three aspects of argument quality: sufficiency (H = 6.42, \*p\* = 0.040 < 0.05, ε2 = 0.10), elaboration (H = 7.61, \*p\* = 0.022 < 0.05, ε2 = 0.12), and addressing the opposing view (H = 9.44, \*p\* = 0.009 < 0.05, ε2 = 0.17). Pairwise comparisons showed that elaboration was significantly higher in the GenAI-PF group (Mean = 0.32, SD = 0.32) than in the PF group (Mean = 0.08, SD = 0.18), \*p\* = 0.026 < 0.05. The PS-GenAI PF group (Mean = 0.25, SD = 0.25) scored significantly higher than the PF group (Mean = 0.08, SD = 0.18) in addressing the opposing view scores, \*p\* = 0.038 < 0.05. The PS-GenAI PF group (Mean = 0.25, SD = 0.25) had a significantly higher score of addressing the opposing view than the GenAI-PF group (Mean = 0.09, SD = 0.27), \*p\* = 0.015 < 0.05. ## Discussion
### GenAI-supported collaborative argumentation peer feedback facilitate collaborative argumentation performance
Our study explored the impact of GenAI-supported peer feedback on collaborative argumentation performance (RQ1). The results showed that peer feedback can significantly facilitate students’ collaborative argumentation performance. The peer evaluation and feedback activity promotes peer dialogues in which students are able to critically analyze, integrate, and reconstruct knowledge, helping students to improve argumentative artifacts by monitoring and refining their ideas (Yi et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR99)). Specifically, the peer feedback groups that received GenAI support (GenAI PF group and PS-GenAI PF group) outperformed the PF group in their overall argumentation performance. This demonstrated that GenAI-generated content provided students with more structured and targeted argumentation content, thereby facilitating their argument skills, which is consistent with previous findings that GenAI-supported collaborative learning improved students’ cognitive processing (Zhang & Xu, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR104)).
It is noteworthy that students in the PS-GenAI PF group performed the best in the aspect of structural complexity, specifically in constructing rebuttal elements. These finding echoes research on how cognitive scaffolding promotes students’ cognitive development. As evidenced by Xu et al. ([2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR95)), metacognitive prompts in GenAI-supported collaborative learning may positively influence learning outcomes. Structured scaffolding enhances learners’ higher-order thinking and metacognitive capabilities during the integration of new concepts with prior knowledge (Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR50); Shao et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR72)). Knowledge integration prompt scaffolds increase students’ cognitive engagement while cultivating meaning-making and innovative thinking (Lee & Wu, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR49)). This partially aligns with the qualitative findings that student teachers in the PS-GenAI PF group (such as \*Student JXC\*, \*Student CC\*, \*Student CXJ\*, \*and Student YYH\*) indicated that they avoided overreliance on GenAI outputs by critically appraising, selecting, and optimizing the feedback content through their own judgment. In summary, prompt scaffolding facilitated students’ problem identification, reflective thinking, and counterargument construction in subsequent collaborative argumentation tasks, while helping them focus on argumentation structures and rebuttal claims during peer feedback.
Regarding argumentation quality, the findings indicate different effects of GenAI support and prompt scaffolding. The GenAI-PF group demonstrated significantly greater argument elaboration than the PF group, suggesting that GenAI-generated feedback can function as a cognitive and linguistic resource that supports idea expansion and argument development. By providing exemplars, alternative expressions, and structured suggestions, GenAI may help students elaborate claims with richer details and clearer reasoning structures, thereby enhancing the depth of their arguments (Xia et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR94)). More importantly, the PS-GenAI PF group not only outperformed the PF group in argument elaboration but also demonstrated significantly higher-quality in addressing the opposing view than both other groups. This finding suggested that prompt scaffolding played a critical role in shaping how students engage with GenAI feedback (Liang et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR51); Zhang & Xu, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR104)). Rather than passively adopting AI-generated suggestions, prompt scaffolding guided students to critically evaluate, compare, and integrate feedback from both peers and GenAI (Guo et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR32), [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR33)). These results further implied that while GenAI alone can moderately enhance argument elaboration, the integration of prompt scaffolding amplified its impact by activating students’ metacognitive monitoring and reflective judgment (Xu et al. [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR95); Wu et al. [2025a](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR91), [b](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR92)). Through prompt scaffolding, students are encouraged to question the adequacy of arguments, identify potential weaknesses, and generate targeted counterarguments supported by evidence (Jang et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR39); Yang et al., [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR97)). This process not only expands the breadth of argumentative ideas but also deepens argumentative reasoning, thereby contributing to higher-quality collaborative argumentation performance (Lee & Wu, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR49)).
### GenAI-supported collaborative argumentation peer feedback improve the feedback quality
The study revealed significant differences in feedback quality across three peer feedback conditions, illuminating the multidimensional impact on students’ feedback content and emotional expression (RQ2). This finding enriched current understanding of GenAI-supported peer feedback. First, the effects of different peer feedback conditions on feedback quality were examined based on the distribution of feedback elements and feedback affective. The results revealed significant differences across the three conditions. In the PF group, feedback was primarily characterized by identification and description, suggesting that students tended to point out strengths or weaknesses without consistently providing elaborated or actionable guidance. This pattern echoes prior findings that traditional peer feedback often remains at a descriptive or surface-evaluative level, with limited elaboration into constructive suggestions (Nelson & Schunn, [2009](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR60); Lu & Law, [2012](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR55); Gao et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR28)).
Relative to the PF group, both GenAI-supported groups produced a markedly higher proportion of suggestions, indicating that GenAI assistance may encourage students to formulate more improvement-oriented and solution-focused feedback (Corbin et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR16)). This aligns with recent studies that GenAI can provide structured exemplars and revision cues that scaffold actionable recommendations (Noroozi et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR62); Usher, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR84)). However, the PS-GenAI-PF group generated a lower proportion of suggestions than the GenAI-PF group. One possible explanation is that prompt scaffolding redistributed students’ cognitive attention across multiple evaluative components, fostering more balanced and reflective feedback (e.g., identification and explanation) rather than suggestion-dominant responses. While GenAI may directly generate suggestion-rich outputs (Noroozi et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR62)), prompt scaffolding encourages students to critically evaluate argument structure, justification, and evidence before formulating feedback (Ba et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR3); Tam, [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR75)). In this sense, a slightly lower proportion of suggestions does not necessarily indicate weaker feedback quality. Rather, it may reflect deeper cognitive engagement and more integrated evaluative processing. This interpretation is further supported by the superior counterargument performance observed in the PS-GenAI-PF group, suggesting that prompt scaffolding facilitated higher-order evaluative and rebuttal thinking.
Further epistemic network analysis (ENA) provided a more fine-grained understanding of peer feedback quality. GenAI-PF groups manifested the stronger connection between description and suggestions, indicating students’ tendency to propose improvement suggestions following objective evaluations. This suggests that feedback exemplars and content provided by GenAI facilitate students’ ability to formulate constructive suggestions based on objective assessment, which echoes findings in related research on the positive role of AI tools in stimulating students’ generative feedback (Xia et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR94)). More importantly, the PS-GenAI PF group demonstrated strong connectivity related to explanations and suggestions that students were able to give targeted explanations in response to their peers’ questions, which in turn provided suggestions for further improvement. This finding provided further evidence that prompt scaffolding plays a key role in guiding students in problem analysis, cause reasoning, and targeted suggestion generation. This also confirmed the findings of previous studies on the facilitative effects of cognitive scaffolding on students’ high-quality feedback (Shao et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR72); Ba et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR3)). Scripts and self-monitoring scaffolding enhanced the quality of peer feedback, and students critically scrutinized peer’ argumentation artifacts, generating longer, more in-depth and targeted feedback (Lahza et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR47)).
Taken together, these findings suggest that GenAI integration does not simply increase feedback quantity, but reshapes its internal structure—shifting emphasis from descriptive identification toward actionable recommendations and cognitively oriented evaluation. GenAI does provide valuable feedback (Banihashem et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR4)), and this study further highlighted the potential complementary role of GenAI in the peer feedback process, combining the strengths of both GenAI and humans to provide more personalized, accurate, and actionable feedback (Er et al., [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR21)). More importantly, the addition of prompt scaffolding appears to foster a more cognitively balanced feedback profile, potentially strengthening students’ evaluative depth and critical reasoning capacity. GenAI tools combined with effective pedagogical scaffolding (e.g., prompts for peer feedback) help students to explore and relate to the argument content and to think critically about the depth and rationality of the argument.
In terms of feedback affective, neutral expressions accounted for the largest proportion across all three groups, which is consistent with previous research on students’ general tendency to use neutral evaluative language in peer feedback to maintain peer relationships (Cho & MacArthur, [2010](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR14)). The PF group had the highest percentage of positive emotions, probably because students were more inclined to express encouragement when evaluating their peers’ artifacts to enhance the social atmosphere. Whereas GenAI-supported groups demonstrated comparatively moderated emotional tones. This indicated that GenAI-mediated feedback environments may emphasize task-oriented evaluation over socio-emotional expression. Notably, the GenAI-PF group showed the lowest proportion of negative comments, which may indicate that GenAI-generated feedback tends to adopt a neutral tone, thereby reducing overtly critical expressions. Integrating the efficiency of generative AI with human interaction is a key strategy for addressing emotional engagement (Teng, [2026](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR79)).
Further epistemic network analysis (ENA) provided a more fine-grained understanding of feedback affective. The GenAI- PF group showed a strong correlation between positive and neutral emotions, perhaps reflecting the input effect of the AI example language on students’ positive evaluative terms (Xia et al., [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR94)). Notably, the PS-GenAI PF group had the highest proportion of negative emotion and showed a clear shift between negative and neutral emotion. This finding may be attributable to the prompt scaffolding, which systematically guides students to identify weaknesses in their peers’ artifacts and encourages the provision of more critical, problem-focused feedback (Wu et al. [2025a](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR91), [b](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR92); Zhang et al. [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR106)). As student teacher LJL in group 4 reported that prompt scaffolding supported deeper reflection on strengths and weaknesses in collaborative argumentation, but that overly general GenAI feedback sometimes led to uncertainty about how to make concrete improvements. This illustrated not only the perceived benefits of prompt scaffolding-guided GenAI interactions in fostering a richer understanding of argumentation diversity, but also the emotional challenges students experienced when confronted with feedback that lacked actionable detail. Students expressed more negative affective, such as frustration or confusion, which can emerge when the cognitive load of interpreting vague feedback outweighs its perceived utility. Such emotions can impede the uptake of feedback and reduce motivation to engage in subsequent revisions, echoing prior research suggesting that the clarity and specificity of feedback are critical to its effectiveness in collaborative learning (Chen et al. [2023](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR12); Lahza et al. [2025](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR47); Wu et al. [2025a](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR91), [b](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR92)). This finding also emphasizes the need for subsequent research and instructional design to balance the effects of scaffolding on students’ critical thinking and emotional expression, thereby preventing adverse effects on students’ feedback motivation and peer relationships due to the accumulation of negative emotions.
### Practical implication
This study enriched the empirical evidence of GenAI-supported peer feedback, provided new practical insights into the instructional design of peer feedback in collaborative argumentation learning. First, given that GenAI-supported peer feedback significantly increased the proportion of improvement-oriented suggestions and enhanced argument elaboration, instructors may strategically integrate GenAI tools into peer feedback activities to stimulate solution-focused comments. Rather than using GenAI as a replacement for peer interaction, it can serve as a dialogic support tool that encourages students to articulate more concrete revision strategies.
Second, the results suggest that prompt scaffolding plays a critical role in deepening argumentation performance and feedback quality, particularly in fostering high-quality rebuttals and strengthening the connection between evaluative descriptions and explanations. Therefore, teachers should not only provide access to GenAI tools but also design structured prompt scaffolding that guide students to identify weaknesses, justify evaluations with evidence, and consider opposing views. Embedding prompts that explicitly require counterargument generation may be especially effective in advancing students’ higher-order argumentative ability. Well-designed prompts can activate students’ metacognitive monitoring and prevent over-reliance on surface-level suggestions.
Third, students should be guided to critically evaluate and selectively adopt GenAI-generated feedback. Instructional designs can incorporate structured reflection tasks that require students to explain which GenAI-generated suggestions they choose to adopt, modify, or reject and to provide reasons for their decisions. Such practices can strengthen students’ critical judgment, metacognitive awareness, and responsible use of GenAI in peer feedback activities.
### Limitations and implications for future research
This study has several limitations that need to be further addressed in future studies. First, the generalizability of the findings is constrained by both the sample composition and the group-level experimental design. The participants were 45 third-year undergraduates majoring in educational technology at a single university in central China, resulting in a relatively homogeneous sample. More importantly, the study involved 12 collaborative groups in total, with only four groups assigned to each experimental condition. Despite the longitudinal design involving four rounds of collaborative argumentation, the number of groups per condition remained limited, which may constrain statistical power and the generalizability of the findings. Future studies should replicate the design with a larger number of groups across multiple disciplines and contexts to strengthen the robustness and external validity of the conclusions.
Second, it has been noted that excessive cognitive load may affect students’ motivation and engagement (Sweller, [2024](/article/10.1186/s41239-026-00586-w#ref-CR74)), which in turn adversely affects feedback quality and argumentation performance. Future studies should incorporate the cognitive load theory to systematically examine the effects of different scaffold designs on students’ cognitive resource allocation and learning outcomes from multiple dimensions, including perceived load, intrinsic load, and extrinsic load.
Third, the type of scaffolding used in this study was static and standardized, failing to provide individualized support based on students’ different abilities and cognitive levels in the peer feedback. Considering the different learning needs of students in the process of interactive feedback with GenAI, future studies can explore the design and application of adaptive prompt scaffolding. For example, the GenAI prompts, evaluation rubrics, and self-monitoring scaffolding can be dynamically adjusted according to the developmental stage of students’ peer feedback skills. And the external scaffolding can be weakened at the right time to promote students’ intrinsic feedback skills when they gradually master the ability to generate feedback in conversation with GenAI.
Finally, it is of attention that existing studies have not yet adequately considered the risk of cognitive offload that may exist when students use GenAI for peer feedback. Future research should further explore how to design and apply cognitive scaffolding in GenAI-supported environments appropriately. The aim is to avoid students neglecting their cognitive engagement due to their dependence on AI output, and to ensure the development of feedback agency and higher-order thinking.
## Conclusions
This study examined the effects of GenAI-supported peer feedback on students’ collaborative argumentation performance and feedback quality through a quasi-experimental design. The results indicated that GenAI-supported peer feedback was effective in facilitating students’ collaborative argumentation performance. Particularly, peer feedback supported by GenAI with prompt scaffolding led to better performance in rebuttal claim, rebuttal data and warrant, argument elaboration, and addressing opposing views. In addition, GenAI-supported peer feedback contributed to improved feedback quality, as students were more likely to provide suggestions accompanied by descriptions or explanations. The ENA results further showed that the prompt-scaffolded GenAI peer feedback group exhibited stronger connections related to explanations, indicating that prompt scaffolding helped students link evaluative descriptions with improvement-oriented suggestions. Overall, these findings highlight that GenAI is most effective when embedded within scaffolded instructional designs, offering important implications for the design of peer feedback activities in collaborative argumentation learning.

### Appendix A Feedback rubric for argumentation artifacts (Group X)
| Aspects of argument quality | Definition | Score | Score |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
| Organisation of Arguments | The claims is well organised (coherent and flows well) | The artifact lacks coherence and flow | The artifact has some organisation, but it is somewhat difficult to follow | The artifact is generally well organised and flows well | The artifact is well organised and flows smoothly | The artifact is exceptionally well organised and flows seamlessly |
| Sufficiency of Arguments | The number of arguments is adequate to support the thesis | There are too few arguments, and they are insufficient to support the thesis | There are some arguments, but they are not sufficient to support the thesis | There are adequate arguments to support the thesis, but some more could have been added | There are adequate arguments to support the thesis | There are considerable arguments, all of which are sufficient to support the thesis |
| Clarity of Arguments | The artifact is comprehensible. The arguments are clearly formulated | The arguments are poorly formulated, and the artifact is difficult to understand | The arguments are somewhat clear, but the artifact requires effort to comprehend | The arguments are generally clear and easy to understand | The arguments are clear and well formulated | The arguments are exceptionally clear and easy to comprehend |
| Elaboration of Arguments | Arguments are well elaborated (e.g., with examples, analogies, citing authorities) | The arguments are not elaborated or supported by any evidence/examples | The arguments are somewhat elaborated, but additional evidence or examples could be added | The arguments are adequately elaborated, with some supporting evidence or examples | The arguments are well elaborated, with supporting evidence or examples | The arguments are exceptionally well elaborated, with strong and nuanced supporting evidence or examples |
| Relevance of Arguments | All arguments presented are relevant to the topic/thesis | The arguments are irrelevant to the thesis | Some arguments do not directly support the thesis | The majority of arguments are relevant and support the thesis | All arguments are relevant and support the thesis | Arguments are not only relevant but also insightful, original, and add significant value to the topic |
| Persuasiveness of Arguments | Arguments are convincing and compel agreement | The arguments are not at all convincing | Some arguments are somewhat convincing, but the essay is overall unconvincing | Arguments are generally convincing but may lack depth and insight | Arguments are highly convincing, with well-supported evidence and insights | Arguments are exceptionally persuasive, with powerful, nuanced reasoning and evidence |
| Addressing Opposing Views | The artifact adequately addresses the opposing view | The artifact does not address any opposing views | The artifact acknowledges some opposing views but provides a weak or inadequate response | The artifact addresses some opposing views adequately, but could be more thorough | The artifact addresses opposing views comprehensively and provides a thoughtful response | The artifact addresses opposing views exceptionally well, with a nuanced or insightful response that strengthens the argument |
Please score the argumentative artifact based on the above assessment rubric, indicating the extent to which the group's argumentative artifact elaborates on the various elements of the argumentation. What are the problems in the argumentative artifact and what are the suggestions for improvement? Feedback should be no less than 300 words.
### Appendix B Prompt scaffolding to support peer feedback with GenAI
GenAI () has powerful text processing and contextual understanding capabilities to support argumentation feedback, and how the feedback is collaborated with the GenAI tool involves the design and input of prompts.
1. 1.
Request to generate evaluations and feedback on the peers’ artifacts.
2. 2.
Use GenAI to assist in adding relevant reasons and evidence.
3. 3.
Use GenAI to compose positive, specific feedback that affirms the peers’ argument.
4. 4.
Use GenAI to assist in thinking from multiple viewpoints and identifying possible gaps or shortcomings in peers’ artifacts.
Please integrate the following five key elements to interact with the GenAI to accomplish higher quality evaluation and feedback (of which, 1. Input content; 2. Context; and 3 Objective, which are the core parts of prompt design).
| Key elements | Instruction | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Input content | Input content that the model is expected to process and provide a response to | Input the content of arguments that require GenAI-assisted evaluation |
| Context | Provide background knowledge or additional context | We need to collaborate on the evaluation and feedback of the results of a collaborative argumentation on the topic XXXXX, please help us evaluate the content of the argumentation |
| Objective | State the individual's intent and formulate the task you want the GenAI tool to perform in the form of a proposition, including posing a question, articulating a need, etc | Please evaluate how well the above argumentation supports the `positive view' of a topic, and what suggestions do you have to better provide reasons and evidence in support of the position |
| Role | Add hypothetical roles (identities, explicit holders) to allow the GenAI to conduct thought experiments | You are a student who supports XXXX's views You are an education policy maker You are a teacher who advocates for online education |
| Examples | Provide accurate templates for the GenAI to inspire it to generate the expected content | Please output the following: description (a concise summary of the argument content), identification (pointing out and specifying problems in the argument content), and suggestions (providing suggestions for further modification) |
Consider setting objectives to interact with the GenAI along the following evaluation points to more efficiently accomplish evaluation and feedback on the artifacts of intergroup collaborative argumentation.
| Evaluation points | Prompts |
| --- | --- |
| Clarity of position on the topic | To what extent has the group stated their clear position on the topic? What suggestions do you have for the group to (better) provide clarity on the topic? |
| Claims in support of the position | To what extent did the group provide claims in favor of the position? What suggestions do you have for the group to better provide claims in favor of the position? |
| Evidence to support claims (e.g., facts, expert opinion, etc.) | To what extent did the group provide evidence in support of the claims? What suggestions do you have for the group to better provide evidence in support of the claims? |
| Rebuttal argument | To what extent did the group provide rebuttal arguments? What suggestions do you have for the group to better provide arguments against the position? |
| Rebuttal evidence (e.g., facts, expert opinion, etc.) | To what extent did the group provide evidence against the position? What suggestions do you have for the group to better provide evidence against the position? |
| Summary | To what extent did the group draw conclusions based on their positions? What advice would you give to the group to (better) draw conclusions based on their positions? |

### Appendix C Analytical Framework for Collaborative Argumentation Artifacts
| Dimension | Component | Definition |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Argument structure | Claim | An assertion that works as a supporting argument in a main idea in response to a contentious topic |
| Data and warrant | Evidence to support a claim, such as facts, statistics, research studies, expert opinions, and analogies, and the supportive link between the evidence and the claim |
| Rebuttal claim | Statements in which the writer responds to a counterargument by pointing out the possible weakness in the claim, data, or warrant, such as logical fallacies, insufficient support, invalid assumptions, and immoral values |
| Rebuttal data and warrant | Evidence to support a rebuttal claim, and the supportive link between the evidence and the rebuttal claim |
| Argument quality | Organisation of Arguments | The claims are well organised (coherent and flows well) |
| Sufficiency of Arguments | The number of arguments is adequate to support the thesis |
| Clarity of Arguments | The artifact is comprehensible. The arguments are clearly formulated |
| Elaboration of Arguments | The arguments are well elaborated (e.g., with examples, analogies, and citing authorities) |
| Relevance of Arguments | All arguments presented are relevant to the topic |
| Persuasiveness of Arguments | The arguments are convincing and compel agreement |
| Addressing Opposing Views | The artifact adequately addresses the opposing view |
