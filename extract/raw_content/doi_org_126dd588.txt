## Abstract
Although computational thinking (CT) concepts are widely used as an indicator of students’ CT learning gains, few studies have employed latent profile analysis (LPA) to unveil patterns of students’ CT conceptual understanding, classify students accordingly, and identify student subgroups most in need of support. With a sample of 12,641 primary students, we conducted LPAs with respect to their understandings of three basic CT concepts, namely repetition, conditionals, and sequences, before and after they studied a CT curriculum for one year. Before the course, four profiles were identified: high in all concepts, high in repetition, high in sequences, and low in all concepts. After the one-year course, only two profiles emerged: high and low in all concepts, designated as High Performers (HP) and Low Performers (LP), respectively. This suggested that the students’ understanding of CT concepts showed substantial variation before the course, but after one year of formal study of CT, their abilities became more uniform, which is a unique finding not identified previously. Before studying CT, more male than female students were classified as HP, but no gender differences were found after the one-year course. Two thirds of LP students remained as LP in the post-test, suggesting that this subgroup was the most in need of support, and students with no access to technology were more likely to remain as LP. These results imply that the curriculum was effective in fostering students’ holistic development of CT concepts, and that access to technology might facilitate the upward mobility of LP students.
## Introduction
In an era where digital literacy shapes the future, computational thinking (CT) has emerged as a cornerstone of 21st-century education. CT involves the ability to decompose complex problems, craft step-by-step solutions, and represent algorithms (Aho, 2012; Wing, 2006). CT enhances students’ cognitive abilities and problem-solving skills (Montuori et al., 2024), and it also benefits their learning across various subjects (e.g., Aytekin & Topçu, 2024; Cui et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). From early childhood to higher education, cultivating CT empowers students to navigate an increasingly digital world with confidence and creativity (Abelson & Kong, 2024; Åkerfeldt et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2023).
A prominent approach to developing CT is through block-based programming, which simplifies complex concepts and makes coding accessible to young learners (Jin & Cutumisu, 2024). Numerous interventions aim to nurture CT competence, with research often measuring students’ understanding of core concepts, such as sequences, loops, and conditionals, to gauge learning progress through pre-post designs (e.g., El-Hamamsy et al., 2025; Shen et al., 2024). However, less research has employed latent profile analysis (LPA) to classify students according to their CT performances (Guo et al., 2024), especially on the configural patterns of their understanding of various CT concepts and how the patterns change after an intervention. LPA considers the interplay between variables in identifying subgroups of individuals sharing a similar pattern of variables (Spurk et al., 2020). By analysing the characteristics of various student subgroups, educators can identify whether there are subgroups most in need of support. Longitudinally applying LPA further allows us to examine how students’ profiles evolve over time, shedding light on the stability and transformation of students’ conceptual understanding before and after targeted interventions (Jeong & Feldon, 2023).
Despite the growing use of LPA in educational research, its application to students’ conceptual understanding of CT remains limited. Existing studies have explored latent profiles related to creativity, cooperation, identities, and problem-solving behaviors (Guggemos et al., 2022; Uslu, 2023; Guo et al., 2024), rather than their understanding of CT concepts. Moreover, prior research often captures one snapshot in time (Guggemos et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2024), neglecting how students’ profiles might shift with instruction or experience. To bridge these gaps, our study employs a longitudinal latent profile analysis to explore primary students’ diverse patterns of CT conceptual understanding before and after a year-long intervention. We aim to identify distinct student subgroups, examine how their profiles change over time, and investigate factors, such as access to technology and self-efficacy, that relate to these dynamics. By pinpointing which student subgroups need targeted support and uncovering factors that facilitate growth, our research offers valuable insights for educators worldwide striving to cultivate computational thinkers ready for the digital age.
