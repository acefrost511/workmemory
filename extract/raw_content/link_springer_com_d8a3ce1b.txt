Abstract
--------

As virtual reality (VR) and augmented reality (AR) continue to become popular, there remains a noticeable gap in understanding their potential for enriching science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM) learning among K-12 students. To address this knowledge gap, this study systematically reviewed 117 articles published from 2010 to 2022 on the basis of the technology-based learning model. Following the PRISMA systematic review method, research methods, research participants, learning devices, learning strategies, advantages and challenges of each article were coded and analyzed. In the last six years (i.e., 2017–2022), the overall number of studies experienced rapid growth. The largest number of articles focused on VR/AR-supported K-12 science learning, followed by mathematics and integrated STEM learning. Smartphone/tablet-based AR was the most widely used technology, while very few studies used dedicated immersive AR devices. Most studies focused on the elementary school stage, while VR/AR-supported STEM learning at senior high schools was underexplored. Studies tended to measure the effectiveness of VR/AR by quantitative methods. Situated learning, task-based learning, and game-based learning were the most widely used learning strategies in VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning. The advantages of VR/AR in K-12 STEM learning include a positive impact on individual learners and on learning and teaching processes. In contrast, VR/AR also poses a series of challenges to learners, teachers, and schools. The challenges learners face include distractions, decreased efficiency and effectiveness, diminished inquiry and imagination, operational issues, and discomfort. Teachers encounter difficulties maintaining classroom control, designing activities, acquiring technical competence, and adapting to the new role of facilitating student-centered learning. Schools grapple with high costs, inadequate infrastructure, a lack of private virtual spaces, and privacy concerns. Based on our findings, we propose six research agendas to enrich studies on VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning, along with a series of significant implications for practitioners to effectively address the intricate challenges of VR/AR implementation and fully harness its potential.

1 Introduction
--------------

Virtual reality (VR) is “an artificial environment that is created with software and presented to the user in such a way that the user suspends belief and accepts it as a real environment” (Joiner, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR63), p. 112). Augmented reality (AR) expands the functions of VR by incorporating virtual objects in a realistic environment so that users can interact with both real and virtual environments in three-dimensional space and receive real-time feedback (Joiner, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR63)). Over the past decade, virtual reality and augmented reality (VR/AR) have been widely applied in various education areas (Garzón, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR42); Hincapie et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR45)), including science, technology, engineering and mathematics (STEM) education[Footnote 1](#Fn1) (Sirakaya & Sirakaya, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR115); Zhang & Wang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR143)). Conspicuously, STEM subjects are consistently considered challenging, characterized by complex concepts that can prove hard to learn (Li & Schoenfeld, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR72)). However, the application of VR/AR in K-12 STEM education may help students better understand complex STEM spatial concepts, provide students with opportunities for scientific inquiry, develop students’ practical skills, and thus make STEM subjects less difficult, abstract, and boring (Bujak et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR14); Ibanez & Delgado-Kloos, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR57)).

As VR/AR use is becoming more prominent in STEM education, STEM education researchers and practitioners are increasingly concerned about harnessing its benefits while mitigating potential risks and calling for a better understanding of the advantages and challenges of VR/AR-supported STEM learning (Ibanez & Delgado-Kloos, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR57)). Several prior review studies have identified some of its advantages and challenges. For example, Ibanez and Delgado-Kloos ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR57)) reviewed AR-supported STEM education spanning from 2010 to 2017. Their analysis found that the most common cognitive outcome of AR-supported STEM learning was students’ ability to remember information, and other outcomes including students’ effectiveness in applying procedures to solve STEM problems, high-level inquiry behaviors, and self-evaluation (Ibanez & Delgado-Kloos, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR57)). They also revealed concerns about AR’s risks of distracting students’ attention and imposing cognitive load. Zhang and Wang ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR143)) systematically reviewed 61 empirical studies on VR-supported science learning. They concluded that AR could help students achieve cognitive, affective, and behavioral goals in their science learning while it has some technological and pedagogical limitations and may cause social problems (Zhang & Wang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR143)). Sirakaya and Sirakaya ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR115)) analyzed 42 articles on AR-supported STEM education (including K-12 education, higher education, and teacher education) published between 2012 and 2018. They summarized that AR could have several effects on STEM education, such as increasing motivation, developing spatial ability, promoting cooperative learning and individualized teaching, and facilitating interaction, while the challenges encountered in AR-supported STEM learning were usually related to technical problems. Although prior review studies have provided several insights into the advantages and challenges of AR/VR-supported STEM learning, they are not without limitations. On the one hand, most prior literature reviews focused on empirical studies before 2018 (e.g., Ibanez & Delgado-Kloos, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR57); Sirakaya & Sirakaya, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR115)). However, it is worth noting that VR/AR has been rapidly developing in recent years (Chen et al., [2021a](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR25), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR26)), and its usage in STEM learning is growing significantly every year (Ibanez & Delgado-Kloos, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR57); Sirakaya & Sirakaya, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR115); Zhang & Wang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR143)). Hence, a holistic overview depicting the latest application of VR/AR (especially in 2018–2022) is needed. On the other hand, some prior literature reviews only focus on applying VR/AR in one subject area, such as science (e.g., Zhang & Wang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR143)). Considering the emphasis on the significance of STEM learning, especially integrated STEM learning (iSTEM)[Footnote 2](#Fn2), worldwide (Jiang et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR5); Jiang et al., [2023b](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR6); Jiang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR7)), it is significant to figure out the potential of VR/AR in a larger scope (i.e., STEM learning including iSTEM).

This study will picture the trends, advantages, and challenges of applying VR/AR in K-12 STEM learning. To this end, relevant empirical studies published over the past thirteen years (i.e., 2010–2022) were systematically reviewed. This paper poses the following research questions:

1. (1)
   
   What are the trends of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning studies (including the publication trends, learning subjects, learning devices, participants, methods, sample size and learning strategies)?
2. (2)
   
   What are the advantages and challenges of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning?

2 Literature review
-------------------

### 2.1 Theoretical foundations of VR/AR-supported learning

STEM learning is inherently student-centered, fostering an educational approach that prioritizes learner engagement and autonomy (Jiang et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR5); Lee et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR71); Li & Schoenfeld, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR72)). VR/AR technologies are shifting students’ learning experiences from passive reception to active engagement (Pang & Cai, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR103)), aligning with STEM education reforms’ objectives (Jiang et al, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR5)). Notably, the efficacy of VR/AR-supported learning is undergirded by a series of theoretical foundations, such as behaviorism, constructivism, cognitivism, experientialism and connectivism (Radianti et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR109); Yen et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR136)), which suggest that learning is most effective when it is active, meaningful, and contextually relevant.

Specifically, behaviorism assumes that knowledge is a repertoire of behavioral responses to environmental stimuli (Shuell, [1986](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR113)). Behaviorist theory further asserts that learning manifests in behavioral changes, and requires repetition and extrinsic motivation along with applying both positive and negative reinforcement (Radianti et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR109)). VR/AR technologies offer controlled and adaptive environments where learners can engage in repeated practices and receive immediate feedback, which is at the core of behaviorist learning theory. For instance, behaviorism’s emphasis on reinforcement and punishment can be translated into VR/AR-supported learning contexts through the use of rewards for correct responses and guided corrections for incorrect ones. Behaviorist strategies (e.g., game-based learning and role-playing) are frequently applied in VR/AR-supported learning (Radianti et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR109)).

Cognitivism posits that learning is an active information-processing activity where knowledge is mentally structured and organized, and further emphasizes the importance of learning through thinking and problem-solving (Shuell, [1986](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR113)). VR/AR technologies facilitate the cognitivist learning approach by providing virtual environments that stimulate students’ cognitive processes (Radianti et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR109)). For instance, VR/AR can create complex cognitive tasks or problems in simulated environments, where students can process and manipulate the content knowledge (Yen et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR136)). Cognitivist strategies (e.g., problem-based learning, task-based learning) are also usually used in VR/AR-supported learning (Yen et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR136)).

Constructivist theory is one of the most important theoretical foundations of VR/AR-supported learning (Radianti et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR109); Yen et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR136)). Constructivism posits that learning is an active, constructive process and further suggests that learners should construct knowledge through active exploration and manipulation of their environments (Fosnot, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR40); Smeda et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR117)). VR/AR technologies provide learners with the tools to explore virtual environments, manipulate virtual objects, and engage in simulated STEM experiments, facilitating the constructivist learning approach (Yen et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR136)). Constructivist strategies (e.g., project-based learning, inquiry-based learning, situated learning, storytelling) are frequently applied in VR/AR-supported learning (Radianti et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR109)).

Experientialism is rooted in the belief that knowledge is acquired through experience and reflection on that experience (Kolb & Kolb, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR67)). It further characterizes learning as a cycle of experiential stages, including concrete experience, reflective observation, abstract conceptualization, and active experimentation (Kolb & Kolb, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR67)). VR/AR can simulate a wide range of concrete experiences in STEM learning (e.g., dissecting a virtual frog in biology, designing and testing a bridge in engineering). This aligns with the experientialism that learners construct knowledge through experiences. Experiential learning strategies are sometimes used in VR/AR-supported educational environments (Yen et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR136)).

Connectivism suggests that learning is a networked process where knowledge is shared by forming connections (Siemens, [2005](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR114)). VR/AR technologies enable collaborative learning experiences, allowing students to connect, communicate, and build knowledge together in virtual environments. Connectivism supports the use of collaborative learning strategies, which are frequently employed in VR/AR-supported educational environments (Yen et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR136)).

In summary, VR/AR-supported learning is grounded in various theoretical foundations (Radianti et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR109); Yen et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR136)). These foundations provide a guide for leveraging VR/AR technologies to enhance students’ learning experiences and improve educational outcomes. Furthermore, different theoretical foundations can lead to various VR/AR-supported learning strategies. Of course, it should be acknowledged that while behaviorism, cognitivism, constructivism, experientialism and connectivism are key foundations, many other theories can inform and enrich VR/AR-supported learning experiences (Radianti et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR109)). Meanwhile, one learning strategy might also be influenced by several theoretical foundations.

### 2.2 Theoretical framework for analyzing VR/AR-supported STEM learning

The technology-based learning model is a widely used theoretical framework to help analyze and understand the details of technology-supported learning studies (Wu et al., [2021a](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR130), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR131)). It was first proposed by Hsu et al. ([2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR50)), and then adapted by many scholars to analyze technology-supported learning in various specific areas (e.g., Ashraf et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR2); Chang et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR19); Chang & Hwang, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR18); Chen et al., [2021a](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR25), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR26); Huang & Hwang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR53); Lin & Hwang, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR78); Qiu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR108); Wu et al., [2021a](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR130), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR131)). Notably, the latest technology-based learning model includes six dimensions: learning devices, learning domains, learning strategies, research methods, participants and research issues (Chang et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR19)). Our study also adopts this model to analyze VR/AR-supported STEM learning.

The goals of our review studies are twofold. One goal is to analyze the trends of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning studies. Employing Chang et al.’s ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR19)) technology-based learning model as our theoretical framework, we analyze the overall trends, which include publication trends, learning subjects, learning devices, methods, participants and sample size, and learning strategies. First, learning subjects can be categorized as science, technology, engineering, mathematics and integrated STEM learning (iSTEM)[Footnote 3](#Fn3). Second, learning devices can be divided into immersive and non-immersive (Camba et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR17); Mystakidis et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR94); Qiu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR108)). Immersive learning devices have one important feature. Specifically, by using immersive learning devices, learners are able to fully engulf themselves in closed, three-dimensional virtual environments where their links with the real world are cut off (Qiu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR108)). In other words, the immersive virtual learning activities will not be interrupted by the outside world (Qiu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR108)). For instance, headset VR devices are typical immersive learning devices (Qiu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR108)). Non-immersive learning devices mainly include desktop VR/AR and smartphone/tablet-based VR/AR (Camba et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR17); Mystakidis et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR94); Qiu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR108)). When using these devices, learners can interact with the characters or activities in the multi-dimensional virtual environments through computers, smartphones or tablets while remaining aware of their physical surroundings in the real world (Liu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR85); Qiu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR108)). Third, research methods can be categorized as quantitative, qualitative, and mixed (Östlund et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR99)). Fourth, participants can be categorized as elementary school students, junior high school students, and senior high school students[Footnote 4](#Fn4). Fifth, ten strategies are frequently used in technology-based learning (Chang et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR19); Reisoğlu et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR106)). We further describe their definitions in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Fig1). The other goal was to synthesize the findings of research issues (i.e., advantages and challenges of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning). In light of Sirakaya and Sirikaya’s ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR115)) study on the application of AR/VR in learning, advantages include two categories: positive impact on individual learners and positive impact on learning and teaching processes. Challenges include three categories, namely challenges to learners, challenges to teachers, and challenges to schools.

The elements in our theoretical framework are depicted in Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Fig2).

3 Method
--------

### 3.1 Data collection

Systematic literature reviews serve as the benchmark for sourcing, selecting, and synthesizing published literature to answer predefined research questions, so the search strategy, inclusion/exclusion criteria, and literature sources should be clearly identified (Turnbull et al. [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR8)). According to Abazi-Bexheti et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR1)), the increase in research publications in the area of AR/VR in education started around 2010. Hence, this study reviews empirical articles focusing on VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning over the past 13 years (i.e., 2010–2022)[Footnote 5](#Fn5). According to Darmawansah et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR36)), Fu and Hwang ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR41)), Huang et al., ([2022a](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR51)), Slavin ([1986](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR116)) and Xie et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR132)), it is crucial to include high-quality data sources in literature reviews. We used the search terms shown in Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Fig3)[Footnote 6](#Fn6), and entered them into the Web of Science (WOS) database to search only for Social Science Citation Index (SSCI), Science Citation Index Expanded (SCIE), and Arts & Humanities Citation Index (A&HCI) papers due to their wide recognition as being high-quality in the education research area (Darmawansah et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR36)). Reliance on a single database such as WOS is acceptable within the PRISMA framework (Moher et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR93)). As a result of the search, 2701 articles were retrieved from the WOS database. As the WOS database updates its data almost every day, our final data collection was conducted on 30th June 2023. The screening process, based on the PRISMA systematic review method (Page et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR102)), consisted of three stages. The specific data retrieval steps are shown in Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Fig3).

In stage 1, 480 non-journal articles (e.g., conference papers, abstracts, and review papers) were removed, and 2221 papers remained. These non-journal articles were excluded according to Bradford’s Law (Testa, [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR124)), which claims that most noteworthy discoveries are published in journal papers. Furthermore, 24 articles not written in English were deleted, and 2197 articles remained.

Although we used the keywords “K-12”, “STEM”, and “VR/AR” in the search terms, there was little guarantee that the retrieved articles were relevant. Hence, in stage 2, we screened articles to ensure their relevance to K-12, STEM, and the use of VR/AR. To this end, we further reviewed these articles’ abstracts and full texts to remove studies that were not relevant to K-12 education, had no relevance to STEM education, or lacked a specific focus on the usage of VR/AR. For example, some abstracts included the term STEM to describe the essence of the study, in contrast to the discussion and conclusion sections, which focused on the use of VR/AR in social sciences. Specifically, 1996 articles unrelated to K-12 education were removed, followed by 46 articles without emphasis on STEM education and 12 articles irrelevant to the usage of VR/AR. We removed 2054 articles during this stage, leaving 143 articles for the next stage.

In stage 3, we aimed to screen the empirical articles with an emphasis on student learning. Firstly, eight theoretical articles that conceptually described the usage of VR/AR technologies were excluded as they were not empirical studies. Secondly, we removed 18 articles that only analyzed teachers’ learning. After the three screening stages, 117 articles were collected for this review.

The first and the second authors independently conducted the screening process. After independently completing the process, the two authors cross-checked and compared their results, identifying two instances of inconsistency. Subsequently, the original articles were revisited, and a collaborative discussion ensued to reach a consensus. The final selection of included articles was determined through mutual agreement between the authors.

### 3.2 Data analysis

We analyzed the data using the content analysis method (Cohen et al., [2005](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR35)). The articles were coded independently by the first and second authors. First, we coded the meaningful sentences in the collected articles according to our theoretical framework (see Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Fig1)). For instance, to answer our first research question, all sentences indicating research methods, participants, learning devices, learning subjects, and learning strategies should be considered meaningful and marked and coded. Second, we combed the codes and combined codes with similar meanings. Third, we placed the combined codes in the corresponding categories. For instance, if one empirical study involved both immersive VR and immersive AR, one code would be added to the category of immersive VR and another to immersive AR. If participants in one empirical study were at both junior and senior high school levels, one code would be added to the category of junior and another to senior high school students. If learning strategies in one empirical study include both collaborative learning and task-based learning, one code would be added to the category of collaborative learning and another to task-based learning. After concluding the coding process, we assessed the inter-rater agreement, which surpassed 95%. Following the guidelines outlined by Zade et al. ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR140)) for addressing code discrepancies, we meticulously revisited the original sentences and engaged in collaborative discussions to achieve consensus. Subsequently, through this deliberative process, a unanimous agreement was reached on all codes.

4 Results
---------

We present the results of our study in terms of their relevance to our research questions: RQ1 and RQ2. These are outlined in the following sub-sections.

### 4.1 RQ1. What are the trends of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM education studies?

Figure [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Fig4) illustrates the number of published articles on VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning from 2010 to 2022. There was a significant upward trend in the total number of articles over this timeframe. The number of published articles in 2022 is four times more than in 2010. In the first six years (i.e., 2010–2015), the publication numbers did not fluctuate significantly, and the number of publications published each year was five or less. In 2016, the number of publications increased to 9. However, in 2017, there was a notable dip in the number of articles (only two articles were published). Although there were some fluctuations, the average number of papers published in 2016 and 2017 was still close to 5. Subsequently, the number of articles has consistently grown over the last six years (i.e., 2017–2022). In 2022, the publication number increased to 24. This trend reflects a gradual increase in researchers’ interest in VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning and indicates a promising domain for AR/VR technologies in K-12 STEM learning.

Simultaneously, this study also examined the distribution of VR/AR applications across subjects within K-12 STEM education (see Fig. [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Fig4)). We categorized 117 articles according to the disciplines of S (Science), T (Technology), M (Mathematics), and iSTEM. In all of the empirical articles, the largest number of articles (77 articles) focused on VR/AR-supported K-12 science learning, followed by mathematics (22 articles) and iSTEM (16 articles). There was no article focusing on K-12 engineering learning, and only two articles focused on technology learning.

**Fig. 4**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z/figures/4)

Distribution status of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning studies in 2010–2022 (by type of learning subjects)

We investigated the learning devices (technology type) utilized in the 117 empirical articles (see Fig. [5](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Fig5)). Among them, VR devices were used in 62 studies, and AR devices were used in 62 studies, too. This indicated that AR devices were as popular as VR devices in K-12 STEM learning. No studies used AR devices in 2010 and 2011. Between 2013 and 2019, studies using AR devices were more than those using VR devices. This was especially true in 2019 when AR devices were used in 10 studies while VR devices were used in only three studies. Between 2020 and 2022, studies using VR devices were slightly more than those using AR devices.

Non-immersive devices were used in 96 studies, while immersive devices were used in 28 studies. In other words, non-immersive devices were much more popular than immersive devices in K-12 STEM learning. As for non-immersive devices, the largest number of studies (42 studies) used smartphone/tablet-based AR to support K-12 STEM learning, and its use significantly increased over the last five years (i.e., 2018–2022). The second largest number of studies (27 studies) used Desktop VR. Smartphone/tablet-based VR were the least used non-immersive devices (used in 10 studies). In 2015 and before, there were no studies involving immersive VR/AR devices. In other words, immersive VR/AR devices appeared later than non-immersive ones in K-12 STEM education studies. The use of immersive VR significantly increased over the past three years (i.e., 2020–2022). However, immersive AR devices (i.e., AR head-mounted displays) were used in very few studies (*N* = 3).

As shown in Fig. [6](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Fig6), most studies (*n* = 63) focused on the elementary school stage. In contrast, VR/AR-supported STEM learning at senior high schools is underexplored (19 studies). Notably, from 2018 to 2020, the number of articles focusing on elementary school students increased significantly compared to those focusing on the other two levels. In subsequent years (i.e., 2021 and 2022), articles focusing on elementary school students declined from 13 to 9. In contrast, the number of articles focusing on junior and senior high school students showed an upward trend. Although the total number of articles focusing on junior high school students was less than the number of articles focusing on elementary school students, the number of articles focusing on junior high school students in 2022 exceeded the number focusing on elementary school students, suggesting that researchers have been more enthusiastic about VR/AR-supported STEM learning at junior high schools in 2022.

As shown in Fig. [7](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Fig7), the most prevalent research methods were quantitative (60 studies), followed by mixed methods (53 studies). Only four studies used qualitative studies. In 2021 and 2022, the number of articles using quantitative methods is larger than those using mixed methods, suggesting that studies in recent years have tended to measure the effectiveness of VR/AR by quantitative methods. The main data collection tools used for qualitative research methods were interviews (45 articles) and observations (12 articles), and the main data collection tools used for quantitative research methods were achievement tests (66 articles), surveys/questionnaires (83 articles), and attitude, personality or aptitude scales (45 articles).

According to Fig. [8](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Fig8), the sample size of most studies was between 31 and 100. There was only one study with fewer than 10 participants, and one with more than 1000 participants. Notably, 72.65% of the studies in the 13 years had less than 100 participants. Although the number of participants may be associated with the research methods, this result, to some degree, indicated that AR/VR devices were not applied on a large scale in most studies.

As shown in Fig. [9](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Fig9), the top three applied learning strategies are situated learning (51 articles), task-based learning (37 articles), and game-based learning (35 articles). Firstly, VR/AR can simulate various authentic situations and thus provide numerous opportunities for implementing situated learning. For instance, in Markowitz et al.’s ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR89)) study, with the help of VR, students were invited to experience an immersive underwater world, where the processes and effects of rising sea water acidity were simulated. Their results also showed that this immersive experience helped students understand ocean acidification, which was the consequence of climate change. Secondly, VR/AR can also provide students with inquisitive and interesting virtual tasks. By employing task-based learning strategies, teachers can support students in reaching their learning goals effectively. For instance, in McElhaney and Lnn’s ([2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR92)) study, students were asked to complete several complex tasks related to car collisions in virtual experimentation environments. With the help of VR, students developed their understanding of physics when completing the tasks in these science laboratory activities (McElhaney & Lnn, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR92)). It should be noted that most studies frequently used task-based learning and project-based learning or inquiry-based learning synchronously (e.g., Chang & Hwang, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR22); McElhaney & Lnn, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR92); Pedersen & Irby, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR105)). Thirdly, VR/AR can be used as a platform where students can learn with games. Considering that most studies of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning focused on the elementary school stage, game-based learning is prevalent in these studies. For instance, Laine et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR69)) built an AR platform where context-aware science learning games could be created. Laine et al.’s ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR69)) results indicated that fifth-grade students preferred learning with AR games instead of regular classes. The least used learning strategy is storytelling (used in only six studies), although it is recognized as an effective way to enhance students’ science and mathematics interests (Laine et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR69)).

### 4.2 RQ2. What are the advantages and challenges of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM education?

The advantages of VR/AR in K-12 STEM learning include a positive impact on individual learners and on learning and teaching processes (see Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Tab1)).

**Table 1 Advantages of VR/AR in K-12 STEM education**

[Full size table](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z/tables/1)

The use of VR/AR in K-12 STEM learning greatly benefited individual learners. First of all, the largest number of studies (60 studies) showed that VR/AR could help improve academic achievement. For instance, Tsai et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR125)) conducted a quasi-experiment and found that the test scores of students who learned in an AR-supported laboratory environment were significantly higher than those of students who learned in a traditional classroom. However, it should be noted that using AR/VR sometimes could not help students achieve better than traditional educational technologies (e.g., digital videos). For instance, Chang et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR20)) found no significant difference between the learning achievement of students who learn with AR and with digital video. Second, 11 studies showed that VR/AR could help develop students’ cognitive abilities, such as metacognitive awareness (Ketelhut & Nelson, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR65)), analytical ability (Chien et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR30)), critical thinking skills (Chang & Hwang, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR22)), creativity (Wei et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR127)) and problem-solving skills (Wu et al., [2021a](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR130), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR131)). Third, 1 study demonstrated that the application of VR/AR could enhance learning efficiency (Iordache et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR59)), while nine studies suggested that VR/AR could enhance learning effectiveness (e.g., Chang et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR21)). The former indicates that students utilizing VR/AR support were able to attain their learning objectives more rapidly compared to those without such support, whereas the latter implies that students with VR/AR support achieved their learning goals more proficiently than those without it. Fourth, a total of 27 studies demonstrated that incorporating VR/AR devices in STEM learning could significantly enhance students’ learning motivation. For instance, Lu and Liu ([2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR84)) discovered a significant enhancement in students’ learning motivation through the use of AR in marine education. This augmentation can be attributed to the captivating three-dimensional visual effects generated by AR technology. Fifth, 14 studies indicated that students’ self-efficacy was significantly enhanced when VR/AR was applied (e.g., Cai et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR15)). VR/AR has the potential to transform abstract content into vivid and tangible experiences, thereby mitigating the challenges associated with STEM learning and further boosting students’ self-efficacy in STEM learning (Cai et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR15)). Sixth, 13 articles showed that using VR/AR in K-12 STEM learning improved students’ learning attitudes. The impact of VR/AR on attitude could also be explained by the properties of AR, which were novel and entertaining for K-12 students and could provide them with extraordinary learning experiences (Sahin & Yilmaz, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR112)). Seventh, 17 studies demonstrated that VR/AR usage could mitigate students’ cognitive load. For instance, Cho et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR33)) found that the mental load, mental effort and cognitive load of students who learned in the virtual environment were significantly lower than those of students who learned in the real environment. However, inconsistent conclusions were reached in Chiang et al.’s ([2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR29)) and Lai et al.’s ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR68)) study. Chiang et al. ([2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR29)) did not detect any significant differences between the cognitive load of students who learned with the help of mobile AR and those who learned without it. Lai et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR68)) detected a significant difference between the mental effort of students employing AR-based science learning and traditional science learning. However, they did not find any significant differences between the two groups’ mental load. Eighth, eight studies indicated that applying VR/AR could increase students’ learning satisfaction. For instance, Wei et al. ([2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR127)) found that students were more satisfied with the technology course where AR was used than the traditional one, and students’ learning satisfaction was also linked with the level of their interaction with the virtual environment. Ninth, three studies showed that VR/AR-supported STEM learning could enhance students’ cooperative ability, including leadership skills (Bouta et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR13)), decision-making skills (Bouta et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR13)), and trust-building ability among group members (Bouta et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR13)). Tenth, 12 studies revealed that VR/AR made it easier for students to concentrate in class. For instance, Lai et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR68)) claimed that the AR-based science learning approach exerted a large effect on students’ attention to learning materials with *d* = 1.28 (*d* > 0.80). In addition, 1 study pointed out that AR could improve students’ scientific literacy (Yang et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR134)). This enhancement stems from the immersive nature of AR, allowing students to actively engage in the scientific inquiry process by experiencing firsthand the collection of evidence, information processing, and communication, thereby deepening their understanding (Yang et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR134)).

The most reported impact of VR/AR on the learning and teaching processes was increased class engagement (found in 12 studies). For instance, Lim et al. ([2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR77)) indicated that class engagement could be increased as students immerse themselves in virtual environments. Second, three articles indicated that VR/AR could help visualize unobservable/invisible scientific concepts or objects. For instance, in Gun and Atasoy’s ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR44)) study, AR was used to concretize geometric objects, which students could observe from different angles and move in their hands. Third, Chang and Hwang ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR22)) proposed that using VR/AR in K-12 science experiments could provide students with personalized guidance. They claimed that AR-based systems could actively alert students when they made a wrong step and guide them based on their individual learning progression (Chang & Hwang, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR22)). Additionally, VR/AR can also support collaborative learning (e.g., Cho et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR33)), inquiry learning (Ketelhut & Nelson, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR65)), and meaningful and active learning (Guerrero et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR43)). Finally, utilizing VR/AR devices offered a means to enhance interaction within educational settings. These technologies facilitated real-time interaction, which was particularly beneficial in STEM classrooms, where they could swiftly provide feedback, thereby enhancing student learning (Cai et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR16)). VR/AR could also foster student-peer interactions, as evidenced by studies such as Ng and Chu ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR95)). The potential of VR/AR extended to enriching teacher-student interaction, further contributing to the overall educational experience (Beyoglu et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR11)).

The challenges of VR/AR in K-12 STEM learning include challenges to learners, teachers, and schools (see Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Tab2)).

**Table 2 Challenges of VR/AR in STEM education**

[Full size table](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z/tables/2)

As for challenges to learners, eight articles explicitly stated that VR/AR may induce learning distractions. For example, when presenting three-dimensional materials through VR/AR, teachers found that almost 15% of students were attracted by the three-dimensional effects, which negatively impacted their concentration on the explanation of scientific concepts (Wrzesien & Raya, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR129)). This underscored students’ need to cultivate self-regulation to adapt effectively to this innovative pedagogical mode (Lim et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR77)). This further posed challenges to the efficiency and effectiveness of teaching and learning, as stated in 2 studies. On the one hand, the use of VR/AR helped visualize abstract scientific concepts, thus increasing learning efficiency and effectiveness (Ibanez et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR58)). On the other hand, students had to spend some time adapting to its novel learning environments (Guerrero et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR43)), which may also result in inefficiency and ineffective teaching and learning processes. Furthermore, a lack of understanding of inquiry-oriented learning may also result in students “losing their way” in the virtual world, especially if they do not possess sufficient knowledge acquisition skills (Lim et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR77)). In addition, VR/AR may also have a negative effect on student imagination (Cheng, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR28)). As VR/AR can transform abstract information into concrete experiences, they may diminish students’ imagination (Cheng, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR28)). Most importantly, as five studies mentioned, students faced problems when operating VR/AR devices (e.g., Guerrero et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR43); Gun & Atasoy, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR44); Ibanez et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR58); Lim et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR77)), including difficulty in controlling the behavior of avatars, difficulty in keeping the device in one hand, and unfamiliarity with the AR materials and lack of technical competence. Lastly, the immersive nature of VR/AR could trigger discomfort (e.g., dizziness), potentially hindering students’ learning process (Holly et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR48)).

Teachers also encountered classroom management dilemmas when using VR/AR devices (Ketelhut & Nelson, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR65)). For instance, some teachers commented that VR/AR-supported science teaching was likely to be disorganized, and the loss of control in their classroom was a big challenge (Ketelhut & Nelson, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR65)). Moreover, it was difficult for them to design activities and materials with VR/AR elements and fit them into the existing curriculum (Guerrero et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR43)). Meanwhile, operating complex VR/AR equipment was also a significant challenge, as most teachers lacked the technical competence to handle VR/AR devices (Guerrero et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR43)). Lastly, as VR/AR-supported STEM learning is usually student-centered, teachers should undertake a new role to implement a series of student-centered instructional strategies (e.g., game-based teaching) (Lim et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR77)).This was also a challenge for many teachers (Lim et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR77)).

As for the challenges to schools, the cost of VR/AR equipment was a concern. The elevated VR/AR equipment price emerged as a financial burden for K-12 schools (Holly et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR48)). Furthermore, VR/AR-supported learning requires not only VR/AR equipment but also a range of supporting infrastructure, such as computers equipped with high-quality graphics cards. It is important to note that certain schools, particularly those in underdeveloped areas, may lack access to these necessary resources (Guerrero et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR43)). A dearth of dedicated virtual spaces for group activities in some schools further impacted students’ experience in VR/AR-supported STEM learning (Hsu et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR49)). Moreover, K-12 schools harbored apprehensions regarding the security of students’ and teachers’ data, privacy, and cybersecurity when employing VR/AR technologies (Hsu et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR49)).

5 Discussion
------------

Based on our findings, we initially propose six research agendas to enhance studies on AR/VR-supported K-12 STEM learning. Subsequently, our review discusses a range of significant implications for practitioners, including teachers, educators, and policymakers, addressing the intricate challenges of VR/AR implementation and maximizing its potential.

### 5.1 Recommendations for future research

#### 5.1.1 More research is needed to focus on VR/AR-supported K-12 technology and engineering learning

Between 2017 and 2022, the overall number of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning studies experienced rapid growth. This trend indicates that VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning can potentially become a hot topic of STEM education theory and practice. Meanwhile, K-12 STEM learning is also likely to be a big market for VR/AR technologies. In this sense, technology companies are expected to develop more advanced, useful and easy-to-use AR/VR devices to meet the evolving requirements of STEM learning.

Despite the increasing attention on VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning, an uneven distribution of VR/AR applications across subjects within K-12 STEM education was detected. It was found that more than 65% of the shortlisted articles focused on VR/AR-supported K-12 science learning. In other words, VR/AR-supported K-12 science learning was the largest part of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning. First, this finding indicated that researchers and educators were more interested in developing VR/AR-supported science courses than other courses, and there were more learning scenarios where AR/VR devices were suitable to be used in science disciplines than in other disciplines. Second, this finding implied a strong need for integrating VR/AR into science learning. As Zhang and Wang ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR143)) pointed out, real scientific inquiries or experiments were frequently restricted due to the lack of materials and equipment. With the support of VR/AR, scientific inquiries or experiments could be carried out in virtual environments as if they were real (Zhang & Wang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR143)). Hence, AR/VR devices may be essential for scientific inquiries or experiments in K-12 schools. Third, this finding also explained why some prior systematic reviews only focused on VR/AR-supported K-12 science learning (e.g., Zhang & Wang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR143)). Notably, our study went beyond these prior systematic reviews (e.g., Zhang & Wang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR143)), and pictured the application of VR/AR in a larger scope (i.e., STEM learning). We also found that less than 2% of the shortlisted articles focused on VR/AR-supported K-12 technology learning, and none of them focused on VR/AR-supported K-12 engineering learning. In many countries, technology is not a main subject and has a low status in K-12 schools (e.g., Nordlöf et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR96)), and engineering is not mandatory either. This may lead K-12 schools, educators and researchers to neglect technology and engineering education. Hence, little attention was paid to integrating emerging technologies into K-12 technology and engineering education. As the future society will not live without technology and engineering (Maiorca & Mohr-Schroeder, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR86)), the significance of technology education in K-12 schools has recently been underscored (Lin et al., [2024a](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR79), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR80)), and the inclusion of engineering in K-12 curriculum standards has also strongly been advocated in recent years (e.g., Jiang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR61)a; Lilly et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR76)). Considering the unexploited fields of VR/AR-supported K-12 technology and engineering, there is a pressing need to focus directly on the integration of VR/AR to support K-12 technology and engineering learning needs in the times ahead. Particularly, our study suggests that more VR/AR-supported K-12 technology and engineering materials and courses should be developed, and the unique advantages of using VR/AR in K-12 technology and engineering learning should be examined. For instance, as VR/AR may enable students to design and create in virtual environments, the application of VR/AR in K-12 engineering learning may greatly help develop students’ design thinking.

#### 5.1.2 More research is needed to focus on using immersive VR/AR devices in K-12 STEM learning

Our findings showed that non-immersive VR/AR devices (e.g., smartphone/tablet-based AR) were much more popular than immersive VR/AR devices (e.g., AR head-mounted displays) because of their small size, ease of use, and compatibility with multiple software systems. However, previous studies clearly exhibited that participants could perceive a much higher degree of realism and stronger involvement in the environments created by immersive devices than non-immersive devices (Liu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR85)). This enabled immersive devices to exert a stronger impact on students’ STEM learning than non-immersive devices (Klippel et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR66); Liu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR85)). Matovu et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR91)) claimed that science learning particularly needed the support of immersive devices. This was because immersive devices enabled science learners to conduct hands-on experiments in virtual laboratories, while it was hard for science learners to develop practical skills for doing experiments in non-immersive learning environments (Matovu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR91)). Furthermore, as Chng et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR32)) stated, AI and immersive technologies may be the next wave of emerging technologies. Hence, more research is needed to focus on using immersive VR/AR devices in K-12 STEM learning. For instance, researchers could invite K-12 students to visit field sites and conduct geological surveys with the help of immersive VR/AR devices in geography classes. As another instance, the longitudinal effects of experimenting or inquiring in immersive environments on K-12 students’ STEM learning could also be examined. In addition, it is also crucial to investigate the most recent technological development in immersive VR/AR and its potential impact on STEM education. Firstly, developing and applying more wearable immersive AR/VR technologies could enhance ease of use (Sun et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR121)). Secondly, integrating immersive AR and VR into mixed reality (MR) would allow learners to “physically interact with virtual objects in the real world” (Maas & Hughes, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR90), p. 233). As Suryodiningrat et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR122)) noted, MR technologies equipped with forward-facing cameras can enhance immersion by facilitating interaction with the physical environments, providing seamless experiences for learners. Given the particular need for immersive environments in STEM learning (e.g., Matovu et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR91)), it is important to explore the application and potential of MR in this context. Thirdly, integrating immersive AR or VR technologies with AI is essential (Lampropoulos, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR70)). For example, Yannier et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR133)) developed a mixed-reality AI system to monitor physical objects and learners’ actions during STEM experiments. Such systems can create engaging and interactive environments, while AI offers personalized experiences, significantly enhancing students’ STEM learning (Lampropoulos, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR70)). In the future, more AI-embedded immersive AR or VR technologies can be developed and applied in STEM learning.

#### 5.1.3 More research is needed to focus on VR/AR-supported STEM learning in senior high schools

Some prior literature reviews found that technology-supported learning studies often paid more attention to the high school stage than the elementary school stage (e.g., Crompton et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR34); Van Mechelen et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR126); Yohannes & Chen, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR138); Zafari et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR141); Zhang & Tur, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR142); Zhu, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR144)). However, our findings showed that most studies of VR/AR-supported STEM learning focused on the elementary school stage, while VR/AR-supported STEM learning at senior high schools was underexplored. There were two possible reasons for this. First, VR/AR’s features (e.g., interactivity, immersion, novelty, imagination) may make them more attractive to elementary school students than senior high school students. Second, our findings also reported that VR/AR in STEM learning was combined with game-based learning. Considering that game-based learning was much more suitable for elementary school students than senior high school students (Hussein et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR56)), VR/AR may also be more popular among elementary schools than senior high schools. As complex science experiments are usually involved in senior high schools, more research is needed to focus on VR/AR-supported STEM learning in senior high schools to demonstrate the unique advantages of AR/VR in supporting scientific experiments and inquiries. Meanwhile, senior high school students may encounter more abstract and complex concepts in their STEM learning. Hence, more research is needed to help senior high school students turn these abstract concepts into concrete ones by using VR/AR in the future. More importantly, senior high school students are at an important stage of choosing majors and career decision-making (Jiang et al., [2023b](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR6)). As minorities and females are underrepresented in STEM professionals (Chiu & Li, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR31)), more exploration of AR/VR to expose minority and female senior high school students to positive STEM learning and career experiences is also needed. Exploration in this direction will also contribute to a more inclusive and diverse STEM education, as called for by Chiu and Li ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR31)).

#### 5.1.4 More research is needed to pay attention to the impact of VR/AR applications on learners’ 21st-century skills

Our findings showed that most studies paid attention to the impact of VR/AR application on learners’ academic achievement and motivation. A mere 9% and 2% of the gathered studies, respectively, focused on the influence of VR/AR applications on learners’ high-order thinking skills and cooperative abilities, both integral components of 21st-century skills (Stehle & Peters-Burton, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR119)). Although learners’ academic achievement and motivation are significant in STEM learning, the emphasis of STEM education is on developing students’ 21st -century skills (e.g., Anwar et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR4); Jiang et al, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR7); Anderson & Li, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR3); Chiu & Li, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR31); Li et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR73); Li et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR75); Stehle & Peters-Burton, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR119)). Hence, more research is needed to pay attention to the impact of VR/AR applications on learners’ 21st-century skills. Particularly, as Chng et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR32)) pointed out, researchers usually focus on technologies instead of pedagogies in the field of technology-supported learning. As such, despite incorporating emerging technologies (e.g., AR/VR), it is still hard to foster the skills needed in the future with traditional pedagogies. In this sense, researchers should explore innovative, effective and feasible pedagogical approaches that harmonize with VR/AR to foster learners’ 21st-century skills (e.g., creativity, critical thinking skills, problem-solving skills, information literacy, and cooperative ability). Furthermore, given that fab labs serve as crucial environments for fostering 21st -century skills (Rayna & Striukova, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR111)), it is imperative to explore the potential of utilizing VR/AR technologies to develop enhanced virtual fab labs. These virtual environments could significantly augment STEM learning experiences, offering students innovative ways to engage with hands-on activities and collaborative problem-solving, thereby furthering the development of vital skills for the 21st century.

#### 5.1.5 More research is needed to uncover the mechanisms through which VR/AR application impacts learners

Our findings also reported some inconsistent impacts of VR/AR application on learners. For instance, regarding the impact of VR/AR application on students’ academic achievement, although it was found in 60 studies (e.g., Huang et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR54), [2022b](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR52); Liu et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR82); Onal & Onal, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR98); Tarng et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR123); Wu et al., [2021a](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR130), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR131)), it could not be confirmed in Chang et al.’s ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR20)) quasi-experiment. Regarding the advantage of VR/AR application in reducing students’ cognitive load, although it was confirmed in 17 studies (e.g., Cho et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR33)), it could not be fully verified in Chiang et al.’s ([2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR29)) and Lai et al.’s ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR68)) studies. Although it is challenging to identify the reasons for these inconsistencies, we believe that the real impact of VR/AR application will, to a large extent, depend on the ways VR/AR is used in learning, the design of learning activities, the learning content and materials, and the participants (e.g., participants’ technical competence). We found in our review that most prior studies were designed to detect the impact of VR/AR applications on learners using quantitative methods, but the studies did not adequately explain why and how VR/AR applications could impact learners. Hence, we call for more multidisciplinary research on the mechanisms through which VR/AR applications impact learners. For instance, from the neuroscience perspective, research may reveal how VR/AR affects learners’ brain activities, and how brain activities affect STEM learning and further provide insights into the design of VR/AR-supported STEM learning activities. By delving into the underlying reasons and mechanisms behind the impact of VR/AR applications on learners, researchers can ensure that the design of VR/AR-supported STEM learning activities is grounded in evidence rather than experience. This approach not only enhances the effectiveness of VR/AR applications but also secures a dependable and consistent impact.

#### 5.1.6 More research is needed to develop comprehensive frameworks to address the identified challenges of using VR/AR in K-12 STEM learning

This review systematically identified the challenges of using VR/AR in K-12 STEM learning, most of which warrant further attention. However, as we found in our review, only less than one-quarter of the collected studies mentioned AR/VR use challenges, and hardly any studies specifically investigated and discussed the challenges. Most of them only mentioned one or two challenges as their limitations and did not provide solutions to these challenges. Hence, more research is needed to develop comprehensive frameworks to systematically respond to and address the identified challenges on different levels (e.g., student, teacher, school, and policy levels). On the one hand, as aforementioned, VR/AR application has the potential to attract more minority students into STEM fields and thus make STEM education more inclusive, diverse and equitable (Chiu & Li, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR31)). On the other hand, minority students are much more likely than white students to be in high-poverty schools (Fahle et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR39); Oakes et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR97)), which may not possess VR/AR devices and infrastructures (servers, ports, viewers) (Guerrero et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR43); Holly et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR48)). Hence, minority students may have fewer chances to participate in VR/AR-supported STEM learning than their white peers. This means that the emergence of VR/AR may exacerbate educational inequality, which contradicts the initiatives for inclusive, diverse and equitable STEM education (Chiu & Li, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR31)). Therefore, it is incumbent upon researchers to reconsider strategies for addressing educational inequality stemming from the limited availability of VR/AR equipment.

Aside from the six research agendas put forward for researchers, it is also worth noting that 72.65% of the studies in the 13 years had less than 100 participants. This result notably aligned with the outcomes of a comprehensive review on AR-supported STEM education by Ibanez and Delgado-Kloos ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR57)), which reported that 75% of the studies had a sample size of 90 or below. As we pointed out, VR/AR devices were not applied on a large scale in most studies due to their complex operation and high expenses. In the future, there is potential for an increased focus among researchers on investigating the feasibility of implementing VR/AR on a larger scale.

#### 5.1.7 Practical implications

VR/AR presents numerous strengths and advantages in bolstering STEM learning, and practitioners are encouraged to leverage these benefits to enhance K-12 STEM education practices. First of all, experiments and investigations play crucial roles in STEM learning (Zhang & Wang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR143)). However, real experiments and investigations are often bound by time, space, materials and equipment in K-12 schools (Zhang & Wang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR143)). VR/AR can create virtual laboratories, which allow students to conduct STEM experiments safely (Liu et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR81)). Hence, teachers can utilise VR/AR (especially immersive VR/AR) to facilitate experimental teaching and hands-on inquiry, and further enrich students’ authentic STEM learning experiences and develop practical skills. K-12 schools can also create more VR/AR-based STEM laboratories, where teachers and students can design and implement STEM experiments easily. Furthermore, it is reported that VR/AR can provide students with individualized assistance and timely feedback, thus allowing students to perform the experiments at their own pace with appropriate prompts and instructions (Chang & Hwang, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR22)). Given the intricate nature of scientific experiments, teachers are expected to employ VR/AR to offer personalized guidance to students through each step, as suggested by Chang and Hwang ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR22)). Furthermore, VR/AR technology facilitates the visualization of three-dimensional objects that are often imperceptible to the naked eye, as highlighted by Gun and Atasoy ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR44)). Given the prevalence of abstract concepts and objects in STEM learning, teachers should utilize VR/AR to render these concepts tangible, aiding students in comprehending them more effectively. Notably, leveraging VR/AR features for timely feedback and visualizing abstract STEM concepts can enhance students’ learning efficiency, efficacy, and academic outcomes while concurrently reducing cognitive load, as evidenced by studies conducted by Huang et al., ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR54), [2022b](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR52)), Liu et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR82)), Onal and Onal ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR98)), Tarng et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR123))and Wu et al. ([2021a](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR130), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR131)).

VR/AR can vividly present a wealth of interesting images and animations (Cai et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR16)). Hence, teachers and curriculum developers can make full use of VR/AR to help enhance students’ interest, motivation and learning satisfaction in STEM education and develop students’ positive attitudes towards STEM. For instance, teachers and curriculum developers can create interesting, novel and lively VR/AR-supported games with STEM content incorporated to enhance students’ STEM interest. Furthermore, considering that some VR/AR devices can provide students with a strong sense of presence and immersion (Makransky & Mayer, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR87)), teachers can use AR/VR to improve students’ concentration and engagement (Lu & Liu, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR84); Sun et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR120)).

VR/AR can also be used to support the development of 21st-century skills (Kamarainen et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR64)). Hence, teachers should incorporate problem-solving, design, brainstorming, planning, and creative thinking activities into their VR/AR-supported STEM learning design. Teachers can also enhance students’ collaboration and communication skills by allowing students to interact with each other through their virtual avatars (Cho et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR33)). Notably, VR/AR can support the design and implementation of learning strategies, especially student-centered ones (e.g., project-based learning, inquiry-based learning), providing more possibilities for STEM curriculum development, teaching, and learning (Chen et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR24); Hachaj & Baraniewicz, 2015; Hwa, 2018). It should be noted that these student-centered activities in STEM learning can have a more positive influence on students’ 21st-century skills than traditional teacher-centered learning (Jiang et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR5); Chen & Liu, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR27)). Hence, STEM teachers can be expected to better design and implement student-centered pedagogical strategies with the support of VR/AR.

However, on the other hand, VR/AR has weaknesses too. First of all, the technical operation of VR/AR is complex and not straightforward, especially for teachers and students unfamiliar with VR/AR materials (Gun & Atasoy, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR44); Hsu et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR49); Lim et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR77)). Teachers have to spend a significant amount of time selecting the right equipment and developing the curriculum if they choose to incorporate VR/AR into STEM curriculum, and students also have to spend much time mastering the operation of VR/AR (Guerrero et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR43); Gun & Atasoy, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR44); Ibanez et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR58)). In addition, a lack of understanding of inquiry learning and teacher-student relationships may affect the effectiveness of VR/AR-supported STEM learning (Lim et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR77)). To address these issues, it is suggested that schools and institutions should establish teacher-learning communities to help teachers reduce the barriers to AR technological integration (Lin et al., [2024a](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR79), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR80)). Professional development programs tailored to teachers’ needs should be implemented to enhance their understanding of their roles in VR/AR-supported STEM teaching and to refine their pedagogical approaches. Simultaneously, technical staff should prioritize the development of more user-friendly VR/AR technologies and streamline their implementation and operation.

Regrettably, VR/AR can sometimes result in student distraction stemming from its fun and innovative environments (Wrzesien & Raya, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR129)). Hence, it is important to develop students’ self-regulation, help them master the strategies for managing distraction, and keep them from being addicted to virtual environments. Teachers and students should also ensure they do not stray from their goals when using VR/AR in STEM learning. Furthermore, like many emerging technologies, VR/AR may also lead to privacy and cybersecurity issues (Hsu et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR49)). Nowadays, some substandard equipment cannot protect teachers’ and students’ privacy well (Hsu et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR49)). Hence, greater attention is needed to protect the data generated in VR/AR-supported learning. Policymakers should formulate policies to regulate the technical standards of VR/AR.

Finally, the cost of most VR/AR devices is very high, which can become a deterrent (Ozcakir & Cakiroglu, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR100)). The use of some VR/AR devices requires a large amount of space, which brings additional educational expenses (Holly et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR48); Hsu et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR49)). In this sense, many students in underdeveloped areas may not have opportunities to use VR/AR in their learning. This may further widen educational inequity. Hence, strategies for addressing access disparities must be devised. For instance, K-12 schools in developed areas may invite students in underdeveloped areas to come and experience VR/AR-supported STEM learning during holidays.

6 Conclusion
------------

VR and AR have become valuable tools in K-12 STEM education due to their immersive and interactive capabilities. This systematic review delved into the trends, advantages, and challenges surrounding VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning. The selection of the literature, based on the PRISMA method and the scrutiny of 117 shortlisted articles, yielded significant insights into the landscape of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning.

The trends identified in this review underline the escalating interest in VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning. A consistent growth in the number of articles from 2017 to 2022 reflects a progressive surge in researchers’ attention to this field. Although science and mathematics dominated the focus of these studies, a noticeable absence of articles pertaining to engineering and technology education was observed. Smartphone/tablet-based AR emerged as the most widely adopted platform, witnessing increased utilization in recent years. Furthermore, the review highlighted an imbalance in the school study stage-specific exploration of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning, with more attention directed towards elementary and junior high school levels than senior high schools. Our findings also indicated that about three-fourths of the studies conducted over the last 13 years had sample sizes of less than 100 participants. Research methodologies leaned heavily towards quantitative methods, while the most commonly applied learning strategies encompass situated learning, task-based learning, and game-based learning.

The advantages of integrating VR/AR in K-12 STEM education are multifaceted. VR/AR technologies significantly impact learners by improving academic achievement, developing cognitive abilities, increasing learning efficiency and effectiveness, enhancing motivation, self-efficacy and satisfaction, cultivating positive attitudes, reducing cognitive load, developing cooperative ability and enhancing concentration. VR/AR improves learning and teaching processes by boosting class engagement, supporting individualized teaching, cooperative learning, inquiry learning, meaningful learning and active learning, visualizing invisible objects and concepts, and facilitating real-time interactions among students, peers, and teachers.

However, this review also highlighted challenges associated with VR/AR use. Learners faced hurdles like distractions, potential hindrances to inquiry and imagination, and a learning curve due to the novelty of these technologies. Teachers encountered challenges such as classroom management, technical unfamiliarity, and the time-intensive process of incorporating VR/AR into the curriculum. Schools, as a whole, grappled with cost-related deterrents, concerns of educational inequality stemming from varying access to technology, and apprehensions about privacy and cybersecurity.

This study also has limitations. Firstly, the reliance on the shortlisted journal articles and the exclusion of other types of literature might lead to some information bias. Future research avenues could include other literature, particularly grey reports tracking the influence of VR/AR integration over extended periods. Secondly, to ensure the inclusion of high-quality data sources in our literature review, we adhered to the recommendations of Darmawansah et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR36)), Fu and Hwang ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR41)), Huang et al., ([2022a](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR51)), Slavin ([1986](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR116)) and Xie et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR132)) by utilizing the Web of Science databases while disregarding other databases (e.g., Scopus). Future studies could consider incorporating additional databases to enhance the comprehensiveness of their reviews. Thirdly, non-English publications were excluded from our review due to language barriers. Subsequent studies could consider incorporating non-English publications and exploring VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning within diverse cultural and educational contexts. Moreover, the rapid evolution of technology could render certain findings obsolete in the near future. Hence, future research can incorporate more current literature to expand our findings. Finally, we employed Chang et al.’s ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR19)) technology-based learning model as our theoretical framework to analyze VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning. However, our review does not involve other elements (e.g., curricula design and implementation, students’ viewpoints). Future studies can explore other aspects of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning.

In conclusion, this systematic review paints a comprehensive picture of the trends, advantages, and challenges of VR/AR-supported K-12 STEM learning. The synthesis of existing literature lays the foundation for informed decisions, helping researchers, educators, and policymakers harness the potential of these technologies while navigating the associated complexities. As VR/AR technologies become more accessible, their role in K-12 STEM education will continue to expand, preparing students for the increasingly tech-driven world.

Articles were placed under mathematics (M) if their primary focus was on using VR/AR in learning algebra, geometry, analytic geometry, probability and statistics, and calculus. Articles were categorized under integrated STEM learning (iSTEM) if they primarily focused on using VR/AR in integrated STEM learning activities, where at least two of the S, T, E, and M disciplines were integrated.
4. We classified kindergarten to fifth grade into elementary schools, sixth grade to eighth grade into junior high schools, and ninth grade to twelfth grade into senior high schools (Beets et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR9); Ran et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR110)).
5. Using the Web of Science database and focusing on a decade-long publication period can ensure sufficient data for observing research trends (Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR132)).
6. Our search terms include three parts (see Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#Fig1)). The search terms for K-12-related articles were derived from Ye et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR135)), those pertaining to STEM were sourced from Sirakaya and Sirakaya ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR115)), and those associated with VR/AR were obtained from Qiu et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-024-13210-z#ref-CR108)).

### Author information

Haozhe Jiang is a ZJU100 Young Professor at College of Education, Zhejiang University. His research has appeared in many prestigious STEM Education and Educational Technology journals. His expertise spans educational technology, STEM education, and teacher education, and he excels in curriculum development and technology-enhanced learning.

Ritesh Chugh is an Associate Professor in the School of Engineering and Technology at Central Queensland University, Australia. As a socio-technological expert, his research investigates the multifaceted interactions between information technologies, individuals, and organisations, focusing on their influence, adoption, and integration in societal and organisational contexts. Ritesh is listed among the Stanford/Elsevier World’s Top 2% Scientists, recognising his impactful research. He is also known for his commitment to improving student outcomes through innovative teaching practices, earning several prestigious awards, including the International Education Association of Australia’s Best Practice in International Education Award and the Dean’s Award for Mid-Career Research Excellence. He has been recognised for his contributions to learning and teaching with the Vice-Chancellor’s Award for Good Practice and a Staff Award for International Education. Additionally, he regularly shares his expertise in socio-tech topics through public scholarship in both national and international media. Ritesh is a Senior Fellow of the Higher Education Academy, reflecting his dedication to advancing educational excellence.

Darren Turnbull received his PhD in Learning Management Systems’ implementation and deployment from Central Queensland University, Australia. His professional and research interests include learning management systems, database systems and vocational education and training in cross-national environments. He is an Information Technology Certified Professional (ITCP) of the Canadian Information Processing Society.
