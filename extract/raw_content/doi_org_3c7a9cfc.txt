## Abstract
Effective feedback is crucial for learning, especially in the English as a Foreign Language (EFL) writing context. However, teacher feedback is often delayed and difficult to scale, while automated writing evaluation systems may struggle to address complex reasoning skills such as argumentation. Although large language models (LLMs) offer new possibilities because of their ability to interpret context, their feedback remains generic, lacks transparency, and can diminish students’ mental effort. To address these challenges, this study introduces a novel chain-of-metacognitive reasoning (CMR) framework, implemented through group relative policy optimization (GRPO) using reward functions. This training procedure was used to fine-tune a small-scale open-source LLM. The resulting CMR-GRPO system, \_ArguCoach\_, generates structured reasoning chains that assess argumentative writing. Unlike existing systems, \_ArguCoach\_ drives the model to “think aloud,” modeling metacognitive strategies such as planning, monitoring, and evaluation. In the first stage, \_ArguCoach\_ achieved higher agreement and fewer errors than did leading LLMs on over 5000 argumentative essays. In the second stage, undergraduate EFL students (n = 50) receiving \_ArguCoach\_ feedback exhibited significantly greater writing performance and mental effort than did those (n = 60) with traditional teacher feedback in an eight-week quasi-experiment; notably, the writing performance gains persisted even \_after\_ the intervention concluded. Feedback acceptance was similar across groups. However, students who received \_ArguCoach\_ feedback were more likely to elaborate on suggestions with new content, whereas those who received teacher feedback were more likely to adopt suggestions directly. These findings indicate that aligning LLMs with metacognitive reasoning via reinforcement learning enables them to serve as effective partners in human–AI collaborative language education.
## Introduction
Feedback, defined as information from an agent such as a teacher regarding one's performance, is a foundation of effective learning (Hattie & Timperley, 2007). This is especially true in domains emphasizing production skills, such as English as a foreign language (EFL) writing, where feedback guides revision and improvement (Zou et al., 2025). To be educationally useful, feedback must be actionable, clearly identify areas requiring attention, and be personalized to each learner (Dawson et al., 2018). Timeliness is also critical. Feedback delivered within 48 h helps preserve task context and maximize instructional value (McCarthy, 2016), a factor particularly salient for Generation Z students, who are accustomed to instant responses and tend to dislike delays (Gabrielova & Buchko, 2021).
In EFL education, feedback has traditionally relied on human assessors such as teachers (Wang & Gayed, 2024). However, teacher feedback is time-intensive and resource-demanding (Godwin-Jones, 2022), often resulting in delays. Human judgments are also vulnerable to fatigue and bias, which can undermine reliability (Choi et al., 2025). These constraints have generated growing interest in automated writing evaluation (AWE) tools that promise timely, scalable feedback.
Despite their ability to provide immediate responses, most AWE systems are designed around surface-level features. They tend to focus on grammar (Ramesh & Sanampudi, 2022), punctuation or spelling, vocabulary (Wang & Gayed, 2024), transitional phrases (Escalante et al., 2023), organization (Liew & Tan, 2025), or essay length (Ramesh & Sanampudi, 2022). They rarely address skills essential for argumentative writing (Godwin-Jones, 2022; Wang & Gayed, 2024), such as constructing clear claims, supporting them with appropriate evidence, developing counterarguments, and sustaining persuasiveness. This limitation is likely due to traditional AWEs’ restricted capacity to understand nuanced contextual meanings.
We focus on argumentative writing because it is a cognitively demanding task closely tied to critical thinking (Hirvela, 2017). Many EFL learners struggle to engage in argumentation and to produce high-quality argumentative texts (Zou et al., 2021). Feedback that merely corrects language form is therefore insufficient; students also need support that helps them monitor and regulate their reasoning, structure their arguments, and critically evaluate their own texts. In other words, effective feedback for argumentative writing should not only be accurate and timely but should also scaffold learners’ metacognitive engagement with argumentative quality.
Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) offer new possibilities for such higher-order feedback. Unlike traditional AWE systems, LLMs can process words in context and identify argumentative moves such as claims, counterclaims, and rebuttals (Qin et al., 2025). However, the educational utility of generic LLMs remains contested. Recent empirical studies by Bui and Barrot (2025a, 2025b) indicate that while models like ChatGPT can assign scores, they often exhibit inconsistency across different time points and may apply stricter, less nuanced criteria compared to human raters. Furthermore, standard LLM feedback often suffers from a lack of transparency, functioning as a “black box” that outputs a score or revision without revealing the evaluative logic. According to the Explainable AI in Education (XAI-ED) framework proposed by Khosravi et al. (2022), this opacity hinders trust and limits the pedagogical value of the tool. Stakeholders, particularly learners, require “actionable explanations” that clarify not just what is wrong, but why it does not meet the standard, thereby enabling them to bridge the gap between their current performance and the learning goal.
To address the previous limitations, we argue that LLMs must be adapted to perform what we term “Chain-of-Metacognitive Reasoning” (CMR). While previous attempts using supervised fine-tuning (SFT) have improved score alignment, they frequently produce formulaic "pseudo-reasoning" that lacks pedagogical depth (Chen, Tu, et al., 2025; Trung et al., 2024). In contrast, \*\*CMR constrains the LLM to follow a reasoning sequence aligned with the three theoretical phases of metacognitive regulation\*\* (Flavell, 1987; Zimmerman, 2002) before arriving at a holistic score. Specifically, the architecture requires the model to: analyze rubric requirements and identify what needs to be checked (planning), inspect specific text spans against those criteria (monitoring), and synthesize these observations into a justified judgment (evaluation).
Because the final score is conditioned on this preceding evaluative output, the design enforces deliberative, criterion-referenced analysis rather than direct score prediction. Importantly, this intermediate reasoning chain is surfaced to learners as a visible think-aloud demonstration. This approach differs from standard feedback by making the underlying evaluative process transparent. This does not guarantee that learners themselves engage in metacognitive regulation, but it makes the evaluative process transparent. Rather than simply delivering a score, the ArguCoach system articulates how a writer would check argument quality. For example, the system explicitly notes whether a rebuttal is present before judging the counterargument dimension. By exposing this step-by-step analytic process, CMR provides a metacognitively structured scaffold that students can observe and apply to support their own writing.
To implement this framework, we utilize GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), a reinforcement learning technique that directly optimizes the quality of these reasoning chains without relying on extensive human annotations. The resulting system, ArguCoach, drives the model to “think aloud” in a structured, rubric-aligned manner. This approach aims to mitigate the rating inconsistency issues identified by Bui and Barrot (2025a) while fulfilling the XAI-ED goal of transparency. By producing feedback that is both technically accurate and pedagogically interpretable, ArguCoach is designed to scaffold students’ metacognitive engagement with their writing.
Therefore, this study has two overarching objectives: to determine whether ArguCoach can provide valid, rubric-based assessments of argumentative essays compared with leading large language models (LLMs), and to examine how ArguCoach-supported feedback affects EFL students’ outcomes in authentic classroom settings.
Accordingly, we address four research questions. First, how does ArguCoach perform, relative to leading commercial and open-source LLMs, in rubric-based assessment of argumentative essays at the technical level? Second, what is the effect of ArguCoach's feedback, compared with traditional teacher feedback, on Chinese EFL students' argumentative writing performance, and to what extent are any gains retained after the intervention? Third, how do students receiving ArguCoach feedback differ from those receiving teacher feedback in their invested mental effort, feedback acceptance, and metacognitive behaviors? Fourth, what are students' perceptions of ArguCoach's feedback?

## Participants
Stage 2 included 110 undergraduate engineering-major students who were taking an EFL course. Their average age was 19 years. They were grouped according to their existing classes at the start of the Spring 2025 semester. Following a quasi-experimental design, one intact class (N = 50) was assigned as the experimental group (EG) and another (N = 60) as the control group (CG).
