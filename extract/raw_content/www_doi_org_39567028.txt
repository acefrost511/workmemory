## Abstract
Given the significant and unique contributions of both in-class and out-of-class learning, pedagogical initiatives that connect learners' experiences across these two learning spheres would bolster language development. Technology can catalyse the integration. Whether and how teachers utilize this potential of technology to engage in digital bridging initiatives, initiatives that support the connection of students' in-class and out-of-class learning experiences with digital resources, deserve attention. Thematic analysis of 13 interview responses and exploratory and confirmatory factor analyses of 1101 survey responses of primary and secondary school Chinese EFL teachers unravelled two dimensions of digital bridging initiatives: inward bridging practices and outward bridging practices. SEM analysis of the survey responses further revealed various determinants of teachers' bridging initiatives, underscoring the strong and direct influence of teachers' awareness of students' out-of-class digital behaviours and the mediated influence of school culture. The study further highlighted the prominence of teacher internal factors, such as educator-oriented identity belief, awareness of students' out-of-class digital behaviours, and TPACK, for outward bridging. The study advocates attention to the nuances of teachers' digital bridging initiatives and influencing factors across contexts.
### Practitioner notes
## What is already known about this topic
\* Connecting formal and informal learning is beneficial to language development.
\* Teachers exhibit limited engagement in bridging digital resources across learning spheres.
## What this paper adds
\* A two-dimensional taxonomy of teachers' digital bridging initiatives in utilizing digital resources to connect formal and informal learning—inward and outward bridging.
\* Teachers' awareness of students' out-of-class digital behaviours is prominent predictor in the context of digital bridging.
\* The influencing factors of inward and outward bridging initiatives differed.
\* Value belief significantly predicted inward bridging but not outward bridging.
\* Educator-role identity belief was more important for outward bridging.
\* The mediation role of internal factors on the influence of external factors was more salient in outward bridging than inward bridging.
## Implications for practice and/or policy
\* Teacher training program needs to pay greater attention to promoting primary and secondary teachers' digital bridging initiatives.
\* Educators need to support two types of digital bridging—inward bridging and outward bridging.
\* Educators need to pay attention to different factors when supporting these two types of digital bridging initiatives.
\* Shaping teachers' role identity beliefs and enhancing their resourcefulness to circumvent sociocultural constraints are essential when promoting outward bridging.
## INTRODUCTION
Learning is a holistic experience that involves boundary-crossing across various formal and informal spaces and practices, and educational practices that link spheres of learning are essential to augmenting interest-driven and meaningful learning. In-class and out-of-class learning form a “dialectical unity”, where one brings the other into existence and the capacities fostered in one inform and transform those in the other (Marsick et al., [2017](#bjet13595-bib-0043)). Connecting the two is not only desirable but also plausible, and researchers have theorized and empirically substantiated the trans-contextual transferability of motivation and learning (e.g., Lai, [2015a](#bjet13595-bib-0074); Hagger & Chatzisarantis, [2012](#bjet13595-bib-0015); Lamb & Arisandy, [2020](#bjet13595-bib-0032)). Teachers are hence expected to bridge learners' experiences inside and outside the classroom to bolster in-class learning and boost learners' out-of-class learning dispositions and behaviours (Hagger & Chatzisarantis, [2012](#bjet13595-bib-0015)).
In the field of second language learning, the omnipresence of digital and online resources has sparked a growing scholarly interest in learners' out-of-class digital experiences (Reinders et al., [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0054); Soyoof et al., [2023](#bjet13595-bib-0061)). Research studies have substantiated cognitive and affective benefits of informal digital experiences for language development (Lai, [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0076); Cole & Vanderplank, [2016](#bjet13595-bib-0007); Liu et al., [2023](#bjet13595-bib-0038); Soyoof et al., [2023](#bjet13595-bib-0061)). Researchers further point out that both in-class and out-of-class learning contribute significantly to language development, serving different yet complementary functions (Lai, [2015a](#bjet13595-bib-0074); Reinders et al., [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0054)). Synergizing students' in-class and out-of-class learning experiences benefits language development. For instance, Holm ([2020](#bjet13595-bib-0019)) discovered that incorporating students' daily life interests in assignments could help boost students' interest in the language and enhance their in-class participation. Class activities that engage students in metalinguistic analyses of language use in authentic online social spaces are reported to enhance students' socio-pragmatic awareness and cyberpragmatics, which strengthened their intention for informal digital practices (Reinhardt & Ryu, [2013](#bjet13595-bib-0055); Yeh & Mitric, [2023](#bjet13595-bib-0072)).
Technology may catalyse the integration, blurring the boundaries and creating co-located spaces of formal and informal learning (Lewin et al., [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0035)). How teachers utilize the bridging potential of technology warrants further research. Research has shown that although teachers value online informal learning for language development and acknowledge the importance of establishing a connection between in-class and out-of-class learning, they exhibit limited engagement in bridging activities (Lai et al., [2016](#bjet13595-bib-0031); Toffoli & Sockett, [2015](#bjet13595-bib-0065)). Interviews with Hong Kong university foreign language teachers in Lai et al. ([2016](#bjet13595-bib-0031)) revealed that these teachers assumed a limited role in facilitating students' online informal language learning. Only half of the French EFL university professors in Toffoli and Sockett's ([2015](#bjet13595-bib-0065)) study reported having engaged in bridging activities, which included integrating online resources into teaching materials or designing activities to raise learners' awareness of the relevance of everyday experiences to English learning. Few studies have examined the nature of teachers' digital bridging initiatives, i.e., teaching practices that involve the use of digital resources to synergise students' in-class and out-of-class learning, and the influencing factors (Schurz & Sundqvist, [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0060)). The study aimed to fill the research gap by answering the following research questions:
\* RQ1: How do second language teachers engage in digital bridging initiatives?
\* RQ2: What factors influence second language teachers' digital bridging initiatives?
## RESEARCH BACKGROUND
### Teachers' digital bridging initiatives
Scholars have put forth two major arguments in support of bridging in-class and out-of-class learning. One argument lies in bridging the authenticity gap and “motivational dissonances” of the two learning contexts to the effect of boosting in-class engagement and deepening knowledge construction (Henry et al., [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0018); Michel et al., [2021](#bjet13595-bib-0044); Schurz & Sundqvist, [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0060)). These scholars advocate integrating digital wild artefacts into the curriculum and connecting class contents with students' out-of-class experiences (Godwin-Jones, [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0012); Hafner et al., [2015](#bjet13595-bib-0014)). The other argument lies in broadening students' learning experience (Lai, [2015b](#bjet13595-bib-0075); Hubbard, [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0024); Lewin et al., [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0035)). These scholars emphasize reorienting class instruction towards supporting learners' everyday practices in the target language and raising their metacognitive awareness and metalinguistic skills for effective participation to the effect of empowering learners to extend learning beyond the classroom (Eskildsen et al., [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0010); Thorne & Reinhardt, [2008](#bjet13595-bib-0064)).
Schurz and Sundqvist ([2022](#bjet13595-bib-0060)) is so far the only study that examined the nature of language teachers' digital bridging initiatives. Surveying 534 secondary school English teachers from four European countries on their perceptions and practices of connecting English language instruction with students' extramural English, the researchers identified three factors: perceived importance of establishing the connection, bringing extramural English material to class, and compensating for the weakness of informal extramural English language use. They found that teachers from all four countries consistently expressed positive perceptions towards the importance of establishing the connection and reported integrating extramural materials into class to link the two spheres. However, teachers from Sweden and Finland, countries with greater extramural English exposure, reported greater engagement in using class instruction to compensate for the language skills and aspects not supported in extramural English exposure. The study thus revealed that teachers' digital bridging initiatives may vary in response to linguistic and socio-cultural circumstances. The factor structure of digital bridging initiatives might be different in EFL contexts where learners have constrained exposure to extramural English resources. Moreover, their survey items were developed based on Henry et al.'s ([2018](#bjet13595-bib-0018)) findings on teachers' motivation strategies in classroom activity design, and the factor structure derived hence centered primarily around bridging for class engagement and learning, with little consideration of bridging for broadening and diversifying students' learning experiences. Thus, more studies are needed to explore the nature of teachers' digital bridging initiatives.
Language learners in China do not have convenient access to online English spaces such as Google, YouTube, Netflix, and Instagram, as quite a few online spaces are blocked due to the country's internet censorship policy. Yet, there are abundant digital English learning resources, mostly instructional English materials, developed within China. Studies in this context have found that English language learners engage actively in informal digital English learning, circumventing the inconvenient access to online English spaces (An et al., [2023](#bjet13595-bib-0001); Lai et al., [2024](#bjet13595-bib-0079); Lai, Liu, et al., [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0030); Zhang & Liu, [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0073)). Encouragement and support from significant others, such as teachers, has been consistently reported as a robust determinant of these learners' informal digital behaviours (Lai, Liu, et al., [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0030); Zhang & Liu, [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0073)). English language teachers in Liu and Wang ([2024](#bjet13595-bib-0039)) showed positive intentions to encourage and support students' informal digital English learning and report integrating students' informal digital activities into extracurricular tasks and course assessment. However, the nature of Chinese EFL teachers' digital bridging initiatives remained unknown.
### Factors that influence teachers' digital bridging initiatives
According to Ertmer and Ottenbreit-Leftwich ([2010](#bjet13595-bib-0008)), teaching behaviours that involve technological resources are situated at the interplay of two types of barriers: the first-order/external barriers and the second-order/internal barriers. The first-order/external barriers are barriers at the culture and resource level, such as school expectation and support, technological and instructional resources, and institutional policy and plans. The second-order/internal barriers are underlying beliefs and knowledge (Kopcha, [2012](#bjet13595-bib-0029)). The influence of first-order/external barriers (culture and resource) on technological behaviours is often mediated by the second-order/internal barriers (knowledge and belief), where teachers' knowledge and beliefs often reduce or magnify the influence of school culture and support on teacher technology behaviours (Ertmer & Ottenbreit-Leftwich, [2010](#bjet13595-bib-0008); Vongkulluksn et al., [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0069)). As digital bridging initiatives are teaching behaviours that involve digital resources, this theoretical framework was referred to in conceptualizing potential influencing factors. In this study, we focused on two constitutive dimensions of the second-order barriers: belief and knowledge, and two first-order factors that are persistent barriers to teachers' technology integration efforts: school culture and professional learning (Ertmer et al., [2012](#bjet13595-bib-0009); Hsu & Kuan, [2013](#bjet13595-bib-0021)).
#### Belief
Teacher belief is teachers' implicit assumptions “about their work, their students, their subject matter and their roles and responsibilities” (Pajares, [1992](#bjet13595-bib-0050), p. 314). Among various dimensions of teacher belief, teachers' identity belief about their roles and responsibilities concerning educational goals, namely, visions of education and concomitant role identification, is found to influence their likelihood of legitimizing and leveraging students' experiences in informal learning environments (Schneider, [2004](#bjet13595-bib-0059)). Teachers who hold educator role orientation, i.e. identification with the role of fostering students' holistic development as a learner and as a person beyond subject qualifications, are observed to show greater tendency towards technology use in support of student learning (Lai & Jin, [2021](#bjet13595-bib-0077); Li et al., [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0036)). This identity belief may contribute positively to digital bridging initiatives as well.
Another belief factor is value belief on the usefulness of technology in learning. Value belief is the strongest determinant of teachers' technology integration (Cheng & Xie, [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0005); Hsu, [2016](#bjet13595-bib-0020); Vongkulluksn et al., [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0069)), and may very well predict their digital initiatives to connect in-class and out-of-class learning. Educator-oriented identity belief may contribute to value belief, as teachers' self-construal and epistemological beliefs, such as role identity belief, lie in the central positions of one's belief system and may shape beliefs in relation to teaching and learning (Lai & Jin, [2021](#bjet13595-bib-0077); Rokeach, [1968](#bjet13595-bib-0056)). Thus, we hypothesized:
H1.Educator-oriented identity belief predicted digital bridging initiatives both directly and indirectly via value belief.
H2.Value belief directly predicted digital bridging initiatives.
#### Knowledge
Teachers' knowledge about themselves and about their students may also shape classroom digital behaviours. Teachers' perception of their professional knowledge to interweave technology with content and pedagogy, i.e., Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge (TPACK: Mishra & Koehler, [2006](#bjet13595-bib-0046)), is widely acknowledged as a significant predictor of technology integration (Tseng et al., [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0066); Vongkulluksn et al., [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0069)). Self-appraisal of this knowledge base might also be critical to their decision-making on using digital resources to connect in-class and out-of-class learning. In effect, Liu and Wang ([2024](#bjet13595-bib-0039)) found that teachers' subjective evaluation of their ability to integrate informal English materials into course instruction positively predicted intention of digital bridging.
Teachers' knowledge about student characteristics, such as learning preference and digital competence, is critical contextual knowledge that shapes their digital teaching behaviours (Angeli & Valanides, [2009](#bjet13595-bib-0002)). Studies have shown that teachers' beliefs about students' self-initiated use of technology beyond the classroom could incentivize their leverage of technology for educational purposes (Ertmer et al., [2012](#bjet13595-bib-0009)). This aspect of teacher knowledge might be even more critical to teachers' intention for digital activities that bridge in-class and out-of-class learning. Liu and Wang ([2024](#bjet13595-bib-0039)) found that teachers' perception of students' engagement in informal digital learning predicted intention of digital bridging. We hence hypothesized:
H3.TPACK directly predicted digital bridging initiatives.
H4.Awareness of students' out-of-class digital behaviours directly predicted digital bridging initiatives.
#### School culture
School culture refers to schools' expectations and support of teachers' technology use. It is a prominent external factor that influences teacher technology integration (Huang et al., [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0023); Uslu & Usluel, [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0068)). Liu and Wang ([2024](#bjet13595-bib-0039)) found that teachers' perception of school-level ideological structure co-constructed by colleagues, students and supervisors pertaining to the integration of informal digital learning into courses could shape their intention of bridging. Similarly, Liu ([2023](#bjet13595-bib-0037)) revealed that institutional constraints such as the test culture and workload also shaped teachers' digital initiative in support of critical digital literacy. Moreover, school culture also influences teacher digital behaviours via technology-related belief and knowledge factors (Ertmer & Ottenbreit-Leftwich, [2010](#bjet13595-bib-0008); Vongkulluksn et al., [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0069)). The stronger teachers perceive school culture in support of digital behaviours, the more likely they may perceive the value of such behaviours and direct attention to their students' digital behaviours and their own technological competency. Previous studies have evidenced that perceived expectations and support from school predict teachers' technology behaviours indirectly via value belief (Bice & Tang, [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0004); Huang & Teo, [2020](#bjet13595-bib-0022)) and TPACK self-efficacy (Lai, Wang et al., [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0078); Nelson et al., [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0048)). We hence hypothesized:
H5.School culture predicted digital bridging initiatives both directly and indirectly via value belief, TPACK and awareness of students' out-of-class digital behaviours.
#### Professional learning
Another robust external factor of digital behaviours is professional learning. Professional learning can be in the form of both organized training events and self-experimentation, self-initiated learning, and peer discussions. Both formal and informal professional learning contribute significantly to teachers' technology integration (Han et al., [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0017); Nazari & Xodabande, [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0047)). Researchers also found that professional learning predicts technology behaviours indirectly via TPACK (Lai, Wang et al., [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0078); Xie et al., [2017](#bjet13595-bib-0071)). Moreover, teachers' perception of school expectations on technology is an antecedent to participation in professional learning initiatives (Lai, Wang et al., [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0078)). Thus, we hypothesized:
H6.Professional learning predicted digital bridging initiatives both directly and indirectly via TPACK.
H7.School culture directly predicted professional learning.

## METHOD
This study adopted an explanatory sequential mixed method design with a survey followed up by individual semi-structured interviews (Ivankova et al., [2006](#bjet13595-bib-0025)). The survey was to determine and validate the factor structure of digital bridging activities and to test the hypothesized relationships of the predicting factors and digital bridging activities. The interview was to explain the quantitative findings and tap deeper into the reasons behind the observed relationships.
### Participants
The survey participants were 1101 primary and secondary EFL teachers at a province in southern China. We mass emailed the questionnaire link to English panel heads and English language education officers in both urban and rural regions to elicit their help in recruiting participants. The sample size was sufficient to ensure adequate statistical power and reliable parameter estimates for Structural Equation Modelling (SEM) analysis, the statistical analysis adopted in this study, as the estimated parameters for the assessed model were 60 and thus observations per estimated parameter exceeded 10 (Bentler & Chou, [1987](#bjet13595-bib-0003)). The participants were mainly primary school teachers, with a large proportion from rural schools. They had a wide range of teaching experience, with an average of 8 years of technology integration experience. Their self-rated ICT efficacy ranged from average to good. The majority reported technological access in classrooms and students having access to personal digital devices at home (see Appendix Table [A1](#bjet13595-tbl-0004) for the participant profiles). Ethical clearance approval was obtained from the university prior to the study. The consent letter was posted on the first page of the online survey, and the participants were instructed to check the consent box before proceeding to the survey. All of the teachers participated anonymously in this study.
13 teachers were recruited from those who indicated willingness to participate in follow-up interviews. The interview participants were selected based on maximum variation criteria. We strove to include teachers who reported different levels of engagement in digital bridging activities, from both rural and urban schools, taught at different grade levels, and had varying teaching experience (See Appendix Table [A2](#bjet13595-tbl-0005) for the participant profiles).
### Data sources and collection
Data sources consist of questionnaire and interview responses. The questionnaire assessed the participants' digital bridging activities and influencing factors. The follow-up interview was conducted to tap into their decision-making around different types of digital bridging activities.
#### Questionnaire
The questionnaire measured teachers' engagement in digital bridging activities, the two belief factors (educator-oriented identity belief and value belief), the two knowledge factors (TPACK and awareness of students' out-of-class digital behaviours), school culture, and professional learning.
\*\*Digital bridging activities\*\* (\*n\* = 10, ɑ = 0.94) measured the extent of agreement (1 = strongly disagree; 6 = strongly agree) on an array of class teaching activities that involve the use of digital resources to enhance in-class learning and broaden students' learning experience (Eskildsen et al., [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0010); Henry et al., [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0018); Michel et al., [2021](#bjet13595-bib-0044)). Both adapted items from existing literature (Schurz & Sundqvist, [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0060); Upara & Chusanachoti, [2023](#bjet13595-bib-0067)) and researcher-constructed items were used to measure this construct, using items like “I encourage students to use English digital resources on their own beyond the classroom”.
\*\*Educator-oriented identity belief\*\* (\*n\* = 3, ɑ = 0.88) measured agreement with their roles in supporting students' holistic development beyond subject knowledge and skills, using items from Lai and Jin ([2021](#bjet13595-bib-0077)) such as “My primary focus is to make a difference in students' life”. \*\*Value belief\*\* (\*n\* = 3, ɑ = 0.95) measured agreement with the value of technology in students' English learning, using researcher-constructed items such as “I believe that technology can enhance English learning effects”. Both constructs were measured using 6-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree; 6 = strongly agree).
\*\*TPACK\*\* (\*n\* = 7, ɑ = 0.95) assessed self-appraisal of knowledge in using technology to achieve pedagogical goals, focusing on the central and outcome component of the TPACK model, TPCK (Cheng & Xie, [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0005)). It was measured using existing items from Lai, Wang et al., ([2022](#bjet13595-bib-0078)), such as “I know how to enrich students' computer-supported self-directed English learning experience”. \*\*Awareness of students' out-of-class digital behaviours\*\* (\*n\* = 6, ɑ = 0.97) measured the participants' appraisal of students' use of digital and online English resources for incidental and intentional learning beyond the classroom (Lee, [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0033)), using researcher-constructed items such as “My students use digital resources to access English beyond the classroom”. Both constructs were measured using 6-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree; 6 = strongly agree).
\*\*School culture\*\* (\*n\* = 3, ɑ = 0.87) measured both institutional and colleague culture concerning expectations and support on the integration of technology for English teaching and learning (King & Boyatt, [2015](#bjet13595-bib-0026)), using researcher-constructed items such as “My school has expectations on us to use ICT resources and tools to support English teaching and learning” on a 6-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree; 6 = strongly agree). \*\*Professional learning\*\* (\*n\* = 5, ɑ = 0.85) measured the frequency of various formal and informal professional learning activities, using items adapted from Petko et al. ([2015](#bjet13595-bib-0051)) such as “I attend pedagogical training related to technology integration in English instruction” on a 5-point Likert scale items (1 = never; 5 = often).
The questionnaire was administered online in Chinese via a commonly used online survey platform, Wenjuanxin (). The questionnaire was pilot tested with 23 primary and secondary school English language teachers from different schools (not included in the main study) to check the representativeness of the digital bridging activities. Teachers in the pilot test did not suggest additional digital bridging activities, indicating that the measured items were a full representation of their digital bridging activities. Yet, these teachers did suggest changes to some wordings in the measuring items. For example, they suggested changing “digital resources” to “digital resources related to the teaching topics” for the item “I ask students to bring back to class the digital resources related to the teaching topics that they encounter outside of class”.
#### Interview
Individual semi-structured interviews were conducted to elicit each participant's reflections on the activities they partook in using digital resources to connect students' in-class with out-of-class learning, the motives behind engaging in these activities, the challenges they encountered in the process, and their responses to the challenges. The interview was phrased in ways that allow questions to be “formulated in, developed through and shaped by” the conversation (Mishler, [1986](#bjet13595-bib-0045), p. 52). The interview was conducted in Chinese, the participants' native language, on a one-on-one basis via an online conferencing platform. Each interview lasted around 40–50 minutes, and was recorded and transcribed automatically by the online conferencing system and double checked by a research assistant.
### Data analysis
Factor analyses were conducted to determine the factor structure of digital bridging, with exploratory factor analysis (EFA) on half of the data (\*n\* = 521) to determine the factor structure, and confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) on the other half (\*n\* = 580) to confirm the structure. Principal axis factoring and promax rotation was used for EFA since the constructs are related. Bartlett's test and Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin (KMO) (>0.60) were used to assess scale factorability, and scree plot was used to determine the number of factors (Kline, [2016](#bjet13595-bib-0027); Worthington & Whittaker, [2006](#bjet13595-bib-0070)). For item screening, cut-off values of 0.32 was used for item loadings and 0.15 difference for cross-loadings (Worthington & Whittaker, [2006](#bjet13595-bib-0070)). CFA, using Maximum Likelihood Estimation as the estimation method, was conducted in AMOS 28.0, with the following model fit indices (Tabachnick et al., [2013](#bjet13595-bib-0062)): Chi-square divided by Degrees of Freedom (CMIN/DF) < 3 and Standardized Root Mean Square Residual (SRMR) < 0.08 (the absolute indices), Root Mean Square Error of Approximation (RMSEA) < 0.05 (the parsimonious indices), and Comparative Fit Index (CFL) > 0.95 and Tucker-Lewis Index (TLI) > 0.95 (the incremental fit indices). Structural equation modelling (SEM) was then conducted on the whole dataset, controlling for teachers' educational level, teaching experience, ICT self-efficacy, and technology integration experience, to evaluate the conceptualized relationships. The model testing used Maximum Likelihood Estimation as the estimation method and a bootstrap method with 5000 resamples and a 95% confidence level in the bias-corrected confidence interval. Before the model testing, potential common method bias had been checked via full collinearity variance inflation factors (VIFs) and Harman's single factor test via principal component factor analysis (Kock & Lynn, [2012](#bjet13595-bib-0028); Podsakoff et al., [2003](#bjet13595-bib-0052)). Both tests were satisfactory, with Harman's test yielding a value of 40% (<50%) and all VIFs smaller than 3.
Thematic analysis was conducted on the interview through a reiterative process of coding and recoding (Saldaña & Omasta, [2016](#bjet13595-bib-0057)). Structural coding was conducted in reference to the interview questions to generate data segments of interests (e.g., segments on the challenges of digital bridging activities). In-vivo and summary codes were used to code each data segment. Second cycle analysis were conducted to assess the utility of the codes, recode, categorize or discard codes accordingly. Pattern coding was then conducted to categorize codes that conveyed similar meanings under overarching codes. For instance, codes such as “Internet addiction”, “find excuses to play games” and “goof around on mobile” were grouped into the overarching category of “concern over students' lack of control over digital devices”. Axial coding was later performed to capture the relationships between the categories to form analytical themes (e.g., “concern over students' lack of control induced intervention on digital habits”), which were then compared across participants to reveal how participant attributes may moderate the relationships. Analytic memos were used to facilitate data coding and categorization (see Appendix Table [A3](#bjet13595-tbl-0006) for the coding scheme).
## RESULTS
### Dimensions of digital bridging activities
#### Factors revealed in EFA and CFA analyses
EFA analysis revealed satisfactory scale factorability. One item (“I adjust teaching to focus on enhancing students' language use strategies, such as comprehension and inferencing strategies”) had cross-loading issues and showed problematic communality value, below the threshold value of 0.40 (Osborne, [2008](#bjet13595-bib-0049)), and was hence dropped. EFA analysis on the remaining 10 items yielded satisfactory scale factorability: KMO = 0.91 and a significant Barlett's test result (\*p\* < 0.000). The communalities of all items exceeded the threshold value. The scree plot suggested 2 factors, and the 2 factors explained 67% of the variance in the dataset (see Appendix Table [A4](#bjet13595-tbl-0007) for the factor structure). CFA analysis revealed satisfactory model fit (CMIN/DF = 2.38, SRMR = 0.02, CFI = 0.99, TLI = 0.99, RMSEA = 0.049(0.034; 0.064)), confirming the factor structure. Factor 1 was bridging activities that involved adaptation in teaching content and materials, referred to as Inward Bridging Practices. Factor 2 was bridging activities that aimed at broadening student learning beyond the classroom, referred to as Outward Bridging Practices. Survey participants reported positive yet low levels of engagement in both Inward Bridging Practices (\*M\* = 4.39, \*M\* = 0.75), and Outward Bridging Practices (\*M\* = 4.29, \*SD\* = 0.75) falling between “slightly agree” and “agree”.
#### Teachers' interview report on bridging activities and reasons
The interview responses revealed various manifestations of Inward Bridging Practices, such as “play(ing) videos related to the topic of the day to draw their attention”, and “ask(ing) (students) to look for relevant resources in Bilibili and play in class to develop a comprehensive view of the topic”. The participants reported engaging in such activities mainly to enhance class engagement and enriching and strengthening in-class learning. The interview responses also revealed examples of Outward Bridging Practices include encouraging students to “access authentic resources and read widely to know what's happening in the outside world”, creating resource portal for students to access on their own, teaching students comprehension strategies such as “the genre characteristics”, and engaging students “to share what they read outside the classroom in the past month” to “boost their self-efficacy and sense of pride”. The participants reported engaging in such activities for a myriad of purposes, including students' holistic development, such as broadening horizons, and the development of positive learning attitudes and motivation.
The interview responses revealed some reasons behind the low levels of engagement in both types of digital bridging activities. The lack of appropriate digital resources and the constraining sociocultural circumstances in the teaching context were frequently brought up as hurdles to digital bridging. For instance, Chen, a rural primary school teacher, reflected that her bridging attempt was challenged by colleagues with remarks such as “Your class is interesting and fun, but how much do students learn? Do you have enough time to cover the textbook?”. She also observed incompatible ideologies from parents: “At our town, parents care about exam grades only and feel that English learning should be based on textbook only”. To Wang, an urban secondary school teacher, the hurdle lay in the time needed to locate and prepare appropriate digital resources: “The online texts are too difficult for my students. I have to adapt them to fit my students' proficiency, which takes a lot of time”.
### Influencing factors of digital bridging activities
CFA on the conceptual model revealed that the indicators were well represented by the constructs: CMIN/DF = 2.95, SRMR = 0.04, CFI = 0.97, TLI = 0.97, RMSEA = 0.042(0.040; 0.044). The unidimensionality test result was satisfactory, with all standardized loadings above 0.60 (Hair et al., [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0016)). All the AVEs were above 0.50, indicating good convergent validity, and the square root of AVEs was all larger than the inter-construct correlations, suggesting good discriminant validity (see Table [1](#bjet13595-tbl-0001)). Cronbach alpha values of all the constructs were above 0.85 (>0.70), suggesting good construct reliability. Composite reliability values were all above 0.80 (>0.70). Thus, both reliability indicators suggested good construct reliability (see Table [1](#bjet13595-tbl-0001) and Appendix Table [A5](#bjet13595-tbl-0008)). SEM analysis yielded satisfactory model fit: CMIN/DF = 2.99; SRMR = 0.04; CFI = 0.96; TLI = 0.96; RMSEA = 0.044(0.041; 0.045).

The effect of school culture was fully mediated, and the hypothesized direct effect was not significant (\*β\* = −0.04, \*p\* > 0.05). While the participants reported that various school culture constraints (e.g., “school's no mobile policy”, “school surveillance on online resource access”, “panel head's expectation on covering the standardized content in the curriculum”, and colleagues' challenges) hindered inward bridging initiatives, their interview responses showed that a supportive school culture did not necessarily contribute to inward bridging either. Teachers recounted being constrained by considerations more integral to classroom technological behaviours, such as conception of teaching and learning, and assessment of student situations. For instance, although Luo's school supported the incorporation of digital resources, helping her students grasp the challenging contents in the textbook occupied most of her instructional time, which caused “insufficient time” to bring in external audiovisual resources despite her intention to do so. Similarly, Zhang, a rural secondary school teacher, attributed his minimal inward bridging to limited instructional hour compounded by internalized belief about textbook: “If I incorporated additional digital resources, I wouldn't be able to cover the textbook. The textbook is the best resource as its quality is safeguarded by experts, much better than what I could find out there”. His appraisal of students' language proficiency level served as a further constraint: “I can't incorporate digital resources to expand and enrich in-class learning just to meet the needs of a few, while the majority of the class could not follow”. Thus, consistent with Vongkulluksn et al.'s ([2018](#bjet13595-bib-0069)) finding, the effect of perceived school support was rather moderated by teachers' beliefs and context understanding that were more integral to teaching.
Awareness of students' out-of-class digital behaviours emerged as a prominent determinant of teachers' inward bridging activities, with the largest direct effect (\*β\* = 0.36, \*p\* < 0.001). It was also the strongest mediator of the influence of school culture (\*β\* = 0.20, \*p\* < 0.001). The direct association was corroborated in interview responses. For instance, Chen, an urban secondary school teacher, remarked that upon observing students' attraction to Internet news, she and her colleagues searched for hot topics online to arouse students' interest so that “they may participate more actively during the class”. Similarly, ChenTu's observation of her urban senior high school students' exposure to up-to-date digital resources pressured her to integrate similar materials and topics into teaching. Interview responses further illustrated that such awareness could shape how they engaged in bridging activities. For instance, Wang's school encouraged the integration of digital resources to bolster students' autonomous learning. She observed that her junior secondary school students tended to consume informal digital resources in a “fast food style without paying attention to the language and content”, and thus “couldn't get much out of interacting with digital resources”. She hence intentionally used online news in class, and guided students on “how to read, and what to pay attention to” when interacting with these materials.

The non-significant negligible association of value belief with outward bridging practices (\*β\* = 0.04, \*p\* > 0.05) contradicted with the hypothesis. This might be because outward bridging was subject to myriad factors that were beyond teachers' control. Take Chenyu, an urban primary school teacher, as an example. Despite believing in the value of songs for incidental English learning, Chenyu seldom recommended songs to her students out of the concern of exposing them to “age-inappropriate lyrics in the pop songs”, which she feared might arouse parents' complaints. She also worried that “[students] might not like the songs [she] recommended” due to the generation gap. Similarly, Ou, a rural primary school teacher, believed strongly in the value of digital resources for English learning: “In backward mountain areas like ours, it's hard to learn English well without digital and online resources”. Thus, she assigned homework that involved the use of digital resources. However, out of the concern of incurring parents' complaints and of violating government's policy and school's reinforcement of no homework for primary school kids, she kept such outward bridging activities to the minimal. Most interview participants echoed concerns over students' undisciplined use of electronic devices, and observed parental surveillance on children's digital consumption, which was compounded by parents' fixation on short-term exam results and tendency to equate English learning solely with textbook study. Such observations made teachers refrain from outward bridging activities despite perceived value of digital resources. Notwithstanding, teachers' educator-oriented identity beliefs buffered against those barriers. For instance, Zhang regarded his primary responsibility as supporting students' autonomous learning and holistic development. He believed that using out-of-class digital resources of personal interest helped expand students' horizons, and was a necessity given the limited access to physical English resources in town. Thus, despite observing his rural secondary school students' addiction to digital tools and being misled by biased information online, instead of reducing outward bridging, he recommended English digital resources as a way to counter students' entertainment-oriented mobile use habits, guiding them on the type of resources to access, and supporting them on critical assumption of online information. Similarly, Liu, an urban secondary school teacher, beholding a role identity of fostering independent thinking and autonomous learning, surmounted the observed misuse of mobile devices through curating and recommending engaging and useful digital resources for students. Wang, self-identifying as a resource provider and a guide on how to learn, focused on helping students develop effective viewing habits and gaining parents' buy-in and support through creating opportunities for parents to see students' progress out of digital activities.
Moreover, the hypothesized direct effects of both school culture and professional learning were not significant (\*β\* = 0.05, \*p\* > 0.05; \*β\* = 0.06, \*p\* > 0.05 respectively). Instead, their associations with outward bridging were fully mediated by internal factors. This might be because outward bridging often adopted the form of encouragement, recommendation, out-of-class assignments or projects, and brief class sharing, and these peripheral activities did not touch the core of in-class teaching arrangements. Thus, external forces like school culture and professional learning that are important for in-class technological behaviours might be less influential in this context. Instead, teachers' role positioning, perceptions of students' digital behaviours, and appraisal of technological knowledge were found to be more integral to this aspect of bridging, not only directly predicting it but also mediating the influence of external factors.
## DISCUSSION
### The dimensions of digital bridging activities
Adding to the limited understanding of language teachers' digital initiatives to bridge in-class and out-of-class learning, this study revealed a two-dimensional taxonomy of digital bridging activities among a group of primary and secondary Chinese EFL teachers. The EFA and CFA validated two types of digital bridging activities that align with scholars' conceptualizations of bridging: to augment in-class learning (Godwin-Jones, [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0012); Hafner et al., [2015](#bjet13595-bib-0014)); and to facilitate informal language practices (Eskildsen et al., [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0010); Thorne & Hellermann, [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0063)). Additionally, the interview responses substantiated different manifestations of these two types of bridging activities among K-12 teachers and the various motives that drove them. The factor structure was different from that in Schurz and Sundqvist's ([2022](#bjet13595-bib-0060)) study in the European context. K-12 English education in China was characterized by form-centered teaching and constrained access to physical and digital English resources. Thus, when bridging in-class and out-of-class learning, teachers might be concerned more about expanding students' English learning venues than about compensating for the weakness of informal English exposure. Nonetheless, the profile of research participants, which consisted of a large proportion of primary school teachers and teachers from resource-deficient rural areas whose students had limited engagement in informal English activities, might have biased the findings. Moreover, a large proportion of the participants had below-undergraduate academic qualifications, which might have represented a teacher population less enthusiastic about ICT integration (see Chiu & Liu, [2017](#bjet13595-bib-0006); Gambari et al., [2016](#bjet13595-bib-0011)). The factor structure might be different if conducted with university EFL teachers or teachers from mostly urban areas. Thus, future research may delve deeper into how linguistic and sociocultural contexts shape bridging initiatives.
### Influencing factors of digital bridging activities
Digital bridging is a unique type of technology integration behaviour that involves incorporating and featuring student-preferred digital resources and relevant competence to strengthen in-class learning and expand learning beyond the classroom. Its characteristics led to some unique findings on the influencing factors. One unique finding is the salient role of teachers' awareness of students' out-of-class digital behaviours. Although teacher technology adoption literature highlights TPACK, which does recognise teachers' knowledge of students as an essential consideration in mapping the intersection of technology, content and pedagogy (Mishra & Koehler, [2006](#bjet13595-bib-0046)), as a robust predictor, knowledge about students' digital behaviours has seldom been singled out as a stand-alone determinant (e.g., Granić, [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0013); Scherer et al., [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0058)). Previous studies have suggested that teachers' appraisal of students' digital competence may influence student-centred technology adoption (Ertmer et al., [2012](#bjet13595-bib-0009); Liu et al., [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0040)). This study substantiated the observation, revealing that teachers' knowledge of students' out-of-class digital behaviours not only directly predicted teachers' digital bridging activities but also mediated the influence of other factors. The interview data revealed that teachers' observation and critical analysis of the advantages and pitfalls of students' out-of-class digital behaviours shaped their bridging focus and strategies. Thus, greater attention is needed on this dimension of teacher knowledge, especially in the digital bridging context. Another unique finding is school culture. Instead of replicating its direct influence prevalent in previous literature (Lai, Wang et al., [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0078); Huang et al., [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0023); Ma et al., [2020](#bjet13595-bib-0042)), this study revealed a fully mediated effect. The finding suggests that perceiving a favourable school culture is not sufficient to drive digital bridging. This finding was corroborated in the interview responses where factors at the teacher, student, and technology levels interplayed with structure-level factors and sociocultural circumstances to shape teachers' bridging strategies and their responses to challenges in bridging. Thus, a holistic perspective needs to be adopted when promoting bridging activities. A positive school culture needs to be augmented by initiatives that strengthen teachers' understanding of students' digital behaviours and their professional knowledge in utilising relevant technological resources.
The study further revealed that the influencing factors of inward and outward bridging differed. Value belief was both a significant direct predictor and a mediator for inward bridging, but it served as neither for outward bridging. The contrastive findings are due to the different nature of the two. Inward bridging involves the adjustment of instructional contents and materials, which is within the sovereign of teachers. In such context, teachers' perception of the value of digital resources for learning may directly drive their digital behaviours and mediate or moderate internalized influence of external forces (Cheng & Xie, [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0005); Hsu, [2016](#bjet13595-bib-0020); Vongkulluksn et al., [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0069)). However, outward bridging pertains to students' out-of-class use of digital resources, which is subject to sociocultural circumstances beyond the classroom, such as parents' beliefs about English learning and attitudes towards digital surveillance, students' digital habits, and governmental regulations on school assignments and children's digital content consumption, as shown in the interview responses. Teachers' internal value belief on the learning potential of digital resources was insufficient to counter these constraining external forces, hence playing a minimal predicting or mediating role. Instead, teachers' educator-role identity belief was a more robust predictor. As the interview responses revealed that teachers with a broadly-defined educator identity employed innovative ways to surmount external constraints to engage in outward bridging. The greater robustness of this belief against sociocultural barriers is understandable, since identity beliefs are self-construal beliefs that are in more central positions in one's belief system and thus play a more powerful role in behaviours (Pajares, [1992](#bjet13595-bib-0050); Rokeach, [1968](#bjet13595-bib-0056)). However, caution needs to be taken when interpreting the findings, as the study was conducted in the Chinese K-12 context where parent and government surveillance has much influence. Value belief might play an equally important role in outward bridging if conducted in university contexts with less external surveillance or in other cultural contexts where parental and community ideologies towards digital learning are different.
Moreover, the mediation role of internal factors on the influences of external factors was more salient in outward bridging than inward bridging, as the influences of school culture and professional learning were fully mediated by teachers' knowledge and belief in the former. The greater saliency of knowledge and belief in determining outward bridging activities might be because this type of bridging targets students' out-of-class behaviours and hence is less subject to the influence of school support and professional learning. This finding aligns with previous research, where teachers' beliefs and attitudes are found to be more determinant of student-oriented technology use (e.g., Li et al., [2019](#bjet13595-bib-0036)). The finding suggests that strengthening teachers' internal factors, namely educator-oriented identity belief, awareness of students' out-of-class digital behaviours and TPACK in particular, might contribute more to promoting teachers' outward bridging activities than to inward bridging activities that rely on both internal and external factors.
The findings carry the following implications: First, the observed low levels of teacher engagement in digital bridging, corroborating previous research in secondary school contexts (Schurz & Sundqvist, [2022](#bjet13595-bib-0060)), suggest the importance of raising K-12 teachers' awareness, especially with regard to using digital bridging to extend learning experience beyond the classroom. The multitude of internal and external factors at play in shaping teachers' bridging initiatives suggests that simply raising awareness is not sufficient. The findings highlight that promoting bridging initiatives depends not only on teachers' knowledge and pedagogical skills (Henry et al., [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0018); Lewin & Charania, [2018](#bjet13595-bib-0034)), but also on teachers' role positioning, access to contextually appropriate digital resource repertories, and resourcefulness to circumvent sociocultural constraints against relevant digital behaviours in specific teaching contexts. Second, the observed differential influencing factors on the two dimensions of digital bridging advocate a differentiated approach to supporting the two bridging initiatives. The greater influence of internal factors on outward bridging activities implies that, to boost outward bridging, shaping teachers' role identity beliefs and helping them come up with innovative ways to counter sociocultural constraints are particularly essential.
## CONCLUSION
Based on the survey and interview responses from a group of primary and secondary Chinese EFL teachers, this study generated a two-dimensional taxonomy of bridging activities: inward bridging and outward bridging, and revealed that these two bridging activities were subject to the influence of different factors. Findings from this study suggest a differential approach to researching and supporting digital bridging activities and influencing factors.
This study has a few limitations: First, the research findings might be biased by the research context and populations. The heavy exam-orientation and strong parental and government surveillance in the educational context, compounded by a research population that consisted of a large portion of primary school teachers, rural school teachers, and teachers with below-undergraduate academic qualifications, might have biased the observed relationships of various internal and external factors with bridging activities. The findings on the nature of digital bridging and the influencing factors might be different for teachers with different profiles working under different sociocultural circumstances. Given that this is an emerging research field, more research is needed in different sociocultural contexts to reveal the nuances in the nature of digital bridging activities and influencing factors. Second, the study focused on testing the explanation power of a pre-defined set of key determinants of teacher technology adoption in the digital bridging context. It found that these factors only explained around 50% of its variance. Thus, future research may include some context-specific challenges, such as parental influence, in model testing. More importantly, future research may explore context-specific factors in different sociocultural and teaching contexts and test the predictive power of these context-specific factors on teachers' bridging initiatives. Third, the study relied on teachers' self-report data (survey and interview) only. The findings are suggestive at most. Future research may triangulate teachers' perspectives with those of the other stakeholders (e.g., students, parents, colleagues) to substantiate the findings and provide nuanced insights into the issue. It may also adopt in-depth longitudinal case studies that complement self-reports with objective data sources, such as class observations to shed light on the dynamic changes in and two-way interactions among bridging activities and influencing factors.
