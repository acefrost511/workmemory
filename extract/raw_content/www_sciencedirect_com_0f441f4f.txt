## Abstract
Blockchain has been widely promoted as a transformative technology for education, yet existing reviews largely remain descriptive, emphasizing publication growth and keyword trends while offering limited insight into pedagogical and conceptual implications. To address this gap, this study conducts a hybrid bibliometric and qualitative content analysis of 673 Web of Science–indexed journal articles published between 2018 and mid-2025. The analysis identifies three dominant thematic configurations and eleven co-citation clusters, with bibliometric mapping revealing a strong concentration on credentialing and verification, technical infrastructures, and emerging governance-oriented applications. Research output is geographically concentrated, led primarily by China, India, and the United States. To move beyond structural mapping, qualitative content analysis examines how blockchain is discursively framed in educational research. Drawing on the concept of sociotechnical imaginaries, understood here as collectively shared visions linking technological capabilities with assumptions about educational value, governance, and social order, the analysis reveals a dominant imaginary centered on efficiency, trust, and institutional accountability. In contrast, pedagogical innovation, learner agency, and equity-oriented perspectives remain comparatively marginal across influential publications. By empirically demonstrating how certain imaginaries dominate the field while others remain peripheral, this study bridges bibliometric evidence with critical interpretation. It contributes to the literature by clarifying not only what blockchain research in education focuses on, but also how and why particular educational futures are prioritized. The findings underscore the need for future research and policy to more explicitly integrate pedagogical theory, equity considerations, and learner-centered design into blockchain-enabled educational initiatives.
## Introduction
Blockchain has attracted increasing attention in education as a potential reconfiguration of credentialing, data governance, and institutional trust systems. Originating in financial transactions, its affordances of immutability, distributed verification, and cryptographic trust have been extended into educational contexts, including portable learning records, micro-credentials, and tamper-resistant certificates (Sun et al., 2018; Turkanović et al., 2018). Within the literature, proponents frequently position blockchain as more than a technical tool, framing it as a sociotechnical project that embeds particular normative visions concerning what education should value and how educational authority should be organized.
Despite this growing scholarly interest, much of the existing research frames blockchain primarily in terms of technical efficiency and administrative optimization, often approaching educational settings as sites of system deployment rather than as pedagogical environments requiring sustained educational inquiry. As a result, claims regarding blockchain's educational relevance are frequently articulated through promises of transparency, trust, and automation, while questions related to teaching–learning processes, learner agency, and educational meaning remain comparatively underdeveloped within influential strands of the literature.
This study examines blockchain in education through the lens of sociotechnical imaginaries, understood as collectively shared and stabilized visions that link technological capacities with assumptions about social order and institutional value (Jasanoff & Kim, 2013). From this perspective, blockchain does not merely enable new technical functions; it is discursively positioned as projecting particular educational futures, such as transparent credential economies, self-sovereign learners, and decentralized governance arrangements. These projected futures raise fundamental questions: What constitutes valuable learning when educational outcomes are rendered as verifiable records? Who defines legitimacy and authority in blockchain-enabled systems? Which learners and institutions may be marginalized when such systems presuppose robust infrastructure, advanced digital literacy, or stable governance conditions?
However, existing reviews rarely interrogate these questions directly. Most bibliometric and systematic surveys (Reis-Marques et al., 2021; Shuhaimi et al., 2024) map publication trends, authorship patterns, or keyword co-occurrences, offering valuable structural overviews but limited insight into how educational futures are conceptually framed within influential and agenda-setting research. Consequently, scholarly attention clusters around credentialing, transcript verification, and institutional efficiency, while themes central to educational research–such as pedagogy, learner participation, and equity–remain comparatively peripheral within dominant scholarly discourse. This imbalance risks narrowing educational futures to what is most readily formalized and tokenized, rather than what is pedagogically meaningful.
Geographical asymmetries further shape these framings. Within indexed blockchain-in-education scholarship, research output is concentrated in a limited number of national contexts, most notably China, India, and the United States, while contributions from Africa and Latin America remain weakly represented in influential publication and collaboration networks. Such asymmetries suggest that imaginaries of blockchain-enabled education are often articulated from positions of infrastructural and institutional advantage and subsequently treated as broadly applicable. Without broader epistemic representation, there is a risk that blockchain research may inadvertently reproduce forms of digital inequality by privileging models developed in resource-rich environments while marginalizing learners and educators in constrained settings.
To address these gaps, this study undertakes a hybrid bibliometric and content analysis of blockchain research in education published between 2018 and 2025. Four research questions guide the inquiry:
\*\*RQ1\*\*
What are the global publication patterns and research outputs on blockchain in education, and how have they evolved over time?
\*\*RQ2\*\*
What intellectual and collaborative structures underpin the research landscape?
\*\*RQ3\*\*
What thematic clusters and conceptual trajectories have emerged?
\*\*RQ4\*\*
What conceptual, ethical, and practical implications emerge from influential publications, and how do these works frame the future of blockchain in education?
To ensure analytical clarity, sociotechnical imaginaries are operationalized in this study as recurring patterns of problem framing, proposed solutions, and normative claims through which blockchain is positioned as educationally valuable. These imaginaries are identified by triangulating citation-dense bibliometric clusters with discursive regularities observed in influential publications, including assumptions concerning governance, accountability, efficiency, and learner roles. When such framings recur across clusters and highly cited works, they are interpreted as shared imaginaries rather than isolated rhetoric or technological speculation.
Given the study's pedagogical orientation, pedagogical engagement is assessed against a minimal yet established educational reference grounded in constructivist and learner-centered learning theories, which emphasize active knowledge construction, learner agency, and meaningful participation. From this standpoint, pedagogical relevance is indicated not simply by the presence of educational contexts, but by explicit attention to teaching–learning design, learner experience, and formative educational processes.
Across these questions, the sociotechnical imaginary lens provides interpretive coherence, enabling examination of not only where scholarly attention is concentrated, but also how particular educational futures are envisioned, whose interests they serve, and what exclusions they may produce. By combining structural mapping with critical interpretation, this study moves beyond descriptive synthesis to foreground pedagogy, equity, and governance as analytically central–yet often weakly institutionalized–dimensions of blockchain-in-education research. In doing so, it positions blockchain not as a neutral infrastructure, but as a contested sociotechnical project through which educational authority, participation, and value are discursively negotiated in the digital age.
