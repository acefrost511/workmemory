Maria Tsolia is a secondary-level philologist and a PhD candidate at the Department of Computer Science and Telecommunications of the University of Thessaly. She holds two Master’s degrees, one in Education from the Hellenic Open University and a second in Information and Communication Technologies in Education from the University of Thessaly. Her research interests center on the digital transformation of the humanities and the pedagogical integration of innovative technologies in educational settings. She has received specialized training in Special Education and Intercultural Education, and her research work has been presented at international conferences and published in peer-reviewed academic journals.

Nikolaos Zygouris graduated from the Department of Psychology at the University of Crete
and obtained his PhD in Clinical Neuropsychology from the Department of Special
Education at the University of Thessaly. He has also received specialized

## Abstract
Early identification of reading difficulties enables timely classroom intervention; however, teachers often have limited time and restricted access to specialist assessment. This study explores a brief, teacher-friendly screening approach based on short oral reading recordings to support classroom decision-making. Oral reading samples were collected from 77 Greek primary school pupils (Grades 3–6) during a standardized reading task. Recordings were segmented into 7 s excerpts, converted into spectrogram images, and analyzed using a deep learning model to classify each excerpt as indicative of reading difficulties or not. To reflect realistic school implementation, model development followed an 80/20 participant-level split, with validation conducted on pupils not included in the training set. At the selected operating threshold, the model achieved approximately 84% overall accuracy and a balanced accuracy of 0.85. For practical applicability, a pupil-level indicator—representing the proportion of excerpts flagged as difficult—showed a strong association with expert judgments (r ≈ 0.74). These findings suggest that brief oral reading recordings can provide teachers with an interpretable screening signal to inform monitoring, prioritization, and early classroom support while underscoring the need for further validation under routine school conditions.

## 1. Introduction
UNESCO [[1](#B1-digital-06-00015)] defines education as a dynamic process that facilitates the acquisition of knowledge, skills, values, and habits. Beyond the transmission of information, education functions as a multidimensional mechanism for social and personal development. Mastery of written language is considered essential for holistic development as it underpins academic achievement, professional participation, and social inclusion. Reading, therefore, represents a fundamental life skill, influencing individuals’ long-term outcomes [[2](#B2-digital-06-00015),[3](#B3-digital-06-00015),[4](#B4-digital-06-00015)]. It relies on complex cognitive, linguistic, and neurobiological processes that support the analysis and interpretation of written language [[5](#B5-digital-06-00015),[6](#B6-digital-06-00015)]. Decoding, comprehension, and fluency constitute the core skills necessary for successful reading [[7](#B7-digital-06-00015)], and strong reading abilities foster progress across all learning domains [[8](#B8-digital-06-00015),[9](#B9-digital-06-00015),[10](#B10-digital-06-00015),[11](#B11-digital-06-00015)].
Within the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition (DSM-5), dyslexia is classified under the broader category of reading impairment. The ability to map phonological representations onto written symbols depends strongly on letter knowledge and phonological awareness, typically assessed through word identification and phonological decoding tasks. Children with dyslexia experience persistent difficulties in developing accurate and fluent reading, with fluency proving particularly resistant to intervention [[12](#B12-digital-06-00015)]. The DSM-5 definition [[13](#B13-digital-06-00015)] is consistent with this profile.
Prevalence estimates for reading impairment vary due to differences in diagnostic criteria and population characteristics. Dyslexia is estimated to affect 5–17% of students [[14](#B14-digital-06-00015)] and is recognized as the most common neurodevelopmental disorder worldwide. Greek data indicate that reading difficulties affect 3–11% of students, with dyslexia specifically present in 5.52–5.9% of cases and a higher prevalence in males [[15](#B15-digital-06-00015),[16](#B16-digital-06-00015)].
Phonological skills are central to early reading development, supporting phoneme awareness and letter–sound mapping [[17](#B17-digital-06-00015)]. Phonological deficits represent a well-established core feature of dyslexia [[18](#B18-digital-06-00015),[19](#B19-digital-06-00015)], disrupting phoneme–grapheme correspondence and making decoding difficult [[20](#B20-digital-06-00015)]. Poor phonological awareness limits phonological memory, and slow rapid automatized naming (RAN) reliably predicts dyslexia [[21](#B21-digital-06-00015),[22](#B22-digital-06-00015)], while neuroimaging evidence indicates atypical neural activity during phonological tasks [[23](#B23-digital-06-00015)]. Interventions targeting phonological processes, such as phoneme segmentation or blending, can significantly improve reading fluency [[24](#B24-digital-06-00015),[25](#B25-digital-06-00015)].
Beyond phonological decoding, children with dyslexia often experience difficulties in sentence-level processing, particularly when dealing with complex syntactic structures. These demands place substantial strain on working memory and undermine comprehension [[26](#B26-digital-06-00015),[27](#B27-digital-06-00015)]. Pseudoword reading tasks provide a sensitive index of these decoding difficulties: performance on pseudoword reading is typically more impaired than on real-word reading, reflecting reliance on stored lexical forms rather than systematic decoding [[28](#B28-digital-06-00015),[29](#B29-digital-06-00015),[30](#B30-digital-06-00015)]. Incorporating both word and pseudoword reading tasks, therefore, enhances diagnostic accuracy [[31](#B31-digital-06-00015)].
Working memory also has a foundational role, enabling the retention and processing of information during decoding and comprehension. Limitations in auditory working memory constrain phonological processing, whereas deficits in visual working memory hinder fluency and comprehension [[32](#B32-digital-06-00015),[33](#B33-digital-06-00015),[34](#B34-digital-06-00015)]. Neurophysiological evidence further suggests difficulties in integrating auditory and visual information in dyslexia [[35](#B35-digital-06-00015)].
Visuospatial processing supports grapheme recognition, visual discrimination, and the coordination of eye movements during reading. Deficits in this domain, including abnormalities in the magnocellular pathway, may contribute to visual attention and motion-processing difficulties observed in dyslexia [[36](#B36-digital-06-00015),[37](#B37-digital-06-00015),[38](#B38-digital-06-00015)].
Finally, executive functions—responsible for regulation, monitoring, and strategic control—also influence reading performance. Children with dyslexia frequently exhibit weaknesses in these higher-order processes, affecting comprehension, error monitoring, and sustained attention during reading tasks [[39](#B39-digital-06-00015),[40](#B40-digital-06-00015),[41](#B41-digital-06-00015),[42](#B42-digital-06-00015),[43](#B43-digital-06-00015)].
From an educational perspective, screening tools are most useful when they assist schools in prioritizing follow-up support rather than attempting to replace comprehensive assessment. This approach aligns well with tiered models of literacy support, in which brief measures inform decisions about monitoring, targeted instruction, and referral when necessary [[44](#B44-digital-06-00015),[45](#B45-digital-06-00015)]. In this context, an automated approach that provides a clear pupil-level indicator may be valuable, provided that its scope and limitations are explicitly stated and that evaluation procedures reflect authentic school use.
In the present study, an AI-assisted approach was investigated that converts short oral reading recordings into time–frequency representations (STFT spectrograms) and applies a deep learning model to classify 7 s excerpts as indicating Reading Difficulties or No Difficulties. The study focuses on Greek primary school pupils (Grades 3–6), and performance was evaluated using a participant-level split so that validation reflected generalization to pupils not seen during training. To support classroom interpretation, a pupil-level “difficulty percentage” was also computed by aggregating excerpt-level predictions across each recording. Importantly, the intended role of the system is to support screening and prioritization for follow-up rather than to provide a diagnosis [[46](#B46-digital-06-00015)].
## 2. Artificial Neural Networks and Education
The rapid expansion of digital technologies, driven by large-scale data streams and increasingly sophisticated algorithms, has established artificial intelligence (AI) as a transformative force in science and innovation [[47](#B47-digital-06-00015)]. AI now permeates everyday life, ranging from voice assistants and recommendation systems to advanced applications in healthcare and education [[48](#B48-digital-06-00015)].
Among AI methodologies, Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) are particularly influential. Inspired by the organizational principles of the human brain, ANNs consist of interconnected nodes that process and learn from data patterns, enabling the detection of complex structures and the adaptive modification of outputs [[49](#B49-digital-06-00015)]. Their versatility supports applications in diagnostics, forecasting, environmental monitoring, and industrial automation. Moreover, ANNs can process text, images, and audio signals, facilitating multimodal analysis [[50](#B50-digital-06-00015)].
In educational contexts, ANNs are increasingly employed to personalize learning, support data-driven instruction, and assist educators in decision-making. A prominent application concerns the early detection of reading difficulties, including dyslexia. Empirical studies indicate that ANNs can analyze reading behavior and identify students at risk. For example, machine learning models have been successfully applied to eye-tracking data to detect struggling readers [[51](#B51-digital-06-00015)], while neural network architectures have demonstrated effectiveness in classifying students based on complex eye-movement patterns [[26](#B26-digital-06-00015)].
Deep learning approaches have also achieved notable diagnostic accuracy through the analysis of speech signals. Styliaras et al. [[52](#B52-digital-06-00015)], for instance, applied convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to spectrogram representations of children’s speech. Beyond reading and speech, handwriting analysis has been explored using state-of-the-art object detection models such as You Only Look Once (YOLOv11) [[53](#B53-digital-06-00015)]. These developments extend a long-standing tradition of employing connectionist models to investigate the neural mechanisms underlying reading [[54](#B54-digital-06-00015)].
Optimizing such models requires careful consideration of the cognitive and neuropsychological characteristics of dyslexia. Subtle visuospatial and memory-related weaknesses may exist beyond the scope of conventional assessments [[55](#B55-digital-06-00015)], and the integration of these markers could enhance diagnostic algorithms. Emotional factors, including anxiety and reduced self-esteem, also influence learning outcomes and should be considered within a comprehensive framework of educational support [[56](#B56-digital-06-00015),[57](#B57-digital-06-00015)].
Overall, the integration of ANN-based methodologies with pedagogical and psychological research offers promising directions for improving dyslexia screening. Despite international progress, a notable research gap remains in Greece, where no studies to date have applied ANNs for dyslexia detection, underscoring an important avenue for future investigation.
## 3. Aims and Hypotheses
The aim of this study was to advance current practices in early literacy screening by developing and systematically evaluating an automated tool for assessing reading proficiency in Greek primary school children. The central methodological innovation lies in converting students’ oral readings of a standardized passage into spectrogram images, which serve as the primary input for a convolutional neural network (CNN) based on the YOLO architecture. This model is designed to generate both categorical performance classifications and a continuous difficulty score that closely aligns with expert clinical evaluations. The system is purposefully engineered to be scalable, developmentally appropriate, and transparent in its decision-making processes.
Beyond its technical design, the approach is situated within a broader digital platform that integrates typical phonological assessments with auditory features derived from the speech signal. The aim of the proposed system is to deliver a tool for identifying children who may be experiencing reading difficulties in the Greek educational context through the integration of established literacy screening practices and state-of-the-art machine learning techniques.
Two central hypotheses were formulated to guide this investigation. First, we hypothesized that students with a prior diagnosis of learning difficulties would require significantly more time to complete the oral reading tasks and would receive higher difficulty scores from the CNN model compared to their typically developing peers. Second, we anticipated that the model would demonstrate a high level of reliability in distinguishing between students with and without reading challenges. Specifically, the CNN was expected to show strong specificity in correctly identifying typically developing readers and high sensitivity in detecting students who exhibit genuine reading difficulties.
It must be noted that while previous systems have often relied on either linguistic analyses or basic auditory features, the present method introduces a novel combination of visual representations of speech and deep learning classification, grounded in established pedagogical understanding. In doing so, it offers a practical, expert-informed tool with the potential to transform early literacy screening and facilitate timely, individualized interventions within educational settings.
## 4. Materials and Methods
### 4.1. Participants
The present study included 77 primary school pupils (39 boys and 38 girls) from Grades 3 to 6 attending a public school in the regional unit of Phthiotis, Greece. The mean age of the participants was 11.6 years. Among the participants, six pupils had previously received a diagnosis of dyslexia from the Centre for Diagnosis, Assessment, and Support in Central Greece, in accordance with national educational legislation. [Table 1](#table\_body\_display\_digital-06-00015-t001) presents the number of participants by age and gender.
\*\*Table 1.\*\*
Summary of the demographics.
![](https://pub.mdpi-res.com/img/table.png)
All participating children had no documented history of major medical illness, psychiatric disorder, neurodevelopmental disorder, or significant visual or auditory impairment, according to school-maintained medical records. Written informed consent permitting each child’s participation was obtained from all parents or legal guardians. All procedures involving human data complied with the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki and were approved in accordance with the guidelines of the Ethics and Deontology Committee of the University of Thessaly Internal Research Ethics Committee (EHDE) on 24 September 2019.
Audio recordings were conducted in a quiet setting to minimize background noise, using a sampling rate of 44.1 kHz and a 16-bit depth to ensure high-quality signal capture. All audio data were anonymized prior to analysis. Informed consent for recording and research use of the data was obtained from both parents and educators.
In this study, the target label represents observed reading performance (difficulty vs. no difficulty) based on expert evaluation of the recorded reading samples. Although a subset of participants had a formal diagnosis of dyslexia, the model’s labels are not intended to replicate a clinical diagnosis. Rather, they reflect a screening-oriented classification aligned with expert judgments of reading fluency and accuracy during the administered tasks.
### 4.2. Tools
Children’s reading skills were assessed using Askisi-Lexia, a well-established and validated neuropsychological screening battery, delivered via a web-based application [[58](#B58-digital-06-00015),[59](#B59-digital-06-00015)].
Specifically, the evaluation included several core tasks. A word and pseudoword reading task was first administered, where students were asked to accurately read a randomized set of 10 real words and 11 pseudowords. Second, a reading discrimination task was employed. In this task, students in the third and fourth grades read three texts and selected the image that best represented the text’s meaning, while those in the fifth and sixth grades answered comprehension questions corresponding to the texts. Lastly, the sentence comprehension task required students to determine whether the meaning of each presented sentence was logically correct. They evaluated 17 sentences in total, comprising 8 semantically correct and 9 incorrect ones. Three tasks from the above-mentioned assessment tool were utilized in the present study. The reading ability of each student was simultaneously recorded in WAV format. The use of the same passage for all participants ensured that differences in the recordings could be attributed primarily to reading ability rather than to content variation.
### 4.3. Implementation
The proposed processing pipeline converts raw audio segments into spectrogram representations that encode both temporal and spectral characteristics of the signal. Such time–frequency representations are well suited for convolutional neural networks (CNNs), which are optimized to learn spatially structured patterns in grid-like data.
Prior work, including that of Karol J. Piczak [[60](#B60-digital-06-00015)], has demonstrated the superior performance of CNN-based approaches compared with methods relying on manually engineered auditory features. The stages of the proposed pipeline are described below and summarized in [Figure 1](#fig\_body\_display\_digital-06-00015-f001).
![](/digital/digital-06-00015/article\_deploy/html/images/digital-06-00015-g001-550.jpg)
\*\*Figure 1.\*\*
Overview of the proposed processing pipeline.
### 4.4. Raw Audio
The initial stage of the implementation involves the acquisition of raw audio signals, which constitute the basis for all subsequent processing. Recordings were carried out in a quiet environment to minimize background noise, using a sampling rate of 44.1 kHz. The signals were stored in uncompressed WAV format with 16-bit resolution, ensuring an adequate dynamic range for detailed auditory analysis. All audio samples were anonymized prior to analysis, and informed consent for recording and research use of the data was obtained from both parents and teachers.
### 4.5. Audio Preprocessing
Following acquisition, the raw audio recordings underwent preprocessing to prepare them for spectral analysis and model training. Each recording was segmented into 7 s excerpts. This duration was selected because it provides sufficient temporal context to capture meaningful auditory patterns in children’s oral reading (e.g., brief pauses, within-utterance fluency variation, and speech transitions) without producing excessively long inputs. Shorter segments are more likely to shorten relevant events and omit transitions, whereas longer segments increase memory demands and prolong training time. In practical terms, a 7 s window represents an effective balance as it is long enough to preserve informative auditory context while remaining computationally efficient and conducive to stable model performance.
### 4.6. Conversion to STFT Spectrograms
Each pre-processed audio segment was transformed into a time–frequency representation using the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT). This transformation captures both spectral and temporal characteristics of the signal, enabling the model to analyze variations in frequency content over time.
Light smoothing/normalization was applied during spectrogram visualization to reduce minor frame-to-frame fluctuations while preserving the temporal evolution of auditory energy, resulting in a consistent representation for model input. Following the conversion of each seven-second audio segment into an STFT spectrogram, the data became well suited for convolutional neural network architectures, which are particularly effective at identifying spatial patterns analogous to those found in images.
Temporal moving-average smoothing was applied to the STFT magnitude spectrograms before visualization to stabilize frame-to-frame variability. In practice, the energy at each time frame was replaced by the mean of its immediately neighboring frames within a narrow temporal window, reducing rapid auditory fluctuations that typically arise from recording noise, breath artifacts, or segmentation boundaries. This procedure preserves the natural progression of speech energy over time while yielding smoother and more physiologically coherent spectrotemporal contours. Each resulting averaged spectrogram was subsequently visualized using Matplotlib 3.9.0 and exported as a consistently resized PNG image for CNN input. These spectrograms constitute the primary feature representations used during model training as they retain behaviorally meaningful auditory patterns; for instance, reading hesitations or mispronunciations may appear as prolonged low-energy intervals, disrupted harmonic structure, or irregular spectral transitions across time.
### 4.7. Annotation
After spectrogram generation, each segment was manually annotated using the makesense.ai web platform. For each 7 s spectrogram image, annotators drew a single anchor box covering the spectrotemporal region of interest and assigned a class label based on the ground-truth expert judgment (Difficulties or No Difficulties). It must be noted that the spectrogram annotations were performed by two licensed psychologists with formal clinical training in developmental reading disorders. Both experts were recruited based on their documented experience in assessing reading difficulties using standardized psychometric and neuropsychological tools. As a result, labels were provided at the frame level rather than inferred from the student-level assessment. Across students, the number of annotated images per recording ranged from approximately 13 to 33, depending on reading duration. Each 7 s audio segment was converted into an STFT spectrogram that preserves how energy changes over time. We applied light smoothing/normalization during visualization to reduce minor frame-to-frame fluctuations while keeping the temporal structure intact, producing a consistent representation for model input. An example of two spectrograms showcasing the contrast between Reading Difficulties and No Reading Difficulties can be seen in [Figure 2](#fig\_body\_display\_digital-06-00015-f002).
![](/digital/digital-06-00015/article\_deploy/html/images/digital-06-00015-g002-550.jpg)
\*\*Figure 2.\*\*
Representative 7 s speech spectrograms for contrasting reading proficiency.
In the left panel, speech energy is distributed in brief, irregular clusters separated by extended low-energy intervals, particularly in the middle portion of the passage, resulting in a fragmented temporal pattern. The harmonic bands and horizontal formant structures within the ~300–3000 Hz range appear faint and discontinuous, while high-frequency components above ~2 kHz occur only sporadically. Overall syllable density is reduced, with few auditory onsets per second and uneven temporal spacing, consistent with slow, effortful reading characterized by frequent pauses.
In contrast, the right panel shows a substantially more continuous spectrotemporal pattern across the 0–7 s interval, with only brief and narrow pauses between utterances. Voiced harmonics are clearly stacked and sustained over time, and the first three formant regions are more prominent and stable, extending consistently into higher frequency ranges. Auditory onsets occur more frequently and at more regular intervals, reflecting a faster and more fluent reading rhythm with stable articulatory control. These visual indicators—greater energy continuity, stable formant structure, shorter pauses, and increased syllable rate—differentiate the two reading profiles.
A fixed STFT parameter configuration was used for all samples (sampling rate, window length, hop size, FFT size, and amplitude scaling), and an identical normalization procedure was applied to minimize variability related to recording intensity. For transparency and reproducibility, these parameters are reported explicitly in the manuscript.
### 4.8. Train/Test Split
Train/validation split (participant-level). To prevent subject-level information leakage, we split the dataset at the participant level, not at the segment level. All 7 s segments from the same student were kept together and assigned exclusively to either the training set or the validation set. This ensures that validation results reflect performance on students who were completely unseen during training, which corresponds to the realistic deployment scenario of screening new individuals. We report the split using both (a) the number of students per set and (b) the number of resulting segments per set because the model is trained on segments but evaluated in terms of its ability to generalize across previously unseen students.
### 4.9. Model Training
We adopted the You Only Look Once (YOLO) framework, a state-of-the-art convolutional neural network originally developed for real-time object detection. YOLO processes an entire image in a single forward pass and predicts object classes and bounding boxes, enabling high computational efficiency. This architectural characteristic makes YOLO particularly suitable for spectrogram-based analysis as spectrograms represent globally distributed time–frequency information rather than isolated local objects. Reading-related auditory patterns—such as rhythm regularity, pause structure, and harmonic continuity—emerge from the overall temporal organization of the signal. YOLO’s ability to capture global spatial context in one pass, therefore, supports the detection of broad spectrotemporal configurations associated with fluent or disrupted reading.
For the present application, the architecture was adapted to perform image-level classification on spectrogram inputs, with the entire spectrogram treated as the region of interest since each image contains at most one relevant target (i.e., an indicator of reading difficulty). Accordingly, the model receives a spectrogram image and outputs a categorical prediction indicating whether the reading segment corresponds to Reading Difficulties or No Reading Difficulties. The convolutional feature extraction layers learn discriminative spectrotemporal patterns associated with reading performance. Analogous to conventional computer vision tasks in which CNNs learn visual features, here the learned “visual” patterns correspond to auditory characteristics embedded in the time–frequency domain.
An additional advantage of YOLO in this context is its favorable balance between computational efficiency and representational capacity. Its lightweight and optimized design supports rapid inference, an important consideration for scalable school-based screening where multiple recordings may be processed within limited time. Moreover, YOLO’s multi-scale feature learning enhances sensitivity to auditory events occurring at different temporal and frequency resolutions, such as brief hesitations, irregular energy bursts, or disruptions in harmonic structure. For these reasons—efficient global context modeling, suitability for time–frequency representations, and multi-scale spectrotemporal sensitivity—YOLO constitutes a methodologically appropriate framework for spectrogram-based reading difficulty screening. Implementation details of the model are available via GitHub and Ultralytics [[61](#B61-digital-06-00015)] (2020).
Key training hyperparameters were determined through an external tuning process conducted prior to the final model training. Preliminary optimization runs were used to identify a learning rate that balanced convergence speed and stability; the batch size was fixed at 20 images, and 200 training epochs were selected as sufficient for convergence without evidence of overfitting. We used a categorical cross-entropy loss function appropriate for two-class classification. Early stopping on the validation loss was employed to prevent overfitting. The CNN learned to extract features from spectrograms that correlate with patterns found in the dataset with children exhibiting signs of reading difficulties; for example, it might learn that spectrograms with many silent gaps or irregular frequency bursts correspond to “Reading Difficulties”. Throughout training, we monitored performance on validation data and adjusted hyperparameters accordingly.
### 4.10. Model Evaluation
We evaluated the model on the test set using standard metrics: accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score for each class, and confusion matrices. Additionally, we examined the learned features indirectly by visualizing some spectrogram segments and the model’s predictions on them, to ensure it was making reasonable distinctions.
Validation dynamics are summarized in the multi-panel learning curves in [Figure 3](#fig\_body\_display\_digital-06-00015-f003). The top row reports the training losses that decrease monotonically across ≈200 epochs, indicating steady optimization. Train/box loss drops from roughly 0.12 at initialization to ≈0.01 by the end, while train/obj loss declines from ~0.026 to ~0.006. Train/cls loss follows a similar downward trend (from ~0.036 to ~0.011). The corresponding validation losses (bottom row) generally mirror these trends: val/box\\_loss and val/obj\\_loss fall smoothly toward low values, and val/cls\\_loss decreases early then fluctuates within a narrow band in late training. This mild non-monotonicity in validation classification loss is consistent with class imbalance in a two-class detection setup (single anchor per image) and does not coincide with degradation in the ranking metrics.

The trajectories of precision, recall, and mean Average Precision (mAP) indicate that discriminative performance improves rapidly during early training and subsequently stabilizes at high levels. Precision increases from low initial values to a sustained plateau in the mid-to-high range, while recall rises quickly and remains elevated, exhibiting only modest oscillations—patterns consistent with a cosine learning-rate schedule and the use of strong data augmentation. ### 4.11. Student-Level Screening Indicator
Because educational decisions are made at the student level rather than at the level of individual 7 s segments, the analysis focused on a student-level screening indicator. Segment-level predictions were used only as intermediate computational steps and were not interpreted independently for diagnostic purposes. For each student, predictions across all segments of the recording were aggregated to calculate the difficulty percentage, defined as the proportion of segments classified as “Reading Difficulties” using the selected decision threshold as described further in the Final Model section. This produces a single, interpretable value per student that can be used for screening and monitoring over time (e.g., repeated administration across the school year).
### 4.12. Final Model
The F1-confidence curve showcased in [Figure 4](#fig\_body\_display\_digital-06-00015-f004) exhibits a rapid increase from near zero at very low confidence values, reaches a peak, and then declines as the decision threshold becomes overly stringent. The global optimum occurs at a confidence level of 0.634, where the macro F1 achieves 0.89. The 0.634 threshold provides an optimal balance between precision and recall across all classes and was adopted as the default decision rule for subsequent analyses. Interestingly, the No\\_Difficulties (No Reading Difficulties) curve remains relatively high across a broader range of confidence values, reflecting the prevalence of easy, high-confidence negative samples, whereas the With\\_Difficulties (Reading Difficulties) curve shows a sharper peak, capturing the greater variability present in disfluent frames.

## 5. Results
All performance metrics reported below were derived using a participant-level data split, ensuring that no pupil contributed segments to both the training and validation sets. Training of the convolutional neural network (CNN) based on the You Only Look Once (YOLO) architecture demonstrated stable and effective convergence over approximately 200 epochs. The training loss components (box\\_loss, obj\\_loss, cls\\_loss) showed a consistent monotonic decrease, indicating smooth optimization without evidence of instability or overfitting. The corresponding validation losses followed a comparable pattern, remaining at low levels with only minor fluctuations, which supports the model’s generalization capacity.
Beyond segment-level evaluation, student-level performance is also reported using an aggregated difficulty index, defined as the percentage of segments labeled as “difficulty” for each pupil. This aggregation more closely reflects the intended practical application of the system, namely, prioritizing pupils for further assessment rather than classifying isolated 7 s excerpts in isolation.
Performance metrics confirmed the model’s capacity to discriminate between spectrogram images derived from children with reading difficulties and those without. Specifically, mean Average Precision at IoU 0.5 (mAP@0.5) reached approximately 0.88, while the more stringent mAP@0.5:0.95 metric stabilized around 0.82. Precision and recall improved during training and remained at consistently high levels thereafter. IoU quantifies the spatial agreement between a predicted and a ground-truth bounding box as the area of overlap divided by the area of their union. mAP summarizes detection performance by integrating precision–recall trade-offs (average precision) and then averaging across classes.
The optimal decision threshold, identified from the F1–confidence curve, was approximately 0.634. At this operating point, the macro F1-score reached 0.89, indicating a well-balanced trade-off between precision and recall. Overall classification accuracy was approximately 84%, with a balanced accuracy of 0.85, reflecting stable performance across both the “No Difficulties” class (recall 0.90) and the “With Difficulties” class (recall 0.80).
Analysis of the confusion matrix indicated that most misclassifications occurred in borderline cases characterized by mixed or ambiguous speech patterns. Importantly, these errors were not random but were associated with auditory intermediate profiles, supporting the interpretability and construct validity of the model’s decisions.
Qualitative inspection of spectrogram representations further supported these findings. Samples from children with reading difficulties typically showed sparse and fragmented energy distributions, reduced harmonic structure, and irregular temporal organization within core frequency bands. In contrast, spectrograms from fluent readers demonstrated more continuous energy flow, stable formant structures, and smoother rhythmic patterns. These observations indicate that the model learned acoustically meaningful features associated with reading fluency.
Overall, the findings support the effectiveness of the proposed system for screening and classifying reading difficulties from audio data using CNN-based analysis of spectrogram representations. The combination of strong performance metrics and interpretable spectrotemporal distinctions reinforces the system’s potential utility in educational screening contexts.
## 6. Discussion
The present study contributes to the expanding domain of automated reading assessment by introducing a You Only Look Once (YOLO)-based deep learning model that analyzes spectrogram representations to identify reading difficulties in primary school children. By integrating advances in artificial intelligence with educational practice, this framework links traditional expert-dependent evaluations with scalable, data-driven screening systems [[62](#B62-digital-06-00015),[63](#B63-digital-06-00015),[64](#B64-digital-06-00015),[65](#B65-digital-06-00015),[66](#B66-digital-06-00015)]. Applying YOLO-style analysis to spectrogram images allows the capture of subtle spectrotemporal irregularities associated with disfluent reading, conceptualizing auditory signals as structured visual patterns [[67](#B67-digital-06-00015),[68](#B68-digital-06-00015),[69](#B69-digital-06-00015)].
The model demonstrated stable and strong performance across training and validation phases, with an accuracy of approximately 84%, balanced accuracy of 0.85, and a macro F1-score of 0.89. Recall for fluent readers reached 0.90, while sensitivity for children with reading difficulties was 0.80. Misclassifications occurred primarily in borderline cases, reflecting transitional reading profiles rather than systematic model bias [[70](#B70-digital-06-00015),[71](#B71-digital-06-00015)]. A methodological strength is the end-to-end learning capacity of the YOLO architecture, which can exploit localized time–frequency information in spectrogram space without handcrafted feature engineering [[72](#B72-digital-06-00015),[73](#B73-digital-06-00015)]. While it is plausible that timing- and energy-distribution patterns (e.g., pauses and rhythm irregularities) contribute to classification, we did not perform feature-attribution or perturbation analyses to identify which spectrotemporal regions drive decisions. The convergence of loss curves and stable precision–recall trajectories may further support effective generalization.
These findings are in line with psycholinguistic evidence linking reading difficulties to slower phonological processing, irregular temporal rhythm, and fragmented spectral structure [[70](#B70-digital-06-00015),[71](#B71-digital-06-00015),[74](#B74-digital-06-00015),[75](#B75-digital-06-00015)]. In addition, these results align with a substantial body of research demonstrating that developmental reading difficulties are closely associated with inefficient phonological processing and reduced automatization, which contribute to slow, effortful reading and increased pausing during oral reading [[76](#B76-digital-06-00015),[77](#B77-digital-06-00015)]. Theoretical and empirical accounts also emphasize disruptions in temporal sampling and rhythmic processing in dyslexia, linking reading difficulties to atypical processing of syllabic timing and prosodic structure in speech [[78](#B78-digital-06-00015)]. From a behavioral standpoint, reading fluency is conceptualized as the outcome of coordinated decoding and higher-level processes, with dysfluent reading characterized by irregular pacing and breakdowns in temporal synchronization [[79](#B79-digital-06-00015),[80](#B80-digital-06-00015)].
Auditory and prosodic features of oral reading have been shown to relate systematically to fluency and comprehension, and children with reading difficulties frequently display measurable differences in prosodic realization, including altered pause patterns and reduced prosodic integration during text reading [[81](#B81-digital-06-00015),[82](#B82-digital-06-00015)]. In this context, the observed correlation with expert evaluations (r ≈ 0.74) provides convergent evidence that the model’s output tracks expert-rated oral reading performance in this dataset. Importantly, this should be interpreted as an association with auditory correlations of reading behavior (e.g., timing, pausing, and rhythm), rather than as a direct measurement of latent psycholinguistic constructs [[75](#B75-digital-06-00015),[79](#B79-digital-06-00015),[81](#B81-digital-06-00015)].
Finally, we did not observe large performance differences across age and gender (8–12 years) within this sample [[83](#B83-digital-06-00015)]. We emphasize that this descriptive stability should not be interpreted as a formal fairness claim, as subgroup calibration and error-parity analyses were beyond the scope of the present study.
It is essential to emphasize that the proposed tool is not intended to serve as a diagnostic methodology. Rather, its function is aligned with that of a universal screener designed to assist in the early identification of pupils who may require further evaluation or targeted instructional support. The system is suitable for repeated use, allowing for the ongoing monitoring of students’ reading development over time. This role fits within the framework of tiered literacy support models, where systematic screening and progress monitoring inform the equitable and transparent allocation of educational resources [[58](#B58-digital-06-00015),[59](#B59-digital-06-00015)].
To avoid misinterpretation or misuse, it is important to explicitly state that the model is not a substitute for formal diagnosis or professional expertise. Its outputs are meant to guide, not replace, the clinical and educational judgment of specialists. Positioning the tool clearly as a support mechanism within a broader decision-making process reinforces its appropriate use and underscores its value as a complement to, rather than a replacement for, comprehensive assessment practices in educational or clinical settings [[58](#B58-digital-06-00015)].
From an educational standpoint, the principal contribution is not solely the classification of brief audio excerpts but the translation of a familiar classroom activity—short oral reading—into an interpretable screening signal. In the present dataset, the model differentiated difficulty versus no-difficulty excerpts with strong performance and produced a pupil-level “difficulty percentage” that corresponded closely with expert judgments. This aggregation is educationally meaningful because instructional decisions concern students rather than isolated 7 s segments.
A realistic school-based implementation would involve use as a screening and prioritization tool rather than a diagnostic system. Teachers could record a brief oral reading sample during routine instruction, and the system would return a summary indicator. Pupils with persistently elevated difficulty percentages could be prioritized for closer monitoring, targeted fluency or decoding support, or referral through established school support pathways, whereas pupils with low scores could continue with standard instruction and periodic re-screening. This model of use is consistent with tiered support frameworks, in which brief screening measures facilitate the efficient and transparent allocation of educational resources [[44](#B44-digital-06-00015),[84](#B84-digital-06-00015)].
A practical advantage for educators is that outputs are anchored in observable reading behaviors (e.g., frequent pauses and disrupted rhythm) rather than opaque numerical scores. When teachers and specialists can relate system outputs to recognizable oral reading features, appropriate and informed use is more likely. The proposed system should therefore be viewed as complementary to professional judgment and established assessment tools, not as a replacement.
In practice, the system is designed to integrate into routine school screening with minimal disruption. A teacher or trained support educator records a brief oral reading sample (approximately two minutes) during a standard literacy session using commonly available devices in a quiet classroom area. Recordings follow a consistent prompt to enable meaningful comparison across pupils. Audio files are anonymized and processed automatically through the pipeline described in [Figure 1](#fig\_body\_display\_digital-06-00015-f001) (segmentation into 7 s excerpts, spectrogram conversion, and model inference).
The output supports screening and prioritization rather than diagnosis. Teachers receive a student-level indicator that can inform decisions regarding continuation of typical instruction, provision of additional classroom support and monitoring, or referral for comprehensive follow-up assessment. This workflow mirrors established universal screening practices within tiered educational support systems.
The interpretation of these findings must consider several methodological constraints. The dataset was relatively small and lacked substantial linguistic diversity, and the recordings were collected under controlled auditory conditions that may not reflect real-world variability. Moreover, the reference labels were based on expert clinical judgment rather than a standardized diagnostic benchmark, which may limit the validity of the classification outcomes. As such, the system should be regarded as a supplementary tool for early identification and educational decision-making, with its intended purpose and limitations clearly delineated. The use of participant-level data splitting importantly strengthens the reliability of the reported performance metrics by mitigating the risk of inflated accuracy estimates due to subject overlap between training and validation sets—an issue that often arises with segment-level splitting, where multiple speech samples from the same pupil may appear across different data partitions.
While these limitations are acknowledged, the particularly small number of participants with a confirmed diagnosis of dyslexia warrants more explicit discussion. This imbalance introduces a risk of bias in model training and limits the generalizability of the findings to more diverse populations. An additional limitation is that, although the use of a 7 s segmentation window is practically motivated, we did not conduct a formal sensitivity analysis to determine whether classification performance remains stable across alternative window lengths. Furthermore, although the study highlights as its primary contribution the demonstration that a familiar classroom activity, brief oral reading, can be transformed into an interpretable screening signal for the early screening of reading difficulties, this claim must be evaluated in light of the study’s recording conditions. Specifically, the speech samples were gathered in quiet, controlled environments, which differ markedly from the auditory variability of actual classroom settings. This raises important concerns about the ecological validity of the findings, especially in the context of real-world implementation.
However, in the Greek educational system, this issue is particularly relevant. Children with diagnosed or suspected reading disabilities frequently attend “integration classes”, which are distinct from typical mainstream classrooms and offer more structured, individualized instruction. The learning and auditory environments in these settings differ considerably from standard classroom conditions. It is therefore plausible that the study’s controlled recording environment more closely resembles that of the “integration” class rather than the typical classroom. This contextual factor further limits the system’s generalizability and should be explicitly considered when assessing its potential for broader educational application.
Nevertheless, broader validation remains necessary prior to wider implementation. Future evaluation should include diverse school settings, recording devices, dialectal variation, and more naturalistic classroom noise conditions to ensure ecological validity. Moreover, the current binary classification framework (“Reading Difficulties” vs. “No Difficulties”) represents a simplification of the multidimensional nature of reading performance. Subsequent research should therefore consider more fine-grained performance categories, longitudinal monitoring of reading development, and the integration of multimodal indicators—such as EEG, eye-tracking, or complementary behavioral measures—to enhance both sensitivity and explanatory depth [[85](#B85-digital-06-00015),[86](#B86-digital-06-00015)].
From an educational perspective, early identification of reading difficulties is essential as unrecognized challenges may contribute to broader academic and socio-emotional consequences [[87](#B87-digital-06-00015),[88](#B88-digital-06-00015)]. Traditional assessments, although accurate, are often resource-intensive and delayed [[89](#B89-digital-06-00015)]. The proposed spectrogram-based model offers a low-burden, scalable, and language-sensitive screening approach suitable for diverse contexts, including under-resourced or rural settings. Rapid analysis of short speech samples supports frequent formative monitoring, consistent with Response to Intervention (RTI) principles [[44](#B44-digital-06-00015)].
Interpretability enhances educational value. System outputs correspond to observable reading behaviors—such as irregular rhythm or fragmented articulation—linking automated analysis with expert reasoning [[71](#B71-digital-06-00015),[90](#B90-digital-06-00015)]. This alignment positions the system as a complementary tool within established assessment ecosystems. Integration with validated platforms such as Askisi-Lexia could further support hybrid assessment models combining cognitive, linguistic, and auditory information to inform individualized education plans and targeted intervention strategies.
In conclusion, this research demonstrates the potential of AI-enhanced screening tools to provide proactive, accessible, and context-sensitive support for literacy education [[60](#B60-digital-06-00015),[73](#B73-digital-06-00015),[91](#B91-digital-06-00015),[92](#B92-digital-06-00015)]. Within the Greek educational context, where large-scale reading screening solutions remain limited, the proposed framework offers a foundation for broader implementation and cross-linguistic adaptation, supporting early detection, timely intervention, and more equitable learning opportunities.
## 7. Conclusions
This study introduced a novel and effective methodology for the automated evaluation of reading fluency and difficulty in primary school children, employing spectrogram-based convolutional neural networks within a modified You Only Look Once detection framework. The approach successfully captured relevant temporal and spectral auditory patterns that differentiate fluent from disfluent reading segments, achieving a balanced accuracy of 0.85 and a macro F1-score of 0.89 at the optimal decision threshold.
The findings are consistent with the prior literature, indicating that reading difficulties are reflected in characteristic auditory markers, including fragmented energy distribution, irregular harmonic structure, and increased pause frequency, all of which were associated with the model’s discriminative pattern. Importantly, the system demonstrated stability and generalization across samples, supporting its potential applicability in educational and clinical support contexts.
Moreover, the spectrogram-based methodology provides an interpretable, objective, and non-invasive screening approach that aligns closely with expert evaluations, addressing a notable gap in Greek educational technology. By automating the detection of reading challenges from raw audio recordings, the method can support early identification and timely intervention, with potential benefits for children at risk of dyslexia and related reading disorders.
Future research should prioritize expanding the dataset to include multilingual and dialectal variation, integrating complementary modalities such as EEG and eye-tracking data, and evaluating system performance in naturalistic classroom environments. These steps will strengthen ecological validity and enhance the reliability and applicability of the model in real-world educational settings.
In conclusion, this work establishes a foundation for the development of advanced, scalable, and interpretable AI-assisted approaches for the early identification of reading difficulties, contributing to the fields of educational technology, speech processing, and special education.
