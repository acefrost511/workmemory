Abstract
--------

Kenya’s competency-based curriculum highlights the role of STEM education and the development of skills of the 21st century. However, computer-based assessment (CBA) of these skills has not yet been conducted in Kenya. This study aimed to investigate the applicability of newly developed international computer-based skill assessments for developing scientific inquiry, experimentation, reasoning, argumentation, and collaboration skills in public junior high school students in Kenya (*N* = 60). We employed inductive reasoning (IR), deductive reasoning (DR), control of variables (CoV), collaborative skill (CS), and scientific reasoning (SR) to evaluate their contribution to the development of collaborative scientific reasoning (CSR). Instrument reliabilities and construct validities confirmed the general applicability of the tests; however, the internationally widely used Lawson test was shown to have limited applicability. The IR, DR, and CoV were found to be predictive of CSR when measured alone, the overarching model explaining 60% of the variance in CSR; however, the direct predictive role of IR became non-significant. The results reflect the complexity of CSR and the significant limitations of studies that have individually examined the effects of cognitive and/or affective skills. Therefore, future research should adopt a longitudinal design that assesses these skills. Our findings can inform the development of STEM education in Kenya’s national assessment strategy and ensure alignment with its competency-based curriculum.

1 Introduction
--------------

In the 21st century, many countries, such as Kenya, have made substantial changes in their curricula, teaching approaches, and assessment methods to better prepare students for the increasing educational demands and practical application of relevant skills. Generally, global education reforms are believed to prioritize the development of skills of the 21st century (Koul et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR35)), particularly STEM. These reforms seek to impart scientific knowledge, foster and enhance the scientific skills required in many professional careers, and enable citizens to make informed decisions on socioscientific issues, such as climate change and health crises (Stadler et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR61)). This approach enhances learning efficiency (Hasruddin & Aulia, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR22)) and encourages the development of scientific reasoning (SR) to ensure that students understand the logic behind scientific knowledge generation and develop scientific discourse skills (Zimmerman, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR78)). Beyond STEM education, the need to think and work together has also become an issue, shifting the emphasis from individual efforts to group work and from independence to community.

Collaborative scientific reasoning (CSR) combines these skills by emphasizing cooperation, communication, and the integration of diverse perspectives to build knowledge and develop scientifically sound conclusions. Computerized testing methods have achieved a more effective and successful assessment of it and its potential component skills (Adey & Csapó, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR8)). New computerized testing methods have been developed to assess the success of these new approaches in developing appropriate skills (Adey & Csapó, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR8)). Specifically, computer-based assessments (CBA) incorporate digital components that enrich test items with resources, such as video explanations and interactive elements (Mullis & Martin, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR45)).

CBA also provides immediate feedback, employs innovative item types, reduces test administration costs (Csapó et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR15)) and the time required for assessment and feedback, and promotes student inclusiveness and uniformity (Dyrvold & Bergvall, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR18)). CBA has been used to assess cognitive skills across different grade levels (see OECD PISA assessments, (Authors, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR1); Kambeyo & Wu, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR30); Mousa & Molnár, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR42); Pásztor et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR50)).

However, computer-based assessment has been used for assessing complex cognitive and collaborative skills, and its implementation is highly dependent on the availability of ICT infrastructure. The digital literacy program, a national initiative of the Kenyan government, was conceptualized as a one-laptop-per-child policy with tablets for students and laptops for teachers distributed throughout the country (Government of Kenya, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR19)). Despite the program, the laptop project faced challenges due to lack of funds, electricity, teacher training, storage, and digital devices to run the digital program in schools (Nyaundi, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR46)). Further studies indicate that computer-to-student ratios and weak system maintenance affect the daily use of digital tools for assessment (Mingaine, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR43)). Therefore, the effective implementation of computer-based assessment in Kenya depends on the availability of the required infrastructure.

However, studies have reported infrastructure-related challenges to the implementation of computer-based assessment, and limited empirical studies have examined relationships between inductive reasoning (IR), deductive reasoning (DR), control of variables (CoV), collaborative skills (CS), scientific reasoning (SR), and student CSR performance when assessed through CBA. Therefore, this pilot study seeks to: (1) investigate the practicality of CBA in the Kenyan context; (2) test the reliability, validity and suitability of these instruments to measure theoretically related cognitive skills to CSR among Kenyan students; and (3) identify cognitive predictors of CSR.

2 Collaboration in scientific reasoning: definition, components, and assessments
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

### 2.1 Definition of collaborative scientific reasoning and its components

Zhou et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR77)) define SR skills as basic reasoning processes, such as forming hypotheses, designing experiments to test these hypotheses, distinguishing between definitive and indeterminate evidence, and interpreting results to support or refute the hypotheses. Osborne ([2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR48)) characterizes SR as the logical and deductive application of reasoning using necessary scientific knowledge. Hogan and Fisherkeller ([2005](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR26)) describe SR as the cognitive process of critically analyzing and evaluating scientific knowledge. Kuhn ([2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR36)) sees SR as a specific type of information seeking driven by the same fundamental reasoning processes as other forms of curiosity-driven inquiry.

Lawson ([1978](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR37)) states that SR involves the application of specific cognitive processes, such as formulating and testing hypotheses and drawing conclusions, and that assessment of these cognitive skills includes hypothesis testing, logical reasoning, and understanding cause-and-effect relationships. Therefore, from these definitions, it could be argued that SR consists of IR and DR skills and the ability to control variables, understand probability, and engage in collaborative correlational reasoning. CSR involves a team-based framework in which the component skills of each play a role in achieving a collective scientific understanding, with IR allowing members to collaborate in sharing observations and DR helping to make predictions about the expected outcome.

SR skills allow team members to draw scientifically valid conclusions from the team’s observations. For example, CoV skills enable a team to explore scientific problems, and CSs ensure that the team works toward a common goal. Therefore, SR is closely related to cognitive transversal skills (Greiff et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR21); Authors, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR1)), such as IR and DR (Wesiak, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR71)) and CoV skills (Zimmermann, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR78)).

In summary, CSR involves collective brainstorming (Hesse et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR25)), hypothesis formation (Zimmerman, [2007](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR79)), and group experiments, all of which involve interpreting research data, evaluating research findings, and drawing conclusions (Kuhn, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR36)). In other words, CSR is the ability to control variables (Zimmerman, [2007](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR79)) and employ IR and DR (Osborne, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR48)) within collaborative contexts (Kuhn, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR36)).

### 2.2 Inductive reasoning and assessment

IR is a cognitive process that involves the derivation of general principles or conclusions from specific observations or cases (Hayes & Heit, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR23)). There are two main aspects to IR: direction, in which the observation of specific instances leads to the formulation of more general rules, and confidence in dealing with uncertainty when making these hypotheses about the rules (Perret, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR52)). Therefore, IR involves pattern identification, hypothesis formation, generalization, and the ability to draw relevant conclusions. Students with high IR generally achieve higher SR assessment scores (Vo, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR67)).

Previous studies have evaluated IR with various item types, such as series completion, analogy, geometric matrices (verbal, numerical, and figural elements), and categorization (Adey and Csapó [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR8); Authors [2018a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR3), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR4), [c](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR5); Vo and Csapó [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR69)). In series completion tasks, participants use extrapolation to identify the missing piece from a set of items (numbers, letters, words and figures) that are arranged according to their relationships, the difficulty of which can be influenced by quantity, pattern presentation, and relational rules (Wesiak, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR71)). Analogies follow a format where A is to B as C is to D (A: B: C: D). The test taker first identifies the relationship between items A and B and then uses this relationship to find a match for item C, the difficulty of which is determined by semantic relationships, the relevance of the components, and the content (Vo & Csapó, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR67)). Geometric matrix tasks involve arranging figures in a matrix with one missing cell, and categorization tasks, which involve identifying the correct alternatives to items in a series, are similar to analogy and series completion tasks, as participants discover rules within a set and identify the items that follow those rules (Vo & Csapó, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR67)).

### 2.3 Deductive reasoning and assessment

DR involves concluding from known premises based on formal logical rules (Ayalon & Even, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR9)); that is, conclusions are drawn from existing information without the need for further testing. There are two types of DR: conditional and syllogistic (Sternberg, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR62)). Conditional reasoning is used when a statement is used to complete a given task and reach a logical conclusion. Syllogistic reasoning is a form of DR that concludes with two premises. Unlike IR, deductive arguments rely on premises that provide evidence to support conclusions (Radulovic & Stojanoic, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR54)). Hypothetical DR, a special form of DR, allows students to formulate hypotheses (Ardelean, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR80)) and reach logical conclusions.

Several methods have been used to assess DR. For example, Carreira et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR12)) measured DR with analytical reasoning problems, and De Chantal and Markovits ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR16)) evaluated syllogistic reasoning in preschool children in a game format. Csapó et al. ([2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR15)) assessed early DR and school readiness in first-graders using a CBA with logical reasoning tasks, from which the children drew logical conclusions.

### 2.4 Control of variables strategy and assessment

CoV, an essential element of SR (Kuhn & Dean, [2005](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR81)), is a fundamental skill of SR that is deemed crucial for the design, prediction, and assessment of experimental situations (Chen & Klahr, [1999](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR13)). CoV entails the organized alteration of a single variable while maintaining the constancy of the other variables to guarantee that the observed results are due to the manipulated variable. The general domain approach of CoV well predicts cognitive abilities and applies them in various circumstances. A CoV strategy can enhance student proficiency across many activities (Toth et al., [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR82)), both scientific and nonscientific, and its assessment can predict general reasoning skills (Bryant et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR11)).

CoV operates in both procedural and logical contexts, as it entails differentiating confounded and nonconfounded controlled trials, comprehending the fundamental indeterminacy of confounded trials, and deriving suitable inferences from the results in an experimental framework (Van der Graaf et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR65)). CoV consists of four essential sub-skills: designing controlled experiments, distinguishing confounded and unconfounded trials, analyzing experimental results, and acknowledging the ambiguity of confounded experiments (Chen & Klahr, [1999](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR13)). These cognitive processes facilitate advanced logical reasoning and causal comprehension, which are crucial for scientific investigations and daily decision-making. Studies have indicated that child experimentation skills and scientific knowledge evolve concurrently, even before formal education, suggesting a reciprocal relationship between these abilities (Koerber & Osterhaus, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR84)).

CoV assessments often employ domain-specific instruments that evaluate certain abilities: experimental planning, recognizing confounded designs, and interpreting results. Lawson’s classroom test of scientific reasoning ([2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR38)) uses multiple-choice in various subjects, such as physics, chemistry, and biology, to assess skills related to designing controlled experiments and interpreting results. Similarly, to evaluate the development of the CoV strategy in secondary school students, Vo and Csapó ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR68)) used multiple-choice items focused on physics. In other studies, Osterhaus et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR49)) developed a multiple-choice tool to assess the understanding of primary school children of experimental design, particularly hypotheses and controlled outcomes; Schwichow et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR57)) developed a paper-and-pencil instrument with multiple choices to assess the experimental skills of middle school students, and Zohar and David ([2008](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR76)) explored student comprehension of confounded experiment indeterminacy using open response assessments. Collectively, these instruments provide insights into specific CoV skills and can support educators in identifying and nurturing student SR skills.

### 2.5 Collaboration skills and assessment

Collaboration entails two or more people sharing responsibility, making joint decisions, and working together to learn something (Hernández, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR24)). In educational settings, collaboration involves students working together as a group to solve problems and achieve a common goal (Sahin et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR55)). CS can drive learning, including inductive, deductive, and associative learning. Dillenbourg ([1999](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR17)) points out that collaboration comprises cognitive and social skills, with the cognitive skills used for task regulation and knowledge construction and the social skills used for participation, perspective taking, and social regulation (Piniuta, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR53)), which comprises interpersonal, group management, inquiry, conflict, and presentation subskills (Bosworth, [1994](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR10)).

Collaboration for scientific learning can help student groups understand scientific concepts and solve scientific problems (Yasin et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR75)). CSs have been assessed with observation and assessment rubrics. Students can also self-reflect and assess their CS through self-assessment and peer feedback (Piniuta, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR53)). Technological tools, such as online platforms that facilitate group interactions, track communication patterns and task completion, and provide data on individual contributions and group dynamics (Lin et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR39)).

3 Objectives and research questions
-----------------------------------

The study objectives are as follows. First, our objective was to investigate the applicability of CBA in the Kenyan educational system. Specifically, we examined the reliability, construct validity, and applicability of five assessment instruments that individually measure IR, DR, CoV, and CS in a non-science context and SR in a science context. SR was assessed with the Lawson test and a newly developed SR test.

Second, we monitored the relationships. Third, by combining these separate science and non-science assessments, we developed an overarching model to explore the role of IR, DR, and CoV in the development of CSR. Our focus was on four key research questions:

* RQ1: Are CBAs reliable and valid in the Kenyan educational context?
* RQ1a: Is the internationally used IR test reliable and valid in the Kenyan educational context?
* RQ1b: Is the newly developed DR test reliable and valid in the Kenyan educational context?
* RQ1c: Is the internationally used CoV test reliable and valid in the Kenyan educational context?
* RQ1d: Is the newly developed SR test reliable and valid in the Kenyan educational context?
* RQ1e: Is the internationally used Lawson test reliable and valid in the Kenyan educational context?
* RQ1f: Is the internationally used CS questionnaire reliable and valid in the Kenyan educational context?
* RQ2: To what extent do IR, DR, and CoV relate and what are their respective relationships to SR?
* RQ3: What individual roles do IR, DR, and CoV play in the development of CSR?
* RQ4: To what extent does the overarching model predict CSR latency? What factors and what skill types are the most important?.

4 Methodology
-------------

### 4.1 Participants and sampling

The pilot study involved grade 7 students selected from public junior secondary schools in Kenya. Grade 7 is the entry point to junior secondary school under the competency-based education; therefore, it was selected as the target population. The population comprised all Grade 7 students enrolled in junior secondary school in Kenya. Schools that had a well-established and functional ICT infrastructure, including access to computers, electricity, and internet connectivity required for administering computer-based assessments, were selected. These schools were randomly selected to participate in the study, and students from these schools were selected by convenience sampling. Students who participated in the data collection, obtained parental consent, and provided student assent were eligible to participate. A sample consisted of 60 students (27 boys and 33 girls; mean age = 13.10 years). The sample size was considered appropriate for a pilot study that focused on investigating the feasibility, reliability, and construct validity of computer-based assessment instruments rather than for statistical generalization.

### 4.2 Instruments

The study is based on six tests that measure the different skills that are theoretically essential for CSR: (1) IR, (2) DR, (3) CoV, (4), SR, (5) the Lawson test and, finally, (6) CS. We developed electronic versions of all the instruments and administered them on the eDia platform (Csapó & Molnár, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR14)), which supports automated scoring and provides immediate feedback at the end of each test.

#### 4.2.1 Inductive reasoning test

IR skills were assessed with a well-known 53 item test (Authors [2018a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR3), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR4), [c](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR5)).The test has four subtests: number analogies, in which students identify missing numbers and place them in a yellow box; number series, in which they identify and match the missing numbers in sequences; figural analogies, in which they identify and place correct shapes; and figural series, in which they match shapes to complete a sequence, as shown in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#Fig1). Although the test was not designed for scientific contexts, it provides an in-depth assessment of reasoning. Students receive one point for correct answers and zero points for incorrect ones. The items were adopted from the original instruments without modification of the content to preserve their psychometric properties. The instruments were selected over other IR instruments since they provide a balanced assessment based on numerical and figural reasoning with strong reliability and validity. Therefore, the application of this instrument provides a valid assessment of inductive reasoning as well as demonstrating a practical implementation of computer-based assessment in junior secondary school, providing a suitable model for researchers in assessing cognitive skills using computer-based assessment.

#### 4.2.2 Deductive reasoning test

DR skills were measured with the 27-item DR test, in which test-takers make specific conclusions from general premises or statements (Walton, [2008](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR70)). The elements (see Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#Fig2)) are designed to focus on reasoning processes, with test elements designed based on a syllogistic structure that follows specific assessment principles. The test has three main parts: a major premise, a minor premise, and a conclusion derived from these premises (Khemlani & Johnson-Laird, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR32)). Syllogistic reasoning tasks are often used to measure high-level cognitive skills (Scully, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR58)).

#### 4.2.3 Control of variables strategic test

The CoV strategy test consists of 15 innovative elements adapted from the MicroDYN approach (Greiff et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR20)), which presents systems with interrelated variables, often in simulated environments (Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#Fig3)). Participants must explore these systems to understand how variables interact and how they can be manipulated to achieve the goal. This test simulates real-world situations where it is crucial to control and understand variables for informed decision-making. In the tasks, the participants experiment with the variables to observe their effects. For example, they isolate one variable at a time to examine the effect and minimize any confounding factors, with this systematic exploration allowing for a deduction of the underlying system structure. The test process highlights the ability of the participants to effectively control the variables. At the beginning of the assessment, students watch an introductory video with text-based instructions and practice test tasks on the use of the assessment platform.

#### 4.2.4 Newly developed scientific reasoning test

The newly developed SR test comprises 20 items specifically designed to assess SR in the Kenyan educational context, such as conservation, classification, proportional reasoning, and correlational reasoning. The content is based on fundamental concepts of integrated science subjects for junior secondary school students within Kenya’s competency-based curriculum. Some test items were adapted from the original Korom et al. ([2017a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR33), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR34)) test, and others were based on principles in the Lawson test ([2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR85)), all of which were converted to multiple-choice format, with each question containing one correct answer and three distractors. Figure [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#Fig4) provides a sample item from the self-developed SR test on the eDia platform.

#### 4.2.5 The Lawson classroom test of scientific reasoning

The Lawson classroom test of scientific reasoning (LCTSR; Lawson, 2020) is a well-established assessment tool that has been widely used to assess students’ SR skills. This two-tier test has 24 elements designed to assess different aspects of SR, such as isolating and controlling variables, combinatorial reasoning, correlational reasoning, probabilistic reasoning, and proportional reasoning (Hrouzková & Richterek, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR27)). Originally developed by Lawson in 1978 and updated in 2000, the LCTSR remains the cornerstone of SR assessments.

#### 4.2.6 Collaborative skills questionnaire

### 4.3 Procedure

The pilot test was conducted in the schools’ computer labs on the eDia platform (Authors [2019a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR6), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR7)). Students accessed the platform and logged in with personal codes. The test sessions were divided into four 60-minute sessions: in session one, the IR was tested; in session two, the DR was tested; in session three, the CoV and Lawson tests were administered; and in session four, the self-developed SR test and the CS questionnaire were administered.

### 4.4 Data analysis

SPSS version 27 and Mplus version 8.4 were used to perform the data analyses. Although the sample size (*N* = 60) is modest, structural equation modeling (SEM) was used to determine that the relationship between IR, DR, CoV, CS, and SR can be modeled within the nature of the pilot study. The SEM sample size was not fixed but depends on model characteristics such as the number of indicators and the strength of factor loadings (Wolf et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR72)). Furthermore, the robust maximum likelihood estimator (MLR) of Mplus 8.4 and the bootstrapping procedures were used to enhance the stability of parameter estimates and confidence intervals under small sample conditions. Cronbach’s alpha (α) was used to assess the internal consistency and reliability of the instruments, descriptive statistics were used to describe the test difficulties, confirmatory factor analyses (CFA) allowed us to test the validity of the construct, and the complete SEM (structural equation models) allowed us to individually determine the predictive power of the different cognitive factors in the overarching CSR model. CSR, as a latent variable, was defined in conjunction with the manifest collaboration and SR variables to enhance the generalizability of the results. To evaluate the model fit, an absolute fit index (the root mean square error of approximation (RMSEA) and two incremental fit indices (the Tucker-Lewis index (TLI) and the comparative fit index (CFI) were calculated. A TLI and CFI larger than 0.90 paired with an RMSEA less than 0.08 are commonly considered an acceptable model fit (Hu & Bentler, [1999](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR28)).

5 Results

### 5.1 Applicability of computer-based assessments in the Kenyan educational context (RQ1a–f)

The pilot study was conducted successfully. However, the CoV test proved very difficult for the students (M = 10.59%, SD = 12.9). One fifth (21%) could not complete a single task correctly, an indication that some items were difficult and considered inappropriate. Items with low variance or weak item correlation and that might measure a different construct were deleted to prevent poorly functioning items from affecting reliability estimates. After deleting some inappropriate items from the tests, except for the Lawson test, which had low homogeneity (Cronbach’s alpha = 0.255), the other test reliability coefficients ranged from.714 to.935. ### 5.3 Cognitive factors that predict collaborative scientific reasoning (RQ3)

In the first model, we monitored the predictive power of IR on CSR. 6 Discussion
------------

This study focus on two folds (1) examining the feasibility of computer-based assessment (CBA) in the Kenyan educational context and (2) to explore the relationships among key cognitive domains inductive reasoning (IR), deductive reasoning (DR), control of variables (CoV), and scientific reasoning (SR) in predicting collaborative scientific reasoning (CSR).To this end, six tests were administered online to assess four cognitive domains (IR, DR, CoV, and SR) and one affective domain, which included two self-developed (DR and SR) and four widely used instruments (IR, CoV, Lawson SR test, and CS). These findings clearly indicate that CBA is applicable in the Kenyan education context despite the challenges that the curriculum faces. The reliability and construct validity results indicated that five of the six instruments demonstrated acceptable to good psychometric properties, suggesting that most CBAs were suitable for use with the Kenyan competency-based curriculum. In contrast, the internationally widely used Lawson test showed poor reliability, raising concerns about its suitability in the Kenyan education context. These could be attributed to the following reasons: (1) students’ low mean achievement reflects the underdevelopment of foundation reasoning skills at the early stage of schooling. (2) development and validation of Lawson tests in Western education contexts, likely to introduce poor scientific interpretation of items, leading to a lack of consistency in responses. (3) Lengthy two-tier structured items impose challenges to reading and cognitive loads beyond the intended measurement of scientific reasoning.

The assessment of the students’ mean achievement of the students in the CoV tests raised concerns about the applicability of the innovative third-generation CoV test, primarily because one-fifth of the sample was unable to complete a single task correctly. There could be two reasons for these results: (1) the level of ability of the students in this domain is very low and/or the task was too difficult to be useful; and (2) the innovative CoV tasks required that the students manipulate and control the variables within the tasks, which required greater familiarity with technology than just being able to answer multiple-choice questions. This issue is particularly important in the Kenyan context, where, despite government investment in tablets and international donations of computers, effective ICT integration remains constrained by poor network connectivity, unreliable electricity supply, large class sizes, and inadequate device maintenance (Government of Kenya, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR19)). Limited and inconsistent exposure to digital tools likely restricts students’ ability to engage successfully with technology-intensive assessment formats.

These challenges are not unique to Kenya but reflect broader patterns observed across many low- and middle-income countries. Similar constraints have been reported in Zambia, where the implementation of competency-based curricula and digital assessment has been limited by financial constraints and reliance on external donor support (Mulenga & Kabombwe, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR44)). Comparable infrastructure and capacity challenges have also been documented in Rwanda and Tanzania, rural electricity instability in South Africa (Sithole & Mbukanma, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR59)), and erratic power supply affecting computer-based testing in Nigeria (Usman & Olaleye, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR64)). Collectively, these findings suggest that the feasibility of CBA in such contexts is conditional, depending not only on assessment design but also on systemic infrastructural and institutional support.

In summary, based on descriptive statistics and reliability and construct validity analysis, we concluded that CBAs, particularly first- and second-generation instruments, are mostly applicable but that third-generation tests have limited applicability at this time. The bivariate correlations between IR, DR, and CoV were moderate, indicating that IR, DR, and CoV are related constructs. Partial correlations were nonsignificant; that is, all bivariate relationships were influenced by the third construct. IR, as a basic reasoning skill, had a stronger effect on DR and CoV than DR and CoV had on each other. These findings align with Kazali’s ([2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR31)) findings that IR and DR are correlated and share a common underlying construct.

SEM was used to examine the predictive power of IR, DR, and CoV. Due to the small sample size, IR, DR, and CoV were treated as manifest variables. This approach was supported by the results for RQ1, which provided evidence for the applicability of 1-dimensional models in which all indicators are combined under a single general factor. The CSR was modeled as a latent variable, drawing on the SR and CS results to enhance the generalizability and validity of the findings. We confirmed previous research findings (Vo, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR66)) on the predictive role of IR, the predictive power of IR for CS, and the relationship between IR and SR (Kambeyo, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR29); Korom et al. [2017a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR33), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR34)); that is, IR and its operations play an important role and have a significant predictive power for CSR. Second, we examine the role of DR for CSR in a separate SEM model. Based on the results for RQ2, namely that IR and DR are related constructs, we expected DR to have significant predictive power for CSR, which was confirmed. DR explained 38% of the variance in CSR, which was similar to IR. Finally, the predictive power of CoV for CSR was examined. Because the three constructs were correlated in answering RQ2 and the ‘Control-of-variables strategy (CoV) is considered a hallmark in the development of scientific reasoning’ (Schwichow et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR56)), we also expected significant predictive paths in this model. The results confirmed our hypothesis, but contrary to expectations based on theory, the explained variance for CoV decreased compared to the IR and DR values. The reason for this could be that IR, DR, and CoV were not measured in a scientific context, suggesting that the transfer of knowledge and skills was not as high as in previous studies.

An overarching model with constructs was built to control the common elements of IR, DR, and CoV for CSR. The full SEM model fits the data marginally. The CFI had an excellent fit, but the TLI and RMSEA only indicated a marginal fit. The model explained 61% of the variance in CSR, indicating that these skills play an important role in the development of CSR, while the other skills are also vital. The relatively low but marginally acceptable TLI and RMSEA values (TLI = 0.882, CFI = 0.966, RMSEA = 0.091) were in line with our expectations for this complex SEM model due to the low sample size, which was lower than the 250 suggested by Newsom ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR86)) and Hu and Bentler ([1999](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13981-7#ref-CR28)). As absolute fit indices (e.g., RMSEA) are more sensitive to misspecification than relative fit indices (e.g., CFI), further research is needed on a larger sample to validate this result. In the overarching model, the direct role of IR for CSR was not significant, and CoV and DR played this role.

7 Limitations
-------------

The study had some limitations. First, the small sample size, since the study was conducted as a pilot study to assess the feasibility and psychometric properties of a computer-based assessment instrument in the Kenyan context. This affected the determination of item parameter estimates of the constructs, as well as the construct validity. Therefore, it affected the representation accuracy and generalizability of the findings. Second, the research participants were from the same grade levels; we were unable to analyze student CSR across grades. Furthermore, the study only focused on a subset of CSR component skills, the cognitive variables IR, DR, and CoV, and did not monitor affective variables, such as science motivation.

8 Conclusion and further studies
--------------------------------

This pilot study was a first attempt to use computer-based methods in assessing SR skills, especially CSR skills, among junior secondary school students exposed to a competency-based curriculum in Kenya. Widely used and newly developed instruments were used to assess specific cognitive predictive factors for CSR, IR, DR, and CoV, and SR and CS were measured separately. An important finding was that the well-known Lawson test for SR (Cronbach’s alpha = 0.255) has weak reliability and construct validity to assess Kenyan 7th-grade SR skills, and that third-generation online tests that require more advanced computer skills (such as the CoV test that was used) have limited application due to technological limitations in Kenyan schools. Beyond these limitations, the CBAs were deemed suitable to assess the cognitive and affective skills of Kenyan students. IR, DR, and CoV were found to predict CSR when measured alone, accounting for 36%, 38%, and 26% of the variance, respectively. However, nearly 39% of the unexplained variance, the direct predictive role of IR, became nonsignificant in the overall model, which highlighted the complexity of CSR assessments and the significant limitations of studies that individually assess the effect of cognitive and/or affective skills. We plan to design empirical research with a larger student sample from different grade levels, including other relevant affective variables. A longitudinal study could provide more information on the influence these cognitive and affective skills have on the development of CSR in the Kenyan education context.
