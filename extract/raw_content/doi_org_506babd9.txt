## Abstract
Generative artificial intelligence (GAI) is reshaping educational practices; however, parental perspectives remain underexamined, particularly across cultural contexts. This study investigates how Chinese and Western parents perceive the benefits and risks of using GAI for tutoring through a cross-cultural qualitative analysis grounded in a sociotechnical perspective and the extended valence framework. The findings show that, across cultures, parents recognize key technological advantages of GAI in education, including breadth of knowledge, personalized learning, accessibility, and cost-effectiveness. Nevertheless, motivations and concerns diverge substantially. Chinese parents emphasize the necessity of GAI to maintain academic competitiveness and prepare for future careers, expressing concern that nonadoption may disadvantage their children. By contrast, Western parents exhibit greater skepticism, prioritizing concerns about overreliance on GAI, misinformation, excessive screen time, and social risks. By foregrounding how technological affordances, perceived benefits, and perceived risks interact within distinct sociocultural contexts, this study extends prior research on GAI adoption in education, which has largely focused on teachers, students, and institutions within single cultural settings. The study also offers practical implications for GAI developers, policymakers, and educators, underscoring the need for culturally adaptive strategies that balance innovation, ethical considerations, and parental trust in the use of GAI for tutoring.
## Introduction
The rise of generative artificial intelligence (GAI) has significantly affected multiple domains, with education emerging as one of the most transformative areas (An et al., 2025; Garcia-Varela et al., 2025; Yin et al., 2024). GAI-powered tools can generate contextually relevant responses, provide real-time feedback, and adapt to individual learning needs, making them valuable assets in contemporary educational settings (Deng et al., 2025). Unlike traditional learning approaches that rely on fixed curricula and standardized instruction, GAI enables a more dynamic and interactive mode of learning, allowing students to engage with content in a personalized manner (Shen & Guo, 2024; Wang et al., 2024). These capabilities position GAI as a transformative force in education, with the potential to enhance accessibility, foster self-directed learning, and provide personalized academic support (Lo et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024).
Although GAI-based tutoring holds considerable promise for transforming education, its adoption is influenced not only by technological capabilities but also by the perspectives of key stakeholders, particularly parents (Yu et al., 2025). Parents play a pivotal role in shaping their children's learning environments and make critical decisions regarding the educational tools and technologies their children use (McFayden et al., 2024). Despite growing interest in AI-driven education, existing research on technology adoption in learning contexts has focused primarily on students, educators, and institutions (e.g., An et al., 2025; Avci, 2024; Mogavi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). While this body of work provides valuable insights into how AI can support teaching and learning, it often overlooks the role of parents as gatekeepers of their children's educational experiences (Han et al., 2024). Unlike educators, who typically integrate AI tools within structured classroom settings, parents determine whether and how AI-driven tutoring is incorporated into home-based learning. Consequently, studies centered on educators or students may not fully capture the distinctive decision-making processes and considerations parents weigh when evaluating GAI tutoring for their children (Han et al., 2024; McFayden et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2025).
Cultural differences further shape parental attitudes and decision-making related to GAI-driven tutoring (Cronjé, 2011). Education systems, parenting norms, and perceptions of technology vary substantially across cultures, influencing how parents assess the benefits and risks of integrating GAI into their children's learning environments (De Morentin et al., 2014). For example, in collectivist cultures, parents may prioritize GAI tools that reinforce structured learning and academic achievement, whereas in individualist cultures, parents may value AI-driven tutoring for its capacity to promote independent learning and creativity (Hofstede, 2001; Zhou, 2024). Cultural contexts also influence levels of trust in AI, with some societies demonstrating greater skepticism toward automated decision-making and others adopting technological innovations more readily (Omrani et al., 2022). These differences highlight the need for culturally sensitive approaches to GAI tutoring that align with local educational values and parental expectations. Accordingly, this study aims to \_explore the factors influencing parents' intentions and attitudes toward adopting GAI for tutoring and to examine how cultural differences shape these perceptions\_.
This study focuses on China and Western countries as two representative contexts with distinct educational traditions and technological orientations. In China, shaped by Confucian-heritage and collectivist values, parents tend to emphasize academic excellence, diligence, and the instrumental use of technology to maintain educational competitiveness and promote social mobility (Zhou, 2024). In contrast, Western societies, grounded largely in individualist values, often prioritize learner autonomy, creativity, and balanced technology use that supports self-directed learning (Ortiz, Green, & Lim, 2011). Comparing these two contexts, therefore, provides a meaningful framework for identifying both shared concerns and culturally specific interpretations of GAI-based tutoring.
Given the exploratory nature of this research, a qualitative design is employed to uncover underlying motivations, concerns, and cultural variations in parental adoption of GAI-driven tutoring (Kohnke & Moorhouse, 2025; Lee et al., 2006; Wasko & Faraj, 2000). The study adopts a grounded theory approach to data collection and analysis, supporting an inductive, theory-building process that captures emergent themes from naturally occurring parental discussions (Strauss & Corbin, 1998). Two datasets are drawn from online discussion platforms: Reddit, representing Western parents, and Zhihu, representing Chinese parents. These platforms provide authentic, real-world perspectives on GAI tutoring adoption. Data are collected using keyword-based searches and analyzed through qualitative content analysis. To guide interpretation, the study integrates two theoretical frameworks: the sociotechnical perspective and the extended valence framework (Baxter & Sommerville, 2011; Cenfetelli, 2004; Ji et al., 2025; Lyytinen & Newman, 2008). The sociotechnical perspective examines how technological, social, and individual factors interact to shape GAI adoption, while the extended valence framework organizes parental perceptions into perceived benefits (e.g., knowledge breadth, personalized learning, accessibility, cost-effectiveness), perceived risks (e.g., misinformation, overdependence, social risks), and social influences (e.g., cultural pressures, peer adoption, institutional trust). Together, these frameworks support a comprehensive analysis of how technological affordances, perceived benefits, and perceived risks shape parental attitudes toward GAI tutoring across cultural contexts (Omrani et al., 2022; Wan et al., 2017).
This study makes several theoretical and practical contributions to research on educational technology adoption, GAI-driven tutoring, and cross-cultural technology acceptance. First, it advances the literature on educational technology adoption by integrating the parental perspective into GAI tutoring research and recognizing parents as key decision-makers in the adoption and use of GAI tools. While prior studies have focused largely on educators, students, and institutions (e.g., An et al., 2025; Avci, 2024; Mogavi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), this study demonstrates how parents actively shape the diffusion and implementation of GAI in home-based learning environments. By extending the focus beyond institutional contexts, the study broadens existing adoption frameworks to account for the informal yet influential role of parents as technology gatekeepers (Han et al., 2024; McFayden et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2025).
Second, the study deepens understanding of the adoption and impact of GAI tools for tutoring, an emerging but underexamined area of GAI-driven education research (Deng et al., 2025). Unlike conventional digital learning technologies, GAI tutoring introduces additional adoption considerations related to trust in AI-generated content, perceived educational effectiveness, and concerns about cognitive dependency (Garcia-Varela et al., 2025). By analyzing these issues through a sociotechnical lens and the extended valence framework, the study highlights how GAI-specific attributes influence parental adoption decisions and how these dynamics differ from those associated with traditional educational technologies. This contribution is particularly salient as GAI continues to shape personalized learning, self-directed education, and parental involvement in digital learning practices.
Third, the study contributes a cross-cultural perspective on GAI tutoring adoption by comparing how Western and Chinese parents perceive the benefits and risks of integrating AI into their children's education (De Morentin et al., 2014; Hofstede, 2001; Weber & Hsee, 1998). While existing research has examined GAI adoption in educational contexts, few studies have systematically compared parental decision-making across cultures. By identifying cultural differences in parental motivations, such as Western concerns about overdependence on GAI and Chinese emphasis on GAI literacy and competitive pressure, this study extends research on cross-cultural technology adoption and enhances the applicability of adoption frameworks beyond predominantly Western contexts (Cronjé, 2011; Leidner & Kayworth, 2006).
Finally, the study offers practical implications for policymakers, educational institutions, and AI developers. By identifying the key drivers and barriers shaping parental adoption of GAI tutoring, the findings inform the design, communication, and implementation of AI-based educational tools in ways that better align with parental expectations and concerns. Attention to cultural variation in AI adoption can support the development of adaptive GAI tutoring solutions that enhance educational outcomes while addressing risks related to misinformation, cognitive dependency, and social development.
