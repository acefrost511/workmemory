Abstract
--------

This study examines technology integration in P–12 education by comparing principals’ perceptions of the current state (School Ecosystem of Technology Education Model, SETEM) and the perceived ideal state (Ideal SETEM) of school-level technological readiness and teacher competence. Using a quantitative research design, survey data were collected from 66 school principals in northern Taiwan and analyzed through Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modeling (PLS-SEM). The findings reveal clear discrepancies between perceived importance and current practice. While instructional material resource readiness and human resource readiness are emphasized in the ideal model, the current model prioritizes object readiness. Across both models, leadership support consistently emerges as a key factor influencing teachers’ Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge (TPACK) and its foundational knowledge domains. By adopting a dual-model perspective, this study highlights gaps between ideal expectations and actual practices in school-level technology integration and underscores the critical role of school leadership in shaping conditions that support teachers’ development of technology integration competencies.

1 Introduction
--------------

Much research has been devoted to Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge (TPACK) in the educational field. TPACK is a vital framework that integrates technology into educational practices, ensuring that teachers can effectively use technology to enhance subject-specific teaching (Mishra & Koehler, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR31)). Over recent years, there has been growing recognition of the importance of technology in fostering innovative teaching methods and improving student engagement, especially in the field of science and technology (Koehler et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR22)). However, while the role of teachers in implementing TPACK has been widely studied (Schmid et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR35)), little attention has been paid to how school principals perceive the current state of TPACK in their institutions. Principals play a critical role in supporting and shaping the technological environment within schools (Schrum et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR50)). Their perceptions can influence resource allocation, teacher development, and the integration of technology into teaching and learning practices.

TPACK is especially critical in STEM education, where technological tools are used to explain complex concepts and engage students in interactive learning (Voogt et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR41)). The model emphasizes that technology is not a stand-alone component but must be used in conjunction with sound pedagogical strategies and deep content knowledge. Numerous studies have explored how TPACK empowers teachers to navigate the complexities of teaching with technology, focusing on the development of their competence and confidence in using technology in the classroom (Chai et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR6); Tseng et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR40)). As TPACK evolves, its role in teacher education and its potential to improve student engagement and achievement continues to grow. However, the development of TPACK is not solely dependent on individual teacher knowledge, and it is also shaped by the broader educational technology environment in which teachers operate.

Principals, as educational leaders, are key to fostering environments where TPACK can thrive. Their involvement in strategic decision-making, resource management, and policy development directly impacts how technology is integrated into teaching and learning (Tondeur et al., [2008](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR39)). Principals have the unique responsibility of supporting teachers through professional development, allocating resources for technological upgrades, and ensuring that both infrastructure and human resources are prepared for technology integration (Ruggiero & Mong, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR33)). Additionally, their vision for the school’s future often guides how deeply technology is embedded into school culture. The study has shown that principals who actively support technological innovation within their schools create conditions for teachers to adopt and implement TPACK more effectively (Kong & Wang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR23)). Their leadership plays a critical role in shaping how well technology is integrated into teaching practices and how it contributes to student success (Liu & Zhang, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR28)).

While many studies have been conducted on the role of teacher preparation programs in fostering TPACK (Lachner et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR24); Voogt & McKenney, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR42)), relatively little attention has been paid to how school leadership shapes the broader technological environment in which teachers’ TPACK development takes place. In practice, teachers’ opportunities to integrate technology are often mediated by school-level ICT infrastructures, digital platforms, and the availability of technology-supported instructional systems, which are largely influenced by principals’ leadership decisions. Leadership support (LS) may therefore play a crucial role in creating an environment where teachers feel empowered to enhance their TPACK. This gap is particularly evident when examining the discrepancy between principals’ perceived importance of TPACK and the actual implementation of TPACK-related practices in their schools. Few studies have explored how principals’ readiness and support systems influence teachers’ capacity to integrate technology into their teaching, particularly in the K-12 technology domain. Given the growing emphasis on technology in education, addressing this gap is essential to ensure that school leadership is effectively enabling teachers to achieve TPACK competencies.

The current study aims to address this gap by examining the impact of LS on teachers’ TPACK development. Specifically, we investigate how principals’ perceptions of TPACK, their readiness to support technological integration, and the structures they have in place—such as professional development opportunities and access to resources—impact teachers’ ability to effectively incorporate technology into their teaching practices.

2 Literature review
-------------------

### 2.1 Technological environment at school

The technological environment in schools has evolved significantly, driven by digital advancements and increased reliance on technology in education. MOOCs, with their interactive features, enhance virtual learning but highlight ongoing challenges in resource availability, digital infrastructure, and teacher readiness (Zhao et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR47)).

As technology adoption expanded, studies emphasized teacher readiness as a key factor. Almusawi et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR3)) found that professional development, accessible resources, and user-friendly technologies are essential for effective integration, though disparities remain, particularly in non-traditional subjects like physical education.

More recently, research underscores the role of leadership in technology integration. Karakose et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR21)) highlighted digital leadership as crucial in fostering a digital learning culture and technology-based professional development. Scherer et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR34)) found that teacher readiness during the COVID-19 pandemic was strongly influenced by school leadership support (LS). These findings suggest that successful technology adoption requires not just infrastructure but also teacher training and leadership involvement, making the TPACK framework an essential tool for preparing educators. Recent ICT-oriented research further extends this view by conceptualizing technology integration as a system-level digital capacity shaped by institutional conditions, ICT infrastructure, and organizational support rather than individual readiness alone (Fraillon et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR15); Howard et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR19)).

### 2.2 TPACK preparation

Preparing teachers for TPACK integration requires more than just technological familiarity; it involves effectively combining technology, pedagogy, and content to enhance learning (Ma et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR30)). Teachers must be trained not only to use technology but to apply it meaningfully in their instruction.

Research highlights the importance of comprehensive training programs that incorporate reflective practice, collaboration, and hands-on experiences (Lai & Lin, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR26)). da Silva Bueno & Niess ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR11)) found that pre-service mathematics teachers who engaged in immersive, real-world applications developed stronger TPACK competencies than those who received only theoretical instruction.

Beyond initial training, continuous professional development is essential. Mentorship, feedback, and a strong school culture that encourages experimentation with technology help sustain TPACK growth (Schmid et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR49); Tondeur et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR38); Lai et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR25)).

Finally, systemic support is crucial. Schools must provide resources, infrastructure, and leadership to enable effective technology integration (Weidlich & Kalz, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR43)). Without this support, even well-trained teachers may struggle to fully implement TPACK in their classrooms (Luo & Zou, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR29)).

### 2.3 Principle leadership

In today’s educational landscape, principal leadership has become essential for driving technology integration (Navaridas-Nalda et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR32); Geiger et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR16)). Beyond administrative duties, principals actively shape technology-enhanced learning environments (Witthöft et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR44)), influencing school readiness across key dimensions: object readiness (OR), instructional material resource readiness (IMRR), and human resource readiness (HRR) (Darab & Montazer, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR10)).

Effective leadership ensures schools have OR to support digital education (Timotheou et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR37)). Principals who prioritize hardware, software, and internet access empower both students and teachers. During the COVID-19 pandemic, strong digital leadership helped establish technological infrastructures that enabled better integration of digital tools (Karakose et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR21)).

Beyond infrastructure, IMRR depends on how well principals align digital resources with curriculum needs (Timotheou et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR37)). Visionary leadership helps secure and manage these resources effectively (AlAjmi, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR2)). Additionally, digital leadership fosters collaboration across institutions, helping bridge inequalities in technology access (Laufer et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR27)).

Leadership in the digital age also involves collaboration and communication. Laufer et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR27)) argue that strong digital leadership facilitates collaboration across institutions, helping to bridge inequalities and promote shared digital learning initiatives. This collaborative approach is essential in building a resilient digital education system that can adapt to changing technological demands and address disparities in access to technology.

HRR is equally critical. Technology integration relies on teachers’ preparedness, which is shaped by professional development and continuous learning (Akram et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR1)). Principals with strong e-leadership significantly enhance teachers’ adaptability to technology (Wiyono et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR45)), ensuring that staff are equipped to meet the demands of digital education.

In sum, principals play a key role in school readiness, influencing infrastructure, resource alignment, and teacher preparedness. Their leadership directly impacts a school’s ability to integrate technology effectively into its learning ecosystem.

### 2.4 Focus of study

Given the review of the literature on the role of principals in supporting TPACK development, the following research questions are proposed to guide this study:

* RQ1: How do school principals perceive the influence of leadership support (LS) on different dimensions of school readiness—including object readiness (OR), instructional material resource readiness (IMRR), and human resource readiness (HRR)—in the context of technology integration in P-12 education?
* RQ2: How does leadership support (LS) promote the development of teachers’ TPACK competencies in P-12 education?
* RQ3: How do principals perceive the relationship between TPACK and the three foundational knowledge domains: technological knowledge (KT), pedagogical knowledge (KP), and content knowledge (KC), in the context of P-12 education?
* RQ4: What gaps exist between principals’ perceptions of the current state and the perceived importance of technological readiness factors and TPACK support in P-12 education?

3 Method
--------

### 3.1 Participants

The participants of this study consisted of 66 school principals from the northern region of Taiwan. Among the participants, there were 37 men and 29 women, providing a balanced representation of gender in the leadership positions within the schools. The mean age of the principals was 52.62 years (SD = 5.02), indicating that most participants were in the later stages of their careers, with significant experience in educational leadership.

In terms of professional experience, the principals had an average of 30.52 years of service (SD = 6.29), suggesting that they had a deep familiarity with the dynamics of school administration and were well-positioned to provide insights into the technological and pedagogical frameworks present in their institutions. Their extensive years of service likely contributed to their capacity to assess the integration of technological tools and practices in compulsory education, especially in the context of TPACK.

This diverse and experienced group of principals provided a comprehensive understanding of the current state of technology integration in P-12 schools, with their perspectives being critical to the evaluation of leadership’s role in fostering technological and pedagogical advancements within their schools.

### 3.2 Measured tool

Two scales were employed in this study to assess different aspects of school readiness and teachers’ competencies in the context of technology integration.

The first scale is based on the mobile learning readiness model initially proposed by Darab & Montazer ([2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR10)) and later developed into a readiness questionnaire for mobile learning by Yu et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR46)). Hsu ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR4)) modified their scale to suit the context of this study, focusing on four dimensions: OR, IMRR, HRR, and LS. This scale assesses the readiness of schools in terms of the availability of technological tools and infrastructure (OR), the sufficiency of instructional materials (IMRR), the preparedness of human resources, particularly teachers (HRR), and the level of support provided by school leadership for technology integration (LS). These dimensions provide a comprehensive view of how well-equipped schools are for integrating technology into education.

The second scale is grounded in the TPACK framework. This scale evaluates teachers’ competencies across four areas: KT, KP, and KC, and the integration of these areas into TPACK. The TPACK framework is widely used to assess how effectively teachers integrate technology into their instructional practices by combining these different knowledge areas (Schmidt et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR36)). It provides insights into how well teachers can use technology to enhance both pedagogy and content delivery in the classroom.

The measurement instruments employed in this study were adapted from previously validated scales and guided by established theoretical frameworks. Item wording was adjusted to reflect the P–12 school leadership context, while the underlying construct definitions and factor structures were retained. As all items demonstrated satisfactory psychometric properties in the PLS-SEM analysis, no item removal was performed.

This study measured principals’ perceptions of these two scales under two distinct situations: the perceived importance of these factors and their current state. By comparing these two perspectives, the research aims to uncover gaps between what principals consider essential for technology integration and the actual conditions in their schools. This dual approach provides a comprehensive understanding of the alignment (or misalignment) between principals’ priorities and the realities of technology integration in the education system.

### 3.3 Theoretical background and research hypothesis

#### 3.3.1 Leadership support and readiness factors

School leadership, particularly the role of principals, plays a crucial role in the integration of technology in educational settings. According to Yu et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR46)), LS is a critical factor in ensuring that both teachers and schools are prepared to adopt new technologies. Effective school leaders not only promote the use of technology but also ensure that the necessary resources—both material and human—are available to teachers (Karakose et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR21)). LS often manifests in providing instructional material resources, ensuring infrastructure readiness, and offering professional development to teachers, all of which contribute to the overall readiness of the school to integrate technology.

Research by AlAjmi ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR2)) further highlights that strong LS creates an environment where teachers feel confident and supported in adopting technology-enhanced teaching practices. This readiness is not just limited to material resources but also extends to OR (the infrastructure and digital tools) and HRR (teacher skills and knowledge). As such, LS is hypothesized to positively influence IMRR, OR, and HRR.

#### 3.3.2 Leadership support and TPACK

The TPACK framework, introduced by Mishra & Koehler ([2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR31)), is a well-established model for understanding the complex knowledge required by teachers to effectively integrate technology into their pedagogy. However, while much of the research on TPACK has focused on teacher preparation and self-efficacy, LS is increasingly being recognized as a key factor in promoting TPACK development. Principals who provide strong leadership and foster an environment conducive to technology use enable teachers to develop the necessary TPACK skills (Wiyono et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR45)).

By providing resources, professional development, and support, leadership can create opportunities for teachers to build their TPACK competencies. As such, we hypothesize that LS will have a direct positive impact on TPACK, contributing to the development of teachers’ technological, pedagogical, and content knowledge.

#### 3.3.3 TPACK and knowledge domains (KT, KP, KC)

The TPACK framework integrates three primary forms of knowledge: Technological Knowledge (KT), Pedagogical Knowledge (KP), and Content Knowledge (KC). These domains of knowledge interact to support teachers in creating meaningful learning experiences using technology (Harris et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR48)). Numerous studies have demonstrated that TPACK serves as an overarching framework that influences each of these individual knowledge domains (Voogt et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR41)).

When teachers develop a strong TPACK foundation, they are better able to understand how technology can be integrated into their teaching practices in a way that supports both pedagogy and content. Therefore, we hypothesize that TPACK will positively influence the three domains of knowledge—KT, KP, and KC.

#### 3.3.4 The school ecosystem of technology education model (SETEM)

In summary, the literature strongly supports the notion that LS plays a critical role in shaping a school’s readiness to integrate technology and in promoting teachers’ TPACK development. Furthermore, TPACK itself is linked to improved KT, KP, and KC. Based on this theoretical foundation, we propose the following research hypotheses, as depicted in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#Fig1), which is called the School Ecosystem of Technology Education Model (SETEM) for P-12.

**Fig. 1**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9/figures/1)

Research hypotheses of SETEM for P-12

Unlike prior studies that primarily examine technology integration from teachers’ perspectives or focus on isolated readiness dimensions, this study adopts a school ecosystem perspective centered on principals’ perceptions. By comparing the perceived importance and current implementation of readiness factors and TPACK through two structural models (SETEM and Ideal SETEM), the study explicitly captures tensions between leadership priorities and practical constraints in school-level technology integration.

To further investigate these relationships, we will construct two models: SETEM and Ideal SETEM. SETEM represents the current situation, capturing the principals’ perceptions of their schools’ readiness and the current situation of TPACK integration. In contrast, Ideal SETEM represents the principals’ perceived importance, highlighting their priorities and aspirations for technology integration in their schools.

* Hypothesis 6: TPACK will significantly cause KP.
* Hypothesis 7: TPACK will significantly cause KC.

### 3.4 Data analysis

Due to the relatively small sample size (*n* = 66), SmartPLS was selected, as PLS-SEM is well suited for exploratory and theory-building research with limited samples and does not impose strict distributional assumptions (Hair et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR17)). Due to the relatively small sample size (*n* = 66), SmartPLS was selected, as PLS-SEM is well suited for exploratory and theory-building research with limited samples and does not impose strict distributional assumptions (Hair et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR17)). Following the 10-times rule, the minimum required sample size was determined by the maximum number of structural paths pointing at any endogenous construct, which in this model is four, resulting in a minimum requirement of 40 cases. The final sample size therefore exceeds this criterion (Hair et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR18)). SmartPLS is particularly suited for Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modeling (PLS-SEM), which allows for the analysis of complex models with small sample sizes while maintaining statistical power. PLS-SEM was used to assess the relationships between various constructs in the model.

SmartPLS was selected for its flexibility in handling non-normal data and its ability to simultaneously evaluate both the measurement model and the structural model. The analysis involved assessing the reliability and validity of the measurement model by evaluating factor loadings, composite reliability (CR), and average variance extracted (AVE) for each construct.

Additionally, discriminant validity was assessed using the Fornell-Larcker criterion, confirming that each construct was unique and distinct from the others. The structural model was then evaluated by examining path coefficients and R-squared values to understand the strength and significance of the relationships between constructs. Overall, SmartPLS provided a robust method for analyzing the structural model despite the sample size limitations, allowing for reliable insights into the relationships between the various constructs related to TPACK and leadership in the educational setting.

4 Result
--------

### 4.1 Result of SETEM

To evaluate the quality of the model, both convergent validity and discriminant validity were assessed. Convergent validity was confirmed by examining the factor loadings, CR, Cronbach’s alpha, and AVE, as shown in Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#Tab1). According to Fornell & Larcker ([1981](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR14)), CR values above 0.6 indicate strong internal consistency. In this model, all constructs demonstrated CR values within the range of 0.753 to 0.970, surpassing this threshold and confirming reliable measurement of the constructs. Cronbach’s alpha values ranged from 0.742 to 0.969, further supporting internal consistency. Additionally, the AVE values for all constructs ranged between 0.661 and 0.915, exceeding the 0.5 benchmark set by Fornell & Larcker ([1981](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR14)) and Bagozzi & Yi ([1988](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR4)), thus ensuring that the items adequately capture the variance of their corresponding constructs.

[Full size table](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9/tables/1)

Discriminant validity was evaluated using the Fornell-Larcker criterion, which compares the square root of the AVE for each construct with its correlations with other constructs. As presented in Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#Tab2), each construct’s AVE was greater than the squared correlations with other constructs, indicating that the constructs are distinct from one another. This confirms that the model possesses adequate discriminant validity.

**Table 2 Discriminant validity of SETEM**

[Full size table](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9/tables/2)

In addition to examining validity, the model fit was assessed using the Standardized Root Mean Square Residual (SRMR). It is important to note that HRR and IMRR were initially included in the model. However, when HRR and IMRR were incorporated, the model fit was negatively impacted, as evidenced by a poor SRMR value. After assessing the fit indices, it became clear that HRR and IMRR contributed to significant discrepancies between the observed and predicted values, leading to an unacceptable model fit. To resolve this issue, the model was adjusted by removing HRR and IMRR, which subsequently improved the fit, bringing the SRMR below the acceptable threshold of 0.1 (Finch, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR13)). The SRMR for this model was 0.093, indicating an acceptable model fit. This suggests that the model is well-specified and that the residuals between the observed and predicted values are within an acceptable range.

SETEM was constructed using PLS-SEM. The results of this model are illustrated in Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#Fig2).

**Fig. 2**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9/figures/2)

Result of SETEM

Initially, the inclusion of IMRR and HRR in the model negatively impacted the model’s fit. This indicated that these factors do not align with the principals’ current focus. Therefore, they were excluded from the final model to ensure better model fitness. The absence of these variables implies that principals may not consider IMRR and HRR as critical factors in assessing the current situation of their schools regarding teachers’ TPACK development. This adjustment allows for a more accurate representation of the relationships between the other variables. Thus, while IMRR and HRR may be theoretically relevant, their exclusion signifies those principals are less focused on these areas under the current context, highlighting a gap in attention to these specific readiness factors.

In the SETEM model, Hypotheses 2 and 4, which proposed positive relationships between LS and OR and between LS and teachers’ TPACK, respectively, were supported. Hypotheses 5, 6, and 7, examining the relationships between TPACK and KT, KP, and KC, were also supported. In contrast, Hypotheses 1 and 3, which involved IMRR and HRR, were not supported and were therefore excluded from the final model due to model fit considerations.

### 4.2 Results of ideal SETEM

As with SETEM, both convergent validity and discriminant validity were assessed to ensure the robustness of ideal SETEM. The results of these assessments are detailed in Tables [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#Tab3) and [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#Tab4). Convergent validity was confirmed with CR values ranging from 0.779 to 0.978, all surpassing the 0.6 threshold set by Fornell & Larcker ([1981](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR14)). Cronbach’s alpha values also demonstrated strong internal consistency, with values ranging from 0.759 to 0.978. Additionally, AVE values ranged from 0.679 to 0.930, exceeding the 0.5 standard recommended by Bagozzi & Yi ([1988](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR4)). These results confirm that the model effectively captures the variance in the data and demonstrates robust internal consistency across the constructs.

In terms of discriminant validity, the Fornell-Larcker criterion was applied. This method confirmed that each construct was distinct from the others, with the square root of the AVE for each construct being greater than the corresponding inter-construct correlations, as shown in Table [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#Tab4). These findings indicate that the constructs measured in ideal SETEM are adequately separated and not redundant.

Additionally, the model fit was evaluated using the Standardized Root Mean Square Residual (SRMR). The SRMR for this model was 0.098, which falls below the 0.1 threshold recommended by Finch ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR13)), indicating a good fit. This suggests that the model’s predicted relationships between the variables align closely with the observed data, further supporting the overall validity and reliability of ideal SETEM.

5 Discussion
------------

### 5.1 Discussion of SETEM

#### 5.1.1 Leadership support and readiness factors

The analysis of SETEM shows that LS positively influences OR but does not strongly impact IMRR and HRR, leading to their exclusion from the model. This result suggests that principals perceive their support as more effective in ensuring the readiness of tangible educational resources, such as technology and infrastructure, while viewing other readiness factors as secondary in the immediate technological context. This finding aligns with the readiness model of Darab & Montazer ([2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR10)), emphasizing the principal’s role in facilitating object-related technological readiness. Furthermore, these findings are consistent with research by Chai et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR7)), which highlights the importance of readiness and positive attitudes toward technology integration for enhancing instructional quality. Together, these results indicate that principals’ focus on ensuring object and resource readiness through their support lays a foundational environment for effective TPACK-based learning integration.

#### 5.1.2 Leadership support’s influence on TPACK development

The model demonstrates that LS significantly impacts TPACK, confirming principals’ perceptions of the importance of leadership in fostering teachers’ competencies across technology, pedagogy, and content. Effective principal leadership is essential for creating environments where teachers can grow in TPACK, as principals are often the ones to provide the necessary resources and support (Chai et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR7)). Moreover, Scherer et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR34)) consider that strong leadership fosters teacher readiness for technology integration by offering consistent professional development and support networks. This correlation between leadership and TPACK aligns with findings in this study, suggesting that principals who prioritize TPACK provide the strategic backing needed for teachers to effectively integrate technology into their pedagogical practices.

#### 5.1.3 TPACK’s impact on knowledge domains (KT, KP, KC)

The findings indicate that TPACK significantly influences the three foundational knowledge domains: KT, KP, and KC. This is consistent with Voogt et al. ([2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR41)), who emphasize that TPACK serves as an integrative framework, reinforcing specific teacher competencies across technology, pedagogy, and content knowledge. In the context of principals’ perspectives, this study shows that a strong foundation in TPACK correlates with principals’ confidence in their teachers’ abilities to deliver effective instruction in these domains. Additionally, Mishra & Koehler ([2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR31)) support the idea that TPACK enables teachers to blend these knowledge areas seamlessly, underscoring the importance of principals valuing TPACK as it directly impacts these core teaching skills.

### 5.2 Discussion of ideal SETEM

#### 5.2.1 Leadership support and readiness factors

The model of perceived importance reveals that LS significantly impacts IMRR, OR, and HRR. This result indicates that principals recognize their leadership role as essential in ensuring that resources and personnel are prepared for effective technology integration. Almusawi et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR3)) highlight that leadership’s provision of resources, from digital tools to professional development, is critical to creating an environment where technology readiness can thrive. Moreover, Tondeur et al. ([2008](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR39)) underscore that principals play a pivotal role in ensuring resource allocation aligns with instructional needs. This alignment in the model illustrates that principals perceive their LS as a foundation for preparing both tangible and human resources to integrate technology effectively.

#### 5.2.2 Leadership support’s influence on TPACK development

The model also confirms a strong perceived influence of LS on TPACK, reflecting principals’ views that their leadership significantly fosters teachers’ ability to integrate technological, pedagogical, and content knowledge. This aligns with Schmid et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR35)), who emphasize the importance of leadership in creating conditions conducive to TPACK development through strategic resource allocation and professional growth opportunities. Chai et al. ([2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR6)) further suggest that LS is essential in building TPACK competencies, as principals often provide the resources and training that enable teachers to integrate technology meaningfully into their teaching. Principals thus perceive that by prioritizing TPACK, they can directly enhance their teachers’ capabilities to apply technology in a pedagogically sound manner.

#### 5.2.3 TPACK’s impact on knowledge domains (KT, KP, KC)

Consistent with 4.2, the model of perceived importance suggests that TPACK significantly influences KT, KP, and KC. This finding aligns with Mishra & Koehler ([2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR31)), who originally identified TPACK as a framework that strengthens teachers’ ability to blend technology with pedagogical and content knowledge effectively. Voogt et al. ([2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR41)) further support that TPACK enhances specific knowledge domains, as teachers who are well-versed in TPACK are likely to exhibit greater competencies in technological, pedagogical, and content areas. The model thus reinforces the idea that when principals view TPACK as crucial, they believe it will support teachers’ development in these core areas, ultimately contributing to more comprehensive and effective teaching practices.

### 5.3 Comparison of SETEM and ideal SETEM

In ideal SETEM, principals emphasize the importance of IMRR, HRR, and TPACK as essential conditions for effective technology integration, with leadership playing a central role in supporting teachers’ knowledge development across technological, pedagogical, and content domains, consistent with the TPACK framework (Mishra & Koehler, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR31)). In contrast, SETEM indicates that IMRR and HRR receive less emphasis in practice, while OR and LS emerge as the primary enablers of TPACK development. This divergence reflects structural, financial, and cultural constraints that limit schools’ ability to fully realize their perceived ideal conditions. Importantly, it also suggests that neither TPACK nor LS should be interpreted as self-sufficient or deterministic frameworks, as they do not fully capture the broader organizational and policy constraints shaping what is practically feasible in school contexts.

Although principals are aware of the importance of a comprehensive SETEM, implementing ideal conditions remains challenging. Prior research highlights that access to digital tools, user-friendly technologies, and continuous teacher training is essential yet frequently insufficient (Almusawi et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR3)). As a result, despite recognizing the value of IMRR, funding limitations, restricted access to advanced technologies, and inequitable resource distribution across subjects hinder schools from achieving the ideal model. Such financial constraints are common in education, particularly when schools must balance immediate operational demands with long-term technological investments (Tondeur et al., [2008](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR39)).

A similar gap is evident for HRR. While principals acknowledge the theoretical importance of adequate training and confidence for TPACK integration, achieving HRR requires sustained professional development, mentoring, and hands-on experience that are difficult to maintain within existing school structures. Although principals’ support has been shown to foster teachers’ readiness for technology integration, particularly during digital initiatives in the COVID-19 pandemic (Karakose et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR21)), teachers’ readiness remains constrained without stable funding and systematic support. Consequently, HRR tends to be valued in principle but only partially enacted in SETEM due to practical limitations related to time, funding, and access to professional development.

LS also represents a critical area of divergence between ideal and current models. In ideal SETEM, leadership is expected to guide technological initiatives, articulate a vision for digital integration, and align resources with instructional needs (Ruggiero & Mong, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR33)). In practice, however, principals often struggle to devote sufficient time and organizational resources to supporting TPACK development. Although LS plays an important role in shaping technology integration, its influence is frequently mediated by limited professional development opportunities and constrained institutional conditions (Tondeur et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR38)). These constraints help explain the persistent misalignment between principals’ priorities and what can be feasibly implemented.

This discrepancy further underscores the importance of systemic support beyond the school level. From an ICT systems perspective, digital transformation in schools is shaped by institutional logics, governance structures, and policy conditions that define what is practically achievable, regardless of principals’ intentions or leadership priorities (El-Hamamsy et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR12)). Consistent with this view, broader educational policies and administrative structures play a decisive role in enabling or constraining technology integration (Luo & Zou, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR29)). Without sufficient resources, infrastructure, and administrative backing, even well-prepared teachers may face substantial barriers to implementing TPACK in classroom practice (Weidlich & Kalz, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR43)).

To address these gaps, prior research emphasizes sustained investment in professional development and collaborative learning structures. Practice-oriented training has been shown to support TPACK development, particularly when grounded in authentic instructional contexts (da Silva Bueno & Niess, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR11)). In addition, collaborative cultures and cross-institutional networks may help mitigate resource disparities and promote shared expertise (Laufer et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR27)). At a broader level, policy alignment and equitable access to technological resources remain essential for strengthening IMRR and HRR across schools and subjects (Cheah et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR9)).

In sum, although principals recognize the importance of TPACK, LS, IMRR, and HRR, they face substantial challenges in translating these priorities into practice. The divergence between ideal SETEM and SETEM reflects the interplay of financial, organizational, and systemic constraints shaping technology integration in schools. These findings caution against techno-solutionist assumptions that leadership initiatives or technological investments alone can ensure meaningful integration, highlighting the need for sustained systemic and contextual support.

6 Conclusions
-------------

This study examined the impact of principals’ LS on the TPACK development of teachers in K-12 education. By constructing two models (ideal SETEM and SETEM), this research offers insights into how principals perceive their role in supporting TPACK and the actual conditions of technological readiness in their schools. Through these models, the study explored three key areas: the influence of LS on school readiness factors, the impact of LS on TPACK, and the relationship between TPACK and teachers’ knowledge domains in KT, KP, and KC.

This study contributes to the literature by introducing a dual-model approach that contrasts principals’ perceived importance and current enactment of technology integration readiness and TPACK, thereby making visible gaps that are often obscured in single-model or teacher-centered studies. By comparing the perceived importance of readiness factors with their current application, this research highlights a persistent gap in school readiness, illuminating areas where additional resources or policy adjustments may be required. The focus on principals, a less frequently studied group in TPACK literature, adds valuable insight into leadership’s strategic role in shaping school-wide technological practices and aligning resources with instructional goals. Moreover, the study’s findings reinforce the role of TPACK as a multi-dimensional framework that influences core teaching knowledge areas, underscoring its importance in professional development and educational leadership.

While the integration of educational technologies offers substantial promise for enhancing teaching and learning, this study acknowledges that education technology implementation is not without its pitfalls. Technological investments alone do not guarantee pedagogical effectiveness; without sufficient teacher support, infrastructure maintenance, and ongoing evaluation, such initiatives may exacerbate existing inequalities or lead to superficial forms of integration. This further reinforces that leadership support and TPACK should be understood as necessary but insufficient conditions for sustainable technology integration. Furthermore, overreliance on digital tools can risk reducing complex teaching practices to mere technical solutions—a phenomenon often referred to as techno-solutionism. This highlights the importance of critically examining not only the readiness of schools but also the systemic conditions for adopting and sustaining educational technology.

However, this study also has limitations. Researches highlight the critical role of principals in technology leadership, emphasizing key dimensions such as vision, staff development, infrastructure support, and evaluation (Chang et al., [2008](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR8)). Standards of The International Society for Technology in Education (ISTE) underscore the importance of visionary leadership and fostering a digital-age learning culture (Banoglu, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13963-9#ref-CR5)). Data were collected exclusively from principals, providing a leadership perspective but potentially missing insights from teachers, who directly apply TPACK in classrooms. Future research could extend this study by including teachers’ viewpoints to create a more comprehensive model of school readiness and technology integration. Future studies might also examine how specific leadership practices impact the sustained development of TPACK over time, providing a longitudinal perspective on technology integration in schools.

In conclusion, this research underscores the significant role of LS in advancing TPACK and bridging the gap between perceived and actual readiness for technology integration. By prioritizing TPACK in professional development and aligning resources with instructional needs, principals can play a crucial role in fostering an educational environment that effectively integrates technology to enhance learning outcomes.
