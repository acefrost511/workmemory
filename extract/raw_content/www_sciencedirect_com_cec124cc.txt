## Abstract
Metacognitive scaffolding plays a pivotal role in supporting the development of computational thinking (CT) among elementary students. Compared to planned scaffolding, adaptive scaffolding supported by generative AI (GAI) agents offers a promising option by providing real–time, adaptive and personalized response. This study investigated how adaptive metacognitive scaffolding (AMS) and planned metacognitive scaffolding (PMS) influenced elementary students’ CT in a GAI–supported programming environment. 74 students were assigned to AMS, PMS, or control conditions. Using a mixed-methods approach, we examined CT task performance, CT skill use, metacognitive regulation, and cognitive load through standardized assessments, programming artifacts, discourse logs, video-coded behaviors, classroom observation records, cognitive load ratings. Results showed that AMS significantly enhanced contextualized CT performance and promoted more complex, diverse skill use compared to PMS and control groups. AMS also fostered more recursive metacognitive behaviors and reduced mental efforts, while PMS supported higher meaningful engagement. These findings highlight the potential of adaptive scaffolding powered by GAI to support CT development in elementary programming education.
## Introduction
The rise of generative AI (GAI), such as ChatGPT and other large language models (LLMs), plays a pivotal role in developing elementary students’ computational thinking (CT) (Yilmaz & Yilmaz, 2023). CT encompasses abilities such as decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, algorithmic design, and evaluation, which has been recognized as a foundational skill for addressing complex problems across academic and real-world domains (Israel-Fishelson & Hershkovitz, 2022). In response, CT has been increasingly integrated into K–12 education globally, often via visual programming platforms like Scratch, which aim to lower entry barriers for novice learners (Grover, 2021; Xu et al., 2023). Yet, many elementary students struggle to grasp abstract programming constructs—especially loops and conditionals—and frequently encounter difficulties in designing structured algorithmic solutions (Hsu & Gainsburg, 2021; Weintrop & Wilensky, 2019). Through natural language interaction, GAI agents can provide timely, adaptive, and personalized feedback, helping students understand abstract concepts and navigate ill-structured problems (Rahman & Watanobe, 2023). Recent studies report encouraging results from using GAI tools to assist novice programmers, including improved comprehension and enhanced CT skills (Hijón-Neira et al., 2024; Weng et al., 2024).
However, concerns have emerged regarding potential overreliance on GAI-generated solutions (Rahman & Watanobe, 2023). Some argue that depending too heavily on automated feedback may undermine students’ active cognitive engagement and weaken their problem–solving abilities (Ifelebuegu et al., 2023; Memarian & Doleck, 2023). Elementary students, in particular, may lack the metacognitive strategies necessary to critically evaluate GAI responses or reflect on their own learning processes (Mosaiyebzadeh et al., 2023). These findings suggest that while GAI holds educational promise, its effectiveness depends on instructional supports that actively mitigate over-reliance risks (Li & Samah, 2025). Specifically, well-designed scaffolding should intervene by prompting students to reflect GAI-generated solutions and consciously regulate their problem-solving strategies (Avcı, 2024; Mittal et al., 2024). By embedding appropriate cognitive prompts and encouraging justification of answers, scaffolding helps students shift from passive reception of GAI outputs to active cognitive processing, thereby reducing the likelihood of disengagement from automation (Ba et al., 2025; Karaoglan Yilmaz & Yilmaz, 2025; Lee & Wu, 2025)—especially for younger learners with no prior experience (Chang et al., 2023).
To address this challenge, researchers have increasingly emphasized the value of metacognitive scaffolding, which helps students regulate their thinking through planning, monitoring, and adjusting actions rather than passively accepting GAI outputs (Wu et al., 2025). Scaffolding mechanisms—such as prompting students to justify GAI-provided solutions or reflect on the reasoning process—are designed to activate students’ metacognition and foster strategic engagement during problem-solving tasks (Joksimovic et al., 2023; Urban et al., 2024), especially in programming contexts (Kim et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023). A key distinction in this domain lies between planned metacognitive scaffolding (PMS), which provides pre-set prompts in a uniform manner, and adaptive metacognitive scaffolding (AMS), which delivers personalized, real-time feedback tailored to learners’ ongoing needs (Saye & Brush, 2002). While PMS is easier to implement, its static nature may not sufficiently address students’ varying levels of reliance on GAI feedback. In contrast, AMS—especially when powered by GAI—can dynamically detect students’ problem-solving behaviors and deliver timely prompts that encourage deeper cognitive engagement, actively correcting tendencies toward passive acceptance (Al-Fattal, 2025; Sardi et al., 2025; Yan et al., 2025). The latest research suggests that integrating AMS with GAI agents may enhance college students’ deep learning by aligning support with learning trajectories (Wu et al., 2025).
Despite this promise, most existing research on GAI and CT has focused on higher education or generalized outcomes (Crompton & Burke, 2023), with limited attention to how different scaffolding types function in elementary programming education (Liao et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023). Aligned with the special issue, we frame this study within personalized learning—adapting instruction to individual learners’ needs, strengths, and interests—and conceptualize AMS as the in-situ mechanism that delivers such personalization in GAI-supported tasks.
To address this gap, the present quasi-experimental study investigates the effects of adaptive and planned metacognitive scaffolding on elementary students’ CT learning in a visual programming environment (VPE) supported by GAI agents. The findings aim to inform the design of personalized instructional support systems and contribute new insights into how different types of scaffolding influence CT development in younger learners.

## Conclusion
This study investigated the differential effects of adaptive and planned metacognitive scaffolding on elementary students’ development of CT in a GAI–supported VPE. AMS yielded significant advantages on contextualized tasks (Bebras; open-ended goal attainment and program complexity) and reduced ICL during problem solving, whereas PMS increased GCL through structured reflection. Trends on CT skill integration favored AMS but were not statistically significant.
