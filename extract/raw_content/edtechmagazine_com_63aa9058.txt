Six Artificial Intelligence Data Trends to Watch
================================================

Successfully implementing AI in K–12 districts requires a cohesive data strategy.
[![]()](/k12/author/amy-mcintosh)

by  [Amy McIntosh](/k12/author/amy-mcintosh)



Amy McIntosh is the managing editor of *EdTech: Focus on Higher Education* and *EdTech: Focus on K–12*.










Listen
Pause

 








In the classroom and in district offices, K–12 schools are increasingly [implementing artificial intelligence](https://edtechmagazine.com/k12/article/2025/04/educators-share-how-they-implement-ai-their-classrooms). According to a report from [Cambium Learning Group](https://www.cambiumlearning.com/about-us/news/6062/cambium-learning-survey-uncovers-how-k-12-teachers-and-administrators-are-using-a), 61% of administrators are using AI to interpret or analyze student data, and 56% are using it to manage student records. In order for districts to use AI to its highest potential, a cohesive data strategy must be in place. Rex Washburn, chief architect and head of engineering for data at CDW, outlines six data trends that are shaping the future of AI.

[![]()](https://edtechmagazine.com/k12/cdw_subscription_v3/register-web?form=newsletter)

1. **Data platforms become AI platforms.** Today’s data platforms now natively deliver vector search, large language model inference and embedding generation. It is vital to optimize the investment in your current platform.
2. **Governance as activation.** Governance’s value is now being measured by what it makes possible rather than what it prevents. AI systems require governed and secure data to function, not to check a compliance but as an operational prerequisite.
3. **Semantic layers as foundation for trusted AI.** Without context, AI systems hallucinate confidently. The semantic layer provides the shared vocabulary that makes outputs accurate and delivers value from a district’s data.
4. **Data products as operational discipline.** Data should be treated as a product with clear ownership, documented interfaces and defined service-level agreements. AI systems need this predictability and clear access to data.
5. **Observability expands into unified platform performance management.** You can’t trust AI outputs if you can’t monitor data inputs. You can’t sustain AI investments if you can’t manage costs. Understand data- and AI-related costs to realize true value.
6. **Unstructured data becomes AI’s primary feedstock.** Retrieval-augmented generation, agentic workflows and multimodal models depend on unlocking a district’s unstructured data. This opens a new vista of collaboration among data, security and compliance.

### Tech Translators

Sometimes, the technology that teachers want to use in the classroom can be at odds with IT-approved applications. At [FETC 2026](https://edtechmagazine.com/k12/events/fetc), Natalia LeMoyne Hernández, an education community manager for CDW, outlined a framework for IT leaders to better communicate with instructors. 

1. Identify the core ask. Clearly define what teachers are trying to accomplish while acknowledging the technical requirements.
2. Clarify the impact and constraints. Assess the technical limitations, resource availability and potential business impact of implementing the technology.
3. Map shared goals. Align technical specifications with student learning outcomes to establish common success metrics.
4. Co-create solutions. Work together to develop technical implementations that meet teachers’ needs while satisfying technical requirements.
5. Communicate in both languages. Using terms all parties understand helps to bridge the communication gap.
6. Evaluate and adjust. Continuously assess solution effectiveness and make iterative improvements based on performance data.
