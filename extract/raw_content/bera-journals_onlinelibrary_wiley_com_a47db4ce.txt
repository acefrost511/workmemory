## Abstract
As artificial intelligence (AI) reshapes the educational landscape, promoting AI literacy in secondary education has become increasingly important. Understanding how secondary students perceive AI, particularly the machine learning (ML) decision-making process, is critical for equipping them to navigate an AI-driven future. While prior research has highlighted the potential of AI education to foster meaningful engagement with AI concepts, little is known about how students conceptualize and understand the underlying mechanisms of ML. This study developed and implemented an AI module within English Language Arts classes in middle and high schools to demystify the ML decision-making process. A total of 437 students from five schools participated in this research. Qualitative analyses of student responses revealed five distinct categories of understanding and misconceptions: (1) data-based thinking, (2) divergent thinking, (3) anthropomorphic thinking, (4) awareness of limitations and (5) tale-spin simplification. By mapping a progression in students' understanding from anthropomorphic interpretations towards a robust, data-driven perspective, this study fills a critical research gap, offers valuable insights for fostering AI literacy in secondary education contexts, and provides actionable implications for curriculum design and future research aimed at addressing specific misconceptions about ML decision making.
### Practitioner notes
What is already known about this topic
\* Understanding the decision-making process of machine learning models is essential for secondary learners to prepare them for navigating an AI-driven world.
\* Educators face significant challenges in making complex AI concepts accessible to younger students.
\* Secondary students often hold misconceptions and fragmented understandings of the machine learning decision-making process, resulting in a loss of control and reduced interest in further exploring AI concepts.
What this paper adds
\* This study developed and implemented an AI module for text mining and narrative modelling for secondary learners to demystify ML decision-making process.
\* This study introduces a robust coding framework to capture these evolving understandings of machine learning model decision making, including five key categories: ‘data-based thinking’, ‘divergent thinking’, ‘anthropomorphic thinking’, ‘awareness of limitations’ and ‘tale-spin simplification’.
\* This study also demonstrates a progression in students' understanding, shifting from anthropomorphic interpretations to data-driven perspectives.
\* This study highlights the importance of helping students decouple their humanistic associations with language from the statistical, pattern-based operations that drive language-based AI models.
Implications for Practice and/or Policy
\* Educators and researchers might consider including ‘conceptual decoupling’ as a core competency within AI literacy, emphasizing the skills needed to differentiate human cognitive abilities from data-driven processing in machine learning models.
\* Educators can address these misconceptions by using analogies that emphasize AI's statistical nature, designing activities that compare human decision making with algorithmic processes and encouraging students to reflect on examples where AI ‘errors’ reveal the limitations of pattern-based, non-human reasoning.
\* Educators should integrate culturally relevant examples to deepen their understanding of language patterns in model decision making.
## INTRODUCTION
The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence (AI) has brought increased attention to machine learning (ML; a fundamental component of most AI technologies), particularly in the context of secondary education. Traditionally, AI curricula for secondary students mainly focus on the acquisition of AI knowledge and the ability to use, evaluate and create AI models through various teaching strategies and contexts (eg, Tedre et al., [2021](#bjet70024-bib-0037); Voulgari et al., [2021](#bjet70024-bib-0042); Wu et al., [2024](#bjet70024-bib-0046)). Meanwhile, young learners regularly interact with AI in their daily lives through smart devices and intelligent systems, and their understandings of AI concepts are often shaped by how AI is portrayed in media, news or movies (Kreinsen & Schulz, [2021](#bjet70024-bib-0016)). As a result, many secondary learners often hold preconceptions or misconceptions of AI, due to the ‘black-box’ nature of ML processes (Bewersdorff et al., [2023](#bjet70024-bib-0004); Hitron et al., [2019](#bjet70024-bib-0011)).
Given the increasing societal and educational significance of AI, there is a clear need for secondary learners to develop a nuanced understanding of AI, especially the underlying mechanisms and technical knowledge behind AI. Therefore, educators should help secondary learners better conceptualize how AI systems make predictions and inform decisions through direct engagement with ML models, rather than simply using AI tools (Ng et al., [2023](#bjet70024-bib-0028); Tedre et al., [2021](#bjet70024-bib-0037)). A more accurate understanding of ML model decision-making processes could allow learners to critically and meaningfully engage with AI, thereby fostering robust AI literacy and equipping them to contribute effectively in an AI-driven society (Long & Magerko, [2020](#bjet70024-bib-0019); Sanusi et al., [2023](#bjet70024-bib-0034); Touretzky et al., [2019](#bjet70024-bib-0039)). This study examines secondary students' conceptual understanding of ML model decision-making processes, especially the progression of students' conceptualizations. Our findings contribute to the growing body of research on AI education by informing practices that deepen ML conceptual understanding and promote robust AI literacy.
## LITERATURE REVIEW AND THEORETICAL FOUNDATIONS
### AI literacy in secondary education
In the twenty-first century, AI technologies play critical roles and become increasingly integrated into various aspects of daily life. Consequently, AI literacy, emerging as a critical digital competency, has gained significant attention among educators and researchers. It has been conceptualized in various ways across studies. For example, Long and Magerko ([2020](#bjet70024-bib-0019)) conducted an exploratory interdisciplinary review to define 17 AI literacy competencies and propose 15 design considerations for enhancing students' understanding of AI. Their findings emphasize that AI literacy encompasses the knowledge, skills and understanding for individuals to effectively interact with, use and critically evaluate AI systems (Long & Magerko, [2020](#bjet70024-bib-0019)). Later, Ng et al. ([2021](#bjet70024-bib-0026)) conceptualize four aspects (ie, know and understand, use and apply, evaluate and create, and ethical issues) for developing AI literacy, which provides a direction for future AI literacy research. Furthermore, Ng, Wu, et al.'s ([2024](#bjet70024-bib-0029)) framework organizes AI literacy into four key learning dimensions: affective, behavioural, cognitive and ethical. Although a consensus definition of AI literacy has yet to be established, research suggests that developing AI literacy is essential for learners not only to understand the technical mechanisms underlying AI but also to critically assess its broader societal implications (Long & Magerko, [2020](#bjet70024-bib-0019); Ng, Su, & Chu, [2024](#bjet70024-bib-0027)). This includes recognizing AI's potential impacts on society, fostering ethical awareness and promoting responsible engagement with AI-driven technologies.
Secondary education serves as a crucial stage for fostering AI literacy among learners as they are growing up with AI applications. Veldhuis et al. ([2025](#bjet70024-bib-0041)) highlight that most AI literacy research focuses on students aged 11 to 18. However, Shrestha et al. ([2024](#bjet70024-bib-0035)) note that while adolescents aged 16 to 19 frequently use AI-powered applications, they often lack a clear understanding of how these systems work, making them more vulnerable to privacy risks. Similarly, Zhang et al. ([2025](#bjet70024-bib-0048)) suggest that young adolescents struggle with understanding AI literacy concepts, particularly in identifying bias from multiple perspectives. Therefore, fostering AI literacy is essential as learners gain greater digital independence and begin shaping their perspectives during this developmental stage (Shrestha et al., [2024](#bjet70024-bib-0035)). Given the widespread integration of AI technologies in daily life and future career landscapes, it is crucial for secondary learners to develop a foundational understanding of AI concepts, skills and ethical considerations to prepare them for the future (Burgsteiner et al., [2016](#bjet70024-bib-0005); Kandlhofer et al., [2016](#bjet70024-bib-0014); Lin et al., [2020](#bjet70024-bib-0018); Ng et al., [2023](#bjet70024-bib-0028)).
ML, an important knowledge area and critical aspect of AI, utilizes large datasets of inputs and outputs to find patterns and ‘learn’ from them, enabling the system to make decisions (Huang & Ma, [2018](#bjet70024-bib-0012); Injadat et al., [2021](#bjet70024-bib-0013); Sanusi et al., [2023](#bjet70024-bib-0034); Touretzky et al., [2019](#bjet70024-bib-0039)). Understanding the ML decision-making process is essential for fostering AI literacy and enhancing students' overall AI competencies, enabling them to better interpret how ML models function (Long & Magerko, [2020](#bjet70024-bib-0019); Ma et al., [2023](#bjet70024-bib-0020); Walter, [2024](#bjet70024-bib-0043)). Furthermore, it has significant implications for the design of learning activities and curricula and helps educators craft more targeted learning activities and employ specific teaching strategies (Mühling & Große-Bölting, [2023](#bjet70024-bib-0024)).
### Secondary students' understanding and misconceptions of machine learning
Research has shown that secondary learners have obscure understandings of the underlying mechanisms of ML because of the complexity of ML algorithms. For instance, Kreinsen and Schulz ([2021](#bjet70024-bib-0016)) found that secondary learners often believed AI systems have human-like feelings and brains, attributing cognitive abilities similar to humans. Similarly, Kim et al. ([2023](#bjet70024-bib-0015)) investigated middle school students' understandings and misconceptions about AI, including ML, by analysing their AI learning experiences and artefacts. This study identified six key misconceptions, such as ‘AI serves as a universal solution to all problems’ and ‘AI is equivalent to automation’. However, they only included 14 students, which may limit their ability to generate more comprehensive findings. Some secondary learners think AI systems are fully autonomous technologies that can function and develop without the need for human intervention (Mertala et al., [2022](#bjet70024-bib-0023)). Others believe AI-based solely on their exposure to specific applications, such as robotics, digital assistants or recommender systems (Kreinsen & Schulz, [2021](#bjet70024-bib-0016)). In conclusion, secondary learners have a relatively narrow view of AI and its capabilities, which may hinder students' ability to grasp the underlying mechanisms of ML, resulting in a loss of control and reduced interest in further exploring AI concepts, and negatively affect their learning achievement and continuous learning (Fast & Horvitz, [2017](#bjet70024-bib-0010); Kim et al., [2023](#bjet70024-bib-0015)). Therefore, uncovering a broader range of misconceptions and understandings of AI is essential for both educators and curriculum designers in determining how to better teach and integrate AI in future. However, few studies address how secondary learners develop their understandings in the ML decision-making process over time as well as their persistent misconceptions that remain even after engaging in various ML exploration activities.
### Conceptual change
As misconceptions about AI concepts are widespread among secondary learners, it is essential for educators to support students in refining these misunderstandings and aligning their conceptions in more scientifically accurate ways (Tsai, [2003](#bjet70024-bib-0040); Zhang et al., [2024](#bjet70024-bib-0049)). Conceptual change theory provides a valuable framework for this process, focusing on transforming students' misconceptions into scientifically accurate conceptions (Duit & Treagust, [2003](#bjet70024-bib-0008)). Effective conceptual change requires students to experience cognitive conflict and meet four conditions: (1) dissatisfaction with existing conceptions, (2) the new conception is intelligible, (3) the new conception is plausible and (4) the new conception should demonstrate the potential for broad expansion (Duit & Treagust, [2003](#bjet70024-bib-0008); Posner et al., [1982](#bjet70024-bib-0031)). In this study, we build on the conceptual change theory and introduce the idea of conceptual decoupling (Rosenbaum et al., [2024](#bjet70024-bib-0033)). Prior research has demonstrated that secondary learners inherently associate words with positive, negative or neutral meanings. Moreover, they can rapidly decouple and recouple these associations depending on the context. When interacting with language in digital interfaces, learners tend to assume that language-based ML models (e.g., text classification and text generation) can discern the intrinsic meanings of words, similar to the way humans think (Rosenbaum et al., [2024](#bjet70024-bib-0033)). However, it is difficult for ML models to infer meaning from data or make predictions without training. To develop a more accurate understanding of ML algorithms, learners need instructional support to decouple meanings from words and other linguistic symbols. By fostering this ability, educators can help students move beyond the assumption that ML models process language like humans, laying a crucial foundation for understanding how these models work.
### Demystifying AI
Researchers and educators have explored diverse approaches and pedagogical strategies to foster secondary learners' AI literacy. Strategies such as maker education, game-based learning and project-based learning have shown potential in enhancing young learners' AI literacy development (Ng, Su, & Chu, [2024](#bjet70024-bib-0027); Sulmont et al., [2019](#bjet70024-bib-0036); Tedre et al., [2021](#bjet70024-bib-0037); Wan et al., [2020](#bjet70024-bib-0044)). By incorporating these strategies, learners could not only gain hands-on experience but also develop their understanding of AI (Ng, Su, & Chu, [2024](#bjet70024-bib-0027)). Among which, the concept of demystification was also widely recognized and adopted for teaching AI in secondary education by some researchers. It is a process aimed at simplifying intricate AI concepts to make them more accessible and understandable to a broader audience (Lacerda Queiroz et al., [2021](#bjet70024-bib-0017)), which is crucial in secondary education where students may have little or no prior knowledge of AI. With this strategy, learners can develop a deeper understanding, fostering both critical thinking and informed engagement with AI technologies (Dadhich & Bhaumik, [2023](#bjet70024-bib-0007)). To date, several studies have assessed the effect of strategies for demystifying AI for secondary students. For instance, Mertala and Fagerlund ([2024](#bjet70024-bib-0022)) employed an open-ended questionnaire to demystify AI concepts for secondary learners, revealing students often viewed AI as anthropomorphic, pervasive and task-simplifying. This process reflects the effort to break down AI concepts into relatable ideas for younger learners. Similarly, Rodríguez-García et al. ([2021](#bjet70024-bib-0032)) applied the demystification approach through the development of LearningML, allowing students to effectively simplify complex AI concepts for practical understanding. Overall, the process of demystifying AI can offer valuable insights that facilitate learners' understanding of complex AI concepts.
## RESEARCH QUESTIONS
This study aimed to investigate secondary students' initial understandings of ML model decision making, identify persistent misconceptions, and track how their understandings developed. Specifically, the following research questions (RQs) were addressed in this study:
\* RQ1: What are secondary students' initial understandings of ML model decision making?
\* RQ2: What persistent misconceptions do secondary students have about ML model decision making after participating in the StoryQ module?
\* RQ3: How do these understandings evolve as they engage with ML concepts in the module?
## METHODOLOGY
### Context and participants
The study took place in English Language Arts (ELA) classrooms across three high schools and two middle schools in the Northeastern United States between Winter 2022 and Spring 2023. A total of 437 students and seven teachers participated in this study. Among the students, 220 were from high schools and 217 were from middle schools. Specifically, 217 of the participants were in 8th grade, 60 were in 9th grade, and 160 were in 11th grade. Additionally, out of 364 students who reported their gender, 46.7% identified themselves as female, 48.9% as male, 1.6% as non-binary, and the remaining 2.7% as other, or preferred not to answer. The racial demographics are as follows: 52.5% White, 19.2% Black or African American, 15.3% Latinx, 10.4% mixed race, 7.1% Asian, 1.1% American Indian or Alaska Native, 0.8% Native Hawaiian or other Pacific Islander, 5.8% Others, and 3.0% preferred not to answer.
### Learning module
Guided by the concept of demystification, we developed StoryQ (Figure [1](#bjet70024-fig-0001)), a web-based learning module for text mining and narrative modelling. StoryQ is designed to simplify complex AI concepts by connecting them to practical applications that make the text mining process more interactive and visually intuitive. This module allows users to iteratively explore and build text classification models, serving as an effective tool for demystifying ML model decision-making processes. By offering dynamic, hands-on engagement, the module helps learners develop a deeper understanding of how ML models function and make predictions. The platform employs logistic regression over unigram features (ie, single words) as the classification model, allowing users to predict discrete labels (eg, positive or negative) for text data by analysing relationships within a dataset of human-labelled ratings.

As illustrated in Figure [1](#bjet70024-fig-0001), students can navigate this module following procedures: (a) Learners can add features, such as ‘ends with!’ and ‘contains the word delicious’, to construct a two-feature model; (b) they can then investigate the distribution of these features across positive and negative reviews. For example, learners can draw from the distribution graph that the feature ‘ends with!’ appears in 90 positive reviews and 33 negative reviews, indicating its association with positive sentiment. By examining the feature's weight in the model, students discover that it has positive values, which contribute to predictions favouring positive reviews. This process provides students with an accessible way to understand how ML models make decisions based on specific text features; (c) learners can also find the feature table that is linked with training data and feature distribution graph. By directly engaging with features, weights and their effects on predictions, students gain insight into the reasoning behind model classifications. This exploration not only clarifies the mechanics of ML but also fosters critical thinking about the reliability and implications of algorithmic decision making, equipping learners with essential AI literacy skills.
### Curriculum design
Our five-lesson curriculum leveraged StoryQ to guide students through building, analysing and interpreting text classification models. Each lesson lasted approximately one hour. To support successful implementation, teachers participated in professional development workshops designed to familiarize them with the curriculum. During these workshops, teachers explored the curriculum's content and interactive features, engaged in hands-on activities using the StoryQ platform and collaborated with the research team to plan classroom implementation tailored to their specific contexts. Following the workshops, the research team provided ongoing remote support during the curriculum's delivery, assisting teachers with implementation and facilitating data collection processes.
The curriculum began with an introduction to basic text classification, where students experimented with their own written samples to explore how ML models process and classify data (Lesson 1). In subsequent lessons, students delved into interpretative aspects of ML, learning to analyse word weighting and numerical values that influenced model predictions (Lessons 2 and 3). They examined how individual features shaped predictions and investigated how combining features could enhance predictive accuracy, fostering a more nuanced understanding of model functionality. Building on prior research (Tatar et al., [2021](#bjet70024-bib-0002)), which found that students often attributed meaning to texts despite understanding that ML models base predictions solely on patterns in data, we designed Lesson 4 as a unique activity using an ‘alien language’. This activity replaced text with abstract symbols representing an alien language, compelling students to reason about ML decision making without relying on human-language comprehension. This approach encouraged students to focus on the patterns and relationships that underpin ML predictions, reinforcing the concept that ML models operate without the contextual understanding humans attribute to language. Lesson 5 was designed to broaden the scope beyond technical skills, prompting students to critically reflect on the societal implications of AI. They explored how AI technologies impacted the job market and considered their potential roles in engaging with these systems in the future.
### Data collection
We collected students' responses to a series of questions in each lesson, which were designed to scaffold and assess their understanding of AI and ML concepts. Specifically, the module includes 15 questions in Lesson 1, 36 questions in Lesson 2, 39 questions in Lesson 3, 29 questions in Lesson 4, and 23 questions in Lesson 5. To analyse students’ understanding of model decision making, we focused on three key questions that directly addressed the concept of ML decision making. These include: ‘Take a guess! How do you think the model is making its predictions?’ from Lesson 1, ‘In what ways does the model predict the sentiment in a review differently from how humans do?’ from Lesson 2, and ‘Why do you think the model learned a negative weight for this word?’ from Lesson 4. The overall completion rates for these questions are 88.6%, 74.8%, and 45.8% respectively. However, it is important to note that not all students answered every question, as teachers had the flexibility to adjust the instructional pace and could choose to skip certain questions.
Although each question was phrased differently, they were designed to probe students' evolving understanding of how AI models function and make decisions. Our focus was on examining students' understanding of model decision making through their responses to these three key questions. Therefore, we excluded data from students who did not answer any of these questions. As a result, the final analysis was conducted using data from 413 students who answered at least one of the three questions.
### Data analysis
The data were analysed using a hybrid approach that combined deductive and inductive reasoning. In the first phase of analysis, two coders used inductive reasoning and started by reading students' whole responses. The purpose of using this approach is to allow the research findings to be drawn from common, dominant, or significant themes inherent in raw data without any restraints (Thomas, [2006](#bjet70024-bib-0038)). Coders coded together with the first 20 responses to each question; then they sorted data into several basic categories. For instance, in response to the question ‘Take a guess! How do you think the model is making its predictions?’ from Lesson 1, students responded, ‘by looking for positive words’. We regarded such responses as ‘humanization’ because they thought that the model could identify positive or negative words like human beings. Another example involved students stating, ‘by using keywords’, which we labelled as ‘keywords matching’. These categories provide a foundation for analysing and interpreting students' responses in the next phase. Based on the agreement they achieved, the two coders collated the rest of the responses with similar contents and coded independently.
In the second phase of analysis, we used deductive reasoning, building on and expanding the coding framework proposed by Marx et al. ([2024](#bjet70024-bib-0021)). The deductive approach is a top-down approach from theory to data (Creswell & Clark, [2017](#bjet70024-bib-0006)), which helps us elaborate on the results from the first phase of analysis. In this phase, two coders reviewed, modified, and developed the coding themes. Beyond the initial categories of ‘data-based thinking’ and ‘anthropomorphic thinking’, they generated three more categories by combining survey questions and answers. The first is the category of ‘divergent thinking’, which indicates learners realize the difference between ML and human intelligence but do not expand their thinking. Similarly, we have the second category called ‘awareness of limitations’, which means students realize the limitations of ML but don't think deeply about the algorithms behind ML. Finally, we introduced the category ‘tale-spin simplification’ to describe cases where learners understand the internal mechanisms of ML in a simple way, despite their inherent complexity (Long & Magerko, [2020](#bjet70024-bib-0019); Wardrip-Fruin, [2007](#bjet70024-bib-0045)). With this new coding framework, these two coders revised the coding results, and differences between coders were resolved by discussion.
### Coding scheme
The coding scheme for data analysis was adapted from Marx et al. ([2024](#bjet70024-bib-0021)), which originally included three categories of general perspectives of ML: ‘anthropomorphic thinking’, ‘algorithmic thinking’ and ‘data-based thinking’. To better align with our research questions and the data collected, we developed and expanded the framework to capture the development of students' understanding of the model decision-making process. As a result, we added three additional categories: ‘divergent thinking’, ‘awareness of limitations’ and ‘tale-spin simplification’. ### Overview
Table [2](#bjet70024-tbl-0002) presents the distribution of coding categories across Lessons 1, 2, and 4, showing the frequency (raw count) and percentage of each category per lesson. In Lesson 1, ‘anthropomorphic thinking: word’ emerges as the most prominent category, appearing in 41.9% of responses (\*n\* = 181), indicating that students frequently attribute human-like qualities to AI, perceiving it as capable of understanding language similarly to humans. This tendency is closely followed by ‘tale-spin simplification: keywords matching’, which accounts for 39.6% of responses (\*n\* = 171), suggesting that many students perceive AI as relying on simplistic, keyword-based mechanisms rather than complex processing strategies.
As the lessons progress, ‘awareness of limitations: surface level’ gains prominence in Lesson 2, making up 19.9% of responses (\*n\* = 73), reflecting an initial shift towards recognizing AI's operational limitations. Similarly, ‘data-based thinking: basic understanding of key concepts’ rises to 16.3% of responses (\*n\* = 60), signifying students' gradual awareness of data-driven processes, though still in a foundational capacity. By Lesson 4, the category ‘data-based thinking: quantitative understanding of key concepts’ dominates, representing 49.4% of responses (\*n\* = 86), and is accompanied by ‘data-based thinking: semantic analysis’ at 18.4% (\*n\* = 32). This shift illustrates an advanced understanding as students begin to conceptualize AI decision making in terms of quantitative analysis and semantic patterns rather than anthropomorphic or overly simplistic terms.
### RQ1: Initial understandings: Human-like interpretation and simplified mechanisms
Figure [2](#bjet70024-fig-0002) is a stacked area chart illustrating the percentage composition of coding categories across three lessons (Lesson 1, Lesson 2 and Lesson 4), and each colour area represents a distinct category. Initially, the dominant categories in students' understanding of ML model decision making were ‘anthropomorphic thinking’ and ‘tale-spin simplification’. Many students attributed human-like qualities to AI, interpreting its language processing as if it comprehended words similarly to humans. For instance, a common example was students expecting the AI to ‘look for positive words like “friendly,” “nice,” “good” for positive reviews and words like “horrible,” “bad,” “crappy” for negative reviews’, which reflects ‘anthropomorphic thinking’ at the word level. Another student suggested, ‘I think it is noticing the tones of the person writing the review’, an example of anthropomorphizing the AI's ability to detect tone. Yet another comment, ‘It's taking our information, putting it in the system, and scanning it’, illustrates a broader attribution of human-like cognitive processes to the ML system (‘anthropomorphic thinking: whole’). These responses collectively reveal a tendency among students to ascribe intent, understanding, and even agency to ML models, framing the technology as if it engaged in human-like decision making.

Simultaneously, ‘tale-spin simplification’ was also prevalent, with students perceiving ML models as functioning through basic mechanisms, such as simple ‘keyword matching’. For instance, one student explained, ‘The computer did not analyze the text as a whole; it instead scanned for keywords. I typed ‘lacks when it comes to differing flavors and toppings,’ and it read ‘differing’ and determined it as a positive review’. In this case, the student not only reduced the model's functionality to a mechanical keyword-matching process but also assumed that the AI ‘understood’ these keywords, reflecting ‘anthropomorphic thinking’ at the word level. This interpretation suggested a dual cognitive tendency among students: first, to simplify AI processes to isolate word recognition, and second, to imbue those processes with human-like comprehension. This conflation between basic pattern recognition and genuine understanding highlighted a gap in students' reasoning about the complex, layered mechanisms that drove model decision making in AI systems.
Besides some students (\*n\* = 57) who displayed a combination of ‘anthropomorphic thinking’ and ‘tale-spin simplification’, 15 students demonstrated a blend of ‘anthropomorphic thinking’ with ‘basic understandings of key concepts’. For example, one student explained, ‘They input sample data and then use labels such as definitions of words, the syntax, length of reviews, and overall quantity of punctuation used’. This response reflected both ‘anthropomorphic thinking’, as the students attributed word-level understanding to the AI, and a basic data-based perspective, where they acknowledged specific features that the model might use in processing text. These students showed a partial, yet promising, understanding of how AI models utilize data, even as they continued to ascribe human-like qualities to model operations. Together, these themes suggested that while students were beginning to engage with model operations, they often interpreted them through human-centred or overly simplified frameworks.
### RQ2: Persistent misconception: Models understand language
Despite notable improvements in students' understanding of AI's data-driven processes, a persistent misconception remained for a small subset (\*n\* = 13) who demonstrated ‘anthropomorphic thinking’, particularly at the word level (\*n\* = 10). These students held a belief that AI models genuinely ‘understood’ language. This assumption was prevalent even as these students engaged with the technical underpinnings of model-based learning in Lessons 3 and 4. For example, in Lesson 4, one student shared, ‘The model probably learned a negative weight for this word because it thought that the overall sentiment was negative. It probably saw other words that seemed negative to the model and assumed the worst’. This response illustrated a tendency to attribute human-like interpretative abilities to the model, reflecting an assumption that the AI ‘thought’ or ‘understood’ in a way comparable to human cognition—for instance, that the model ‘knew’ which words were negative.
While students developed an understanding that model decision making relies on data and patterns, they nonetheless held an expectation that AI models should possess a fundamental, intrinsic understanding of individual word meanings. This belief suggests that, for students, interpreting words with semantic depth is so basic that they assume even non-human systems must inherently ‘know’ what each word signifies. This misconception reflects the natural human tendency to associate language with meaning and emotion. As AI models process words and generate responses, students often project human-like interpretative capabilities onto the AI, presuming it engages in cognitive processes similar to their own.
This perception of AI as an ‘understanding’ agent highlights a critical educational challenge: helping students decouple their humanistic associations with language from the statistical, pattern-based operations that drive language-based AI models. To foster a more accurate understanding of AI's limitations, it is important to clarify that models lack interpretative abilities and function purely as statistical tools that recognize and replicate patterns without genuine intent or understanding.
### RQ3: Key cognitive shift: Progression from anthropomorphic to data-based thinking
A key cognitive progression emerged in students' understanding of AI model decision making, characterized by distinct phases that reflect increasingly sophisticated interpretations of AI processes. As described in the first section of findings, students exhibited ‘anthropomorphic thinking’, attributing human-like qualities and reasoning abilities to AI systems in Lesson 1. As their engagement deepened in Lesson 2, students were encouraged to explore the distinctions between model and human decision making, with an emphasis on cultivating curiosity about why AI models might ‘reason’ differently from humans and developing a deep appreciation of human intelligence. In this lesson, interestingly, a small subset of students (\*n\* = 18) transitioned to a phase of ‘divergent thinking’, where they began perceiving AI as possessing a unique form of ‘intelligence’ that operates differently from human cognition. More specifically, when AI models produced errors or unexpected outcomes, these students interpreted these occurrences as signs of the model possessing a distinct form of ‘intelligence’, rather than recognizing these as limitations of AI models. One student, for instance, expressed this view by stating, ‘It (referring to the AI model) sees words and can comprehend in another way’. This perspective suggests that students may attribute a form of agency or alternative logic to the AI, potentially mistaking model prediction for a human-like cognitive process.
Ultimately, in Lesson 4, most students (\*n\* = 151) reached a stage of in-depth ‘data-based thinking’, where they developed a technical understanding that AI models do not ‘understand’ or ‘reason’ but instead rely on data-driven statistical patterns to generate outputs. By moving away from viewing AI as an intelligent agent and towards understanding it as a statistical tool, students cultivated a more accurate and sophisticated understanding of how models function. Most students recognized that AI models do not comprehend meaning in the way humans do; rather, models assign weights to features based on word usage patterns in the data. The highest level of understanding observed among students was when they began to decouple the semantic meaning of words from the statistical patterns that AI models learn in Lesson 4.
More specifically, in Lesson 4, nearly half of the students (\*n\* = 86) demonstrated a ‘quantitative understanding of key concepts’, acknowledging that models assign weights to words based on their statistical association with certain outcomes. For example, one student stated, ‘I think the model has learned a negative weight for this word because it is most often used in a negative review’. This response reflects an understanding that word associations, rather than intrinsic meaning, drive the model's decision-making process. Conversely, other students (\*n\* = 28) exhibited a more ‘contextual analysis’ of word usage, indicating an awareness of how certain words are contextually embedded in negative reviews. For instance, one student explained, ‘Because a lot of negative reviews like to compare to negative things, and they often show uncertainty. Also, there are better synonyms for positive words that are similar to “like”’. This response shows an emerging understanding of how context and word choice contribute to feature weighting. Additionally, some students (\*n\* = 32) demonstrated a nuanced view of ‘semantic analysis’, noting that models may rely on specific phrases due to perceived connotations. As one student commented, ‘Because they assume when people say ‘it tastes like,’ they think people use that phrase more in a negative connotation rather than positive’. Such observations indicate an understanding of how models statistically learn from connotative usage without understanding the sentiment itself.
In summary, the progression from ‘anthropomorphic thinking’ to in-depth ‘data-based thinking’ revealed a clear trajectory in students' understanding of AI model decision making. This shift from human-like attributes to a more sophisticated, statistical perspective illustrates growing sophistication in their interpretations of AI processes, including an acknowledgment of the limitations of AI in understanding language as humans do.
## DISCUSSION AND IMPLICATIONS
The findings of this study showed a progression in students' understanding of ML model decision making. Initially, many students displayed ‘anthropomorphic thinking’ or viewed models as relying on simple keyword-matching techniques. While some persistent misconceptions, such as the belief that AI ‘understands’ language, remained for a subset of students, most gradually shifted towards a data-driven understanding, ultimately recognizing that AI models operate through statistical pattern recognition rather than human-like reasoning. In the following, we discuss the implications of these findings for secondary AI education, particularly the need to develop instructional strategies that guide students from anthropomorphic interpretations towards a robust understanding of data-driven processes.
First, our study makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that ‘anthropomorphic thinking’ was not only the initial but also the dominant misconception among secondary learners, thereby providing concrete evidence of a critical barrier in AI literacy. This aligns with prior research indicating that learners often ascribe human attributes to ML models, perceiving them as capable of understanding language and tone in ways that mirror human cognition (Kim et al., [2023](#bjet70024-bib-0015); Kreinsen & Schulz, [2021](#bjet70024-bib-0016); Mertala et al., [2022](#bjet70024-bib-0023)). In addition, students believed ML decision making was primarily based on simple matching keywords. Thus, learners tended to perceive AI through a ‘tail-spin’ effect, a form of misconception that leads them to view AI in a simple way from the outside rather than recognizing intricate mechanisms internally (Long & Magerko, [2020](#bjet70024-bib-0019); Wardrip-Fruin, [2007](#bjet70024-bib-0045)). The prevalence of anthropomorphic and simplified understandings calls for the creation of diagnostic assessments that can identify specific misconceptions in students' mental models of AI and ML. Researchers could develop tools to measure shifts in students' understanding, from human-like interpretations to data-driven perspectives, providing valuable metrics for evaluating the effectiveness of AI education interventions.
Second, we also found that, despite efforts to reduce anthropomorphic misconceptions through symbol-based ML activities in Lesson 4, a small group of students still perceived ML as capable of ‘understanding’ word meaning, even if not fully interpreting nuances like tone. This persistence is consistent with findings suggesting that some students' tendency to attribute understanding to ML might be deeply rooted in their inherent association of language with meaning (Placani, [2024](#bjet70024-bib-0030)). Therefore, researchers could examine whether alternative analogies or interactive models (eg, visualizations that show statistical correlations without any semantic associations) are more effective in helping students understand ML as pattern-based rather than comprehension-based. Additionally, this persistence of ‘anthropomorphic thinking’, despite attempts to simplify the representations, could inform theoretical frameworks in AI literacy. Researchers might consider including ‘conceptual decoupling’ as a core competency within AI literacy, emphasizing the skills needed to differentiate human cognitive abilities from data-driven processing in ML models. As such, our results call for refined instructional designs and assessment strategies that specifically target these entrenched misconceptions.
Third, unlike previous studies that primarily focused on identifying students' misconceptions or surface-level understandings of ML (eg, Kim et al., [2023](#bjet70024-bib-0015); Marx et al., [2024](#bjet70024-bib-0021); Mertala et al., [2022](#bjet70024-bib-0023)), our study revealed a conceptual change from anthropomorphic views to a more sophisticated, data-driven perspective, recognizing that ML models operate based on statistical patterns rather than human-like reasoning. This shift aligns with findings from previous studies that indicate the potential for students to develop a more technical understanding of AI processes when guided to consider models as statistical tools (Walter, [2024](#bjet70024-bib-0043); Zhang et al., [2023](#bjet70024-bib-0047)). However, our findings also highlight the need for additional efforts to support students in conducting ‘contextual analysis’ using their own linguistic and cultural knowledge, which could help students develop a more nuanced understanding of how language features inform model decision making. For example, understanding why reviews with comparison words might convey negativity often depends on cultural insights, as comparisons could imply dissatisfaction or unmet expectations. Therefore, secondary ELA teachers in future language learning classrooms should consider incorporating instructional approaches such as culturally responsive pedagogy that empower students to critically analyse and interpret AI outputs based on their own cultural knowledge and life experiences (Eguchi et al., [2021](#bjet70024-bib-0009)). The topics and activities for teaching AI literacy should be contextualized to help students deeply engage with AI content by making meaningful connections between new knowledge and their existing experiences and familiar contexts to ensure inclusive AI learning designs. In addition, these findings open new avenues for future research to explore how students apply ELA skills in contextual analysis and to investigate whether such applications can simultaneously support effective ELA practices.
Finally, the presence of ‘divergent thinking’ among some students—who, when faced with unexpected or incorrect outputs, interpreted AI as possessing unique, non-human intelligence—carries important implications for AI education. This finding not only enriches the literature by demonstrating that students might interpret AI errors in creative ways, but it also underscores the need to reframe these errors as indicators of data limitations or biases, rather than as signs of inherent intelligence. Future studies could explore whether such reframing could enhance students' ability to critically evaluate AI outputs. Additionally, ‘divergent thinking’ might be influenced by cultural perceptions of technology and intelligence (Amershi, [2020](#bjet70024-bib-0001)). Researchers could explore whether students from different cultural backgrounds are more likely to attribute unique intelligence to AI and how these perceptions impact their engagement with AI concepts.
The findings of this study have meaningful implications for teachers. As students often begin with anthropomorphic misconceptions, teachers play a crucial role in guiding them towards a more accurate, data-driven understanding of AI. Teachers can address these misconceptions by using analogies that emphasize AI's statistical nature, designing activities that compare human decision making with algorithmic processes, and encouraging students to reflect on examples where AI ‘errors’ reveal the limitations of pattern-based, non-human reasoning. Moreover, as students may benefit from a contextual analysis that draws on their linguistic and cultural knowledge (Jiang et al., [2022](#bjet70024-bib-0003)), teachers should integrate culturally relevant examples to deepen their understanding of language patterns in model decision making (Eguchi et al., [2021](#bjet70024-bib-0009)). For instance, in some Western cultures, comparison language in reviews may imply dissatisfaction, as comparisons are often used to highlight perceived shortcomings or unmet expectations (Nakayama & Wan, [2019](#bjet70024-bib-0025)). Conversely, in other cultures, such as in East Asia, comparisons may serve as respectful, indirect communication to offer feedback or acknowledge different perspectives, sometimes carrying a more neutral or even positive tone (Nakayama & Wan, [2019](#bjet70024-bib-0025)). Using these diverse examples can help students recognize how cultural context influences interpretation, fostering a nuanced approach to evaluating language in AI systems.
In summary, this study offers significant contributions to improve learners' AI literacy in secondary education by illuminating the conceptual progression students undergo as they move from anthropomorphic interpretations to data-driven understandings of AI. In addition, we developed a robust coding framework to capture these evolving understandings, including five key categories: ‘data-based thinking’, ‘divergent thinking’, ‘anthropomorphic thinking’, ‘awareness of limitations’ and ‘tale-spin simplification’. This framework not only provides researchers and educators with a structured lens through which to assess student understanding but also serves as a foundation for designing targeted interventions that address specific misconceptions. As a result, secondary students will be able to critically evaluate and understand the technical mechanisms underlying AI technologies, which is essential for fostering AI literacy (Long & Magerko, [2020](#bjet70024-bib-0019); Ng et al., [2021](#bjet70024-bib-0026)). We invite future research to build on this framework to further explore students' nuanced conceptions of AI, examine how these categories evolve across diverse educational contexts and age groups and develop tailored strategies that promote a deeper, more accurate understanding of AI's capabilities and limitations. Additionally, we encourage learners to make connections between ML decision making and practical use, which can help them critically evaluate AI's role in their real lives and future careers. Most importantly, with the rapid advancement of generative AI in recent years, this study could also serve as a conceptual foundation for future research investigating how learners understand, engage with, and critically respond to generative AI technologies.
## LIMITATIONS
This study also has several limitations. First, the duration of intervention in this AI module was only one week, which may have limited students' depth of understanding and engagement with ML concepts. Future studies could implement longer term interventions so that students can have a deeper exploration and reflection on these concepts. Second, some students might have had some prior knowledge of ML and AI when they entered the study, which may have influenced how they interpreted and engaged with these concepts. Future studies should consider diverse levels of prior knowledge among participants and tailor their instructional strategies accordingly. Finally, this study only employed a qualitative method, which may limit its findings due to the absence of quantitative data. Future studies should also consider incorporating quantitative methods to support qualitative insights.
