Abstract
--------

Effective teaching plays a vital role in fostering cognitive and psychological development in humans. Virtual reality simulations, along with the embedded, artificial intelligence (AI)-powered virtual student agents, offer opportunities for preservice teachers to practice teaching iteratively in a dynamic, context-rich manner. However, preservice teachers face challenges when practicing in these AI-enhanced VR simulations. In this study, we designed and investigated model-based supports aimed at scaffolding preservice teachers during simulation-based pretraining, in-simulation, and post-simulation stages. This experimental study with 57 preservice teachers indicated significant positive impacts of model-based support on teaching knowledge and skills development. Furthermore, significant improvements in knowledge of teaching were observed in the experimental groups using model-based support in the AI-enhanced VR simulation, from pre- to post-test. However, the difference between the experimental and control groups in teaching self-efficacy was nonsignificant. The implications of these findings and potential future directions for designing learning support in VR simulation-based teacher education are discussed.

Introduction
------------

Effective teaching plays a crucial role in fostering cognitive and psychological development in humans (e.g., Cohen, [2011](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR6)). It has been suggested that students’ learning performance heavily relies on quality teaching, particularly in today’s multicultural and dynamic classrooms, whether online or in-person (Copur-Gencturk et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR7); OECD, [2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR43)). Teachers need to be equipped with the knowledge and skills to meet the demands of a rapidly changing society and to prepare students for future challenges. Nonetheless, prior research suggested that additional research and investment are necessary to further support the effectiveness of teacher learning programs on student learning (Kraft & Hill, [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR32)). Specifically, Kraft and Hill ([2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR32)) suggested that investments in teacher learning should prioritize reforming teachers’ knowledge and skills to transform classroom discourse, enabling students to reason and investigate while engaging in problem solving and critical thinking. Teachers are also encouraged to develop competencies in fostering personalized learning and in cultivating students’ dynamic and complex thinking within contemporary education (Schleicher, [2012](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR48); Thompson et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR53)), particularly in the fields of science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM, Milner-Bolotin, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR42)). Ambitious teaching practices are characterized by these qualities (Stroupe & Gotwals, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR52); Windschitl et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR58)).

Supporting teachers in adopting ambitious teaching practices requires reforms that build on the foundations of traditional teacher education. Darling-Hammond ([2000](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR12)) identified insufficient structured knowledge development and limited experiential learning as major shortcomings in teacher education. Gibbons et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR20)) emphasized the need for teachers to make “in-the-moment decisions” (p. 2) during ambitious teaching, which involves navigating pedagogical dilemmas and uncertainties related to students’ dialogues, thinking, and actions (Gibbons et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR20); Kavanagh et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR25); Lampert, [1985](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR33); Stroupe & Gotwals, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR52)). To better prepare preservice teachers to handle these challenges, teacher educators have advocated for practice-based learning programs (Gibbons et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR20); Grossman et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR22); Kavanagh et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR25)). These programs emphasize extensive practice and real-time in situ feedback, enabling teachers to develop systematic knowledge and skills through reflection and reasoning.

In line with constructivist perspectives on teacher learning (Davis, [2003](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR13); Loughran, [2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR39)), authentic school or classroom settings provide valuable opportunities for teachers to develop their teaching practices. However, the disconnect between coursework training and practical field experience remains a challenge (Girod & Girod, [2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR21); Zeichner, [2010](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR61)), and this disconnect contributes to “reality shock” that challenges new teachers as they transition from educational programs to actual teaching environments (Dicke et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR14)). This difficult transition can negatively impact teacher retention (Dicke et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR14)) and teaching self-efficacy (Zee & Koomen, [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR60)). Virtual reality (VR) simulations offer an effective solution to bridge this gap and support teacher learning and development (Dai et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR9); Richter et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR46)).

Practicing in a VR teaching simulation requires teachers to adapt and make decisions dynamically. A *comprehensive learning support system* is essential to help teachers enact a high level of professional performance and stay encouraged when encountering challenges in the VR simulation scenarios.

However, research on learning support for VR simulation-based learning has often focused on individualistic strategies, such as questioning (Chernikova et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR5)) and reflection prompts (Richter et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR46)). Indeed, there is a lack of empirical study examining an integrative *model-based support system* in VR simulation at different stages of the experience, especially when learner’s interactions in VR simulations become complex with artificial intelligence (AI)-powered virtual agents.

Integrative, *model-based learning support* can potentially address the needs of preservice teachers during highly interactive, simulation-based learning. We defined *model-based learning support* as “the widgets that are integrated into virtual reality simulation for teacher learning that contain prompts, hints, modeling, and reflection in the pretraining, in-simulation, and post-simulation phases.” In this exploratory study, we examined the effects of model-based learning support on preservice teachers’ learning outcomes in an AI-enhanced VR teaching simulation. In addition to our primary interest in understanding the development of teachers’ knowledge and skills development, we also focus on teachers’ self-efficacy. While model-based learning support has been highlighted in both VR simulation literature (Gegenfurtner et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR19)) and teacher education studies (Zee & Koomen, [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR60)), research specific to its application in VR simulations with AI-powered virtual agents for preservice teachers remains limited. Specifically, we asked:

* **RQ1:** Is there a significant impact on *teaching knowledge and skills development* of preservice teachers between the two VR simulation-based learning groups (i.e., model-based support with AI-powered virtual student agents versus AI-powered virtual-student-agent only) and the control group?
* **RQ2:** Is there a significant impact on *teaching self-efficacy* of preservice teachers between the two VR simulation-based learning groups (i.e., model-based support with AI-powered virtual student agents versus AI-powered virtual-student-agent only) and the control group?

Theoretical background
----------------------

### AI-powered virtual agents in VR simulation-based learning

Advances in computing power and transformer-based large language models (LLMs) have made generative AI increasingly viable for use in VR simulation-based learning. In particular, generative AI-powered virtual agents can address a key challenge identified in earlier research: misclassification of user input (Veletsianos & Russell, [2013](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR56)). By generating contextually appropriate and responsive dialogues, these agents can enhance the coherence and pedagogical relevance of learner interactions, supporting design scenarios that simulate real-world classroom experiences. Research on virtual agent design in VR simulation-based learning has long focused on improving the realism and coherence of interactions. For instance, Girod and Girod ([2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR21)) developed archetypal student profiles to enhance the authenticity of simulated characters, making the experience more comparable to real classroom settings. Similarly, Chen ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR4)) argued that using real-time actors can address issues of inauthentic dialogue and low linguistic fidelity, which are common in scripted virtual agents (see also Ke et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR27)). However, these approaches are resource-intensive and often impractical for large-scale implementation (Kaufman & Ireland, [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR64)). Generative AI offers a scalable alternative to address these limitations.

Virtual agents, powered by generative AI, are increasingly capable of achieving human-like semantic coherence and discourse responsiveness in simulated interactions, with their responses informed by educational theories and pedagogical goals (Dai & Ke, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR8); Liaw et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR38)). For example, Liaw et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR38)) applied experiential learning and game-based models to design AI-driven VR simulations for interprofessional communication training. Their findings showed positive effects on nursing students’ communication skills and self-efficacy, underscoring the potential of AI-enabled VR environments for professional education. While these results are promising, the use of generative AI in teacher education warrants further investigation due to the unique demands of pedagogical interactions. These studies (Dai & Ke, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR8); Liaw et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR38)) point to a promising opportunity to leverage generative AI for creating authentic, responsive virtual student interactions—an essential component of practice-based learning for preservice teachers. However, its effectiveness in this context remains unclear.

### Learning support in simulation-based teacher learning with AI-powered virtual students

Providing support for teachers when they are learning and enacting professional practices in VR simulation is essential (Copur-Gencturk et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR7); Grossman et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR22); Ledger et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR36)). Building on the previous literature in teacher education and VR simulation, we designed an integrative model-based support widget for preservice teachers participating in the AI-powered virtual teaching simulation. This model-based support widget comprises three stages: support at the pretraining stage, support during in-situ (or in-simulation) practice, and support at the post-simulation stage.

#### Model-based support at the pretraining stage

For effective practice-based teacher learning, it is crucial to address teachers’ preconceptions, knowledge, and planning before the practicum. Activities should be designed to prompt preservice teachers to reflect on their teaching beliefs and interpretations of pedagogies and disciplines. Preparing teachers to plan microlessons and student–teacher interactions is also beneficial for *in-situ* practice (Martins et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR40)). Additionally, Ledger et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR36)) noted the challenge of translating practices between simulations and classrooms, which requires preservice teachers to recontextualize and adapt. As such, it is necessary to orient and prepare preservice teachers on how to preplan and perform simulation-based teaching actions during the pretraining stage. Informed by these notions, support at the pretraining stage should include an orientation on navigating the VR environment, purposeful discussions with preservice teachers about how the experience might differ from real-world teaching. Introductory videos of the VR environment and the training session can also be provided to familiarize preservice teachers before they begin.

#### Model-based support during in-situ (or in-simulation) practice

During teaching practice, preservice teachers may face difficulties and pedagogical dilemmas (Kavanagh et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR25)). They need to adjust their pedagogical talk moves dynamically and make appropriate pedagogical decisions with teaching judgments, responding to virtual students’ reactions or feedback. For novices, learning supports to scaffold these actions are essential (Grossman et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR22)). Common forms of support include questioning supports, prompts, modeling, and coaching (Chernikova et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR5)). For example, preservice teachers can engage with well-designed materials that present classroom scenarios featuring student–teacher dialogues, accompanied by thoughtfully crafted questions designed to help preservice teachers reflect on how their pedagogical decisions influences interactions with students. These materials can also model effective responses to challenging situations, drawing on the expertise of experienced teachers.

#### Model-based support at the post-simulation stage

At the post-simulation stage, purposeful supports for reflection can facilitate teachers’ reflection on their actions and decisions (Fanning & Gaba, [2007](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR17); Ke et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR27), [2021a](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR28), [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29); U.S. Department of the Army, [1993](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR55)). Our design includes a facilitated debriefing session to support reflection—a key component of STEM teacher learning (Milner-Bolotin, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR42)). Informed by Fanning and Gaba’s ([2007](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR17)) research on debriefing in simulation-based learning, this session is intended to support preservice teachers to critically analyze their simulation experiences and revise future pedagogical actions accordingly. The session prioritized on interactions that foster a safe, trusting environment for discussion and experience sharing, using adopting verbal debriefing with a human facilitator rather than written self-debriefing (Fanning & Gaba, [2007](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR17)).

#### A design framework for model-based support in simulation-based teacher education

A design framework was conceptualized and proposed to support the adoption of model-based support in simulation-based teacher education with AI-powered virtual students (see Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#Fig1)). This framework integrates (1) Grossman et al.’s ([2009](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR22)) teaching practice components (representations, approximations, and decompositions of practice), (2) Kavanagh et al.’s ([2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR25)) pedagogical reasoning model, and (3) Kolb’s ([1984](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR31)) experiential learning theory. The aim of model-based learning support is to guide preservice teachers in making pedagogical decisions, especially when they face dilemmas while interacting with AI-powered virtual students. The goal of model-based learning support is achieved through pedagogical talk-moves and the assimilation of ambitious teaching practices.

### Teacher knowledge and skills development in simulation-based learning

Teachers’ knowledge and skills development through simulation-based learning reflects recent shifts in teacher education (e.g., Stroupe & Gotwals, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR52)). Teachers are trained not just to develop disciplinary and pedagogical knowledge, but to implement teaching practices that embody the essence of the discipline and facilitate deep learning for the students (Stroupe & Gotwals, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR52)). Specifically, this shift emphasizes ambitious teaching, in which educators engage students in meaningful learning and reasoning through disciplinary-specific discussions and problem solving in authentic contexts: practices that are the focus of this study. Throughout the learning trajectories, teachers gradually withdraw their direct support, enabling all students, including those from underprivileged backgrounds, to independently engage in problem solving and learning (Stroupe & Gotwals, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR52)).

To achieve this, it’s crucial to develop teachers’ skills in content pedagogy, questioning, and discussion orchestration (Capraro et al., [2010](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR3); Lampert et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR35); Redfield & Rousseau, [1981](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR45)). These skills are rooted in interactive pedagogical moves, such as managing materials, time and student engagement, and assessing understanding (Lampert et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR35)). Effective communication and questioning are vital; in particular, higher-order cognitive questioning could enhance students’ organization of thought and learning outcomes (Redfield & Rousseau, [1981](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR45)). To this end, the VR teaching simulation was designed to support this type of practice, and the teaching knowledge and skills instrument developed to align with it (see the “Teaching Knowledge and Skills Test” section).

### Teaching self-efficacy in simulation-based learning

Research in teacher education and simulation-based learning shares a common interest in the concept of self-efficacy, which focuses on individuals’ beliefs regarding their competence to perform a task. This sense of self-efficacy is essential in motivating human actions and facilitating behavioral changes (Bandura, [1977](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR1); Zee & Koomen, [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR60)).

Teachers’ self-efficacy is crucial as it impacts pedagogical practices, teaching quality, and career prospects (Zee & Koomen, [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR60)). Teaching is complex and varied. The complexity and variety of teaching practices can influence self-efficacy in numerous ways (Lampert, [2010](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR34)). As such, the literature highlighted various components of teaching self-efficacy. Commonly mentioned components include teachers’ confidence in instructional strategies, student engagement, and classroom management (Klassen et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR30); Tschannen-Moran & Woolfolk Hoy, [2001](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR54)). Self-efficacy is also vital in simulation-based learning for applying knowledge in real-world contexts (Gegenfurtner et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR19)). Research on preservice teacher practicum showed that pre-practice planning, in-situ teaching, and post-practice debriefing are crucial for developing self-efficacy (Martins et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR40)). However, teacher self-efficacy in simulation-based teacher learning remains under-theorized and understudied.

Building on prior research on teaching self-efficacy in VR simulation-based teacher learning, this study investigates the effects of AI-powered virtual student agents and model-based learning support on teachers’ self-efficacy, focusing on instructional strategies, student engagement, and classroom management.

Method
------

### Research design and participants

We adopted an experimental design in this exploratory study with pre and posttests. We recruited 65 participants from various preservice teacher pools with Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval. We recruited participants via professional organization channels and personal networks. Seven participants didn’t show up, and one dropped, resulted in 57 participants.

Specifically, we conducted an a *priori* power analysis using G\*Power (Version 3.1) to determine the minimum sample size required to detect a meaningful effect with sufficient power and precision. Based on prior meta-analyses in similar contexts and comparable target populations (Chernikova et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR5); Dai et al., [2024a](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR10), [2024b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR11)), we used an expected effect size of \({f}^{2}=0.43\), which falls within the medium-to-large range (Cohen, [1992](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR65)). To achieve 0.80 power (Power (\(1-\beta\) error probability)) at an alpha level of 0.05 for a design with three experimental conditions (specified as “Number of groups = 3” in G\*Power), the power analysis indicated a minimum total sample size of 57 participants—19 per group. Each participant was randomly assigned to one of the study conditions.

The selection criteria for the participants are as follows: (1) a preservice teacher who enrolled in a college of education program; (2) proficient in typing in English on a computer; (3) capable of using a mouse and keyboard to control an avatar and interact in the VR simulation-based learning environment; and (4) having no prior experience with the treatment used.

Forty of the consented participants self-reported as female. Regarding their year in the education program, most participants were in their senior year or beyond (*n*= 27), followed by freshman (*n*= 13), junior (*n*= 7), graduate school (*n*= 6), and sophomore (*n*= 4). The majority of preservice teachers indicated that their primary focus was on science (*n*= 33), with math as the second most common focus (*n*= 12). A small number specialized in language arts (*n*= 3) and social sciences (*n*= 5). Noting that participants could list multiple focused subjects.

Most preservice teachers earned fewer than ten credits in their education program (*n*= 18), 11 had earned between 10 and 20 credits, eight had earned between 21 and 40 credits, and 17 had accumulated more than 40 credits; three didn’t provide this information. Regarding self-reported teaching experience, many had less than six months (*n*= 27), six had more than six months but less than 12 months, eight had more than 12 months but less then 24 months, and 16 reported more than 24 months. The backgrounds of the preservice teachers were generally similar across the three study group conditions.

Aligning with the purpose of this exploratory study, participants signed up for an individual session and were then assigned to one of the three study groups (two experimental groups and one control group) using simple random assignment, with group placement determined by chance (Shadish et al., [2002](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR49)). Participants in the first experimental group interacted with the VR teaching simulation embedding *both* model-based support and AI-powered virtual students whereas the participants in the second experimental group interacted with the simulation with only AI-powered virtual students. The control group received no treatment. They completed the pretests, remained in the Zoom room for the duration of the study session with their microphones and cameras off, and were free to engage in any activity of their choosing. Afterward, they completed the posttests.

The use of a no-treatment control group was intentional in this exploratory study and in alignment with established approaches in experimental design (Dunlosky et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR15); Guck et al., [1985](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR23)). In this design, the control group’s non-exposure to any structured intervention allows us to isolate the effects of the VR teaching simulation and its features, minimizing the influence of external factors such as task engagement or additional instructional exposure. By withholding pedagogical or interactive support (e.g., Shute et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR51)), the control group provides a clear contrast, helping us determine whether observed changes in the experimental groups can be attributed to the VR intervention itself, rather than to natural maturation or the passage of time. This approach is especially useful in emerging studies where establishing the unique contributions of the intervention is critical.

### Model-based support and the AI-powered virtual student

#### Model-based support

Grounded in prior research (Grossman et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR22); Kavanagh et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR25); Kolb, [1984](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR31)), model-based support was integrated into a VR simulation-based learning environment called Enactive Virtual Environment for teaching (*EVETeach*) (Ke et al., [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29)). *EVETeach* was developed using *OpenSimulator*, an open-source VR platform (Ke et al., [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29)). The model-based support consisted of three phases, pretraining stage, in-situ practice stage, and post-simulation stage. Learning support in the pretraining stage contained one AI-powered virtual student as a sample to orient participants, along with orientation videos, a lesson plan template, mini-modules on interacting with AI-powered virtual students, and pretraining discussion prompts. In this stage, learners were also given the opportunity to practice maneuvering and interacting with virtual gadgets and characters in *EVETeach* (Ke et al., [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29)). Second, the learning support during the *in-simulation* practice is designed to integrate pedagogical dilemmas (Kavanagh et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR25); Lampert, [1985](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR33)), refection-in-action prompts (e.g., a mini rehearsal prompt for “what would you say?” during student–teacher conversations), modeling videos, and questioning support with sentence openers and templates. Third, learning support in the post-simulation stage includes reflection-on-action prompts and metacognitive checklists (U.S. Department of the Army, [1993](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR55)). These multi-phase support features were designed and developed using Articulate e-learning software. Figure [2](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#Fig2) showed the model-based support in (a) pretraining stage, (b) in-simulation practice stage, and (c) post-simulation stage.

#### The AI-powered virtual student agent

In *EVETeach* (Ke et al., [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29)), AI-powered virtual student agent, called *Evelyn*, was the core element of simulation-based practice and learning. We designed AI-powered virtual student agent by refining a generative AI model with a localized dataset that comprises the transcripts from the ambitious teaching classroom videos (Windschitl et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR58)) and TIMSS (The Trends in International Mathematics and Science Study) video clips. In doing so, the AI-powered virtual student agent can question, respond and interact with the preservice teachers in an authentic and domain-specific manner. The architecture is shown in Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#Fig3).

### Procedures

Participants signed up for an individual video conferencing session in Zoom with a facilitator. Upon consent, the participant was introduced to the session, their tasks, and the agenda. They were then instructed to complete the pretest of teaching knowledge and skills as well as the teaching self-efficacy questionnaire. Immediately after the pretests in individual sessions, the participants in experimental groups one and two began teaching practices in *EVETeach* (Ke et al., [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29)). Participants accessed *EVETeach* (Ke et al., [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29)) remotely via Zoom using screen sharing features for interactions. That is, the facilitator enabled *EVETeach* (Ke et al., [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29)) and the AI-powered virtual student agents, then shared their screen and granted control of the mouse and keyboard to the participant. The procedures were illustrated in Fig. [4](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#Fig4).

### Measures

#### Teaching knowledge and skills test

The teaching knowledge and skills test assessed how well the participants can conduct classroom discussions, maintain productive discourse, and manage classroom situations in ambitious math and science teaching. We adapted this test from previous studies (Ke & Xu, [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR26); Ke et al., [2021a](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR28), [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29)). In Ke et al., ([2021a](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR28), [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29)), the test was used for chemistry student instructors, with a coefficient alpha of.88 for chemistry lab teaching and.61 for open-domain lecturing and teaching.

The teaching knowledge test consisted of ten scenario-based questions, aligned with the disciplines and scenarios designed in *EVETeach* (Ke et al., [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29)). We adapted the teaching knowledge test to replace existing items with five questions on “engage” and five on “consensus building.” We tailored the test to reflect the conversational and interactive nature of *EVETeach* (Ke et al., [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29)). Each question provided a scenario narrative, and preservice teachers were asked to choose the best practices from five options in response to the scenario and story problem. They were also given space to explain why they believed these practices were the best. The scenario-based conversational story problems in the test were adapted from authentic classroom interactions in Ambitious Science Teaching (Windschitl et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR58)) and TIMSS. Specifically, the test was adapted from a class-teaching story problem scenario to a version that required preservice teachers to engage students and build consensus with students (see Appendix A).

The answers to these problems were responsive to the context of the scenarios. The test was validated by a native English speaker and two experts—one in learning technologies and the other in science education at a research university in the United States. Each correct choice was awarded one point, with the maximum possible score being 20.

The coefficient alpha reliability in the current study was .70 for the pretest and .61 for the posttest. Considering the brief length of the teaching knowledge and skills test, these results demonstrated acceptable reliability for the pretest and reasonable reliability for the posttest, with potential for enhancement (*α* >.60). For both tests, removing any items would not significantly increase the scale’s reliability.

#### Teaching self-efficacy

The teaching self-efficacy scale used was adapted from Klassen et al. ([2009](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR30)), a tool validated with elementary/middle and secondary school teachers across various countries, including the United States. In this study, we replaced one irrelevant item (i.e., “How much can you do to assist families in helping their children do well in school?”) with a task-relevant item (i.e., “I can orchestrate students’ ideas in discussions”). Additionally, we modified the items from “how much do you think…” to “I can…” as suggested by Bandura ([1994](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR2), [2006](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR66)). For instance, the original item “how much can you do to craft good questions for students” (Klassen et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR30), p. 73) was revised to “I can craft good questions for students.”

The scale comprised three components: self-efficacy for instructional strategies (five items), self-efficacy for student engagement (three items), and self-efficacy for classroom management (four items). In the original Klassen et al. ([2009](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR30)) study, responses were recorded on a nine-point scale (1 = Disagree strongly, 9 = Agree strongly). The coefficient alpha for the self-efficacy scale among U.S. preservice and in-service elementary and middle school teachers (*n*= 137) was.87 overall (*M* = 90.92, *SD* = 9.20). In the current study, responses were recorded using a more common 5-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly disagree, 5 = Strongly agree).

The coefficient alpha reliability for the pre-intervention self-efficacy measure in the current study was.92. The post-intervention self-efficacy measure was.93, indicating high reliability.

### Data analysis

To examine the treatment effects of model-based support and AI-powered virtual student agents on participants’ teaching knowledge and skills as well as teaching self-efficacy, we analyzed the collected data with parametric statistical techniques (i.e., ANCOVA). We checked the test assumptions of ANCOVA. The normality of residuals was checked using a histogram and the Shapiro–Wilk test (Shaphiro & Wilk, [1965](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR50)). Linearity was checked using a scatterplot and a P-P plot. Homogeneity of variance was tested using Levene’s Test within the ANCOVA framework. If any assumptions were violated, a non-parametric test (i.e., Kruskal–Wallis H test) was additionally used to examine the effects, followed by a within-group pre-post significance examination with a paired sample t-test. We described the details and statistical techniques used for each RQ in the results section.

Results
-------

### RQ1—The impacts on *teaching knowledge and skills development*

The analysis investigated the impact of three experimental conditions on preservice teachers’ *teaching knowledge and skills*, as measured by a scenario-based, multiple-choice teaching knowledge and skills test: (1) model-based support with AI-powered virtual students, (2) AI-powered virtual students only, and (3) a no-treatment control group.

We first tested normality with Shapiro–Wilk tests (Shaphiro & Wilk, [1965](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR50)). The results showed no evidence for non-normality with model-based support group (*W* =.96, *p*=.47), AI-powered virtual-student-only group (*W* =.94, *p*=.26), and the control group (*W* =.96, *p*=.66). Further, Levene’s Test for homogeneity of variance indicated that the sample variances were equal to the population variances, confirming that the assumption of equal variances was not violated (*F* (2, 54) =.04, *p*=.96).

The ANCOVA analysis results, controlling for the pretest, demonstrated a significant difference between the experimental groups and the control group, *F* (2, 57) = 11.59, *p*<.001, *η*2 =.30, R2 =.47. Table [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#Tab1) illustrated the mean and standard deviation of the pre and posttests of teaching knowledge and skills, as well as adjusted posttest mean and standard error, among the three groups. Figure [5](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#Fig5) illustrated the differences between pre- and post-tests across the three groups in teaching knowledge and skills.

### RQ2—The impacts on *teaching self-efficacy*

We investigated the effects of three experimental conditions on preservice teachers’ teaching self-efficacy for the second research question (RQ2), as measured by the teaching self-efficacy questionnaire. The conditions were: (1) model-based learning support with AI-powered virtual students, (2) AI-powered virtual students only, and (3) a no-treatment control group. The assumption of homogeneity of variance was initially tested using Levene’s Test. The results indicated that the variances did not violate this assumption (*F* (2, 54) = 1.02, *p*=.37).

Due to the violation of the normality assumption in the model-based support group and the AI-powered virtual students only group, both parametric (ANCOVA) and non-parametric (Kruskal–Wallis one-way analysis of variance) tests were conducted for this research question.

Two considerations justified the use of both parametric and non-parametric analyses. First, ANCOVA was employed because it is robust to violations of the normality assumption, particularly when group sizes are equal, and the sample size is adequate (Levy, [1980](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR63)). ANCOVA is generally considered superior for drawing accurate conclusions in randomized experimental studies compared to non-parametric methods (Vickers, [2005](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR57)). Second, conducting a non-parametric analysis provided additional confidence in the findings despite the normality violation.

The ANCOVA results, controlling for the pre-experience responses of teaching self-efficacy, indicated no significant difference between the experimental groups and the control group: *F* (2, 57) = 2.42, *p*=.09, *η*2 =.08, R2 =.52. Table [2](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#Tab2) presented the mean and standard deviation of teaching self-efficacy scores for the three groups in the pre- and post-questionnaires, as well as the adjusted means and standard errors. Figure [6](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#Fig6) depicted the differences in teaching self-efficacy between the three groups based on the pre- and post-questionnaire (adjusted) results.

The Bonferroni post hoc comparisons revealed no significant differences among the three groups. Specifically, there was no statistically significant difference between the *model-based support group* (*M* = 4.54, *SE* =.09) and the *no-treatment control group* (*M* = 4.33, *SE* =.09), with *p*=.14, 95% CI [−.06,.59]. Similarly, there was no statistically significant difference (*p*= 1.00, 95% CI [−.28, −.37]) between the *AI-powered virtual student only group* (*M* = 4.31, *SE* =.09) and the *no-treatment control group* (*M* = 4.33, *SE* =.09). Finally, no significant difference was found between the model-based support group (*M* = 4.54, *SE* =.09) and the AI-powered virtual-student-only group (*M* = 4.31, *SE* =.09), with *p*=.28, 95% CI [−.09,.54].

The Kruskal–Wallis H test, a non-parametric statistics approach, confirmed that there was no significant difference in teaching self-efficacy between the different groups, \({\chi }^{2}\)(2) = 5.48, *p*=.065. The mean rank post self-efficacy score (i.e., combined samples of Kruskal–Wallis H test) was 34.13 for the *model-based support group*, 30.82 for the *AI-powered virtual-student-only group,* and 22.05 for the *no-treatment control group*. The original overall post self-efficacy mean score was 4.39.

We then examined pre-to-post teaching self-efficacy improvements within individual groups using paired sample t-tests. Among the study groups, only the *model-based support group* (*n*= 19) showed a significant improvement (*t* (18) = − 3.03, *p*=.007) from pre-intervention (*M* = 4.27, *SD* =.63) to post-intervention (*M* = 4.58, *SD* =.43). Neither *the AI-powered virtual-student-only group* (*t* (18) = −.95, *p*=.35) nor the control group (*t* (18) = −.96, *p*=.35) showed a significant improvement.

Discussion and implications
---------------------------

### Discussion

In this study, we examined the effects of model-based support––the widgets that are integrated into VR simulation for teacher learning that contain prompts, hints, modeling, and reflection in the phases of pretraining, in-simulation, and post-simulation––on VR simulation-based learning with AI-powered virtual students for preservice teachers. We focused on two outcomes in this context—teaching knowledge and skills and teaching self-efficacy.

Our study offers novel insights into a design framework that guides the use of model-based support for VR simulation-based teacher learning featuring AI-powered virtual student agents. This design framework provides practical values for implementing model-based support during the pretraining stage, in-simulation practice stage, and post-simulation stage of VR simulation-based learning. These elements are based on prior research of teacher education (Grossman et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR22); Kavanagh et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR25)) and VR simulation-based learning (Fanning & Gaba, [2007](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR17); Ke et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR27), [2021a](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR28), [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29); U.S. Department of the Army, [1993](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR55)). Our empirical results, guided by the design framework, offer evidence of using model-based support in VR simulation-based teacher learning with AI-powered virtual students.

For RQ1, the results showed a significant improvement in preservice teachers’ teaching knowledge and skills from pre- to post-intervention in the experimental groups. Additionally, model-based support provided numerical advantages over using the AI-powered virtual student agent alone. This finding contributes to the literature in multiple ways. First, it offers empirical evidence for using AI-powered virtual student agents driven by transformer-based large language models in teacher learning (Dai et al., [2024a](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR10), [2024b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR11); Zhang et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR62)). Second, while prior research has focused on integrating learning supports in K-12 math and science education (e.g., Eckhardt et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR16); Matsuda et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR41); Yaman et al., [2008](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR59)), this study expands the scope to VR simulation-based teacher learning. The significant effects of model-based support and AI-powered virtual student agents on teaching knowledge and skills, compared to a no-treatment group, align with Chernikova et al.’s ([2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR5)) findings, but extended the context to the exploration of VR simulation-based learning for preservice teachers. These results affirm the positive impact of model-based support and AI-powered virtual student agents. This study is an initial exploration of model-based support that provides a coherent and integrative experience to VR-supported preservice teacher learning. In response to the design framework of model-based support, the emerging evidence suggests that adding model-based support at different stages of the VR experience can contribute to preservice teachers’ learning when they encountered pedagogical dilemma (Kavanagh et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR25)).

For RQ2, the results did not reveal a significant between-group effect of using model-based support versus an AI-powered virtual student agent on teaching self-efficacy. However, we found improvement in pre- and post-test scores for those who *used both* model-based support and AI-powered virtual student agents, but not for those who used only the AI-powered virtual student agents or those in the no-treatment control group. While studying the effects of teacher learners’ self-efficacy using learning support (i.e., model-based support in this study) and AI-powered virtual student is limited in the literature, the experimental results aligned with the previous study (Ke et al., [2021a](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR28), [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29)) in which Ke et al., ([2021a](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR28), [2021b](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR29)) revealed that the different simulation modes (i.e., VR and live simulations) did not significantly impact the development of participants’ teaching self-efficacy in 4-h sessions. In the context of simulations for preservice teachers, Gundel et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR24)) found that exposure time played a crucial role in fostering teaching self-efficacy. Specifically, they observed an initial decline in teaching self-efficacy among participants with only 30 min of total exposure, whereas those with longer exposure demonstrated increases. Gundel et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR24)) posit that the initial confrontation with the realities of the simulated classroom context may have influenced this outcome.

The between-group difference was reported in a study investigating learners’ self-efficacy using AI-based chatbots in higher education settings focused on public health (Lee et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR37)). Lee et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR37)) found that the experimental group who used AI-based chatbot on a mobile phone after two 50-min sessions demonstrated significantly higher learning self-efficacy than the control group, with a large effect size (*p*<.01, *η*2 = 0.21) in a public health course. While the chatbot in Lee et al.’s ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR37)) study demonstrated significant results, their findings underscore the need for further research on AI-powered virtual student agents in VR environments that incorporate diverse, human-like, multimodal features, building on the findings from basic AI chatbots used on mobile devices. Notably, Franklin and Lee’s ([2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR18)) study on novice nurses’ self-efficacy in simulation environments suggests that “self-efficacy after simulation may depend on the context of simulation” (p. 611), given that “simulation commonly encompasses cognitive, affective, psychomotor, and behavioral domains of learning” (p. 611).

Meanwhile, the significant within-group result is encouraging and aligns with previous research. For example, Ross et al. ([1996](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR47)) articulate that teaching experience plays an important role in teaching self-efficacy and that self-efficacy is task specific. Preservice teachers, compared to more experienced teachers, struggle more with tasks that require complex pedagogical decision-making, such as planning and evaluating lessons. The within-group significance observed in the model-based support group in this study, when considered alongside the findings of Ross et al. ([1996](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR47)), suggests that both teacher-related factors and the design of learning support (for planning teaching activities and evaluating students’ progress) may contribute to a deeper understanding of how to enhance preservice teachers’ self-efficacy. The finding also potentially supports Pekrun’s ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR44)) call to investigate within-teacher effects on related psychological constructs such as motivation, emotion, and self-regulation. While this position remains open to further discussion, Pekrun ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR44)) argues that “it is time to complement this approach by studies examining within-teacher effects and the within-person mechanisms that can explain effects of teachers on students” (p. 319).

The findings of this current study also suggest the potential of designing and using AI-powered personalized learning support delivered in real time for preservice teachers in VR simulation. Copur-Gencturk et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR7)) offered a proof-of-concept of a computer-based program using AI-powered virtual agent to provide personalized feedback. More research is needed to investigate in alternative contexts with in-situ practice affordances, such as real-time support in VR-based simulations.

### Implications

Several implications emerge from this study. First, the findings not only demonstrate the effectiveness of AI-powered virtual student agents in supporting the development of teaching knowledge but also offer insights into designing learning support features for preservice teachers. An integrative, model-based approach, incorporating pre-training, in-simulation, and post-simulation support, appeared beneficial for teaching knowledge development and showed potential in enhancing teaching self-efficacy.

To this end, design efforts should incorporate tailored, integrative supports that align with preservice teachers’ developmental stages in the teaching profession as well as their evolving needs when they engage in teaching practice with AI-powered virtual student agents. For example, in addition to the orientation in pretraining stage, customized feedback based on teacher performance can be designed in the in-simulation stage; reflection activities in response to teacher performance can be arranged in the post-simulation stage. Second, the design of simulations can further emphasize elements that specifically target the enhancement of teaching self-efficacy in preservice teachers. An important implication from the study’s findings is the need for intentional design of teaching self-efficacy support, which could help sustain preservice teachers’ engagement and aspirations throughout their teaching endeavors, alongside their clinical experiences (Gundel et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR24)). Specifically, it is crucial to design the simulation experience with careful consideration of the learning context and the characteristics of the teacher-learners, supporting alignment with their real classroom practices (Gundel et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR24)). Future learning design practices could consider self-efficacy theories in simulation-based learning for preservice teachers. Designers could also explore factors such as duration of exposure and the structure and strategies for using learning supports (Franklin & Lee, [2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR18); Gundel et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR24)).

Finally, the study also points to the importance of simulation as a supplemental tool for preservice teachers, particularly when integrated into their clinical experiences. It remains important for teacher educators to consider how simulation can be thoughtfully integrated into teacher learning, considering its pedagogical affordances, as well as the models and frameworks that support overall knowledge and skills development. Such an integration can play a key role in cultivating teaching self-efficacy to the broader teacher education experience.

Limitation and future research
------------------------------

There are several limitations in the current exploratory study. First, the sessions in this study were conducted online using a conferencing tool (i.e., Zoom). More specifically, participants engaged with the VR simulation-based learning environment and the embedded AI-powered virtual student agents through online video conferencing remotely. Variations in Internet speed and computer performance among participants and the session facilitator led to delays in VR interactions for some participants. These delays may have impacted their VR experiences (e.g., potentially experience related to teaching self-efficacy) in the VR simulation-based learning environment. Second, we set one of the recruitment criteria as “a preservice teacher who enrolled in a college of education program” to alleviate the time and resource constraints for participant recruitment. At the same time, participants’ disciplinary practices, experience, and background should also be considered.

The findings of this study should be interpreted with caution due to the modest reliability of the teaching knowledge and skills test. Although the results reached statistical significance, the low internal consistency suggests that the items may not have consistently captured the construct, and observed effects could be attenuated. Nevertheless, the significant findings provide preliminary evidence warranting further investigation using enhanced or more refined measurement tools. The test should also consider the time taken to complete as well as participants’ test-taking characteristics (e.g., fatigue of the participants). Fourth, for teaching self-efficacy, the measure was limited by its self-report nature and the point in time at which it was measured (only at pre- and post-experience). Future research could use repeated measure to track the changes of teaching self-efficacy over time. Moreover, future research may benefit from using objective measures such as observational design or video recording with machine learning algorithms to examine the teaching self-efficacy in addition to the self-report measures.

Fifth, future research could incorporate a control group with a conventional or static non-VR training approach. While our study’s use of a no-treatment control group aligns with the established practices in the literature (Dunlosky et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR15); Guck et al., [1985](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10592-4#ref-CR23)), a more active control group may provide different insights or an alternative perspective on the effectiveness of a dynamic approach. The absence of an active control condition in this exploratory study limits the interpretation of between-group differences. Future research could include a conventional or non-VR training comparison to more clearly attribute observed effects to the VR intervention and to minimize potential novelty effects.

Finally, although random assignment helps mitigate the confounding effects of participant characteristics across groups, some variability in factors such as teaching experience or content area may still arise by chance. These potential differences should be considered when interpreting the results. In addition to these participant-level differences, the power analysis indicated that the sample size is sufficient to detect modest effects. However, smaller effects or greater within-group variability may still increase the risk of a Type II error, highlighting the need to interpret the results with caution in light of these potential limitations.

Conclusion and implications
---------------------------

In conclusion, this exploratory study offers an emerging empirical account of how model-based support in VR simulation-based learning impacts preservice teachers, particularly, when using AI-powered virtual student agents. As an early contribution, the observed positive effects of the intervention on teaching knowledge and skills, along with the within-group improvement in teaching self-efficacy, suggest emerging experimental support for the concept. In both VR simulation and teacher education, it is crucial to design and offer support for the teacher learners to develop the target teaching proficiency through productive, *in-situ*, and reflective practices. This study paves the way for further investigation into the interdisciplinary areas of teacher education and VR simulation-based learning, focusing on how to enhance real-time support and integrate effective design elements to fully leverage the potential of AI-powered virtual student agents and VR simulations.

2. 2.
   
   Ask the class if you need to play the video again, if so, play it again.
3. 3.
   
   Elicit more ideas by asking students to help spark Kevin’s thinking.
4. 4.
   
   Ask Kevin why he doesn’t know, and try to explain it to him.
5. 5.
   
   Ignore Kevin and move on with the class.

* **Scenario Example: Consensus building**

> **Teacher:** So we have been discussing what has happened in the video.
> 
> **Nick:** Yes! I think it’s about steam or something.
> 
> *The student, Nick, doesn’t seem to be able to elaborate on the phenomenon clearly.*

**What should the teacher do?**

1. 1.
   
   Follow up with Nick, and educate him in a strict manner: “You should always be specific in science reasoning!”
2. 2.
   
   The teacher needs to explain the phenomenon directly in a clear manner so that the student can learn.
3. 3.
   
   Identify that Nick knows that “steam” plays a role, follow up with the student and ask for the characteristics of steam that may influence the phenomenon.
4. 4.
   
   Use extra points as an incentive to encourage students to come up with more answers that are better than this answer.
5. 5.
   
   Say “okay, so we heard Nick say that it’s about steam, does anyone want to add more?”
