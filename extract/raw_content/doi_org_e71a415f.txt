## ABSTRACT
This study develops and empirically tests a comprehensive model explaining generative AI (GenAI) use among higher education students by integrating AI characteristics (perceived animacy, intelligence, and anthropomorphism) and user characteristics (self-efficacy, personal innovativeness, and AI literacy). It further examines the moderating role of institutional policy on the relationships between GenAI use, learning performance, and satisfaction. Survey data were collected from 509 university students in Oman and analyzed using a hybrid PLS-SEM and artificial neural network (ANN) approach. The results show that perceived intelligence, AI literacy, self-efficacy, and perceived anthropomorphism significantly influence GenAI use, with perceived intelligence emerging as the strongest predictor, while perceived animacy and personal innovativeness are not significant. GenAI use has a positive effect on learning performance and satisfaction. Moderation analysis reveals a significant negative interaction, indicating that stronger institutional policy increases overall learning performance and satisfaction but reduces the marginal impact of GenAI use on these outcomes. ANN results corroborate the SEM findings and further highlight perceived intelligence and AI literacy as the most influential predictors. The study advances understanding of GenAI adoption by combining sociotechnical and institutional perspectives and offers practical implications for universities and policymakers seeking to promote responsible and effective AI use in education.

## Authors’ contributions
Dr. Tarhini led the study and wrote the introduction, methodology and prepared the proposed conceptual framework and collected the data and also participated drafting the manuscript. He also handled the responsibility of submitting the manuscript and corresponding with journal office. Dr Al-Sharafi analyzed the collected data and wrote the implications and participated in drafting the manuscript. Dr. Al Lawati helped with the literature review and discussion sections and also participated in drafting the manuscript. All authors read and approved the final manuscript.

### Notes on contributors
#### Ali Tarhini
\*\*\*Ali Tarhini\*\*\* is an Associate Professor in the Department of Information Systems at Sultan Qaboos University, Sultanate of Oman. He holds a Master degree in E-commerce from University of Essex, and a PhD in Information Systems from Brunel University London, UK. His research interests include Knowledge Management, ICT in information systems (Technology acceptance and adoption), cross-cultural issues in IT (at individual and national culture, cross-cultural studies), and Quantitative Methods (Psychometrics; Instrument development and validation; Cross-cultural measurement; Issues in survey development and administration; structural equation modeling). Dr. Tarhini has published over 60 research papers in international journals including the Computers in Human Behavior, Information Technology & people, Journal of Enterprise Information Management, Journal of Management Development, Management Research Review, British Journal of Educational Technology, Journal of Computing in Higher Education, Education and Information Technologies.
#### Mohammed A. Al-Sharafi
\*\*\*Mohammed A. Al-Sharafi\*\*\* is a researcher at KFUPM specializing in the socio-economic and environmental impacts of emerging technologies. An expert in Quantitative Methods in IS research, he has authored 90 + publications, including journal articles, books, conference papers, and editorials, contributing to advancements in digital transformation and sustainability.
#### Hidaya Al Lawati
\*\*\*Hidaya Al Lawati\*\*\* is an Assistant Professor in Accounting and the Assistant Dean for Undergraduate Studies at the College of Economics and Political Science, Sultan Qaboos University, Oman. She has joined the university in 2007. In 2010, she has completed her Master’s degree in Accounting at the University of New South Wales, Australia. She has received her PhD in January 2021 from the University of Portsmouth, UK. She has published a number of papers in various fields, such as corporate governance, financial and narrative reporting, and tax avoidance.
