## Abstract
This study examined how cognitive engagement differs between immersive virtual reality (IVR) and non-immersive virtual reality (NIVR) environments among English as a Foreign Language (EFL) learners in a Tourism English course. A total of 105 EFL learners were randomly assigned to three conditions: IVR (\*n\* = 35), NIVR (\*n\* = 35), and a traditional e-learning control group (\*n\* = 35). Cognitive engagement was measured using eye-tracking technology, specifically total fixation duration (TFD) and total fixation count (TFC). Learning outcomes were assessed through pre- and post-tests. Data were analyzed using one-way ANOVA, ANCOVA, and Pearson correlation. Results revealed significant differences in cognitive engagement across conditions. The IVR group demonstrated significantly higher TFD and TFC compared to both NIVR and control groups (\*p\* < .001). Notably, no significant difference was found between NIVR and control conditions (\*p\* = .155), suggesting that moderate technological enhancement may not provide sufficient added value over traditional methods. ANCOVA results indicated that IVR participants achieved significantly higher learning outcomes than NIVR and control groups after controlling for pretest scores. Additionally, significant positive correlations were found between eye-tracking metrics and learning outcomes. These findings suggest that full immersion is critical for enhancing cognitive engagement in VR-based language learning. Practical implications include prioritizing IVR implementation in Tourism English education where resources permit, while reconsidering investments in NIVR solutions that may not yield meaningful engagement benefits over traditional approaches.
