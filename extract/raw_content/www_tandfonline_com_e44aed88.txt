## Abstract
Generative artificial intelligence (AI) has been embraced by educators and students, making it one of the fastest-growing technologies in education. However, unethical uses of AI hinder its acceptance and potential use for good. From a sample of 9,229 K-12 school districts in the United States, this study systematically applied content analysis to search for AI policies from 191 districts, with a balance of district locale. In May 2024, 14.13% of school districts (\*n\* = 27) across 20 states and the District of Columbia had an AI policy. Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) identified underlying patterns: academic integrity, responsible use of AI, teacher guidelines and permissions, data privacy and security, and educational enhancement and support. Implications inform the earliest stages of AI policy development in education.

## Additional information
### Notes on contributors
#### Lauren Eutsler
\*Lauren Eutsler\*, PhD is an Associate Professor in the Department of Learning Technologies at the University of North Texas. Her research focuses on technology integration in teacher preparation, instructional design, and digital literacy to enhance literacy instruction and student learning outcomes. With over a decade of experience in higher education and six years of hands-on teaching in diverse K-6 classrooms, her work examines how emerging technologies can be effectively integrated into teacher education programs while addressing common challenges and misuse in classroom settings. Since 2013, she has conducted extensive research at the intersection of teacher education, instructional technology, and literacy, contributing to best practices in preparing educators to critically evaluate and implement digital tools that support both literacy development and digital literacy skills.
#### Brittany Rivera
\*Brittany Rivera\* received her master’s in library science from the University of North Texas in 2016. Brittany worked as a special education teacher until 2021 when she moved into the role of school librarian. In this role, she has sought out partnerships with classroom teachers and instructional coaches to align library lessons with classroom instruction. Brittany is presently a doctoral candidate in the Department of Learning Technologies at the University of North Texas. While pursuing her doctoral degree, she is studying technology integration into K-12 learning environments, creating accessibility, and facilitating extended learning opportunities using educational technology.
#### Megan Barnes
\*Megan Barnes\* meganblibrarian@gmail.com is a PhD student at the University of North Texas. She has her Master’s of Library and Information Science (University of Washington) and Educational Specialist (University of Central Missouri) degrees. A practitioner-researcher, she has experience in early childhood through 12th grade library services and her interests include decision making, everyday information seeking behaviors, and creativity.
#### Julie Cummings
\*Julie Cummings\*, a high school English and Research with 25 years of experience, specializes in teaching reading and writing while integrating technology into student learning. She holds a BA in English and an MA in Educational Innovations, and is currently pursuing a PhD in Learning Technologies. Julie’s passion lies in balancing fundamental literacy skills with technological advancements in education. Her extensive classroom experience in our evolving world has taught her the importance of prioritizing individuals, recognizing their diversity, and valuing their unique experiences. Julie strives to understand and support all students by maintaining a people-first approach in her teaching methods.
