Abstract
--------

This randomized between-groups experiment examined how guidance modality shapes students’ learning in a virtual museum tour. Fifty-six students (grades 4–7) were alternately assigned to one of two conditions; a gamified, well-structured, task-based mobile guide providing immediate feedback, and a paper guide offering partially structured, text-based prompts without built-in feedback. After completing the tour, students were assessed on museum knowledge, three dimensions of museum learning experience (engagement, meaningful learning, emotional connection), and overall satisfaction. The mobile guide yielded significantly higher museum knowledge, greater meaningful learning, and higher satisfaction than the paper guide; differences in engagement and emotional connection were not observed. These findings indicate that guidance packaged with game mechanics, tight task structuring, and instant feedback can compensate for typical shortcomings of virtual tours and better support learning than text-only guidance. We discuss implications for the design of technology-enhanced guidance in virtual museum environments and recommend future factorial studies to disentangle the unique contributions of gamification, feedback, and delivery medium. However, these results should be interpreted in light of contextual limitations such as significant design imbalance between the richly structured mobile guide and the minimally supported paper guide, device diversity, linear navigation constraints, and the additional cognitive demands of using the mobile guide for the first time.

1 Introduction
--------------

Museums are public environments with specific missions, such as the preservation of cultural heritage, research, information provision, and significant contributions to the accumulation of social knowledge (Lewis, [2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR47)). Common obstacles to school-led museum visits include limited teacher preparation and involvement, logistical and administrative challenges (such as funding, transportation, and parental consent), insufficient collaboration between schools and museums, and a tendency toward guide-centered, didactic activities that restrict meaningful student engagement and learning (Cheng & Tsai, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR18); Sahani & Parakasha, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR66); Wu, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR88)). Thanks to information and communication technologies, museums are now able to present cultural and natural heritage to visitors on digital platforms (Lewis, [2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR47)). Museum virtual tours created for this purpose are digital environments that consider the cultural values and educational status of users, consider user preferences and offer the opportunity to interact with objects (Huang et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR33)). However, today, many museum virtual tours do not provide an additional content related to the museum. Although today’s museum visitors are technology savvy, some factors make the use of museum virtual tours difficult. Many museum virtual tours present users with fragmented or unrelated information, as their overall purpose and narrative structure are often unclear (Styliani et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR76)). Likewise, a recent meta-synthesis of 17 studies reports that while virtual and augmented reality applications generally enhance learning achievement, particularly for conceptual knowledge in science, art, and history, their effectiveness hinges on high-quality content and clear instructional guidance (Mercan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR57)). In her study evaluating 36 different museum virtual tours, Daniela ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR23)) stated that educational elements, such as additional information about museum artifacts, presentation of information in different modes, gamification elements, probing questions, and feedback are missing. Given these gaps, museum virtual tours require stronger instructional design and guidance. This study investigates how different forms of guidance affect the museum experience by comparing a gamified, task-based mobile guide with immediate feedback and a partially structured, text-based paper guide. The study also aims to offer practical insights for designing effective guidance in virtual tours with limited informational content. Accordingly, the following research questions were addressed:

R1. Does the level of museum knowledge (as measured by the knowledge test) differ between the groups after the virtual tour?

R2. Are there any differences between the groups in the dimensions of learning experience, namely, engagement, meaningful learning, and emotional connection, during the virtual tour?

R3. Do the groups differ in their overall satisfaction with the virtual tour experience?

2 Background of the study
-------------------------

### 2.1 Museum virtual tours

Museums offer web-based services that provide not only access to general information about the institution but also direct access to their holdings, digital collections, and online exhibitions (Walsh et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR86)). Thus, interaction with a museum can occur through its online collections or website, without the need for a physical visit (Schweibenz, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR70)). These virtual opportunities create learning environments that are independent of time and place, offering indirect yet meaningful experiences (Styliani et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR76)). Museum virtual tours extend the traditional museum concept by operating on online learning platforms that combine physical and abstract realities within multidimensional, hypermedia environments (Foo, [2008](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR27)). In other words, museum virtual tours enable visitors to explore artifacts, access detailed information about collections, maintain continuous interaction with the museum, and enjoy global accessibility (Schweibenz, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR70)). In doing so, they provide autonomy, encourage active participation, and support interactive learning (Li & Huang, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR48)).

During the COVID-19 pandemic, when physical access to museums was restricted, the demand for virtual tours offering cultural experiences in digital environments increased dramatically, allowing people worldwide to visit museums virtually (Aristeidou et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR5)). Today, virtual museum tours attract far more visitors than physical museums and offer broader access to information through diverse modes of presentation (Li et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR50)). Typically, museum virtual tours simulate existing spaces through a sequence of 360° panoramas, videos, or still images and may integrate additional multimedia components such as sound effects, music, narration, and text (Kim & Hall, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR42); Wu & Lai, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR89)). These tours can authentically recreate real on-site experiences and can be further enhanced with technologies such as augmented reality (AR) and virtual reality (VR) (Caciora et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR11); Kwok & Koh, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR44); Spielmann & Mantonakis, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR75)). As such, virtual museum tours effectively communicate cultural content, convey heritage, and provide authentic, enjoyable, and educational experiences (Barbieri et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR7); Tsita et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR83)). However, only a limited number of virtual museum tours offer sufficiently rich resources such as informative materials, diverse exploration modes and activities, and personalized visitor experiences (da Silva & Padilha, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR22); Ilhan et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR35); Keumala et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR41)). Therefore, exploring effective guidance methods is crucial for designing virtual museum tours that deliver more personalized and meaningful learning experiences.

### 2.2 Learning experiences at real and virtual museums

Museum learning, central to the museum’s mission, is a situated form of learning that affords access to diverse information, stimulates interest, invites interaction with artifacts, and opens multiple opportunities for learning (Sandahl, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR67)). In physical museums, this process can yield wide-ranging gains: situational interest; comprehension of content; acquisition of new artifact knowledge; integration with prior knowledge; shifts in beliefs, values, and attitudes; strong emotional bonds; perspective-taking and empathy; collaborative skills; identity formation; motivation to pursue further inquiry; and even artistic skill development (Gammon, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR29); Karakuş Yılmaz et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR39); Moorhouse et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR60)). Technology can further enhance on-site inquiry, supporting discovery, information seeking, communication, and documentation and has been shown to increase interest and positive attitudes toward museums; technology-integrated visits can also bolster children’s historical thinking while evoking curiosity, excitement, memorable moments, discussion, and discovery (Andre et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR4); Meunier & Bélanger, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR58); Sung et al., [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR79)). In short, physical museums naturally constitute learning spaces where first-hand, embodied experience and social interaction render nearly every corner pedagogically meaningful (Zhang et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR92)).

By contrast, for a virtual museum to deliver a comparable learning experience, it must embed multiple learning elements and often relies on visitors’ interest and motivation to seek additional information (Daniela, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR23)). In practice, many virtual museums underemphasize the learning experience, educational design is thin, and key features such as guiding questions, opportunities for answers, and feedback are missing (Daniela, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR23)). Nevertheless, when virtual museum experiences are interactive, instructionally well-designed, and integrated with coherent educational strategies, they can enhance learning by increasing engagement, supporting knowledge acquisition, and fostering critical skills (Wu et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR90)). Because virtual museums often lack essential instructional support, despite clear evidence that guided and well-designed experiences significantly enhance learning, this study addresses a pressing need to test effective guidance types for improving virtual museum learning.

### 2.3 The role of guidance in virtual museum tours

Museums are designed to communicate knowledge and provide collective access to cultural information; therefore, virtual environments have become powerful extensions of this mission (Carvajal et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR13)). Virtual museums are increasingly integrated into educational practices worldwide (Jadán-Guerrero et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR36); Yow, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR91)). However, the transition from physical to virtual environments brings distinct pedagogical and technical challenges (Aristeidou et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR5)). Learners may struggle to control navigation and interaction with objects (Ratumbuisang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR65); Wu et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR90)) or become disoriented, leading to feelings of isolation and loss of direction (Aytekin & Aktas, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR6); Minocha & Hardy, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR59)). The abundance of visual and interactive stimuli in 3D virtual environments can overload users, causing them to discontinue exploration before completing the experience (Tanaka et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR81)). These issues can hinder meaningful engagement and reduce students’ motivation to explore (Lee et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR46)). Hence, virtual museums must be deliberately designed to encourage sustained exploration, enjoyment, and learning through interaction with content (Meinecke et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR56)). Well-designed interactive systems and, more importantly, effective guidance is crucial to help users navigate content and complete tasks efficiently (Cheng & Tsai, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR18); Cho & Lim, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR20); Li & Huang, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR48)).

Guidance in learning, defined as any structured support that helps users reach learning goals (Weerasinghe et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR87)), plays a central role in improving learning and engagement in virtual contexts (Kang et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR38)). Structured guidance allows personalization of content and pacing, enhances motivation through feedback and challenge, and supports enjoyment, thereby improving overall learning outcomes (Abdul Rahim et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR1); Bower et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR10); Cho & Lim, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR20)). In contrast to unstructured discovery, which may leave learners uncertain or distracted, guided learning stimulates essential cognitive processes such as selecting, organizing, and integrating information (Mayer, [2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR55)). Empirical evidence consistently shows that structured guidance improves learning performance in virtual environments (Baydaş et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR9); Topu & Göktaş, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR82); Tutwiler et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR84)). These findings collectively emphasize that learners benefit most when virtual museum experiences combine autonomy with carefully designed scaffolding that sustains focus and cognitive engagement.

Guidance in virtual environments can take multiple forms, human, system-based, or hybrid, and may appear either within the environment or through external tools. Research suggests that embedding hints, reflective prompts, and questions throughout the exploration process can strengthen learning and retention (Chen et al., [2007](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR16)). In museum virtual tours, navigation aids such as maps, 3D cues, or animated guides help orient visitors (Chittaro & Burigat, [2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR19)). Nevertheless, the literature reveals a lack of robust examples of structured and interactive guidance specifically designed for virtual tours. For instance, evaluations of Italian museum tours showed that, while these experiences enhanced spatial understanding, they offered very limited informational and interpretive support (Kabassi et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR37)). Similarly, students reported a desire for greater controllability, interpretive captions, visual cues, and embedded feedback mechanisms (Wallgrün et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR85)). Studies comparing different guidance approaches have also shown that semi-structured cues like arrows improve learning and satisfaction more than minimal guidance, whereas overly continuous or directive cues can distract users from self-directed exploration (Checa & Bustillo, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR15); Tanaka et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR81)). Despite these insights, most existing studies focus primarily on the guidance provided within the virtual tour itself, and such internal mechanisms remain constrained by the technical and design limitations of the virtual environment. As a result, they often fail to provide the level of support and feedback necessary for sustainable cognitive engagement, particularly in virtual tours with limited information content (López-Martínez et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR52)). On the other hand, the potential of external guidance tools that can offer a richer instructional structure, instant feedback, and task-based challenges independent of the virtual environment’s limitations has been largely overlooked. Therefore, external guides can offer a more flexible and pedagogically powerful tool to support learners as they navigate virtual museum spaces. Investigating the effectiveness of such external guidance is an important step in enhancing the educational value of museum virtual tours.

Mobile and paper-based guidance systems are two common approaches to support learning in both virtual and real museum environments. Comparisons between these methods reveal that mobile-based guidance generally yields higher satisfaction, perceived usefulness, and knowledge retention, even when task completion times are similar (Lam et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR45)). Mobile apps that integrate problem-based guidance encourage deeper engagement with exhibits, peers, and the learning context compared to paper-based formats (Sung et al., [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR79)). Similarly, gamified mobile guides have been shown to more effectively increase interactivity, information access, meaningful learning, and emotional connection compared to static brochures (Othman et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR64)) and also improve enjoyment, usefulness, and perceived engagement (Kang et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR38)). Gamification has emerged as an effective strategy for increasing visitor engagement and interaction with museum collections (Nofal et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR62)). When the museum experience is integrated with gamified activities, visitors move beyond passive observation to active exploration, challenge, and discovery, which can transform the visit into a more enjoyable and meaningful learning experience (Du & Cui, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR26)). Gamified systems that include elements such as points, badges, and feedback loops can sustain motivation and create a sense of accomplishment (Sangamuang et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR68)). In the context of virtual museums, where sensory and social cues are limited, gamification can play a particularly critical role in stimulating curiosity and sustaining cognitive engagement.

However, most of the empirical evidence on gamified guidance comes from physical museum environments; much less is known about how to optimize structured, feedback-rich gamification for virtual tours, where sensory and social cues are reduced and the risks of divided attention are higher. To address this gap, the current study compares a gamified, task-based mobile guide that provides immediate feedback during a museum virtual tour with a partially structured paper guide, examining its effects on museum knowledge, learning experience dimensions (engagement, meaningful learning, emotional connection), and overall satisfaction. This study sought to fill a theoretical gap by demonstrating that previous museum virtual tour research rarely experimentally compared guidance models, particularly for elementary school students, and almost never addressed the role of structured gamified mobile guidance.

3 Materials and methods
-----------------------

In this study, two groups of students will explore a museum via a virtual tour using either a mobile-based guide or paper-based guide were compared with an experimental approach. While the mobile guide offered gamified, well-structured, task-based guidance, instant feedback, the paper guide offered semi-structured guidance with no feedback element.

### 3.1 Environments used in the study

This section introduces the digital learning environments used in the study, namely the museum virtual tour and the guidance tools used during the intervention.

#### 3.1.1 Museum virtual tour

The museum virtual tour used in the study is a virtual tour of an old university (madrasa), which today functions as a museum, offered to the public by the city municipality. Below is a screenshot of the virtual tour of the museum (Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#Fig1)). At the very beginning of the virtual tour, only brief information about the museum is given, and no audio information about the sections or artifacts of the museum is provided. Users can switch to different points of the museum with the visuals at the top of the screen. The virtual tour shows every point of the museum, but the readability of the museum boards is low. Artifacts can be seen and zoomed in on.

Students explored the virtual tour on tablets or laptops and, depending on assignment, used either the author-developed mobile application (Müzemob) or a paper guide.

#### 3.1.2 The mobile guide

Müzemob mobile application developed within the scope of a nationally funded project, was designed to support museum-based and out-of-school learning through structured, task-oriented tour plans created via a web-based authoring interface. The application enables the design and delivery of multiple task formats, including informational content delivery, information collection activities, QR code–supported search-and-find tasks, open- and closed-ended questions, digital storytelling, poster creation, timeline construction, and puzzle-based activities. To foster engagement, the system integrates gamification features such as points, levels, badges, and performance-related feedback. In the present study, students used the Müzemob application while participating in a virtual museum tour, completing tasks that required identifying specific locations or artifacts within the museum environment, verifying their findings via QR codes, and submitting responses directly through the application.

Muzemob delivered a trip plan defined on its web backend comprising 13 information notes, 6 QR-based search-and-find tasks, and 10 quiz items (open-ended and multiple-choice). Information notes summarized content similar to physical museum boards and could be skipped. For search-and-find tasks, the system generated QR codes; because the study took place as a classroom-based virtual tour, researchers handed out the relevant QR codes for students to scan once they identified the correct area. The application provided immediate, automatic feedback for both search-and-find tasks and quizzes, including the correct answer after an incorrect response, and incorporated gamification elements (points, badges, clue-based target finding, and a progression flow).

#### 3.1.3 The paper guide

The paper-based guide consisted of a plain, text-only document presenting 10 tasks accompanied by brief prompts and guiding questions related to the exhibits. Unlike the mobile guide, it did not incorporate any gamified elements, interactive features, or automated feedback mechanisms. The guide provided semi-structured reminders intended to direct students’ attention to key aspects of each exhibit; however, feedback was delivered only verbally by the researchers and solely upon students’ request. All tasks in the paper guide were visible simultaneously, allowing students to revisit, skip, or reorder tasks according to their preferences. Although the overall task flow was linear in intent, this open presentation afforded learners greater navigational flexibility compared to the mobile guide, which enforced a fixed progression without the possibility of returning to previous steps. Figure [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#Fig2) presents a comparative illustration of the task structures used in the mobile and paper guides.

The paper guide was deliberately chosen as the basis for comparison because it reflects the most commonly used and accessible form of guidance in school museum activities. This allowed for an examination of the added value of structured, technology-supported guidance compared to established practices.

### 3.2 Development of mobile and paper guide tasks

Across both conditions, the instructional focus was limited to museum-related content, and all tasks were designed to be content-equivalent. Students in both groups engaged with the same exhibits, concepts, and factual information during the virtual tour; only the form of guidance differed. In order to ensure parity between the groups, particularly in terms of knowledge acquisition, the elements emphasized in the tasks in the mobile guide were also emphasized in the paper guide. Accordingly, each task in the mobile guide was matched with a corresponding task in the paper guide, and the same content was reflected in the museum knowledge test items derived from these tasks. Table [1.](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#Tab1) illustrates how equivalent tasks were structured in the mobile and paper guides and presents the knowledge test questions developed in alignment with these tasks.

### 3.3 Implementation process and sampling

The applications were carried out with students selected from the fourth through the seventh grades of two different elementary schools using a convenience sampling approach, and these 56 students (19 boys, 37 girls) were then randomly divided into two groups. Convenience sampling was chosen due to the practical and ethical necessity of working with intact class groups available in participating schools. The experimental procedure consisted of two parallel conditions: the paper guide group (*n* = 28) and the mobile guide group (*n* = 28). Both groups followed the same sequence of activities and durations. Each session began with a 7-minute pre-test (knowledge test), followed by a 15-minute briefing about the study, the assigned guide, and the virtual museum tour. Participants then spent 30 min exploring the virtual museum according to their respective guides. Finally, both groups completed post-tests, including the knowledge test and the museum learning experience scale, which took approximately 15 min. Randomization was ensured by assigning each student to alternating conditions as they entered the classroom, so that every other participant used a different type of guide (mobile or paper).

Some pictures of the implementation are shown in Figs. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#Fig3) and [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#Fig4). All legal permissions and ethics committee permissions regarding the implementation were obtained within the scope of the project and written consent statements were also received from the parents of the students who participated in the study.

### 3.4 Measurement tools and analysis

In this study, two measurement tools were used to measure the museum knowledge and the museum learning experience. The museum knowledge test consisted of 20 true-false questions selected from topics that they learned within the framework of the developed museum virtual tour plan. As a result of the item analysis, it was seen that the discrimination and difficulty levels of 15 of the questions were excellent. The final 15-item test demonstrated high internal consistency, KR-20 = 0.82, indicating acceptable reliability (Fraenkel et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR28)).

While creating the items to be used to measure the museum learning experience, the 19-item museum learning experience scale developed by Othman et al. ([2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR63)) was used. The scale originally had four factors engagement (four items), knowledge/learning (five items), meaningful experience (five items), and emotional connection (five items). Since the original scale was applied to adults, after the items were translated, the expressions were edited so that they could be understood by elementary students. Additionally, cognitive interviews were conducted with one student from each grade level to observe the understandability of the items, and three items were removed from the scale as a result of these interviews. Since many adjustments were made to the original scale items, an exploratory factor analysis was conducted for the new 16-item scale. For the exploratory factor analysis, data collected from 598 students in the fourth through seventh grades who visited the same museum in physical and virtual ways were used. After exploratory factor analysis, a 13-item, three-factor scale was formed, explaining 65% of the variance. To understand whether the resulting factor distributions constituted a good model and test the applicability of the same scale for museum virtual tour, a confirmatory factor analysis was conducted with 205 students whose data was collected after visiting museum virtual tour. As a result of the analysis, an acceptable model emerged, RMSEA = 0.071. In the data collected after the virtual museum visits, the engagement factor had an internal consistency coefficient of 0.82, meaningful learning factor had an internal consistency coefficient of 0.86, and emotional connection factor had an internal consistency coefficient of 0.71. The final scale used a three-point Likert format (1 = Disagree, 2 = Undecided, 3 = Agree). Accordingly, maximum attainable scores are 9 for engagement (3 items), 18 for meaningful learning (6 items), and 12 for emotional connection (4 items). In the museum learning experience scale, the students were also asked a five-scale question: “What is your level of enjoyment from this virtual tour?” This question was evaluated as the “satisfaction” score in the study. Only the pre-test was applied for the knowledge test, and all other measurement tools were applied as post-tests at the end of the implementation.

All statistical analyses were performed using IBM SPSS Statistics 20. Effect sizes were calculated automatically for between-group comparisons, while Cohen’s d values for within-group comparisons were calculated manually. For the museum knowledge test, total scores were computed by coding correct answers as 1 and incorrect answers as 0 (max score = 15). For the museum learning experience, item scores within each factor were summed and analyzed separately. Prior to inferential analyses, data were screened for normality and outliers; identified outliers were removed where justified (e.g., one case in the paper-guide group for the learning-experience analyses). These scores were then compared between the mobile- and paper-based guidance conditions. For the effect size value for eta square 0.01 was considered small, 0.06 medium, and 0.14 large (Cohen, [1988](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR21)). Effect sizes for the paired-samples t-tests were calculated using the formula d = t/sqrt(n). Following Cohen’s ([1988](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR21)) criteria, values of 0.2, 0.5, and 0.8 were interpreted as small, medium, and large effects, respectively.

5 Discussions
-------------

In this study, elementary students in grades four through seven used either mobile or paper-based guides to complete a virtual museum tour. The results showed that the mobile guide group achieved higher scores in museum knowledge, meaningful learning, and overall satisfaction. However, no significant differences were found between the groups in the dimensions of engagement and emotional connection, which are subfactors of the museum learning experience scale.

### 5.1 Learning outcomes and cognitive mechanisms in gamified virtual guidance

Although both groups showed significant improvement in their knowledge of the museum, the increase was greater for the mobile guide group. Previous studies have shown that clues, challenges, cooperative activities, and games can stimulate cognitive processes during museum visits and enhance cognitive engagement (Shaby et al. [2019b](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR74); Sun et al. [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR78)). In this study, the use of intriguing cues appeared to help students in the mobile group remain more cognitively engaged in their tasks. The feedback provided through the mobile guide, along with gamification elements such as points and clues, contributed to the development of meaningful learning, one of the key dimensions of the museum learning experience. In the paper guide group, students were not monitored to determine whether they located the relevant information in the museum, which may have led them to overlook or abandon certain tasks. Although the mobile guide group was also not directly monitored, the guide itself provided automatic feedback to the students. Gamification in virtual environments has been shown to reduce errors and increase the number of correct decisions, thereby enhancing user engagement and overall experience (Garbaya et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR30)). Similarly, in this study, the feedback and point system integrated into the mobile guide appeared to make students more successful in completing the tasks than those using the paper guide. Thus, it can be concluded that employing a gamified structure in mobile guidance helps students perform tasks more carefully and effectively without requiring constant teacher supervision.

One factor likely underlying the mobile guide’s advantage is its clearer task decomposition. Prior research similarly shows that providing more detailed descriptions of museum discovery activities can improve engagement (Mahatmi & Singgih, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR53)), and that breaking complex tasks into smaller steps clarifies goals and sustains participation (Tan & Hew, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR80)). In our study, a single paper-guide prompt often bundled multiple elements, supplementary information, a provoking question, and an action to complete, whereas the mobile guide distributed these components across sequential screens. Although both conditions supplied museum information, the paper guide embedded it within the question itself, while the mobile app presented it on a dedicated screen. This step-by-step structure likely supported clearer comprehension and execution in the mobile group, whereas the bundled format may have led students in the paper group to overlook specific task elements.

### 5.2 Engagement, emotional connection and satisfaction in virtual museum contexts

The results of the study showed that no differences were observed for engagement and emotional connection in relation to the museum experience. The most important factor that changed the effect of the guidance provided in the study was the fact that the study was conducted in a museum virtual tour. The dimension of engagement in the museum experience is related to feeling the atmosphere of the museum. As immersion increases, engagement in the environment also increases (Diego-Mas et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR25)). Unfortunately, the virtual tour only provided limited immersion (Cardona et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR12)). The emotional connection with a museum experience also requires physical interactions with the museum environment and its artifacts, touching and feeling (Schultz [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR69); Shaby et al. [2019a](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR73)). As such, this dimension was limited as the virtual tour was unable to provide it. Importantly, the absence of significant differences in engagement and emotional connection should not be interpreted solely as a lack of pedagogical impact of the mobile guide. These dimensions may be particularly sensitive to contextual affordances inherent in physical museum environments, such as embodied interaction, multisensory perception, and spatial immersion. In virtual tours, where sensory and physical interaction are constrained, affective responses may be more strongly shaped by the medium itself than by the form of guidance. From a theoretical perspective, this interpretation aligns with the Cognitive Affective Model of Immersive Learning (CAMIL) (Makransky & Petersen, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR54)), which posits that affective outcomes such as engagement and emotional connection are strongly mediated by presence and embodiment. When immersive affordances are limited, as in non-immersive virtual tours, differences between instructional conditions may be attenuated. Therefore, the non-significant findings observed in this study may reflect contextual constraints of the virtual environment rather than insufficient instructional design.

In addition, the scale used to measure the learning experience in the museum was developed for real museums. Although it is a scale that has been validated with studies conducted by researchers utilizing virtual tours, the statements on the scale are aimed at the feelings within a physical museum experience. Therefore, the contextual validity of the instrument in fully virtual environments should be interpreted cautiously. It is possible that certain items capture affective states more characteristic of embodied, in-situ museum visits than screen-based explorations. Consequently, the non-significant findings may partially reflect measurement-context misalignment rather than purely instructional limitations. Thus, if this study was to be conducted in a physical museum, the results may change. Othman et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR64)), in a similar study conducted in a real museum, found that the mobile group had significantly higher scores only in terms of emotional connection. Therefore, it can be stated that the museum environment affects the study results. However, to enrich the museum experience in a virtual tour, it may be effective to have more emotional elements in the tasks, to present the tasks within the framework of a story or to give tasks to develop a social dialog (Katrikh, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR40)).

Consistent with other findings, satisfaction with the virtual tour was higher in the mobile guide condition. On the other hand, while it appears that the experience is generally more positive in mobile conditions, this should be interpreted with caution as satisfaction was measured using a single general item. Learning satisfaction is closely linked to intrinsic motivation, perceived usefulness, perceived cognitive gains, quality of interaction with content, and information quality (Baturay, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR8); Li et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR49); Liaw & Huang, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR51)). Satisfaction is also sensitive to cognitive load during implementation (Hwang et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR34)). In our context, the mobile guide’s step-by-step structure and immediate feedback likely eased navigation and access to learning content, making the experience more enjoyable (Cho & Lim, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR20)). That said, the broader literature is mixed: some studies report that guidance embedded within the virtual environment reduces cognitive load (Ali et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR3)), whereas others find no such reduction (Albus et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR2)). Additional work in physical museums suggests that mobile guides can constrain free exploration and long-term exhibit interaction, introducing split attention, particularly for younger visitors who must switch continuously between the screen and the exhibition (Cesário & Nisi, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR14); Chen et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR17); Gronemann, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR31)). Although mobile technologies are now commonplace in museums, there remains limited evidence on how visitors should coordinate simultaneous interaction with devices and exhibits (Othman et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR64)). Moreover, research on virtual tours has yet to examine how using two digital tools in tandem (a web-based tour plus a mobile guide) affects cognitive load and attention. Future studies should therefore investigate design features that minimize split attention and support a seamless experience across both virtual and physical museum settings.

### 5.3 Implications for design of gamified virtual museum tours

The fact that the environment was a museum virtual tour had both positive and negative effects on the results. First, since the virtual tour was a new experience for the students, it was observed that they enjoyed the process. Second, some navigation problems in the virtual museum (e.g., the images moved too fast for the mouse movement) and the descriptions of the artifacts were not readable enough, which caused difficulties in obtaining information from museum boards. In any case, the virtual museum tour provided a learning experience for both groups. Therefore, virtual tours should be considered as a learning tool in educational environments. However, when utilizing these tours, good guidance is important for these trips to become more meaningful. Guidance offered through additional tools (mobile, paper) outside the virtual museum tour should be considered to provide extra information about virtual tours, keep the content up-to-date, offer different perspectives on the museum, and guide each museum visit differently. This guidance does not always have to be through a unique mobile application. It can be task-based, interactive, collaborative, collaborative, and/or problem-based using many Web 2.0 tools (Seetha & Ayyadurai, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR71)). Besides, although the mobile guide did not support a collaborative structure, this approach could be utilized in future studies to guide virtual museum tours.

In line with Dewey’s view of museums as integral to schooling, to make virtual tours a part of the curriculum, appropriate tasks should be defined, tasks should be linked to the school curriculum, and tasks should transfer the museum experience to real life (Dewey, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR24)). Dewey also suggested that the museum should become part of the school through repeated museum visits (Hein, [2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR32)). Virtual tours can be utilized to address the issues of time limits and budgetary constraints that limit school museum visits in today’s society (Cheng & Tsai, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-026-13959-5#ref-CR18)). This may be made easier by offering virtual museum tour itineraries that align with the curriculum and point educators toward readily available resources so they can offer instruction in these settings.

6 Conclusions
-------------

Taken together, the findings indicate that a purposefully structured mobile guide integrating gamification and immediate feedback more effectively supported students’ museum knowledge development and overall satisfaction during a virtual museum tour than a text-based paper guide. With respect to learning experience, the mobile guide led to higher meaningful learning, while no significant differences were observed between the groups in terms of engagement and emotional connection, suggesting that these affective dimensions are strongly constrained by the limited immersive and sensory affordances of virtual tour environments, regardless of guidance modality. Collectively, these results underscore the importance of designing virtual museum experiences not as stand-alone digital spaces, but as learning environments supported by carefully structured, feedback-rich guidance that enhances cognitive outcomes while acknowledging the experiential limits of virtual settings.

In conclusion, this study illustrates how a purposefully structured mobile guide can render virtual tours a readily accessible learning environment. Extending a literature base dominated by physical museum settings, it provides causal evidence within a virtual tour context that structured, feedback-rich, and gamified guidance enhances knowledge acquisition, meaningful learning, and satisfaction. The work also offers teachers and developers a practical exemplar of design principles for virtual tours, including stepwise task decomposition, immediate feedback, and clearly articulated goals. Overall, the findings indicate that virtual museum tours yield more consistent learning outcomes when designed not as stand-alone experiences but in tandem with structured, interactive guidance.

7 Limitations
-------------

The study may be limited by the fact that the guides in the two groups did not offer similar opportunities. The mobile guide differed from the paper guide not only in terms of the presentation medium, but also in that it included gamification, instant feedback, and structured sequential task design. This design choice was intentional. The study aimed to examine guidance realistically applied in digital learning contexts where mobile systems typically integrate multiple pedagogical features. Consequently, the observed differences should be interpreted as reflecting the combined effect of these design elements rather than a single component. Future research should use factorial or mixed designs to isolate the specific contributions of gamification, feedback, and device type, thereby improving the design of technology-supported guidance in virtual museum learning.

Despite careful planning, several other limitations should be acknowledged. First, only the devices supplied by the researchers were used during the virtual tour, with some students navigating via tablets and others via laptops; these device differences may have affected usability and learning experiences. Second, although the feedback mechanism was a key strength of the mobile guide, its linear structure did not allow students to revisit previous screens or correct mistakes, which may have negatively influenced motivation. In addition, the paper guide condition involved verbal feedback provided by the researchers upon students’ request, which may have introduced observer bias and variability in instructional support. Because such interventions were not standardized or systematically logged, they may have influenced students’ task completion and learning processes in ways that could not be fully controlled. Third, while both groups were unfamiliar with the virtual tour, the mobile group was also using the guide for the first time, dividing their attention between the two tools. This simultaneous use may have introduced an additional cognitive burden and affected their overall experience. Future studies could address these limitations by employing more flexible and familiar tools, offering non-linear navigation, and allowing learners to review content and self-correct during the learning process.

In addition, certain measurement-related constraints should be considered. The museum learning experience scale employed in this study was originally developed for physical museum settings. Although it has been utilized in studies involving virtual tours, several items are conceptually grounded in embodied, sensory, and spatially immersive experiences characteristic of in-situ museum visits. Therefore, the contextual validity of the instrument in fully virtual environments may be limited. The non-significant findings observed in the engagement and emotional connection dimensions may partially reflect this measurement-context misalignment rather than purely instructional limitations. Furthermore, the intervention consisted of a single 30-minute virtual museum tour session. Constructs such as engagement and emotional connection often develop through sustained exposure, repeated interaction, and continuity over time. As such, the short-term nature of the implementation may have constrained the emergence of measurable differences in these affective dimensions. Future research should consider longitudinal or repeated-exposure designs to better capture the temporal development of engagement and emotional attachment in virtual museum environments.

Finally, overall satisfaction was measured using a single global item. While single-item measures are commonly employed in short educational interventions to capture general impressions, they inherently limit psychometric robustness. Internal consistency cannot be estimated, measurement error cannot be directly assessed, and construct validity cannot be examined via factor analysis. Accordingly, the satisfaction findings should be interpreted cautiously, and future studies are encouraged to employ multi-item validated satisfaction instruments to enhance measurement precision.
