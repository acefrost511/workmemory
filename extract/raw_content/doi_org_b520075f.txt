Abstract
--------

The role of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) in K-12 education is a topic of ongoing debate and uncertainty. This exploratory case study aims to shed light on Australian K-12 teachers’ actions and decision-making processes when considering using GenAI in teaching and learning. Drawing on survey responses collected from 44 teachers, this paper provides insight to how Australian teachers are currently using GenAI. The study employs innovation theory to explore the teachers’ decision-making. It reveals that teachers priarily developed their knowledge through a combination of approaches and sources, and GenAI’s accessibility enabled them to experiment with its use. When deciding to incorporate GenAI into their lessons, balancing development of students’ AI literacy with their age and readiness was a key consideration, amongst others. The findings demonstrate a teacher-led innovation, where GenAI is not viewed as a replacement for established practices or technologies but rather as a tool to enhance teachers’ work and to support teaching and learning.m

1 Introduction
--------------

Much like previous technologies, such as computers, tablets, and the internet, GenAI has been positioned as an important, yet potentially disruptive innovation in K-12 education. The public release of ChatGPT triggered substantial debate about its positive and negative implications for teaching, learning and assessment (Chiu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR4); Kasneci et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR21)), including potential changes to teachers’ roles (Gentile et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR14)). While there is evidence that teachers are concerned about AI (Ottenbreit-Leftwich et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR28)), there is also evidence that teachers are interested in using it (Diliberti et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR7); Laak & Aru, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR24); Yun & Kim, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR40)). And although it has been noted that teachers consider GenAI an innovation for K-12 education (Urdan & Marson, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR34)), teacher decision-making has yet to be fully explored. Doing so is important given the paradoxical positioning of GenAI as “a ‘friend’ yet a ‘foe’” (Lim et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR25), p. 1).

This paper is an exploratory case study of the considerations and actions of 44 Australian teachers during this period of uncertainty about the place of GenAI in K-12 education. It aims to provide insight in the initial phase of teacher interest in and exploration of GenAI and provide grounding for further study. The case is examined through the lens of innovation theory (Rogers, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)) to understand teachers’ decision-making processes and the ways they view GenAI as a teaching and learning innovation. The paper answers three questions: How are Australian K-12 teachers currently using GenAI? What is the innovation-decision process for Australian K-12 teachers to use GenAI in education? How do Australian K-12 teachers view GenAI as an innovation for teaching and learning?

### 1.1 Use of GenAI in K-12 education

The growing body of research about GenAI in K-12 education indicates that it is being used in teaching and learning, though the extent varies. International research indicates teachers’ professional use of GenAI varies, 14 to 25% (Diliberti et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR7); Kaplan-Rakowski et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR20); Laak & Aru, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR24); Yang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR38)). Teachers are using GenAI when planning lessons (Adeyele & Ramnarain, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR1); ElSayary, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR9); Hashem et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR16); Moorhouse, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR27)), including to personalise and differentiate lessons for inclusion (Jauhiainen & Guerra, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR17); Laak & Aru, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR24)). Teachers are also using it to generate tests and questions (Laak & Aru, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR24)), and when marking tasks and providing feedback (Jauhiainen & Guerra, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR17)), including automated marking (Tran, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR33)). Two papers reported that using GenAI helped teacher efficiency (Hashem et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR16); Tran, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR33)). At the time of writing, only one publication was found that focussed specifically on Australian teachers’ use of GenAI: Moorhouse ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR27)) interviewed 10 first year language teachers, who reported they were using GenAI as an aid to their teaching.

Research of how GenAI is used in learning activities is currently quite limited. Students are interacting with chatbots, such as ChatGPT, to practice and receive feedback on grammar and communication (Jeon & Lee, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR18); Tran, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR33)). They are also interacting with GenAI-based avatars of historical figures or Shakespearean characters to develop critical questioning skills (Bower et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR2); Tang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR31)). Students are using Midjourney to assist with ideation in craft education (Vartiainen & Tedre, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR36)).

Studies also identify opportunities to use GenAI. For students, it is suggested they could use GenAI as a tutor or coach (Gentile et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR14); Jeon & Lee, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR18)), though Kaplan-Rakowski et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR20)) found that a third of teachers were concerned GenAI would replace them in some way. Other studies found that teachers thought GenAI could support active student engagement in learning (Jeon & Lee, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR18); K.-S. Tang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR32)). For teachers, it is suggested GenAI can assist with their work through increased efficiency (Chiu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR4); Kaplan-Rakowski et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR20)) and when planning lessons and developing resources, including differentiating learning experiences and adjusting resources to match students’ needs (Bower et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR2); Galindo-Domínguez et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR13); Han et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR15); Jauhiainen & Guerra, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR17); Kong & Yang, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR22); K.-S. Tang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR32)).

### 1.2 K-12 teachers’ responses to GenAI

Studies have explored teachers’ perceptions of the implications of GenAI for K-12 education. The importance of developing students’ AI literacy is cited as a justification for using GenAI, so too is the importance of developing teachers’ AI literacy (Bower et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR2); Chiu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR4); ElSayary, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR9); Velander et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR37)). The assessment implications of GenAI are substantial, with particular focus on the disruption of assessment types, like essays, due to concerns about academic integrity (Bower et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR2)). In response, it has been suggested that new modes of assessment unaffected by GenAI are needed (Chiu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR4)) and the re-prioritisation of paper-based assessment under supervised conditions may be required (Bower et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR2); Chiu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR4); Moorhouse, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR27); Yun & Kim, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR40)). Conversely, in acknowledgement of the role of GenAI in life and work, it is suggested that the use of GenAI be embedded in assessment activities to measure students’ AI literacies and associated higher-order thinking skills (Bower et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR2); Chiu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR4)). Teaching GenAI use and assessing AI literacies are intended to prepare students for an “AI integrated future” (Bower et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR2), p. 13).

Additional factors influence teachers’ use of GenAI, including contextual factors, occupational factors, and personal concerns. Collie and Martin ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR6)) found that the degree to which teachers valued GenAI was influenced by perceptions of leaders’ support, personal interest in professional growth, and change-related stress. Kaplan-Rakowski et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR20)) and Yun and Kim ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR40)) report that teachers are concerned about the accuracy of GenAI output, copyright, and privacy, and over-reliance causing the erosion of expertise. These influences are consistent with so-called first- and second-order barriers to the integration of digital technologies in learning and teaching (Ertmer, [1999](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR11); Ertmer et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR12)).

2 Theoretical framework
-----------------------

This paper uses innovation theory as a framework because speculation about the place of GenAI in K-12 education focuses on weighing-up its benefits and challenges. An innovation is broadly defined by its characteristics: an idea, practice or object that is perceived to be new or novel, has value to people, and is adopted because it offers an improvement (Rogers, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)). Rogers ([2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)) determined that prior to adopting or rejecting an innovation, individuals go through an innovation-decision process comprising five stages: knowledge, persuasion, decision-making, implementation, and confirmation. In the knowledge stage, decision-makers become aware of innovation and its broad function. In the persuasion stage, decision-makers form favourable or unfavourable attitudes. During the decision stage, decision-makers choose to adopt and implement the innovation, or they choose to reject it. In the confirmation stage, decision-makers revisit their decision. For decision-makers who implemented the innovation, this stage involves deciding to continue or discontinue. For decision-makers who did not implement the innovation, this stage involves deciding to adopt the innovation or to continue to reject it (Rogers, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)).

Rogers ([2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)) suggests that the decision to adopt or reject an innovation involves consideration of five perceived characteristics: relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, and observability. Relative advantage is the improvement on current practices offered by the innovation. Compatibility is how consistent the innovation is with decision-makers’ values, experiences, and needs. Complexity is how difficult the innovation is to understand or do. Trialability is the extent to which decision-makers can experiment with the innovation before committing to it. Observability is the degree to which the effects of the innovation are visible. Rogers ([2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)) also identifies five adopter categories, but these are not the focus of this study.

Rogers’ innovation theory has been used to research teacher practice. It has been used to theorise how teachers respond new technologies, including AI-powered music technologies (Uzumcu & Acilmis, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR35)) and interactive whiteboards (Erdener et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR10)). It has also been used to elucidate teachers implementing new approaches with digital technologies, including online learning during the COVID-19 (Joshi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR19); Koroleva & Andreeva, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR23)) and blended learning (Sun et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR30)).

Innovation has been researched across various fields, leading to recognition of different innovation types. This paper draws on Edwards-Schachter’s ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR8)) literature review, which identifies product, process, service, business model, disruptive, radical, design-driven, social, and responsible innovation types. Of these, technological, product, and service innovation are relevant. Technological innovations are new or improved technologies and production techniques, for example in K-12 education, interactive whiteboards, virtual reality, and 3D printing. Product innovations offer new or substantial improvements on previous products, for example, modular classroom furniture, hands-on STEM kits, and literacy curriculum packages. Service innovations offer new ways to “organise a solution to a problem (a treatment, an operation), which does not principally involve supplying a good” (Edwards-Schachter, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR8), p. 70). Education is considered a service because its output contributes to economic growth and employment, and hence new ways to support the delivery of education are considered service innovations (Edwards-Schachter, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR8)). For example, student peer tutoring, partnerships with local industries for hands-on learning, and teacher mentoring and coaching. Examining GenAI in K-12 education through the lens of innovation type is important because each different type frames how an innovation is positioned and understood (Edwards-Schachter, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR8)).

3 Method
--------

### 3.1 Research design

As using GenAI in K-12 education is an emerging phenomenon that is primarily driven by agentic teachers exploring what is possible, this research was conducted as an exploratory case study. It uses descriptive statistics from quantitative data and thematic analysis of qualitative data from an online questionnaire. The aim was to provide insight in the initial phase of teacher interest in and exploration of GenAI, and provide grounding for further, larger scale study. The goal is to explore three questions:

* RQ1: How are Australian K-12 teachers currently using GenAI?
* RQ2: What is the innovation-decision process for Australian K-12 teachers to use GenAI in education?
* RQ3: How do Australian K-12 teachers view GenAI as an innovation for teaching and learning?

Methodologically, case studies are useful for elucidating social phenomena (Yin, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR39)), including the integration of technology in learning and teaching (e.g. Cirkony et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR5); Uzumcu & Acilmis, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR35)).

### 3.2 Recruitment and participants

A convenience sampling strategy was used to recruit teachers interested in GenAI via posts on LinkedIn and Facebook, the social media of various teacher associations, and university information email for high school educators. All participants were K-12 teachers working in various states/territories in Australia (see Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#Tab1)). Complete responses were received from 44 people, with a range of teaching experience: 6–15 years (50%), 0–5 years (16%), 26–35 years (16%), 16–25 years (14%), 36 + years (5%). Most (*n* = 33) taught high school-age students (12–18 years), with 10 people teaching primary school-age students (5–11 years) and one person teaching primary and secondary students. They taught a range of learning areas: English (43%), Humanities and Social Sciences (43%), Technologies (32%), Science (27%), The Arts (23%), Mathematics (16%), Health and Physical Education (5%), Languages (2%), and other (11%).

### 3.3 Data collection and analysis

Data were collected using an online questionnaire that drew on well-established factors that influence use of digital technologies in learning and teaching, including the first- and second-order barriers (Ertmer, [1999](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR11); Ertmer et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR12)), to elucidate the teachers’ actions and contextual influences. In addition to asking demographic information (years of teaching experience, learning area(s), age(s) of students), the questions focused on: teachers’ knowledge and use of GenAI; teachers’ methods to develop knowledge and skills; teachers’ encouragement of student use of GenAI; teachers’ access to GenAI and support for its use at school. A combination of multiple-choice, multiple-selection, and longer text response questions were used, with longer text questions used to elucidate teachers’ actions and experiences, opinions and reasoning. The questionnaire was collaboratively developed by the three authors, and prior to deployment, a pilot version was tested with experienced researchers and teachers. The questionnaire was hosted on QUT’s Qualtrics server for data collection in May and June 2024; this study was approved by QUT’s Human Research Ethics Committee (approval number 8389).

Analysis of the qualitative and quantitative data was conducted in two phases. After review of the data for inappropriate or irrelevant response (of which there were none) and the removal of incomplete responses (a few), in the first phase, the quantitative data were analysed using Qualtrics’ descriptive statistics functions. The qualitative data from the open text questions were thematically analysed (Braun & Clarke, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR3)) using inductive coding by the first author that was repeated by the second and third authors. In the second phase, the first and second authors categorised (deductive coding) the first phase findings using innovation theory, specifically the innovation-decision process and the perceived characteristics of innovation (Rogers, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)), and innovation types (Edwards-Schachter, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR8)).

4 Analysis and findings
-----------------------

### 4.1 The innovation-decision process

This section reveals the decision-making processes of becoming aware of GenAI and learning how to use it, through to deciding whether to use it in teaching practice. The findings are categorised according to Rogers’ ([2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)) innovation-decision process. This study was conducted during and focused on the exploratory phase of GenAI’s availability in schools; it does not consider the confirmation stage, which was beyond the study’s timescale.

#### 4.1.1 Knowledge stage

The knowledge stage involved teachers becoming aware of the innovation and learning how to use it. This section presents data related to the “how-to” knowledge, the information necessary to use the innovation (Rogers, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)). The questionnaire asked participants how they developed their GenAI knowledge and skills. As summarised in Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#Tab2), a range of strategies were used. Most teachers used a combination of learning from others and exploration, using three strategies on average. Twelve people used all five, but ten people used only one strategy, most frequently, ‘exploring on my own’ (*n* = 7). Other strategies included teaching others about GenAI (*n* = 4), special projects about using GenAI (*n* = 2), writing publications (*n* = 1), professional reading (*n* = 1), and engaging in formal study (PhD) (*n* = 1).

#### 4.1.2 Persuasion stage

The persuasion stage involved teachers forming an attitude (favourable or unfavourable) about the innovation. This section presents data related to Rogers’ ([2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)) five characteristics of innovations – relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, and observability – to elucidate persuasive factors for the teachers in this study.

### Relative advantage

The relative advantage is the perception of whether the innovation will improve current practices, which was evident in teachers’ statements about the place and opportunities of GenAI in schooling. For teaching, 24 teachers reported that they thought GenAI could assist their planning and administrative work, with eight teachers indicating it offered efficiencies. For instance, “it will reduce the workload of teachers in administrative tasks.” One commonly cited advantage (*n* = 14) related to using GenAI when planning differentiated learning experiences, including, generating ideas for alternative strategies, changing the reading age of texts, and adding additional language captions to videos. One teacher noted, “It offers the ability to truly differentiate the curriculum and help students understand concepts at their level.” For learning, 25 teachers indicated they perceived relative advantages for students, most commonly its value as a tutor (*n* = 10) or writing assistant (*n* = 9) and to assist thinking and ideation (*n* = 12). Four teachers reported they encouraged student use of GenAI to make learning more inclusive and promote wellbeing.

### Compatibility

Compatibility is the perception of how consistent an innovation is with personal values, past experiences, and current needs, and the replies indicated positive and negative compatibility. The key positive compatibility related to teachers’ responsibilities to prepare students for the future. Developing students’ AI literacies, including ethical, responsible, and safe use (*n* = 12), and the place of GenAI in future work and life (*n* = 5) were the most frequently cited reasons for encouraging students to use GenAI. One teacher noted, “Our role as teachers is to prepare students for the real world. GenAI is rapidly becoming a critical tool across most industries, so we should be teaching students how to use it responsibly and ethically.” Conversely, comments about negative compatibility focussed on GenAI’s appropriateness for students’ age and stage of development. For instance, one teacher stated that using GenAI was inconsistent with key priorities, “the focus in primary school is for them to develop their own writing skills.”

### Complexity

Complexity is the apparent difficultly of using an innovation, which was evident in a range of ways. For some teachers (*n* = 5), ensuring safe and responsible use made it complex from them to use GenAI in their contexts. Three teachers commented on wanting structures and guidance: “I’d like a [Department of Education] approved app and some PD [professional development] on how to use it with kids”, and “[the school] has not yet rolled out AI use and the framework for using it with students.”

Some teachers reported complexities related to people who influence use GenAI in schools. As indicated in Table [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#Tab3), the teachers reported a range of responses by school leaders. Although most teachers reported that leaders offered encouragement, a substantial proportion noted that leaders held a neutral position, and a small number indicated that leaders discouraged use. One teacher noted, “We have access to ChatGPT with the Department [employer], however it is not encouraged by our principal.” Another, when asked about what impact GenAI will have on K-12 education, stated: “I hope a positive one, once our leadership team realises that it is not a tool that makes us slack, it is a tool to make us better!” Conversely, one teacher commented:

> [There is an] enormous amount of work for teachers in redeveloping the curriculum, leaders gaslighting staff by saying AI is going to make their workload easier, when its challenges far outweigh its opportunities, the notion that AI can do lesson plans (it can to an extent, but what about teacher expertise, agency and ownership over their own lessons?). Most teachers WANT to plan their own lessons and enjoy curriculum - they just need time to do it.

Colleagues’ attitudes made implementing GenAI complex for some teachers (*n* = 5). One stated: “There are few educators in schools exploring how it is best used … I fear that the best that GenAI can do, will go untouched.” Another noted, “Teachers and schools are very regressive and resistant to change. Subjects like English are very exposed to LLM (large language model) technology and refuse to evolve.”

The influence of governmental policy and curriculum is another reported complexity. One teacher noted:

> GenAI is exactly where the internet was in the mid-nineties, where we know the awesome potential, it can have but we’ve barely scratched the surface. It could be a game changer for good and bad depending on the State and Federal governments’ tactics.

Three teachers identified curriculum-related influences. One noted, “current assessment, curriculum, and prescribed syllabuses are not designed with students having access to GenAI.” Another noted a lack of support in the curriculum: “The Australian education system suggests what it wants but doesn’t provide tangible ways to get digital literacy, particularly generative AI into classrooms in a strategic way.”

### Trialability

Trialability relates to whether teachers can experiment with GenAI before committing to it. As indicated in Figs. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#Fig1) and [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#Fig2), access to GenAI at school was restricted or blocked for some teachers, and more so for students. It is also noteworthy that some teachers were unsure whether access to GenAI was blocked, which suggests they had not attempted to use GenAI while at work. One teacher also noted that GenAI subscription fees restricted access for some people, which in addition to causing inequity, also limits trialability.

### Observability

Observability of GenAI relates to the visibility of its benefits and limitations. Although the questionnaire did not offer evidence of observability in the persuasion stage, some teachers outlined observed benefits. For instance:

> I find some students don’t want a teacher to sit down and explain something - e.g. neurodivergent students can have success with it [GenAI] as it can break down questions or explain a topic in a better way than a teacher can. I have experienced this firsthand.

This illustrates how using GenAI makes its innovativeness observable. To avoid duplication, the teachers’ use of GenAI will be explored in the findings about the implementation stage.

#### 4.1.3 Decision stage

The decision stage involved teachers choosing to adopt or reject the innovation, which was evident in whether teachers encouraged students to use GenAI and the reasons for or against doing so. This involved considering benefits and challenges, as summarised in Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#Fig3). More teachers (*n* = 25) than not encouraged all or some students to use GenAI, with an additional group of teachers discussing GenAI with students but not encouraging them to use it (Table [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#Tab4)). A smaller group of teachers (*n* = 10) did not encourage students to use GenAI, including one teacher who indicated they discouraged students from using it. The teachers’ justifications for whether they encouraged students to use to GenAI provides insight to the decision stage.

Of the teachers who encouraged students to use GenAI, the most frequent reasons for doing so relate to the importance of developing AI literacies (*n* = 12) and preparing for future life/work (*n* = 5). Another frequently cited reason for encouraging student GenAI use was to use it as a tutor (*n* = 10) and as a tool to assist thinking (*n* = 5). As a tutor, teachers cited GenAI’s ability to explain and summarise content. For instance, one teacher noted, “I find that a lot of GenAI talks in a way which is easy to understand for students.” Another reported:

> [Assessment task] requires students to find and analyse data from scientific journals. I encourage students to use ChatGPT to summarise the abstract, methods or discussion of these papers so that they can better understand the paper and determine whether the data presented would be useful for their assessment.

Teachers also encouraged students to use GenAI as a writing assistant to proof-read work and to suggest improvements. As a tool to assist thinking, teachers encouraged students to use GenAI because “it is an amplifier of skills and talent” and a “tool for deeper thinking and questioning.” This includes for ideation of visual and written work, for instance, “to help with ideation and creativity when working through Design Thinking” and “to refine their ideas in Humanities tasks.”

Four teachers reported they encouraged student use of GenAI for inclusion and wellbeing. One reported, “I have a number of students with learning difficulties and GenAI can be used to assist them in demonstrating their understanding”. Two others noted associated student wellbeing benefits, “Reduction in stress, improve wellbeing,” and in relation to writing, “it takes the anxiety away from dealing with a human audience.”

Of the teachers who did not encourage students to use GenAI or who only encouraged some students to use it, the most frequent reasons related to students’ age (*n* = 7) and lack of knowledge (*n* = 4), and associated risks of academic misconduct (*n* = 2). The key priority for teachers was developing other knowledge and skills prior to students using GenAI. For instance, “The user should have a depth of knowledge on the subject, topic etc. they are having AI generate information about” and “they aren’t able to be critical enough yet to analyse a piece of AI (output) and understand all the vocab use.” Associated with this was the risk of academic misconduct due to student readiness, for instance, “Some students don’t have the capabilities/training/understanding to use it appropriately without academic misconduct”. Most teachers of younger students (5–7 and 8–11 years) did not encourage their students to use GenAI, whereas most teachers of older students (12–14 and 15–18 years of age) did encourage students to use GenAI (see Fig. [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#Fig4)).

The teachers’ decision were also shaped by whether GenAI was accessible at school. In schools that allowed students to access GenAI teachers more frequently encouraged students to use GenAI than in schools where GenAI was blocked. When asked about challenges to using GenAI in learning, ten teachers identified that limited or no access was a constraint. One teacher noted the implications of this:

> I want to model ethical use of GenAI, but my school blocks its use on school grounds. Students can use Copilot from their [school-supplied] computers, but only on their home network. So, we give them the tools to use it at home, they don’t even have a choice anymore about whether to use it (unethical??), and we don’t have a way to teach it at school.

The findings indicate teacher encouragement of student use of GenAI is not influenced by teachers’ access to GenAI at work but is influenced by student access to GenAI, which suggests pragmatism on the teachers’ part.

#### 4.1.4 Implementation stage

The implementation stage involves using innovations, and the findings provide insights into teachers’ actions. Almost all teachers (*n* = 41) used GenAI, though with varied frequency. They used it for various purposes, most commonly professionally (see Table [5](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#Tab5)). Most used it in teaching and learning (*n* = 31), with another five (*n* = 5) indicating that may use it in the future. Frequency varied (see Table [6](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#Tab6)) with most (*n* = 26) using GenAI either a few times per week or a few times per day. The teachers reported using GenAI in a range of ways – see Table [7](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#Tab7).

The most frequent uses related to work outside the classroom. This included lesson preparation. For example, “make slide decks or quizzes on popular topics really fast, these are easier to edit than generate from scratch,” and “for languages we can create subtitles and dubbing on videos using Maesto.ae. For example, “contextualise any scenario to what a student knows” and “change the lexile levels for my students so that they can all participate with a version of the same material.” Other uses include improving the clarity of emails, documents, and report comments, and translating messages to families into different languages. Some teachers were testing GenAI (Edexia and Brisk) to provide feedback on students’ work, though they emphasised the importance of checking feedback before sharing it and addressing privacy implications by de-identifying students’ work.

Teachers also reported instructing (*n* = 20) and supporting students (*n* = 16) in the use of GenAI. This involved developing iterative prompting skills and evaluating output for accuracy and relevance. Eight teachers noted the importance of developing students’ critical thinking and raising awareness about issues like deepfakes and troll farms associated GenAI.

Nearly half of the respondents (*n* = 21) reported setting activities in which students use GenAI in class, most commonly in three ways. Firstly, using GenAI during brainstorming and ideation for the purpose of “fast tracking the iteration of ideas (overcoming blank screen paralysis)” and supporting students when “refining their own ideas … to obtain a different perspective.” Secondly, encouraging use of GenAI to summarise long texts and/or multiple sources and simplify language to assist understanding. Thirdly, to assist revision: “I also encourage students to use it to study - e.g. generating exam questions to use for study.”

5 Discussion
------------

The aim of this study was to provide insight in the initial phase of teacher interest in and exploration of GenAI. These findings add to a growing body of literature about how K-12 teachers are using GenAI during this exploratory phase of a relatively new technology. They provide insight to the innovation and decision-making process, and offer a grounding for further, larger scale study.

### 5.1 Teachers’ use of GenAI (RQ1)

For these respondents, their engagement with GenAI was primarily professionally motivated. The frequency of use was quite varied, which mirrors the variation in other studies (Diliberti et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR7); Kaplan-Rakowski et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR20); Laak & Aru, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR24); Yun & Kim, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR40)). Most teachers reported using GenAI more than once per week, which is higher than other studies. This may reflect the timing of this research and be influenced by the smaller sample size. It might also suggest that the respondents to this survey were early adopters of GenAI.

The findings are broadly consistent with other research, illustrating the opportunities of GenAI in schooling. Using GenAI when preparing lessons and differentiating learning experiences has been identified (Adeyele & Ramnarain, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR1); Bower et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR2); ElSayary, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR9); Galindo-Domínguez et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR13); Kong & Yang, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR22)), including its potential to realise efficiencies in teachers’ workload (Chiu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR4); Kaplan-Rakowski et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR20)). The importance placed on developing students’ AI literacies is also a strong theme in the literature (Bower et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR2); Chiu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR4); ElSayary, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR9); Velander et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR37)). Students using GenAI in brainstorming and ideation activities across a range of learning areas is consistent with the findings that students can use GenAI to generate ideas (e.g., Vartiainen & Tedre, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR36)).

### 5.2 Innovation-decision process (RQ2)

When examined using the innovation-decision process (Rogers, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)), these findings provide insight to teachers’ decisions to adopt or reject using GenAI. The teachers developed knowledge in various ways. The differing degrees of encouragement and training provided by school leaders may explain why individual exploration was the most common form of PLD. As an emergent technology, it is reasonable to infer that wide-spread speculation and debate (e.g., Kasneci et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR21)) also influenced teachers’ knowledge.

The findings provide insight into the persuasion and decision stages. Rogers ([2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)) states that most individuals assess an innovation’s usefulness by trialling it prior to full adoption, and easier-to-trial innovations are adopted more rapidly. GenAI’s ease of access allowed teachers to try it, as evidenced by high usage. The higher frequency of out-of-class use suggests using GenAI in planning and preparation may have been preferable to trialling it in-classroom. This may reflect school access restrictions and risks associated with students using GenAI, which is consistent with Urdan and Marson’s ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR34)) findings. The varying support from leaders further evidences Collie and Martin’s ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR6)) findings that the value teachers place on GenAI is influenced by perceptions of whether leaders support or thwart its use. The benefits-challenges findings highlight important considerations in the decision to adopt or reject GenAI that are consistent with other studies about teachers’ responses to innovations with GenAI (Urdan & Marson, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR34)) and other digital technologies (Erdener et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR10); Sun et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR30)).

The reported uses of GenAI provide insight to the implementation stage for teachers who adopted the innovation. More teachers reported using GenAI for their out-of-class work than in-classroom activity, which suggests this is a more adoptable use. The teachers’ rationales for encouraging students to use GenAI focus on realising learning priorities – developing AI literacies, assisting learning, and facilitating inclusion and wellbeing. Conversely, rationales for not encouraging students to use GenAI focus on student preparedness and risks of academic misconduct. This suggests active rejection (Rogers, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)), a decision to not adopt. Some teachers rejected GenAI due to a lack of knowledge or implementation guidelines. The participants’ future adoption or rejection of GenAI (i.e., the confirmation stage) is beyond the scope of this point-in-time case study, and exploring teachers’ responses over time represents an opportunity for further research.

This case study’s findings are important because unlike innovations that are top-down, the instances of GenAI use in this study are bottom-up. Innovations can be introduced by leaders, resources deployed, and strategies employed to assist diffusion (Rogers, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR29)) (i.e., top-down). For example, introduction of mandated pedagogies such as blended learning (Sun et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR30)), or introduction of expensive technologies such as interactive whiteboards (Erdener et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR10)). These findings highlight that, for these teachers, the diffusion of GenAI in learning and teaching was different. The innovation was not instigated through hierarchical means nor dependent on top-down support, rather teachers initiated and enacted the innovation-decision process, occurring ‘bottom-up.’ This may be due to easy accessibility, but it also suggests agentic action by interested individuals during a period of uncertainty.

### 5.3 GenAI as an innovation in teaching and learning (RQ3)

In addition to providing insight to the innovation-decision process, the findings also elucidate teachers’ views of GenAI as an innovation in K-12 education. This is an important contribution to the field because studies of teacher innovation commonly focus on adopter categories (e.g., Uzumcu & Acilmis, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR35)) and the process of diffusion (e.g., Joshi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR19); Koroleva & Andreeva, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR23)) and persuasion (e.g., Urdan & Marson, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR34)). The teachers in this study viewed GenAI as a technological, product and service innovation.

GenAI is undeniably a technological innovation created through research and development (Edwards-Schachter, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR8)), and in the broader debate it is sometimes positioned as a disruptive innovation (Kasneci et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR21)). The actions of some teachers to develop students’ AI literacies, critical thinking, and awareness of associated issues, is evidence of intentional responses to a technological innovation. They recognised the significant implications for students’ futures and acted to address them. However, the findings do not indicate that teachers perceived GenAI as a disruptive innovation that displaces established products or processes (Edwards-Schachter, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR8)). While the risk of academic misconduct for assessment is identified, a clear commentary about the disruption of teaching, learning and assessment is absent. This suggests teachers positioned GenAI as tool in support of teaching and learning, rather than a disruption to it.

The findings suggest that some teachers viewed GenAI as a product innovation. A product innovation offers valuable improvements to users (Edwards-Schachter, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR8)). Teachers’ use of GenAI to improve the clarity of communication and to translate messages to families into different languages exemplifies this. Similarly, the AI-based feedback tools some teachers were trialling. The commentary about some leaders’ suggesting GenAI would reduce teachers’ workload also illustrates how GenAI was perceived to be a product innovation.

Some teachers viewed GenAI as a service innovation. In innovation research, education is considered a service that involves combinations of processes, people, materials, and activities, and innovation focuses on improving these (Edwards-Schachter, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR8)). Teachers’ decisions to use GenAI when preparing lessons suggest its positioning as a service for professional work. The teachers who encouraged students to use GenAI also positioned it as a service for learning: as a tutor and to make learning more inclusive; in ideation activities to address “blank screen paralysis”; to summarise large volumes of information and explain complex ideas; and to support students to communicate more clearly. In essence, they positioned GenAI to be “an amplifier of skills and talent” in the service of learning. The teachers who did not encourage students to use GenAI also seem to view it as a service innovation, but one that was incompatible. They were concerned about the limited usefulness of GenAI due to students’ age and knowledge, and the risks of academic misconduct undermined its usefulness for assessment, leading them to reject it.

Although it is easy to view GenAI as a technological innovation and a new educational product that will disrupt established paradigms, the teachers more commonly viewed it as a service innovation. This may be because most current and popular GenAI technologies are not specifically targeted towards K-12 education (Mishra et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13699-y#ref-CR26)), and may change over time if education-specific GenAI become available in schools. Presently, however, teachers are focused on how GenAI does or does not serve teaching and learning.

### 5.4 Limitations

This is a case study of 44 Australian teachers that draws on data from a questionnaire. As such the findings capture but are limited to teachers’ recount of considerations and actions. Due to this project’s focus on teachers’ initial uses of GenAI, data about the confirmation stage of the innovation-decision process were not generated, which is a limitation of this study. The teachers’ adopter categories were not determined, which is also a limitation. Further research of ongoing use of GenAI will help to understand the confirmation stage and could also explore the influence of adopter categories. Additionally, there may be value in using learning theories to investigate teachers’ pedagogical reasoning when using GenAI in teaching and learning.

6 Conclusion
------------

This exploratory case study provides important insights about the actions and decision-making of Australian teachers during the period of uncertainty about the place of GenAI in K-12 education. In addition to contributing to the growing body of literature about how teachers are using GenAI, this paper provides insight to the processes of teacher-led bottom-up innovation. Rather than adopt an innovation promoted by leaders (i.e. top-down), the teachers primarily developed their knowledge through exploration and by drawing on a range of information sources, including colleagues. The ease of accessibility of GenAI improved its trialability during the persuasion and decision stages. When deciding to use GenAI with students, balancing developing students’ AI literacy with concerns about students’ age and preparedness was a key consideration. For the teachers who implemented GenAI, it was easier and lower risk to trial GenAI in out-of-class work than trial it with students.

Importantly, the findings indicate that teachers did not view nor use GenAI as an innovation to replace established technologies/practices. Although GenAI is undeniably a technological innovation, the teachers valued how it might improve aspects of their work (i.e., a product innovation) and/or how it might serve in support of teaching and learning (i.e., a service innovation). In essence, when in engaging bottom-up innovation with GenAI, teachers prioritised how it would contribute to their work and applied it where they considered the innovation to be situationally and contextually relevant.
