Abstract
--------

Research on AI systems like ChatGPT indicates that there are not only obstacles but also potentials of using AI systems on the learner-level, e.g., for fostering learning processes. On the teacher-level, however, there is not much research yet and an important possibility to use AI systems like ChatGPT could be for, e.g., the development of complex tasks, which could help teachers in lesson planning processes. This study provides explorative and qualitative insights into such a task development process with the help of ChatGPT and for that purpose, *n* = 17 prospective teachers’ interactions with ChatGPT and post-interaction reflections were examined. They used ChatGPT with the assignment to develop complex tasks with the specific subject-matter parameters ‘Operative principle’, ‘Geoboard’ and ‘Multilingualism’. Methodologically, interpretative interaction analyses are conducted to gain abductive insights into the transcribed data and the analysis indicates that teachers, who were trained in the specific subject-matter as well as in prompt engineering, could use ChatGPT in a way that highly viable tasks could be generated whereas non-trained teachers mostly generated non- or less-viable tasks. This leads to the assumption of a threshold for the viable usage on teacher-level: Teachers who are subject-matter- and content-wise proficient and who know about effective prompting can use ChatGPT efficiently and evaluate it as a good tool for helping them in developing tasks they would otherwise not be able to develop, e.g., multilingual tasks, whereas teachers with less proficiency in both facets do not evaluate the usage of AI as useful. These results influence teacher training programs and the future development of AI tools for educational purposes in a two-fold way. Since teachers who know both how to prompt and what to teach are more capable of using AI systems like ChatGPT productively as support in developing learning environments, future teacher training programs should include prompting modules as part of their structure, e.g., following subject-matter training modules. This approach would help prospective teachers learn how to use AI systems like ChatGPT effectively and integrate these systems into their practices, e.g., for developing tasks.

1 Introduction
--------------

AI systems like ChatGPT or Elicit have started to change text creation processes on all levels of education: Students, Teachers and Educators use AI systems to support their text writing processes and solving tasks (Holmes et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR35); Lee & Yeo, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR53); Ouyang & Jiao, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR62); Ray, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR69)). Often, AI systems are perceived as mere “entertainment systems” but research shows the high potential AI has in supporting processes in professional contexts such as schools (Horbach et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR36); Meyer et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR57)), where teachers can use AI to, e.g., solve complex mathematical problems (Kortenkamp & Dohrmann, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR43); Ouyang & Jiao, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR62)) but although there is high potential, there are also specific obstacles and risks. For example, AI can solve tasks falsely and this may lead to a mistaken use of this incorrect knowledge as Kortenkamp and Dohrmann ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR43)) illustrate: AI like ChatGPT might forget mathematical rules and norms in solving equations and has to be ‘remembered’ that specific rules exist for solving equations (see ibid.). A key competence seems to be the ‘prompting’, meaning the usage of specific impulses like ‘words’ or ‘phrases’ that can be used by the AI to revise and change the probability calculation and given answers (Ray, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR69)). Research in ‘prompting’ is necessary but often very general and not very specific with regard to teachers’ necessities, e.g., for prompting processes with subject-didactical and school-related impulses (Holmes et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR35); Kortenkamp & Dohrmann, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR43)).

This leads to a specific research gap: How can teachers be supported in interacting with AI in a *productive* way? Research in AI programming on the level of teacher-education and teacher-usage shows that AI is capable of developing or solving smaller or easy tasks (Lee & Yeo, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR53)), but for a real practicability in school contexts, it is necessary to give insights into interactions with AI under school-related realistic conditions, meaning the development of complex tasks as they were necessary for a usage in school and not only the generation of ‘simple tasks’ (like tasks for the calculation of 5 + 9). In this study, (prospective) teachers had to generate such complex tasks, understood as tasks with at least three complexity attributes (see Sect. [3.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec9)), by using the help of ChatGPT 3.5. The interaction between the teachers and AI was examined with an explorative qualitative research design by interpretatively analyzing Chat sequences and transcriptions of audiographed post-interaction discussions and reflections (see Sect. [3.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec8)). Being a qualitative-explorative study, data from 17 prospective teachers in Germany were extensively analyzed to assess the influence of prompt engineering and subject-matter training. Since research on AI-human interactions is a new research field (see Sect. [2.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec4)), an explorative research question, meaning a set of questions focusing on new phenomena (see Prediger, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR66)), is necessary. Thus, a reconstructive and interrelated set of two questions was posed for the analyses conducted in the context of this study:

* Q1: *How do teachers use prompts to instruct ChatGPT 3.5 for constructing a complex task with the attributes ‘operative principle’*, *‘geoboard’ and ‘multilingualism/translanguaging’?*
* Q2: *How do the prompts of the teachers differ*, *and what are the reasons for prompts leading to a positive or negative outcome with regard to the main goal (construction of a complex task)?*
* *Q3: Which competence forms could be reconstructed in which frequencies of occurrence in the different proband groups?*

In this article, a theoretical overview of the usage of digital media in educational contexts will be given (see Sect. [2.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec3)) before elaborating the research on AI usage in educational contexts (see Sect. [2.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec4)). Since the AI-usage and reflections of prospective teachers in the context of mathematics education in primary school are focused, teacher reflections and knowledge forms in the context of digitality-related knowledge (see Sect. [2.3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec5)) as well as the subject-matter dimension – being the ‘operative principle’, the ‘Geoboard’ and activation of multilingualism for multilingual meaning-making (see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6)) – will be discussed thereafter. In Sect. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec7), the method of the study will be explained and in Sect. [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec10), empirical insights will be given into the prompting processes of prospective teachers who had or had no training in prompt engineering as well as subject-matter. In Sect. [5](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec14), the main insights from the analysis as well as from the study will be discussed.

The main contribution of this article is that prospective teachers, who had a prompt engineering training based on Ray ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR69)) as well as a subject-matter training, were highly efficient in identifying non- or only partially-viable task generations of ChatGPT and in re-prompting with the goal of generating viable tasks, whereas teachers with no training in both facets showed a rather ‘naive’ and non-viable usage of ChatGPT, not being able to re-prompt efficiently and to identify wrong tasks/generations of ChatGPT. This underlines the importance of fostering the usage of AI-systems like ChatGPT in teacher education, e.g., as prompt-engineering modules after subject-matter modules, where future teachers can explore and learn how to prompt efficiently as well as in a subject-matter oriented way, similar to the usage of a ‘calculator’ for efficiently calculating complex tasks. This article provides in-depth qualitative insights into prompting and reflection processes, but future studies should also investigate quantitative correlations with a larger number of participants.

2 Theoretical background
------------------------

For understanding the role AI systems like ChatGPT play in knowledge construction and interaction processes, we will first reflect on the role of ‘digital media’ in learning processes generally (see Sect. [2.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec3)) before providing more specific insights into the role of AI systems in educational contexts (see Sect. [2.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec4)). Furthermore, since this study focuses on teacher reflections on subject-didactical task generation, we will offer insights into teacher reflection processes from a constructivist perspective (see Sect. [2.3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec5)), as well as into the subject-didactical facet (see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6)).

### 2.1 ‘Digital media’ in the context of the constructivist paradigm and learning processes

From a psychological viewpoint, (new) learning-processes can be regarded as so-called accommodation-processes being stimulated by learning-environments (including tasks but also interactions), meaning the change of ‘mental schemes’ through cognitive conflicts (Gogus, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR28); Schneider, Vamvakoussi & van Dooren, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR72)). ‘Mental schemes’ can be defined as viable, partially-viable or non-viable *individual notions* through which individuals can grasp the meaning of objects and phenomena in everyday and subject-specific situations (Büscher, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR12); Fischbein, [1989](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR23); Kuzu, [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR45)). *Viable* individual notions work for a specific context or class of situations and lead to ‘correct’ solutions with regard to all or most of the aspects, whereas *partially-viable* individual notions are interpretations that do not lead to a fully correct ‘solutions’ but still might include viable interpretations of specific aspects (e.g., when children interpret a ‘big minivan’ as a ‘bus’– although both are different ‘objects’, there are shared properties). Non-viable individual notions might be seen as interpretations that are ‘false’ with regard to most aspects in a specific context or class of situations, but that does not mean that it is generally ‘false’ (e.g., interpreting a ‘bus’ as a ‘bike’): The viability might change again if the context is changed (Kuzu, [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR45)), e.g., when in a children’s movie a robotic bike has the ability to transform into a bus.

However, in recent approaches, constructivism is not considered in an isolated manner, instead it is seen as an important facet of *learning with others (in interaction)* and as being *situated in specific contexts* and such a multi-facetted approach including the constructivist background theory is the so-called ‘Situated Learning Theory’: Green et al. ([2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR31)) describe the ‘Situated Learning Theory’ with the three interrelated aspects ‘Constructivist Learning Approach’, ‘Social Interaction’ and ‘Authentic Context’:

* a (mainly) Constructivist Approach instead of a Behavioristic approach, the main difference being the fact that *“the behaviorist educational theory holds weight as a traditional guided learning model through which the teacher guides learning. Students are motivated extrinsically and stimuli are provided to promote learning* […] [whereas] *the constructivist education theory* […] *asserts that discovery learning*, *and more self-directed goal setting are more beneficial to the twenty-first century global learner.”* (Sidney, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR78), p. 2),
* an interactionist view on knowledge construction, which according to, e.g., Blumer ([1969](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR6)) follows the three important premises: *“The first premise is that human beings act toward things on the basis of the meanings that the things have for them. Such things include everything that the human being may note in his world– physical objects*, *such as trees or chairs; other human beings*, *such as a mother or a store clerk; categories of human beings*, *such as friends or enemies; institutions*, *as a school or a government; guiding ideals*, *such as individual independence or honesty; activities or others*, *such as their commands or requests; and such situations as an individual encounters in his daily life. The second premise is that the meaning of such things is derived from*, *or arises out of*, *the social interaction that one has with one’s fellows. The third premise is that these meanings are handled in*, *and modified through*, *an interpretative process used by the person in dealing with the things he encounters.”* (Blumer, [1969](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR6), p. 2), and.
* a context-related approach to knowledge construction, meaning that learning and individual scheme building is not stored in an ahistorical, acontextual and abstractly generalized way, instead, our notions are rather ‘bound’ to domains of knowledge like the ‘money world’, where humans store arithmetical knowledge by linking them to, e.g., adding, taking away values in form of ‘money’, and thus they are domain-specific subjective realities as Bauersfeld ([1980](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR3)) states (Bauersfeld, [1980](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR3); Dilling et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR19)).

Especially with regard to the latter, the context-related knowledge construction, digital media plays an important role: It makes possible that individual learning processes might emerge in digitally constructed ‘worlds’– where the construction of the digital worlds itself is also a process of ‘ongoing accomplishment’ (Garfinkel, [1967](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR26)), thus socially constructed (Nayak et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR60); Meyer et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR57)). Kammerl et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR39)) describe digital realities as “*no object of thematisation*, *instead a constitutive*, *non-ignorable background for the appearance of a living environment* […] *the self-world-relation is bound to medial reality-constructions in highly relevant parts*, *which is why in educational processes the digital media embossing of our world has to be considered.”* (Kammerl et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR39), p. 55 ff., translation by author). Thus, it is important to understand how individuals interpret and socially construct their ‘realities’ and how these interpretations are influenced by digital media. This means that, like every other learning-process, learning-processes in the context of digital media are ‘situated’, meaning that they are stimulated by the digital experiences and impulses students get. Thus, from a constructivist viewpoint, it is important to use ‘digital media’ in a product- as well as process-oriented way, meaning a usage that stimulates *activities of learners*, not (only) the consumption of ‘inputs’ like teacher-explanatory videos (Thyssen et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR83); Kuzu, [2023c](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR47)).

The term ‘digital media’ refers– in a general sense– to receiving systems of binary coded data: These receiving systems consist of (a) terminal devices like PC’s, tablets or smartphones, where the binary coded data arrives, and (b) specific apps or programs on these terminal devices for ‘translating’ the binary coded data, e.g., as pictures, texts or videos on software interfaces (Hassan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR33); Thyssen et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR83)). In educational contexts, mostly apps or tools for creating and interacting with texts, pictures or videos are focused when mentioning ‘digital media’ and using digital media might support the conceptual understanding of learners, if digital apps, formats and tools are used in a dynamic, thinking-accompanying way, e.g., by using apps or tools with digital manipulatives for visualizing the conceptual facet of arithmetic tasks, numbers etc. (Klinger & Walter, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR40); Raßbach et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR68)). According to Thyssen et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR83)), it is important to consider that *“resulting potentials and the users’ changed behavior then trigger further changes via the dynamic processes that go beyond technological facilitations in everyday life and affect the interpersonal sector. Smartphones*, *for example*, *not only offer new technological possibilities for communication*, *but also change the way we communicate”* (Thyssen et al., p.2). Digital media interfaces and formats that generate, e.g., texts through the help of AI, for example ChatGPT, play an important role in the context of an (a) constructivist, (b) interactionist and (c) situated-learning related perspective on the role of digital media with regard to learning processes (see Sect. [2.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec4)).

### 2.2 AI systems in educational contexts in general and in mathematics education in specific

Using AI systems in the educational context, e.g. in the context of mathematics education, is not a new research topic, there is research since the early 1990s (Balacheff, [1993](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR2); Kortenkamp & Dohrmann, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR43)). Yet, since the early 2020s, a specific ‘turning point’ was reached after the world-wide introduction of ChatGPT: Researchers and Educators described chances and risks of using ChatGPT, ranging from a positive ‘hype’ that promises only or mostly positive effects (Holmes et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR35); Lee & Yeo, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR53); Ouyang & Jiao, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR62)) or that saw a risk for ‘integrity’ and education in general (Cotton et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR16); Gozalo-Brizuela & Garrido-Merchan, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR29)). In educational contexts, a key question is how AI might support learning processes (Mohamed et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR58); Oujang & Jiao, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR62)). Ouyang & Jiao, ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR62)) describe at least three main paradigms of AI-implementation: (1) AI-directed learning processes (with learners as recipients), (2) AI-supplemented learning processes (with learners as collaborators) and (3) AI-empowered learning processes (with learners as leaders) (Ouyang & Jiao, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR62)).

A well-known problem is the fact that AI systems like ChatGPT might ‘lie’, thus generate texts that are not based on true papers, facts or false applications of knowledge, like, e.g., the usage of specific formulas. Kortenkamp and Dohrmann ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR43)) give insights into such false calculations and formula applications of ChatGPT that lead to a false solution for a mathematical problem but at the same time they also show that these mistakes can be solved easily by precise ‘prompting’ (Kortenkamp & Dohrmann, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR43)). ‘Prompting’ thus appears to be a key competence in interactions with AI and can be understood as *“input from external users”* (Stahl & Eke, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR80), p. 8). Ray ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR69)) lists five aspects or so-called ‘prompt engineering techniques’ being important for a successful conduction of prompting: 1. Clear and specific prompts (e.g., by unambiguously stating the name, year etc. of the object of interest), 2. Provision of context and background information (e.g., by concisely stating the subject-matter), 3. Specification of desired format and structure (e.g., by demanding lists, rankings, rated assessments etc.), 4. Application of constraints and limitations (e.g., by setting boundaries to the response, such as character limits, timeframes, or scope) and 5. Iterative prompting (e.g., when initial or further responses do not meet the expectations) (Ray, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR69)).

Although it is well known what ‘prompting’ itself means and how it should be conducted, insights into *prompting processes* are still highly necessary for a more profound understanding of human-AI interactions, especially in the case of a usage in educational contexts (Fleckenstein et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR24); Ray, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR69); Stahl & Eke, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR80)). For example, it is known that ChatGPT has multilingual proficiency (Ray, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR69)) but what that means in terms of an active usage in learning environments still has to be explored and is an important resource for teachers and educators (Holmes et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR35); Horbach et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR36)).

Most studies on the AI-usage of teachers focus on the solution capacity of AI in the context of word problems or the solution of mathematical tasks (e.g., Kortenkamp & Dohrmann, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR43)). However, in these studies, AI is used as a problem-solving tool, not as a task-generating supporting system for teachers who want to develop tasks they can pose to their students. In school practice, if AI shall be used as a real supporting tool, it also has to be able to construct realistic and thus complex tasks that fit to the complex pedagogical reality in school contexts and the education contexts and here, further in-depth insights are necessary (Holmes et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR35); Kuzu, [2024a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR49)).

### 2.3 Teacher reflections and knowledge forms in the context of digitality related knowledge

To understand teacher’s motives and competencies in the context of AI-prompting and -usage, an analysis of reflection processes is important to gain insights into experiences and valuation processes (Buchholtz et al. [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR10); Green et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR31); Kortenkamp & Dohrmann, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR43); Schorcht & Baumanns, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR73)). Being based on the constructivist paradigm, ‘reflection’ is understood as an as *processes of cognitive structuring and re-structuring with the aim of further developing one’s own knowledge*, *attitudes and options for action in specific situations by linking these with one’s own and others’ ideas*, *ideally integrated into interactions* (Dewey, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR18); Green et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR31); von Aufschnaiter et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR85); Kuzu, [2023d](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR48)). Two main forms of reflections can be described: *Retrospective* and *prospective reflections*. *Retrospective reflections* are reflections on the assessment/evaluation of past and completed actions and *prospective reflections* are reflections related to the expansion of future options for action, e.g., with regard to what one wants to change in the future. Furthermore, both forms can occur in mixed types, for example when teachers reflect on their past actions and draw conclusions for their future actions (Desimone, [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR17); Herzog, [1995](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR34); Prediger & Rösike, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR67); Scherer et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR71); Sherin & Drake, [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR75)).

Teacher reflections can provide insights into their competencies and knowledge (Scherer et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR71)). Various frameworks have been developed to categorize distinct forms of teacher knowledge, with a significant portion of these models drawing upon Shulman’s ([1986](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR76)) widely recognized tripartite schema (Prediger & Rösike, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR67); Shulman [1986](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR76)), who operationalizes the knowledge and competence of teachers into.

* *Content Knowledge (CK)*: Knowledge about in-depth structures in language, mathematics, technical disciplines etc. (e.g., knowledge about the ‘rule of constancy’ in mathematics)
* *Pedagogical Content Knowledge (PCK)*: Knowledge about the learning processes with regard to content knowledge (e.g., knowledge about how and when to teach the ‘rule of constancy’ in the school context, about subject-specific didactic principles etc.)
* *Pedagogical-psychological Knowledge (PK)*: Knowledge about general aspects and conditions of learning processes (e.g., knowing that learning in interactions is important) (Koehler et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR41); Huwer et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR37); Prediger & Rösike, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR67); Scherer et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR71); Shulman, [1986](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR23)).

For the usage of digital media and digital technologies, Thyssen et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR83)) have adapted the TPACK-model of Koehler et al., ([2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR41)), which is based on Shulman ([1986](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR23)) and includes the ‘technological knowledge’ (thus being called *T*PACK) (Koehler et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR41)), and further differentiate the aspect of ‘technological knowledge’ (TK) into ‘digitality related knowledge’ (DK) since the prior highly focusses the technology- and informatics-related knowledge, not necessarily including sociocultural knowledge-dimensions that affect reality constructions, thus *“the way we perceive and classify the world”* (Co-Author et al., p. 2). ‘Digitality’ is defined a *“newly coined term that combines the words digital and materiality or reality”*, thus emphasizing the knowledge about the usage of digital media in pedagogical contexts (like digital videos, ChatGPT, VR etc.) and its influence on interactions and knowledge construction (Thyssen et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR83)). In this article, digitality-related knowledge (DK) and technological knowledge (TK) will be subsumed under DK/TK because a differentiation is not possible in the interaction with AI: The interaction affects the perception the AI as an ‘intelligent’ interaction partner, thus co-constructive processes with AI as well as the perception and classification of knowledge through the help of AI systems occurs (see Sect. [2.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec3) and [2.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec4)), and at the same time includes knowledge of precise prompting processes, which is a rather technology-related aspect (see Sect. [2.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec4)). Every prompt thus includes at least technology-related aspects with regard to the prompting process itself and digitality-related aspects with regard to co-construction of knowledge between a human and an AI system.

### 2.4 The subject-matter facet: the ‘operative principle’, the ‘geoboard’ and activation of multilingualism for multilingual meaning-making

For simulating the complexity of real task development, a key pedagogical principle (the operative principle) for understanding concepts (not only in mathematics education but in all subjects as well as in pedagogy in general) and an often-used material (the geoboard) were chosen. The *operative principle* goes back to the early psychological works of Piaget ([1977](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR65)) and Aebli ([1985](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR1)) and refers to transformational operations a child has to make to overcome *“inequalities/imbalances/disparities* […] [with regard to] *meanings of notions/concepts* [that] *can be flexible if we subjugate them to multiple transformations and reflect upon them from different perspectives”* (Aebli, [1985](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR1), p. 4; translated by author). This means that a viable and deep understanding of a concept, e.g., of the concept of a ‘tree’, makes it necessary to reflect upon it in a flexible, non-static way: A static reflection would be the memorization of a specific tree like ‘spruce’ as *the* tree, whereas a flexible view would make it necessary to reflect upon the question (a) if there are *other* trees and (b) how trees *change* depending on specific factors or rather (climatic) ‘operations’, e.g., when the temperature changes (trees in warmer regions often are ‘smaller’) or the weather conditions change (like ‘trees under snow’ or ‘trees burning in a forest fire’). Such an imagined or real-world-observation based subjugation and investigation of the changes an object or concept may undergo, depending on ‘operations’ it is subject to, leads to a multifaceted, multiperspectival and broadened notion of concepts such as ‘tree’ and is essential in the development of a child in the sense of a ‘conceptual enrichment’ (Carey, [1999](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR13); Faatz et al., [2001](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR22)) although this does not mean that an individual can have a ‘fully complete’ or ‘fully enriched’ notion of an object, it rather has to be thought of as a gradual approximation process since a specific ‘vagueness’ of our notions is necessary, typical and can never be overcome fully (Bauersfeld, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR4)) and processes of knowledge re-structuring have to be considered also (Schneider, Vamvakoussi & van Dooren, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR72)).

Wittmann ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR87)) concretizes the ‘operative principle’ with regard to the mathematical understanding and operationalizes it by using three key terms: *Objects*, *operations* and *effects* (Wittmann, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR87)). *Objects* can be numbers, terms, words etc. and *operations* are structured transformations that are conducted on the objects (like doubling, halving or adding objects repeatedly, changing their places repeatedly etc.), leading to *effects*, meaning changes which can be observed on the objects (Duval, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR21); Wittmann, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR87)). Thus, the ‘operative principle’ is a didactic principle which aims at making structures and relations in and of objects through systematic changes/operations reflectable (see Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Fig1)).

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8/figures/1)

A slide from the training in subject-matter for explaining the ‘operative principle’ according to Wittmann (1985) with an example

Such a structure-related analysis of the effects operations have on specific objects leads to the so-called ‘learning through exploration’ principle, which is a constructivist notion and view and learning processes and the geoboard, a board with nails in constant distance to each other and rubber bands to attach them to the nails, is a popular format from the context of ‘geometry’ and is appropriate for stimulating explorations in the sense of the ‘operative principle’ (Mudaly & Sibiya, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR59); Sibiya, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR77); Wittmann, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR87)).

The activation of multilingual resources in mathematics education was implemented according to the ‘translanguaging’-paradigm (Garcia & Wei, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR25); Kuzu, [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR45)): Instead of an additive notion of multilingualism, meaning (only) translations and switches between multiple languages, a thinking-process-related approach to multilingualism was focused, meaning that in multilingual interactions, languages *“are used in a dynamic and functionally integrated manner to organise and mediate mental processes in understanding*, *speaking*, *literacy*, *and*, *not least*, *learning. Translanguaging concerns effective communication*, *function rather than form*, *cognitive activity*, *as well as language production.”* (Lewis et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR54), p. 641). According to Pavlenko ([2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR64)) this is important because it is typical for multilinguals to have different interpretations and notions of concepts *between* languages in their minds, meaning that translations do not lead to identical meanings, rather they are always linked to smaller or bigger differences with regard to associations, ‘cultural frames’, the order of knowledge processing etc. (Greve et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR32); Pavlenko, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR64); Kuzu, [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR45)).

3 Methodology
-------------

Interactions between AI systems and humans have to be regarded as a specific form of interaction which affects knowledge-construction processes (see Sect. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec2)). Thus, as a method of analyzing interactions, the method of interpretative ‘Interaction analysis’ has been chosen and will be explicated in Sect. [3.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec8) before giving more insights into the sample and research context of the study (see Sect. [3.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec9).). The specific research question of the analyses being conducted in this article will be derived in Sect. 3.3.

### 3.1 Interpretative ‘interaction analysis’

Interactions between AI systems like ChatGPT and humans are a specific form of interaction: They are interactions with digital systems that seem ‘alive’ from the perspective of human users and thus have to be considered an essential part of ‘digitality’ (see Sect. [2.3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec5); Thyssen et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR83)). For deeper process insights on this new form of interaction, it is necessary to carefully conduct an abductive, turn-by-turn analysis, which means an interpretative analysis that discusses possible assumptions and marks the most plausible assumptions as so-called ‘Deutungshypothesen’ or explanation-hypotheses (Jungwirth, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR38); Schütte et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR74); Kuzu, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR44), [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR45)). Furthermore, for ensuring the criteria of ‘intersubjective plausibility’, meaning the highest possible degree of plausibility or reliability of assumptions between different interpreters (Steinke, [2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR82)), these abductive analyses should be conducted in groups of researchers since analyses of single persons can be biased or incomplete (Meyer, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR56)). Abductive analyses should be conducted until a ‘theoretical saturation’ can be assumed, meaning that no new assumptions can be made when revising and analyzing the data set (Glaser & Strauss, [1967](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR27); Schütte et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR74)).

Such a method utilizing a careful, abductive, and turn-by-turn analysis in groups of researchers until the emergence of theoretical saturation is the ‘Interaction analysis’, a method being based on Ethnomethodology, Objective Hermeneutics, and Symbolic Interactionism (Krummheuer & Naujok, [1999](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR52); Meyer, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR56); Schütte et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR74); Kuzu, [2023b](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR46)). It is a method that emphasizes a turn-by-turn analysis of utterances with the objective of generating multiple hypothesis due to the *interpretative* nature of human interaction data, meaning two things: (a) Assumptions cannot be regarded as axiomatic or objective reconstructions of ‘truths’ but rather probable hypothetical inferences and possible explanations and (b) knowledge is constituted and constructed by social interaction (Meyer, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR56); Steinbring, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR81); Schütte et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR74); Vergnaud, [2009](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR84)). The goal is to reconstruct explanation hypotheses that are at the same time so-called ‘local theories’, meaning learning- and interaction-related, subject-specific insights into processes, aspects, and facets of how individuals construct meaning interindividually with regard to specific objects or phenomena (Brandt & Krummheuer, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR8); Cobb & Bauersfeld, [1995](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR15); DiSessa & Cobb, [2004](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR20); Gravemeijer & Cobb, [2006](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR30); Kuzu & Ratzke, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR51)) (Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Fig2)).

**Fig. 2**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8/figures/2)

A schematic illustration of the interpretative Interaction analysis (Kuzu & Ratzke, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR51))

In Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Fig2), the interpretative Interaction analysis is depicted in a schematic way: The meticulous process of generating multiple hypotheses per turn – turns meaning *contributions per person* (Clift et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR14)) – and choosing ‘Deutungshypothesen’ from the plurality of possible hypotheses follows a strictly turn-by-turn analysis process in groups of researchers, where Turn A, B, C, D… are analyzed with regard to (a) the social construction process of meaning (Burr, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR11)) and (b) the interrelation of the turns with the key questions “Why that now” and “What’s next?” (Clift et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR14); Kuzu, [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR45); Kuzu & Ratzke, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR51); Schütte et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR74)). The formulation of hypotheses is *abductive* in a Peircean sense, meaning that hypotheses are “*assumptions that emerge from within the data and help the observers/interpreters in arranging and ordering the observed phenomena”* (Kuzu & Ratzke, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR51), p. 233–234; see also Meyer, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR56); Paavola, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR63)). On the abductive level (see Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Fig2)), multiple possible hypotheses are collected and – if possible – the ‘Deutungshypothesen’ are marked (‘Ground/abduction’ level in Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Fig2)). In a further step, the ‘Deutungshypothesen’ are explained by recurring to interdisciplinary theoretical aspects from educational, sociological and psychological research (‘Theorisation level’ in Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Fig2)), which is why interpretative research requires thorough research, an analytical thinking beyond subject boundaries as well as a careful synthetization of interdisciplinary facets (Rosenthal, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR70); Schütte et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR74)). For ensuring that the abductions are no ‘singularities’ with regard to the whole data set and to reflect upon occurring differences in-between groups and cases, a comparison of the abductions in different comparison groups is necessary (‘Comparison level’ in Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Fig2); see also Brandt & Krummheuer, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR8); Brandt & Schütte, [2010](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR9)) with the intention *“to depict the specificity of* […] *segment*[s] *of reality* […] *in their relation to each other.”* (Schütte et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR74), p. 125). In this study, the *criteria of analytical depth and viability* (see Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Fig2)) were met by conducting interpretations in groups of researchers and experts, including experts in AI usage in educational contexts, multilingualism, and mathematics education (in groups of 4–5 experts). Thus, ‘intersubjective plausibility’ was ensured through collective abductions and discussions about their viability. Furthermore, these group interpretations allowed for the development and discussion of multiple hypotheses that extended beyond the subjective assumptions of the individual researcher (Rosenthal, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR70)). Empirical grounding was maintained through the analytical necessity of linking assumptions and hypotheses to specific utterances or utterance parts – an important ‘rule’ within the group interpretations. These sessions were conducted systematically, at least every two weeks. A three-step analysis was conducted according to the ’Interaction analysis’ method (Kuzu & Ratzke, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR51)):

1. 1.
   
   Interpretative analysis and theorisation of interactions between ChatGPT and Teachers (all groups).
2. 2.
   
   Interpretative analysis and theorisation of reflection processes in post-interaction discussions (all groups).
3. 3.
   
   Comparison and analysis of the differences between the comparison groups (see Sect. [2.3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec5)) (all groups).

The analyses in Sect. [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec10) will illustrate the ground-/abduction-level as well as theorisation level insights, thus the process of generating multiple hypothesis, choosing the most probable explanation hypotheses and explaining possible reasons by recurring to further theoretical aspects, but they are only a (small) section from the in-depth analysis of the whole data set (see Sect. [3.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec9)). Due to space constraints, this article will mainly present the in-depth analyses (step 1 and 2). More detailed analyses on the comparison level are discussed in further publications (e.g., in Kuzu, [2024a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR49), [b](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR50)).

### 3.2 Sample and research context of the study

In this study, Chat GPT 3.5 was used and *n* = 17 prospective teachers participated by interacting with ChatGPT 3.5 and discussing the experiences with the facilitator afterwards. It was conducted in the context of university courses and professional development programs. The teachers had to generate a task with the help of ChatGPT which they would classify as ‘viable basis for a usage in school or for further adaptions’ by prompting as much as they wished but the task had to include at least three complexity attributes (see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6)):

1. I.
   
   The Operative Principle,
2. II.
   
   the Geoboard,
3. III.
   
   an activation of multilingual resources (with a free choice of languages).

In total, 739 turns of AI-human interaction data (in the form of chat turns) were gathered, and 16 min of post-interaction discussion data were transcribed and analyzed. The data were collected in the form of chat histories (differentiable and anonymized for each participant) as well as in the form of audiographed and transcribed discussion data (16 min in total). Thus, interaction data in two forms were collected: As interaction processes with AI and in the form of group discussions after the AI-prompting phase (where the participants’ interaction processes were audio-graphed). The transcription followed the transcription guidelines of interpretative Interaction analysis (Brandt & Krummheuer, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR8)). Both variants of interaction data were analyzed according to the interpretative analysis framework (see Sect. [3.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec8)).

The participating teacher groups were differentiated and compared according to the conduction of a prior subject-matter training (regarding the operative principle, geoboard & multilingual mathematics education) and prompt engineering training by the facilitator. In total, there were four proband groups (see Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Tab1)).

The training of the teachers in subject-matter (block 1) was based on the insights from Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6) and was conducted by an experienced tertiary level facilitator (see Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Fig1) for an exemplary slide). It consisted of 90 min. of subject-matter training with regard to the Operative Principle, the Geoboard, and the activation of multilingual resources in the context of mathematical learning processes (see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6)). The prompt engineering training (block 2) was conducted by the same facilitator and was mainly based on the impulses from Ray ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR69)) (see Sect. [2.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec4)). It also consisted of 90 min. and for each prompt engineering facet according to Ray ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR69)), prompting examples were discussed and tested by using vignettes of interactions with ChatGPT. Within these 90 min. prompt engineering training, there was also a free-prompting activity for 15 min, where the teachers could freely prompt and interact with ChatGPT (e.g., for getting lesson planning impulses). In total, both training blocks were realized in a 90 min. course session each and comprised a total of 180 min. All participants were prospective teachers in an early stage of their education (20–25 years old) to ensure that they did not have too much professional pre-knowledge regarding the subject matter as well as prompt engineering. They were studying primary school education with the goal of becoming primary school teachers after completing their studies. There were *n* = 5 male participants and *n* = 12 female participants, with at least one male student in each comparison group. The lower number of male students is due to the fact that in Germany, relatively few males study primary school education, resulting in up to 80–90% female teachers in primary schools. According to their self-reports, the participants had not received any prior prompt engineering training and had little to no experience in using ChatGPT.

Since this study is a qualitative-explorative study, the relatively small sample size of *n* = 17 is adequate due to the extensive and abductive analyses being conducted (Schütte et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR74)). It included a complex transcription process, an extensive and turn-by-turn analysis of interaction processes and a theorisation as well as comparation step after the abductive analyses (see Sect. [3.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec8)). The sampling strategy followed the principles of theoretical sampling (Glaser & Strauss, [1967](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR27)): Initially, the researchers identified a desideratum where further research was necessary, specifically the AI usage of teachers for developing complex tasks. This identification was partly based on theoretical analysis, prior studies on AI usage by teachers, and feedback data from Professional Development programs. Subsequently, an appropriate study design was developed, as described in this section: comparing the prompting processes of (prospective) teachers in multiple groups (see Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Tab1)). Prospective teachers in the early phase of their studies were chosen as subjects to ensure minimal pre-knowledge in both subject-matter and prompt engineering. This lack of prior knowledge was further confirmed through survey data. In contrast, in-service teachers possess significantly more subject-matter knowledge, making their prompting processes less controlled and differentiable. Additionally, the developed subject-matter module could not ensure a first-contact with the topic for in-service teachers, and the control group might have had too much pre-knowledge for a viable comparison. The aim of the analyses was to achieve theoretical saturation for the data-based representation of central theoretical dimensions, meaning an analysis of the data until no new hypotheses could be abducted (Schütte et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR74); Kuzu, [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR45)).

4 Empirical insights: teachers prompting processes in comparison
----------------------------------------------------------------

In Sect. [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec10), teacher interactions with ChatGPT will be analyzed according to the research question of this article (see Sect. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec1)). In Sect. [4.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec11) and [4.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec12), the knowledge forms content knowledge (CK), pedagogical knowledge (PK), pedagogical content knowledge (PCK) and digitality-related/technological knowledge (DK/TK) will be focused in the interaction sequences with ChatGPT and will be interpreted relatively, meaning in comparison between the teachers analyzed qualitatively in this article. ### 4.1 Prompting processes of a teacher who is not trained in subject-matter and prompting

In this short section, Teacher 9, who is a teacher who was not trained in subject-matter and in prompting, interactions with ChatGPT (he had the same task as all teachers: Generate a task with the help of ChatGPT that fits to a) the ‘Operative Principle’, b) the ‘Geoboard’ and c) to the activation of multilingual resources.

| 1 | Teacher 9 | Please provide a task with regard to the operative principle and the geoboard that is child-friendly and can address multilingualism, even for children who do not speak another language. |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 2 | ChatGPT | Invent your own fantasy figure using the geoboard by combining different shapes. Then, draw a picture of your figure and explain why you chose specific shapes. Use words in your language and consider how you would describe the shapes’ attributes in another language, even if you don’t know the language yet. The goal is to be creative and imagine how others might describe the character! |
| 3 | Teacher 9 | Okay, thank you. |

The sequence starts with teacher 9 giving ChatGPT the instruction to generate a ‘child-friendly’ and ‘multilingual’ task with regard to *“the operative principle and the geoboard”* in **turn 1**. At first glance, it is a specific task that includes the necessary parameters (‘operative principle’, ‘geoboard’ and ‘multilingualism’). It includes multilingualism *(“that can address multilingualism”*) as well as non-multilingual students *(“even for children who do not speak another language”*). In his prompt, teacher 9 seems to ‘copy’ the given parameters into a prompt, which then prompts ChatGPT to answer with a task in **turn 2** that seems to fulfil the criteria, but a detailed analysis indicates that the task is not viable: ‘Fantasy figures’ is an ambivalent term that does not allow identifying which geometrical figure was meant and the ‘combination of different shapes’ does not make sense either since normally, single shapes like ‘triangles’ or ‘squares’ are attached with rubber bands on the nails of the geoboard (at least on the first step; in consequent steps, more figures might be attached, but ChatGPT does not mention that explicitly). Interestingly, ChatGPT then states that a picture of the figure shall be drawn which fits well to a didactic approach: Attaching figures to the Geoboard often is a first step, in a second step students often have to draw pictures and iconic representations of the figures (see Mudaly & Sibiya, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR59); Sibiya, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR77)). ChatGPT seems to have known about this didactic sequence of steps, which makes sense, but then, a nonsensical text is generated: It states to *“use words in your language* […] *even if you don’t know the language yet”* (see turn 2). A student cannot use words in a language he or she does not know yet, but it seems that ChatGPT did not realize that or that it aligned and oriented itself towards the phrase *“even for children who do not speak another language”* of teacher 9 (see Sect. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec1)), which it could have interpreted as a prompt to generate the nonsensical task. The phrase of teacher 9 is ambivalent insofar as that the part *“even for children who do not speak another language”* in turn 1 could refer to the task – which then would mean a task for multilinguals as well as monolinguals – or the multilingualism – which then would mean that even monolingual children should try to speak a language they cannot speak – and it seems that ChatGPT interpreted or rather calculated the latter. Thus, from a linguistic viewpoint, especially the structure of phrases and references seems to be challenging and should also be considered in prompting: Not only the content of the prompt but also the minimized ambiguity through a simple(r) sentence structure seems important for viable answers. In **turn 3** then, teacher 9 accepts the task generation of ChatGPT without opposition. It seems that the task layout – the question form, probably its clearness and structure – has convinced teacher 9 so that at least PK competencies can be inferred: Teacher 9 seems to know how a task should look like externally. With regard to the digitality-related/technological knowledge forms, at least a minimal knowledge about prompting seems assumable: Teacher 9 knows how to prompt in the interaction with ChatGPT but reprompting processes are not visible so that a basic DK/TK knowledge is inferable but not yet an in-depth knowledge about prompting-and-reprompting-processes or -cylces (see Sect. [2.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec4)).

### 4.2 Prompting processes of a teacher who is trained in subject-matter and prompting

In the next sequence, a teacher who is trained in subject-matter and prompt engineering (see Sect. [3.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec9)) is interacting with ChatGPT. The interaction is longer than in Sect. [4.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec11) and will be split up into smaller segments for analysis.

| 1 | Teacher 2 | Pose me a task in accordance with the operative principle related to triangles on the geoboard. |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 2 | ChatGPT | Task: Construct an equilateral triangle on the geoboard.  Operative Principle: Use rubber bands as the sides of the triangle, stretching them in a way that all three sides have the same length. Ensure that the corners of the triangle lie on the vertices of the geoboard.  Note: You can simply stretch the rubber bands over the corners of the triangle, making sure they are taut to ensure equilaterality. Good luck! |
| 3 | Teacher 2 | But that is not yet in accordance with the operative principle, is it? |
| 4 | ChatGPT | Apology for the misunderstanding. Let’s modify the task in accordance with the Operative Principle of Constructive Learning:  Operative Principle: Construct an equilateral triangle on the geoboard using only rubber bands as tools, exploring the geometric properties of the triangle independently.  Note: Start by attaching a rubber band to one corner of the geoboard and stretch it to form a line. Then, experiment with other rubber bands to create the remaining two sides of the triangle. Ensure that the side lengths are equal, and the corners of the triangle align with the vertices of the geoboard. Through independent exploration, you will constructively discover the equilateral triangle. |

The interaction starts in **turn 1** with teacher 2 who prompts a sentence which includes the aspect ‘operative principle’ as well as ‘the geoboard’. Thus, the third aspect, ‘multilingualism’, is left out, which might be because the teacher could have forgotten it, or the teacher could have intended to start on a lower complexity level and to add the missing aspect later, which could be indicating that the teacher assumes or knows that the prompting will be more successful if it is done stepwise (DK/TK). In **turn 2** then, ChatGPT gives a task that fits well to the geoboard and the instruction itself is highly precise: The learners shall use the rubber bands, *“stretching them in a way that all three sides have the same length”* (see turn 2). The object to be constructed is an equilateral triangle, thus the instruction of ChatGPT is viable. It even points out that learners should be *“making sure they are taut to ensure equilaterally”* (see turn 2), which is an important facet: Worn-out rubber bands can lead to the problem that learners cannot stretch and construct equilateral triangles. The answer of ChatGPT is very detailed with regard to the material ‘geoboard’ and the facets a teacher should watch out for but with regard to the ‘operative principle’, it becomes obvious that the task is not ‘operative’ at all: The ‘object’ is described in the task, which is a good starting point, but the systematic ‘operations’ on the object are missing as well as the description and analysis of the ‘effects’ (see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6)). The teacher seems to realize the latter too and asks in **turn 3** a question, which seems to be a rhetorical question, since it includes the hint that the task is not in accordance with the ‘operative principle’ and finishes with a yes/no question to which the answer is “no” (since the task is not in accordance with the ‘operative principle’). Here, the teacher seems to know about the pedagogical content knowledge (PCK) insofar as that she can identify missing conceptual aspects of the ‘operative principle’ (see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6)). The fact that the teacher asks such a rhetorical question as a prompt is interesting because it shows how the teacher interacts with ChatGPT as if it were a human: The prompt is not mechanical or clear, a rhetorical question rather is a stylistic device which is commonly used in human interactions (see Neitsch & Niebuhr, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR61)). Thus, she indicates DK/TK-competencies but with a more human-like interaction approach.

In **turn 4** then, ChatGPT responds by apologizing for the misunderstanding and clarifies his intention of correction by stating that it will adapt the task according to the ‘operative principle’. What is more, it also states it adapts according to the ‘operative principle’ *“of Constructive Learning”* (see turn 4), a viable and important addendum since the ‘operative principle’ is part of the constructive learning (see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6)). Here it seems that ChatGPT tries to find out or state the main category under which the term ‘operative principle’ falls, and in either case it is a viable attribution. The task itself then is changed if compared to the task in turn 2: Now, it includes the addition *“exploring the geometric properties of the triangle”* (see turn 4) which fits to the ‘operative principle’ since it is an explicit request for investigating or examining the ‘effect’ on the object. What is still missing is a part in the task which refers to ‘operations’ since the exploration of ‘effects’ cannot be conducted without adequate ‘operations’ (see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6)). An adequate ‘operation’ would be, e.g., to span a corner of the triangle systematically by shifting it one unit to the right, left, top etc.

After this initial sequence, the interaction between teacher 2 and ChatGPT goes on as follows:

| 5 | Teacher 2 | But in terms of the operative principle, one would have to perform repeated/specific operations again, such as systematically altering a corner or side. Can you please adjust that? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 6 | ChatGPT | Certainly, please accept my apologies once again for any misunderstanding. Here is a revised task in line with the operative principle:  Operative Principle: Construct step by step an equilateral triangle on the geoboard by performing repeated, specific operations. Start with a point as one corner of the triangle and systematically apply operations to equalize the side lengths and achieve equilaterality.  Note: For instance, you could begin with a rubber band at one corner and then gradually adjust the positions of the other two corners to balance the side lengths. Repeat these operations until you have constructed an equilateral triangle. Document your steps and considerations throughout.  This task requires active experimentation and systematic adjustments to attain the desired geometric shape. |
| 7 | Teacher 2 | Alright, that’s not quite there yet. For example, one could continuously move a corner around a point/nail and examine the effects of this operation on the objects (i.e., the triangle). Can you please adjust the task accordingly? |
| 8 | ChatGPT | Certainly, I understand. Here is the adjusted task in accordance with the operative principle:  Operative Principle: Incrementally construct an equilateral triangle on the geoboard by performing repeated and specific operations. Start with a rubber band at one corner as the starting point. Systematically move this corner by rotating it around a point/nail and examine the effects on the side lengths and angles of the triangle.  Note: Begin with one corner of the triangle and shift it around a point/nail on the geoboard. Observe how the shape of the triangle changes. Experiment to adjust the other two corners in a way that makes the triangle equilateral. Repeat these operations systematically until you have constructed an equilateral triangle. Document your steps and considerations throughout the process.  This task requires a detailed analysis of the effects of each operation on the geometric properties of the triangle, promoting operative learning. |

In **turn 5**, teacher 2 seems to realize that the ‘operations’ are still missing in the task since he or she explicitly refers to those (*“to perform repeated/specific operations”*, see turn 5), which again indicates that she seems to know about the important facets of the ‘operative principle’ (PCK competence). In comparison to the interaction in Sect. [4.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec11), it is noticeable how teacher 2 re-prompts repeatedly, indicating that a) he or she is not yet content with the task from a subject-matter perspective and b) he or she seems to use prompt engineering techniques, especially the aspect “Clear and specific prompts” and “iterative prompting” (Ray, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR69); Sect. [2.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec4)), thus it is a deeper form of digitality-related/technological knowledge (DK/TK competence) that includes successive and specific re-prompting. In **turn 6**, ChatGPT then again apologizes and changes the task a second time but this time the task becomes nonsensical again as in Sect. [4.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec11): ChatGPT seems to use the phrase*“to perform repeated/specific operation”* of teacher 2 from turn 5 and uses it as an instruction for the construction of the equilateral triangle, where one with *“a point as one corner of the triangle on the geoboard by performing repeated*, *specific operations”* (turn 6). The problem with this instruction is twofold: First, it is not an ‘operation’ on the ‘object’, which would be the equilateral triangle (here, it rather consists of the production process of the ‘object’ itself, it is a construction description and not the analysis/focusing of an ‘operation’ on the ‘object’) and second, the instruction “repeated, specific operations” is highly vague and implicit since it does not become clear *which* operations have to be conducted exactly but to know that is crucial since it is a key aspect for understanding the ‘effects’. In **turn 7**, teacher 2 again re-prompts but evaluates the answer of ChatGPT first (*“that’s not quite there yet”*, turn 7), which again indicates that teacher 2 has a relatively high DK/TK competence level (if compared to teacher 9 from Sect. [4.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec11)). For re-prompting, she now gives an example – which she did in turn 5 too, but less explicitly – by stating that it could be possible to *“continuously move a corner around a point/nail”*, which is a very clear and specific ‘operative’ impulse since it is a systematic change/operation which is conducted on the object (here: the triangle). Moreover, she also states that this now makes the task operative by typing that a learner should *“examine the effects of this operation on the objects (i.e.*, *the triangle)”*. This response of the teacher shows that he or she seems to have a clear expectation of what the task should look like, which indicates to the subject-matter training the teacher had before the interaction with ChatGPT and thus, again, a higher level of PCK competence becomes assumable. In **turn 8** then, ChatGPT adapts the task a third time but conserves the ‘core’ of the task formulation in turn 6 by giving instructions on how to construct an equilateral triangle (the ‘object’ itself) again instead of doing the ‘operations’ on the ‘object’ teacher 2 suggested in turn 7. The interaction then goes on as follows:

In **turn 9**, teacher 2 asks a question which indicates that she tries a different ‘strategy’: She asks a fundamental question with regard to the ‘operative principle’ and this could be (a) because she assumes that ChatGPT did not understand what she meant by the notion of ‘operative principle’ or (b) she might be content with the last task generation and now might intend to go on in another direction, which in both cases would include a DK/TK-related knowledge and a PCK-related knowledge. ChatGPT answers in **turn 10** that it ‘thinks’ that there might have been a confusion: It states that there is no Wittmann in the context of ‘Operative Principle’ (which is not true) and then refers to the next probability, which is Ludwig Wittmann (a mathematician). Interestingly, ChatGPT also states that with regard to the ‘Operative Principle’, it used the notion *“educational method of Operative Learning”* (see turn 10). This hint by ChatGPT indicates that it might have ‘thought’ of the Piagetian and Aeblian and thus more general variant or pre-form of the ‘Operative Principle’, which also operates under the name ‘Operative Learning’ or ‘Operative Phase of Learning’ (see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6)) and which might be a reason for its non- or only partially-viable task generation until turn 10 (since the operationalization of the ‘operative principle’ into ‘object’, ‘operation’ and ‘effect’ comes up with Wittmann). This could also be an explanation for the persistence and minimal modification in turn 8.

In **turn 11** then, teacher 2 immediately corrects the misunderstanding by stating that Erich Wittmann was meant, not Ludwig Wittmann, and this clarification also refers to a specific version of the ‘operative principle’ (the Wittmann’ian version, see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6)), thus again a specific PCK-related process can be abducted. It seems that teacher 2 hopes that this might help ChatGPT in understanding the misunderstanding and in adapting the task more precisely now. With regard to prompt engineering techniques, what happens here is that he or she now uses the technique “Provision of context and background information” since she states the relevant persona (see Sect. [2.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec4)), thus DK/TK becomes assumable. The interaction goes on in **turn 12** with a surprising turn: ChatGPT now states that Erich Wittman exists and describes his notion of the ‘operative principle’ precisely. Its explanation of the persona and the ‘operative principle’ is highly viable if compared with Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6): All elements of the ‘operative principle’, including the ‘objects’, ‘operations’ and ‘effects’ are included and even the constructivist emphasis on exploration processes is mentioned. Here, it seems that ChatGPT ‘lied’ in turn 10 by stating that there is no mentioning of ‘operative principle’ in the context of Wittmann, but that is not really a ‘lie’ since in fact, if Ludwig Wittmann is the reference point for the ‘operative principle’, the statement is true: There are now works of the mathematician *Ludwig* Wittmann regarding the ‘operative principle’. An important correction thus was to be to write ChatGPT that *Erich* Wittmann was meant, which then led to the highly viable, precise and complete answer in turn 12. Here, it becomes visible that ChatGPT had a reference-mismatch in its calculation, which had to be solved by teacher 2 through precise re-promting by giving a) clear and specific prompts and b) more context information.

**Turn 13** then is a prompt by teacher 2 to adapt the original task – he or she seems to assume that this will now change the task (DK/TK) – and **turn 14** then shows what happens if ChatGPT is prompted in such a way: As expected by teacher 2, it now changes the developed task radically (and seems to be ‘convinced’ that such a radical change is necessary) by first giving a brief description of what the task is about (according Erich Wittmann) and then writing the text of the task. The task now is fully viable in terms of the ‘operative principle’ since it consists of an ‘object’ (the starting triangle), an ‘operation’ (the systematic movement of one corner/vertex) and an analysis with regard to the ‘effect’ (*“analyze how the shape of the triangle chances”*, see Sect. 14). This might be considered the core element of the task and ChatGPT goes on by giving further instructions that deepen the exploration process: After systematically changing the vertex, the learners shall also try to make a new equilateral triangle (*“Continue experimenting to adjust the other two corners”*). With regard to the content-and-language-sensitiveness, it is remarkable that ChatGPT adapts his language usage between his meta-description and the task: In the description he writes of, e.g., ‘vertexes’, in the task he seemingly adapts it to assumed student-language by writing ‘corners’ instead of ‘vertexes’. This shows that it seems to have a sensitivity with regard to language registers and register switching processes (Wessel, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR86); Kuzu, [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR45)). In the last part of the task, ChatGPT again switches to the meta-level and comments why it is a good task, which it also did in the prior task developments, but this time it is a viable commentary on the quality of the task: The students shall.

In **turn 15** then teacher 2 seems content since she states *“Now it fits perfectly*, *great!”*, an utterance which indicates relief, which also becomes visible in the following last part of the utterance, where teacher 2 thanks ChatGPT. In **turn 16**, ChatGPT reacts in a friendly and standardized way to the gratefulness of teacher 2. As a last step, in **turn 17**, teacher 2 now demands a multilingual version of the task (a German-Turkish version), which is a specific form of PCK due to the interrelation between multilingualism and subject-matter-teaching processes (see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6)), and in **turn 18**, ChatGPT offers a translation into Turkish for a usage in multilingual learning environments, but it is still framed monolingually, meaning an additive translation into each language (see Sect. [2.4;](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6) Kuzu, [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR45)). In this sequence, a slight change in the prompting – by adding ‘universal design for learning’ (UDL) in **turn 25**, which is now a more PK as well as PCK related prompt since ‘Universal Design for Learning’ is a pedagogical-psychological framework with a strong interdisciplinary focus on learning-process (Böttinger et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR7)) as well as a part of the prompting-related knowledge or strategy of teacher 2 (DK/TK) – changes the answer that ChatGPT gives in **turn 26** radically. It not only changes the length of the answer but also the implementation of multilingualism: It is not additively translated but interwoven with the six core-principles of the Universal-Design-for-Learning, which ChatGPT lists correctly and combines with possibilities of implementing multilingualism. A detailed analysis shows that not all aspects are fully viable or directly usable – like the highly general and abstract listing of UDL-facets like *“Multiple Means of Information Processing and Output”* (see turn 26) – but the implementation of ‘multilingualism’ is now not only much more complex, ChatGPT lists eight possible implementation possibilities in turn 26:

* *“multilingual support for German- and Turkish-speaking learners”*.
* *“Multilingual explanations of basic concepts in German and Turkish”*.
* *“Links to additional materials in both languages”*.
* *“glossary with key terms in German and Turkish”*.
* *“Language Selection: Clear language options (German*, *Turkish)”*.
* *“customizable subtitles in both languages for videos”*.
* *“Translation Assistance: Option for students to ask questions in their preferred language”*.
* *“moderators proficient in both German and Turkish to assist in communication (team-teaching)”*.

These eight possibilities range from viable possibilities to less viable possibilities. For example, the explanation of concepts in both languages is crucial in a translanguaging-context since different notions and interpretations of concepts between languages can be expected (see Kuzu, [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR45)) and the usage of a multilingual glossary as well as a clear language usage, subtitles for videos etc. are useful ideas but in the school-context, normally a teacher cannot react adaptively on all language switches of the students. ChatGPT seems to assume that a teacher will be as proficient as ChatGPT itself in changing and adaptively reacting to languages, or it only refers to the specific example (German-Turkish) by assuming that the teacher will also be proficient in this language-combination. What still is possible is the fact that the teacher could, if an adaptive reaction is necessary, use ChatGPT again for translations (even in school-contexts). Thus, although the answer of ChatGPT in turn 26 is not fully non-additive, it seems that ChatGPT not only is *“proficient in multiple languages”* (Ray, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR69), p. 126) but also proficient in giving ideas in how to implement and activate multilingual learners’ linguistic resources (see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6); Horbach et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR36)). This is surprising since it is a highly complicated topic which a ‘normal teacher’ could not analyze and implement so simply, especially in languages he or she does not speak. ChatGPT seems to be able to do so (see turn 18) and can also plan or describe how to use different languages (see turn 26) and here, it clearly shows a potential where in future, much more research and practice-oriented usage will be necessary since at least 50% of all humans speak more than one language, multilingualism is a widespread phenomenon in all countries of the world (Bialystock et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR5)).

The sequence begins with the facilitator, in **turn 5**, inquiring about the participants’ experiences with the AI interaction process after saying that he will start with the audiography. He or she asks a multitude of questions, and the questions are slightly contrary and accumulated: He or she first asks if there were ‘hurdles’, what ‘went well’ with regard to the ‘small generations’ – indicating an evaluation of the process – , then asks if the generations were ‘good’ or ‘bad’ – indicating an evaluation of the product – and at last asks how it went with the ‘prompts’, which is a very specific part of the process. In **turn 6**, teacher 4 reports of *“technical term issues”* (see turn 6) in a retrospective reflection, explicating that *“ChatGPT didn’t quite understand them”*. It is not clear, which ‘technical terms’ are mentioned but with regard to the task the teachers had, it is assumable that either all of the three key notions ‘operative principle’, ‘geoboard’ and ‘multilingualism’, only a part of these key notions or other technical terms are meant. The facilitator then asks in **turn 7** if it was possible to *“correct it up afterwards”*, indicating or asking if she could re-prompt, or rather if it was possible (see turn 7), and teacher 4 then states in **turn 8**, again retrospectively, that he or she could do correct or re-prompt but that *“it only works to some extent”*, implicating a complex prompting-process. Then, after this short retrospective reflection, a prospective reflection is added since he or she also states what she believes would have been necessary for a more successful correcting/re-prompting process in the future: More time (see turn 7). Her statement probably refers to the activity of prompting itself, not to the course session duration, which only spans over 15 minutes (see Sect. [3.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec9).).

**Turn 9** then goes on with the facilitator asking for further impressions. Teacher 1 then describes in **turn 10**, how he or she would evaluate the interaction as productive, especially since retrospectively, ChatGPT could do something he or she would not have been able to do without the help of AI: Translating into other languages and activating multilingual resources. He or she then states with his or her own capabilities now or in the future, thus prospectively, that *“this multilingual aspect* […] *I wouldn’t know how to properly introduce that in the case of the geoboard. I mean*, *things like square or such in other languages are quite challenging”* (see turn 10), which indicates that it is not just about translations but translations of subject-matter related terms. Not only potentials but also obstacles are described too in turn 10: Teacher 1 describes how ChatGPT *“even came up with a lesson plan for an entire hour. However*, *relating that to just one task was kind of difficult”* (see turn 10), which indicates that he or she might see a potential in the versatility but also an obstacle in the diffuseness of the interaction processes. Teacher 1 then states prospectively that he or she *“don’t think I would use it as is. So*, *there are good impulses*, *and I believe that lesson planning wasn’t that bad*, *but focusing on just one specific task is challenging. But as a stimulus for further thought*, *it was helpful”.* This last part of the reflection of teacher 1 gives important insights since it is a summary or rather a subjective conclusion with regard to the usefulness of ChatGPT: The task-generations are not valuated as being directly usable, so an interaction with AI does not lead to a blind substitution of teacher-led task-generation and lesson-planning, but it gives good impulses to work on it, to adapt it, to implement it etc. (see turn 10). In **turn 11** then, the facilitator asks if teacher 1 could have been able to develop the task with the three parameters (probably referring to the ‘operative principle’, the ‘geoboard’ and ‘multilingualism’) quickly and in **turn 12**, teacher 1 adds that she would not have been able to do so quickly, especially *“because there were these three different elements involved*, *the multilingual aspect*, *the operative principle*, *and then the*, *the fact that it should be done on the geoboard”* (see turn 12), indicating that the high task-complexity was an important factor in the positive valuation of the interaction with the AI. Teacher 1 then goes on and adds *“I believe if it hadn’t been the geoboard but something else with numbers or so*, *I might have come up with something regarding language more easily”* (see turn 12), which confirms the prior hypothesis: The high complexity, not only because of the ‘operative principle’ and the ‘multilingualism’ but also because of the didactical material, here the ‘geoboard’, is mentioned as being problematic if he or she wanted to develop an own task, and using ‘numbers’ instead of the ‘geoboard’ can be assumed as an example for a task variant which is – at least for teacher 1 – easier or less complex. Thus, the task-complexity seems to be an important factor in the rather positive valuation of the interaction process by teacher 1: He or she does not trust herself to be able to find a complex task with all three parameters but sees the potential of ChatGPT in being able to do what he or she would have been able to do it him- or herself. This evaluation of teacher 1 is typical for the group of teachers who had subject-matter training and prompt engineering training: In the end, all teachers evaluated the interaction process as being highly useful for getting ideas and impulses. In **turn 23**, another example is given for such a positive evaluation but with a slightly different emphasis: Another subject, teacher 3, evaluates the task-generation by ChatGPT as useful especially with regard to the multilingual translations since he or she *“tested if it could translate the task and it worked. Honestly*, *I could not translate the task into all of these languages. I tested if it could translate the task into languages I understood*, *English and French*, *and it was a really good translation. Then I tested if it could translate the task into Hebrew and Romanes and at last*, *Elvish language* [a language being based on J.R.R. Tolkien’s novel “The Lord of Rings”]. *It could even translate into Elvish!”* (see turn 23). Especially her surprised statement that ChatGPT could translate into a fictional language – Elvish – shows how she was positively surprised by the capabilities of the AI. He or she did also ‘check’ the translations by letting ChatGPT translate into languages he or she understood, and the fact that teacher 3 does not criticize the translation quality indicates that the translations were all in all good and viable. This hypothesis gets confirmed by the last past of his or her utterance, where he or she states that *“in some of these languages*, *it stated that it cannot translate every technical word*, *like ‘geoboard’ and that it instead circumscribed the notion”* (see turn 23). Circumscribing non-translatable words is a key-competence in the context of translanguaging and the activation of multilingual resources in subject-matter contexts (Kuzu, [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR45)), so it is not surprising that ChatGPT ‘realizes’ that too; interestingly, this observation shows how the AI-logic is analogous to ‘human considerations’ in the context of multilingualism, which is a facet that has to be examined deeper in further studies.

5 Discussion and conclusions
----------------------------

The analyses in Sect. [4.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec11) and [4.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec12) as well as the post-interaction discussion in Sect. [4.3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec13) indicate specific interaction-patterns: Teachers, who are more proficient in the subject-matter and who are trained in prompting (prompt engineering training), have shown considerably more viable interaction processes with AI, meaning processes that led to task-generations that met the requirements from a subject-matter perspective (see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6)) with a high amount of PCK and DK/TK reconstructions (see Sect. [4.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec12)), whereas in the teacher-group not being trained in subject-matter as well as in prompt engineering, only pedagogical-psychological knowledge (PK) and fewer digitality-/technology-related knowledge (DK/TK) could be reconstructed, with a low occurrence rate and in a relatively coarse manner: Teacher 9 seemed to have basic knowledge about designing tasks in the school context (PK) as well as in prompting (DK/TK) but processes of re-prompting were missing, and no further discussion occurred in the interaction with the AI with regard to the subject-matter (see Sect. [4.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec11).). In contrast, in the teacher-group trained in subject-matter as well as in prompt engineering, a high density of pedagogical-content knowledge (PCK) as well as of technology-/digitality-related (DK/TK) knowledge could be reconstructed: The interaction with the AI was led by the teacher insofar as that the teacher seemed to have an idea how the task should look like ideally (see Sect. [4.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec12).). In a reflective discussion phase after the interaction with AI, especially the teachers from the subject-matter- as well as prompt engineering-trained group evaluated their interaction also as very fruitful, mentioning the potential of interacting with AI, especially in the context of complex task designs and multilingualism (see Sect. [4.3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec13)).

These findings could be confirmed in further analyses but due to space constraints, insights could not be given into the crossed experimental groups and will be described:

* In the group with no subject-matter training but prompt engineering training, a slightly higher amount of prompting could be reconstructed (thus DK/TK knowledge) but it was less conducive: Nearly all task-generations were non- or only partially viable in terms of the subject-matter (see Sect. [2.4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec6));
* In the group with subject-matter training but no prompt engineering training, a relatively high amount of PCK-knowledge could be reconstructed and the task-generations were more viable (if compared to the latter group and the group with no training in both facets) but the re-prompting process was often ‘circular’, meaning that less precision in prompting – probably due to missing prompt engineering knowledge – led to a repetition of prompts; in comparison with the group, which had training in both facets (see Sect. [4.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec12).), the task-generations were still considerably less viable.

Summarizing the findings of this study, the in-depth qualitative interaction-analyses indicate that teachers, who had knowledge about prompting (through a prompt engineering training) and about the subject-matter (the ‘operative principle’, the ‘geoboard’ and ‘multilingualism’) did use AI in a way that it supported their task-generation process in a highly productive way (see Sect. [4.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec12)). This indicates that AI-usage in the context of teacher-education and teacher-usage is subject to specific conditions and that a specific ‘threshold’ might exist: For viable and reflected task generations, teachers seem to have prior knowledge of the subject-matter as well as of prompting, then the interactions as well as task generations of ChatGPT are ‘fruitful’ and lead to an assistance of teachers, e.g., through the development of complex tasks via precise re-prompting (see Sect. [4.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec12)), which the teachers also realize and state themselves (see Sect. [4.3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec13)), whereas non-trained teachers do not recognize non- or less-viable task generations and do use less precise prompts and less re-prompting attempts (see Sect. [4.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec11)), which in the worst case might even lead to believing that non-viable task generations are viable (see Sect. [4.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec11)) (see Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Fig3)).

In Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Fig3), a specific ‘threshold’ is described with regard to the PCK, CK, DK/TK and PK dimensions of knowledge, with an emphasis of PCK and DK/TK since the analysis in this study showed a high relevance of both dimensions in the interaction-processes that led to viable task-generations (see Sect. [4.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec12).). This means that teachers, who are more competent in the subject-matter and who had prompt engineering training might develop viable and reflected tasks, whereas teachers who are not trained in both aspects might develop non- or partially viable tasks. With regard to AI-implementation in educational contexts, especially with regard to the usage of AI by teachers for the development of tasks, this leads to two important conclusions:

1. I.
   
   AI-usage cannot replace a thorough tertiary phase since teachers have to be able to reflect upon the quality of text-generations by AI systems like ChatGPT; first, teachers should be trained in subject-matter profoundly and then, AI-usage can support their task-generation processes.
2. II.
   
   Prompt engineering training should be part of teacher-education programs, e.g., as a last or additional part after each seminar or course, and it should be trained specifically with regard to subject-matter requirements.

Concerning the potential of AI-usage, especially regarding a usage in the context of a usage for multilingual text-generations, insights could be given that there is not only a ‘linguistic proficiency’ of ChatGPT as Ray ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR69)) states but also a translanguaging- and multilingualism-proficiency but precise prompts are necessary. E.g., prompts should be careful with regard to the phrase structure, since AI might misinterpret the relations between phrases subjects use in the interaction with AI (see Sect. [4.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec12)).

From the empirical insights, practical implications of significance for educators, pre-service teachers and the broader teacher education community emerge: If the usage of AI-Systems like ChatGPT becomes more effective after having prompt engineering training as well as subject-matter training, as the interaction analyses in Sect. [4.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec12) and [4.3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec13) illustrated, then this means that in the future, educators should also seek ways to teach and use AI-systems in their subject-matter courses or lessons. Furthermore, the analyses provided insights into *when* such an AI-implementation is efficient: *After* a phase of content- and subject-matter-elaboration to ensure a viable understanding of pedagogical and content related aspects, which ensures that teachers can reflect on the quality of the AI-answers and identify non-viable answers, which was a problem in the comparison group with AI-training but no subject-matter training (see Sect. [4.1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec11)) and which was reconstructable in further analyses (Kuzu, [2024a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR49)). With regard to the school context, it is also necessary to develop and evaluate learning environments that focus on the prompt engineering competency of teachers as well as students (Kuzu, [2024b](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR50)). In addition to didactic competences for the use of artificial intelligence, teachers also need competences for the ethical evaluation of artificial intelligence. In order to record these competences, future research on teacher competences could also focus more on media pedagogical competences. For example, competences for reflection, analysis, use and design (Thyssen et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR83)) could also be given more space in future studies with regard to artificial intelligence. This could then lead to the development of new approaches to modelling teacher competencies for artificial intelligence that not only use artificial intelligence in the classroom but are also able to critically reflect on and shape the consequences of the social transformation brought about by artificial intelligence together with students.

6 Limitation and future research needs
--------------------------------------

This article gave explorative and qualitative insights into interaction processes with AI but had also specific limitations: Only GPT 3.5 instead of ChatGPT 4 was used; a quantitative analysis of bigger groups of probands is still necessary to verify the significance of the insights; further qualitative insights are necessary to gain insights into, e.g., interactions with AI in other subject-matter contexts. However, the explorative insights from this study show that in future, teachers and not only students will also have to reflect upon the potentials and usage-possibilities of AI since it might support and help them in processes like complex task-generations. If, in the future, AI systems will also be able to combine pictures, texts and further forms of media, teachers might even be able to generate whole task-sheets but, as it became visible in this study, such complex developmental processes cannot be seen as ‘automatized processes’: Interaction with AI still needs subjects that can reflect the quality of the generations, so a profound training of subjects in subject-matter as well as prompt engineering seems to be important in teacher-education.

Further suggestions for research in the future are on two intertwined levels: Research into the prompting processes and competencies of teachers as well as students, since it is important to have teachers who understand what they want to explain. This also means that teacher trainers and educators have to include learning opportunities into their seminars too, as became evident in the analyses in this article, however, here is another need for research: Teaching concepts on the tertiary level should be carefully developed and evaluated, ideally with an iterative development method, to (a) foster prospective teachers and (b) gain further insights into the effects of specific training methods, approaches etc. Besides these more qualitative research needs, there is also a need for quantitative research regarding the correlations and significances of specific interventions or teaching methods in larger groups of participants.

The empirical insights in this article add up to the existing literature by providing not only in-depth insights into prompting processes of (pre-service) teachers *for solving tasks*– where studies like those of Kortenkamp and Dohrmann ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR43)) give first insights (see Sect. [2.2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#Sec4)) – but also into subject-matter related, complex prompting processes *for developing tasks*. Such a broader perspective on prompting processes is important since in educational practice, teachers are under pressure to develop new tasks on a daily basis and the usage of AI might support their lesson development processes (and is already in usage, respectively); however, conditions and limitations of such a supporting process have to be considered profoundly under ‘realistic conditions’ (meaning that the AI-usage should be researched with regard to processes that mirror the complex reality and practices in educational contexts) and in this article, first important insights into the positive influence of a dual-sided approach to AI-training (consisting of prompt engineering training as well as subject-matter training) were given. Furthermore, recent studies highlight the potential of ChatGPT in multilingual contexts, e.g., in language teaching, emphasizing its support for EFL teachers in lesson planning, implementation, and assessment, while underscoring the need for AI literacy, prompt crafting, and digital competencies for ethical and effective use (Mena Octavio et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR55); Yang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR88); Spasic & Jankovic, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR79); Kohnke et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13673-8#ref-CR42)). Based on our qualitative analyses, following on from T-PACK/D-PACK, we emphasize the relevance of not only promoting technological or digital-related prompting competences, but also examining the interface between prompting competences and subject-specific didactic competences in more detail: Effective usage of AI is not only a question of knowing how to prompt but of assessing what is needed from a subject-related perspective, which is why we plead – based on our empirical insights – for the implementation of AI-modules into subject-didactical modules in higher education.

Ethics declarations
-------------------

### Ethical

All participants in this study were informed about the topic of the study as well as the analysis procedure. Participants whose interaction processes were analyzed in this article have given their consent for the analysis (a consent which they could withdraw from at any time without consequences); participants without a consent were not included. Furthermore, the privacy and confidentiality of interaction data were ensured via rigorous anonymization: No interaction data that could lead to the identity of the participants, such as real names, place of residence, etc., was used in the analysis.
