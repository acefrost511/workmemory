# Students as AI literate designers: a pedagogical framework for learning and teaching AI literacy in elementary education

## Abstract
Although introducing AI education into K-12 schools has attracted significant interest from educators and researchers, research into effective strategies for enhancing elementary students’ AI literacy remains in its infancy. Grounded in design-based learning, we propose a pedagogical framework, “Students as AI literate designers” (SAILD), to facilitate young learners’ acquisition of AI literacy by designing solutions for real-world problem-solving. This paper reports on our mixed-methods study evaluating the effectiveness of SAILD in Grade-5 students’ AI literacy learning and teaching in Hong Kong, regarding AI knowledge, skills, ethics, and attitudes. The quantitative findings show significant differences in students’ AI knowledge, ethics, and attitudes between pre- and post-test scores; the qualitative findings reveal students’ problem-solving processes using AI skills. While the present work provides empirical evidence supporting the adoption of SAILD in elementary AI education, it underlines the challenges of SAILD and suggests areas for further research.

## Introduction
The widespread adoption of artificial intelligence (AI) technologies in daily life has underscored the importance of AI literacy in a technology-driven future, highlighting the need to equip citizens with AI-related competencies (Eguchi et al., [Citation2021](#); Fundi et al., [Citation2024](#); Su et al., [Citation2023](#); UNESCO, [Citation2022](#)). AI education is now receiving attention at the K-12 level, as evidenced by global initiatives like China’s New Generation of Artificial Intelligence Development Plan (State Council of China, [Citation2017](#)), Australia’s Digital Technologies MOOC programs for teachers led by the Computer Science Education Research Group (CSER; Australian Government, [Citation2021](#)), and the AI4K12 Initiative in the United States sponsored by the National Science Foundation (Touretzky et al., [Citation2019](#)). For example, under its New Generation of Artificial Intelligence Development Plan (State Council of China, [Citation2017](#)), China aims to become the global leader in AI innovation by 2030, placing a strategic focus on establishing a robust pipeline of AI talent. A key initiative of this plan emphasizes a full integration of AI into China’s education system by 2025, beginning in primary and secondary schools and continuing through to universities. Similarly, in the US, the “Five Big Ideas in AI” framework advocates for introducing AI technologies as early as kindergarten through Grade-2, suggesting the importance of early exposure to AI in fostering foundational understanding. Indeed, the increasing presence of AI in everyday life exposes students to AI products more frequently, posing risks of misconceptions and overtrust in AI. This calls for the cultivation of a generation of informed and critical citizens, particularly in an AI-infused world (Dai, [Citation2024](#); Mertala et al., [Citation2022](#)). Early introduction to AI can thus help students navigate the complexities of an AI-driven society, foster their adaptability, and unlock opportunities for meaningful contributions in the digital era (Dai et al., [Citation2024](#); Kong & Abelson, [Citation2022](#); Pangrazio & Selwyn, [Citation2021](#); Touretzky et al., [Citation2019](#)).
Despite numerous efforts to embed AI education into the K-12 curriculum through national policies and innovative programs, the incorporation of AI education at the elementary level is still in its infancy, and it faces considerable challenges (Dai, [Citation2024](#)). One major challenge is to determine what to teach for K-12, especially for upper elementary students. Scholars have sought to define what AI literacy—the essential competencies K-12 students require—should entail to ensure that future generations become informed users who can engage with, communicate with, and critically evaluate AI (Long & Magerko, [Citation2020](#); Touretzky et al., [Citation2019](#)). This definition extends beyond merely recognizing AI products; it requires understanding the fundamental concepts of AI and the capability to use AI, coupled with an awareness of its societal impacts and ethical concerns. The complex nature of AI literacy poses substantial challenges for educators who need to familiarize themselves with a new subject area to effectively develop and implement relevant courses (Yue et al., [Citation2024](#)).
Another challenge is the lack of research and established pedagogical foundations for AI education at the K-12 level. Unlike traditional subjects like science and mathematics, which benefit from a long history of evidence-based development, AI education lacks comprehensive empirical studies on effective learning and teaching methods (Wang & Lester, [Citation2023](#)). While K-12 AI courses have started to appear internationally, review studies indicate that many of these courses lack a solid theoretical foundation in pedagogical design (Rizvi et al., [Citation2023](#); Yue et al., [Citation2021](#), [Citation2022](#)). Concepts such as machine learning, big data, and programming are acknowledged as challenging for through effective pedagogical strategies remain insufficient (Dinçer & Doğanay, [Citation2017](#); Ottenbreit-Leftwich et al., [Citation2023](#)). Consequently, it is crucial to develop a theory-driven pedagogical framework and establish an evidence-based foundation for AI literacy education in elementary settings (Kim & Kwon, [Citation2023](#); Yue et al., [Citation2022](#), [Citation2024](#)).
Grounded in Fortus et al. ([Citation2004](#)) theoretical foundation of design-based learning (DBL), we have proposed a pedagogical framework called “Students as AI Literate Designers” (SAILD) to integrate DBL into AI literacy education. DBL, which has been extensively used in engineering and technology education, allows students to apply specialized knowledge to develop an artifact or system, through which students are engaged with design principles inherent in engineering or technology concepts (Gómez Puente et al., [Citation2011](#)). In AI education, designing products for problem-solving requires students not only to understand and apply AI knowledge and use AI-driven tools but also to contemplate their ethical and social issues in real-world scenarios. Therefore, integrating DBL into AI education holds promising potential in addressing the multifaceted demands of AI literacy. As highlighted in UNESCO’s framework on AI literacy (2022), this study aims not only to enhance understanding and engagement with AI technologies, but also to raise awareness of ethical and societal considerations, such as risks related to data privacy, AI misuse, and decision-making bias, thereby fostering critical attitudes and a balanced perspective toward AI.
In sum, this paper has two-folded purposes. First, it is to delineate the pedagogical design of SAILD through a theory-driven approach. Second, it empirically assesses and discusses the pedagogical effectiveness of SAILD on AI literacy with the four aspects: (1) students’ acquisition of AI knowledge; (2) students’ development of AI skills; (3) students’ understanding of AI ethics; and (4) students’ attitudes toward AI.
## Literature review
### AI literacy education in K-12
#### Conceptualizing AI literacy
The scholarly discourse on AI literacy has burgeoned, with significant contributions from researchers such as Kandlhofer et al. ([Citation2016](#)), Aoun ([Citation2017](#)), and Long and Magerko ([Citation2020](#)). Notably, the definition proposed by Long and Magerko ([Citation2020](#)) has been highly influential. They define AI literacy as a set of competencies that enable individuals to critically assess AI technology, effectively engage and collaborate with AI, and proficiently utilize AI tools in diverse environments. More recently, other conceptions and disciplinary-related usages have been applied to further manifest AI literacy, such as connecting it with information literacy, technology literacy, digital literacy and data literacy (Carolus et al., [Citation2023](#); Schüller, [Citation2022](#); Stolpe & Hallström, [Citation2024](#); Wang et al., [Citation2023](#)), contextualizing it in voice-based AI systems or AI-related workplaces (Carolus et al., [Citation2023](#); Cetindamar et al., [Citation2024](#)), and featuring it with the combination of proficiency dimensions and different subject areas (Pinski & Benlian, [Citation2024](#)). There is a growing consensus among scholars that AI literacy will become as crucial as traditional literacy skills like reading and writing, advocating for its integration into education from an early age, as suggested by Kandlhofer et al. ([Citation2016](#)) and further supported by studies focusing on preschool education (Su et al., [Citation2023](#); Williams et al., [Citation2019](#)).
Beyond defining AI literacy, researchers have proposed various frameworks to operationalize this concept by identifying its key components. For instance, Wilton et al. ([Citation2022](#)) emphasizes the importance of acquiring and utilizing AI-related knowledge and skills. However, Miao and colleagues at UNESCO ([Citation2020](#)) argue that mere knowledge and skill acquisition do not constitute comprehensive AI literacy. They underscore the necessity for individuals to comprehend the implications of AI, including its potential benefits and limitations, appropriate applications, ethical considerations, and societal impacts. To address these broader considerations, Zhao et al. ([Citation2022](#)) and Ng et al. ([Citation2021](#)) have proposed multifaceted frameworks for AI literacy, incorporating aspects such as understanding, applying, evaluating AI applications, and understanding AI ethics. Similarly, Wang et al. ([Citation2023](#)) introduce an AI literacy scale encompassing awareness, use, evaluation, and ethics. Kong and Zhang ([Citation2021](#)) divide AI literacy into cognitive, affective, and sociocultural dimensions. They highlight the importance of equipping individuals with the knowledge and skills to understand and use AI effectively, as well as fostering positive attitudes toward AI, which are critical for navigating its widespread application in daily life and the workforce.
In this study, we conceptualize AI literacy through a four-dimensional framework comprising AI knowledge, AI skills, AI ethics, and AI attitudes. This framework reflects the current scholarly discourse on AI literacy and aligns with Gagne’s Theory of Instruction (Gagne, [Citation1974](#)), which categorizes learning outcomes into cognitive, psychomotor, and affective domains. Here, the cognitive domain includes factual and conceptual knowledge, the psychomotor domain focuses on practical skills and hands-on experience, and the affective domain encompasses individual attitudes and values, influencing decision-making and actions. In our framework, beyond the traditional dimensions of knowledge, skills, and attitudes, we aim to underscore the importance of ethical awareness, particularly in the context of an AI-driven world. The rapid development of AI, particularly in Generative AI, brings about benefits, opportunities, risks, and ethical challenges. Ideally, AI technology is designed to solve complex real-life problems effectively. The considerable promise of AI, as indicated by Naik et al. ([Citation2022](#)) and Williams et al. ([Citation2019](#)), raises concerns about AI potentially surpassing human intelligence. Meanwhile, AI is susceptible to inducing biases, generating imprecise outcomes, and presenting ethical quandaries, largely due to the data on which it is trained (Huang et al., [Citation2022](#)). Additionally, AI technologies like large language models (LLM) and cloud computing are associated with notable risks concerning data security and privacy protection (Kirova et al., [Citation2023](#); Sebastian, [Citation2023](#)). Consequently, individuals must acknowledge the dual effects of AI. This means that while recognizing AI’s beneficial societal contributions, it is equally crucial to critically consider its potential limitations and underlying reasons.
#### Current efforts in elementary AI literacy education
Elementary education needs to lay a foundation that encourages students to employ AI knowledge in exploring and solving problems in an age-appropriate way. According to Piaget’s ([Citation1952](#)) cognitive development theory, elementary school students are typically at the concrete operational stage and may begin transitioning to the formal operational stage. These stages underline that students learn best when they can manipulate objects and explore ideas physically, as they shift from concrete reasoning to more abstract thinking. Constructionism, which posits that learners actively construct knowledge by creating meaningful artifacts (Papert, [Citation1996](#)), aligns well with these developmental stages. It supports students’ cognitive abilities by engaging them in hands-on activities that reinforce their understanding of abstract concepts. Additionally, given that AI technologies often involve problem-solving, modeling, and simulation across various contexts, constructionism allows students to create and refine AI models to address real-world challenges (Papavlasopoulou et al., [Citation2019](#); Samuelsson, [Citation2024](#)). Through constructionism-based making activities, students can develop a profound, critical, and nuanced understanding of AI technologies. In higher education, constructionist approaches are reflected in multiple educational strategies, such as robot construction (Bellas & Sousa, [Citation2023](#)), data and comparative visualization (Wan et al., [Citation2020](#)), and training AI models (Kurtz et al., [Citation2024](#)). These core principles can be adapted for younger learners, fostering a deeper and more intuitive understanding of AI and enabling learners to actively engage with AI from an early age. By integrating these strategies, AI literacy programs can create an environment where elementary students learn about AI and apply this knowledge creatively in their learning journey.
Integrating AI literacy into the elementary curriculum is suggested to offer multiple benefits beyond the acquisition of AI-related knowledge. It has the potential to inspire students’ interest in STEM subjects (Liu et al., [Citation2022](#)), while also fostering creativity, curiosity, and critical thinking skills (Ali et al., [Citation2019](#)). There are attempts to incorporate AI literacy into elementary education. For example, from a curriculum design perspective, one of the most popular and widely accepted frameworks is the Five Big Ideas developed by the US AI4K12 project (Touretzky et al., [Citation2019](#)). The framework covers five major aspects of AI-related content (i.e. perception, representation & reasoning, learning, natural interaction, and societal impact) across different levels of K-12, including specific guidelines for the elementary level. From a pedagogical perspective, for example, researchers have adopted playful and inquiry-based approaches to promote students’ understanding of AI. These activities include creating digital stories (Ng et al., [Citation2022](#)), engaging with intelligent agents through the Turing Test (Yang, [Citation2022](#)), developing chatbots, constructing inference algorithms (Wong et al., [Citation2020](#)), and block-based programming projects (Gong et al., [Citation2018](#)). Most recently, scholars proposed a series of pioneering pedagogical designs targeted at student understanding of AI concepts and working mechanisms at upper elementary levels (Dai et al., [Citation2024](#)). However, these approaches have focused on limited dimensions of AI literacy, without addressing its comprehensive scope. To navigate the complexity of AI literacy, pedagogical strategies must be implemented that cultivate not only knowledge construction and skill development, but also ethical awareness and unbiased perspectives regarding AI, encouraging students’ creative and hands-on explorations (Park & Kwon, [Citation2023](#); Weidener & Fischer, [Citation2024](#)).
### Design-based learning (DBL)
Engineering and programming education is considered highly relevant to AI literacy education (Chiu, [Citation2021](#); Dai et al., [Citation2024](#)), where students learn to design, assemble, and program agents with sensors, actuators, and other elements to solve problems. Design-based learning (DBL) is one of the most influential approaches in engineering education (Gómez Puente et al., [Citation2011](#)). Grounded in constructionism (Harel & Papert, [Citation1991](#)), case-based reasoning (Kolodner, [Citation1994](#)), and situated cognition theories (Brown et al., [Citation1989](#)), DBL encourages students to utilize practical skills and domain-specific knowledge through project-based tasks. Consequently, the skills and knowledge gained are readily adaptable and applicable to real-world situations (de Vries, [Citation2006](#)). The design cycle includes five phases: identifying and defining context, conducting background research, developing personal and group ideas, constructing 2D and 3D artifacts, and providing feedback (Fortus et al., [Citation2004](#)). Design acts as a structured and analytical methodology in this learning process, where learners are guided to engage in inquiry, problem-solving, and self-reflection (Song et al., [Citation2016](#)).
Prior research shows that DBL provides a practical context for engaging students in knowledge integration (e.g. Fortus et al., [Citation2004](#); Kolodner et al., [Citation2003](#)), thereby facilitating their development of essential critical thinking skills, including representation, reasoning, and creative thinking (e.g. de Vries, [Citation2006](#); Kafai, [Citation1995](#), [Citation2005](#); Li, [Citation2012](#)). This facilitation occurs through the synthesis and application of content knowledge, underscoring the role of DBL in fostering higher-order cognitive skills. Kafai and her colleagues (Kafai, [Citation1995](#); Kafai et al., [Citation2012](#)) adopted a constructionist lens to aid elementary students in learning fractions through designing computer games with Logo and found student development in mathematical representations, design skills, and mathematical inquiry throughout the design process. Furthermore, Kolodner et al. ([Citation2003](#)) investigated the Learning by Design™ (LBD) curriculum in a middle school science context, which integrated case-based reasoning with problem-based learning. The tailored instructional activities aimed to enhance design-based scientific inquiry. This research revealed that students in the LBD group learned earth and physical science concepts more effectively than their peers and showed superior performance in scientific experiment design, analysis, teamwork, and metacognitive strategies. Similarly, Fortus et al. ([Citation2004](#)) examined the impact of design-based science teaching on the scientific understanding and practical problem-solving capabilities of high school students. After participating in the design-based curriculum, students from various achievement levels showed significant improvements in both areas. More recently, Li et al. ([Citation2023](#)) adopted DBL to improve elementary school students’ computational thinking by solving real-world problems using LabPlus electronic kits and Scratch programming software. The abovementioned research highlights the effectiveness of DBL in enhancing comprehensive cognitive competencies across a varied student population, affirming its value in educational practice.
However, previous research has underscored several issues that need to be addressed when formulating and applying DBL initiatives. One significant challenge is linking design activities directly to the intended domain-specific content knowledge. Often, learners may concentrate on the technical aspects of design, neglecting deeper exploration of content or the formation of structured knowledge (Kafai, [Citation1995](#); [Citation2005](#); Kolodner et al., [Citation2003](#)). This neglect can disconnect design tasks from knowledge construction. Thus, the interaction between design activities and knowledge acquisition is often mismatched, which can impede learning outcomes (Bozkurt Altan & Tan, [Citation2021](#)). Moreover, although there have been various approaches to developing DBL to facilitate learning (Delen & Sen, [Citation2023](#)), existing studies on DBL predominantly center on engineering education (e.g. Doppelt et al., [Citation2008](#); Ke, [Citation2014](#); Mehalik et al., [Citation2008](#)); leaving a gap in exploring the potential of DBL in AI education to further extend the DBL theory. Therefore, it is essential to develop a pedagogical framework grounded in DBL theory and evaluate its effectiveness in fostering elementary students’ AI literacy.
## Theory-driven design: students as AI literate designers (SAILD)
In the present study, we develop a pedagogical framework called SAILD, which integrates DBL into AI literacy education in elementary schools. Informed by the five phases of DBL proposed by Fortus et al. ([Citation2004](#)), this framework involves two interactive parts: design and research (Figure 1). The design part invites students to engage in the design cycle, which includes problem identification, design solution generation, digital artifact creation, and evaluation. The phase of background research originating from the design part is extracted as a distinct research part, parallel to the design part. In the research part, students are encouraged to engage in brainstorming, hands-on exploratory practice, critical discussion, and reflection. These two types of activities alternate in an iterative sequence. Specifically, students are first motivated to identify a real-life problem. Based on a brief introduction to domain-specific knowledge, students generate an initial design solution aimed at addressing the real-life problem. Subsequently, teachers provide guidance to support their comprehension of challenging concepts encountered during the design activities. This newly acquired knowledge enables students to refine their designs. Following this improvement, teachers introduce more advanced knowledge essential for the ongoing design iteration. Students then apply the advanced knowledge to adapt and enhance their designs. To obtain a comprehensive reflection on their design solutions, students are guided to assess their work \*via\* evaluation processes.

The iterative process inherent in SAILD possesses certain key features and benefits. First, design consistently serves as the driving force. By initiating the process with the design of a solution to a real-world problem, this approach fully leverages the advantages of DBL to enhance student engagement and motivation. Second, the subsequent research activities strive to bridge the gap between knowledge acquisition and design tasks, particularly in the context of AI. Given that AI encompasses many abstract and complex concepts, the emphasis on the research process can assist students in acquiring the necessary knowledge for their design solutions. Additionally, it provides justifications and insights that fuel the next cycle of design improvements. Finally, the iterative process seeks to maintain a balance between design and research. On one hand, students apply the learned knowledge to support their design. On the other hand, their involvement in design activities, encounters with design challenges, and attempts to reason and resolve these challenges can deepen their understanding of the underlying AI-related concepts of the design solution.
SAILD is designed to foster young students’ AI literacy. First, it emphasizes the research component, providing students with a scaffold to construct their understanding of AI while actively developing AI-based solutions. This framework effectively integrates intellectual learning with the design cycle. In the design process, students are encouraged to apply AI knowledge to create solutions for real-life problems, thereby enhancing their practical AI skills. Moreover, as students refine their design solutions, they are expected to address the potential issues associated with AI, including inaccurate decision-making, data privacy concerns, job loss to AI, and possible malicious uses of AI. This process enables students to reflect on the societal and ethical implications of AI, promoting their ethical awareness and societal responsibility—critical concerns in an increasingly AI-driven world (Ouchchy et al., [Citation2020](#)). Furthermore, students’ attitudes toward AI can be shaped by contemplating their positions on AI and negotiating with various stakeholders involved in AI-related issues. Thus, it is promising to adopt SAILD as a pedagogical framework to support students in acquiring AI literacy.
## Methodology
This study employed a mixed-methods design with the combination of both quantitative and qualitative methods to enhance the breadth and depth of our understanding of complex research issues (Creswell & Clark, [Citation2017](#)). This methodological approach is particularly valuable for dissecting intricate phenomena from various perspectives, offering a richer analysis than either qualitative or quantitative methods could achieve independently (Teddlie & Tashakkori, [Citation2011](#)).
### Participants and research context
In this study, 50 Grade-5 students (aged between 10 and 12 years) from an elementary school in Hong Kong were recruited. Research consent was obtained from all participating students, their guardians, and the school principal, regarding the research and data collection process. The guardians of these participating students were informed of responsible AI usage and privacy policies in our study. First, students’ online activities (e.g. Google Quick Draw and Google Teachable Machine) are conducted with parental consent and do not require personal accounts, record individual information, or store any personal data in AI systems. This aligns with the regulation outlined in the Children’s Online Privacy and Protection Act (COPPA, [Citation2013](#)) regarding children’s use of AI technologies. Second, the Ethical AI Framework of Hong Kong (Digital Policy Office, [Citation2024](#)) stipulates that personal data must only be used for its intended purpose and should not be retained longer than necessary, a principle that was communicated to participants. The consent form explicitly stated that collected data would only be used for research purposes and would be permanently deleted upon the study’s completion. Therefore, all data and information collected during the research would remain strictly confidential, with no risk of disclosure.
Participating students were divided into ten groups (i.e. five students per group) to partake in collaborative activities and design tasks. Group formation was based on each student’s overall academic performance, aiming to ensure a balanced distribution of ability levels within each group. All learning and teaching activities in this study, apart from the pre- and post-tests, were conducted in a group-based format (Figure 2). This approach was designed to provide students with an immersive and practical learning environment to foster collaborative learning and enhance the educational experience by encouraging students to engage collectively in discussions, problem-solving, and design tasks.

1. Day 1: Pretest. Prior to the lessons, students completed a pretest to evaluate their existing knowledge of AI and their attitudes toward AI and the subject. This assessment comprised a 30-minute knowledge test (KT) and a 15-minute self-reported questionnaire. Although the KT in the pretest consisted of content from all topics covered in the intervention, the pretest results did not influence the teaching approach or content delivery during the intervention. 2. Day 2 to Day 6: Intervention. The teaching content was guided by the Key Learning Area Curriculum Guide of Technology Education in Hong Kong (Education Bureau, [Citation2017](#)) and the Five Big Ideas in AI (Touretzky et al., [Citation2023](#)). This study specifically focused on the topics of “perception,” “learning,” and “societal impact.” The AI-based learning tools utilized in this study included Google Quick Draw and Google Teachable Machine. Over five consecutive days, the teacher implemented the lessons designed for this study. On the first day, students were given a general introduction to AI. The lesson concluded with students’ presentation on the last day. The application of SAILD in each lesson is depicted in Figure 3.
3. Day 7: Post-test. The day after the intervention ended, students were given a post-test that mirrored the structure of the pretest. While the specific questions in the multiple-choice quiz differed, the level of difficulty was maintained for consistency. The post-test was administered solely for research purposes to evaluate the effectiveness of the intervention. Students were not provided with their individual scores, as the primary aim of the post-test was not to serve as a feedback tool for them but rather to gather research data within the constraints of the available time.
4. Day 10: Semi-structured interviews. Focus group interviews were conducted with the same student groups who participated in the lessons. Each focus group interview lasted 30–45 minutes. The interviews aimed to gather more nuanced feedback on students’ learning experiences and perceptions of SAILD and provide a supplementary understanding of students’ AI literacy.

### Data collection and analysis
#### Knowledge test (KT)
A multiple-choice knowledge test (KT) was specifically designed to measure students’ conceptual understanding of AI. It was reviewed, revised, and validated by three AI experts, two elementary school STEM teachers and two AI educators to ensure content accuracy and appropriate difficulty level for elementary students. It was then piloted with 30 Grade-6 students from the same school. We selected Grade-6 students for the pilot test for the following reasons. Primarily, the 50 research participants were already the population of the Grade-5 students in the participating school. To preserve the integrity of the pretest data, these students were not included in the pilot test. Instead, Grade-6 students were chosen, as they share comparable academic experiences with the Grade-5 students (i.e. both grades are allowed to select the same STEM extracurricular activities in the school). Additionally, selecting students from the same school ensures a consistent socioeconomic background with the research participants, thereby maintaining contextual continuity in our study.
The final version of the KT contained 20 questions, consisting of 5 questions on general AI understanding, 5 on perception, 5 on machine learning, and 5 on societal and ethical impacts of AI, covering the entire content of the intervention. Table 3 shows the 4 constructs and a question example in each construct. Items in the pre- and post-tests varied in wording but maintained the same content structure and difficulty level. Paired-sample t-tests were employed to examine the difference between pre- and post-tests.

#### Survey instruments
To investigate students’ AI attitudes and other affective perspectives, several self-reported surveys were adopted. The first was the general attitudes toward AI scale (GAAIS) by Schepman and Rodway ([Citation2020](#)) (see [https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.27073942.v1](https://doi.org/https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.27073942.v1)); six items for positive attitudes and six items for negative attitudes were selected. The internal consistency of the original scale was good, with α = 0.88 for Positive GAAIS and α = 0.83 for Negative GAAIS. The second was the motivation scale by Glynn et al. ([Citation2011](#)) (see ); eight items from the first factor, intrinsic motivation, were selected. The motivation scale also had good internal consistency (α = 0.92). All the items were contextualized in AI. For example, the term “I intend to continue learning XXX” was changed to “I intend to continue learning AI.” Paired-sample t-test was used to examine the difference between pre- and post-survey.
#### Qualitative data
In addition to the knowledge test and surveys, two types of qualitative data were collected. The first was the user portfolios created for each group (10 portfolios in total), which consisted of structured worksheets (WS) and design solution plans (DS). The WS contained questions that targeted students’ conceptual understandings of the teaching content, such as “What are the differences between AI and non-AI technology?.” The DS consisted of four major sections: an overview of the solution, background research, a detailed explanation of the AI-powered solution, and a reflection on the plan. The WS and DS were reviewed, evaluated, and revised by three AI experts, two experienced STEM teachers and two AI educators to ensure their quality. These portfolios served as a rich source of evidence, showcasing students’ statements and reflections on AI knowledge, AI skills, AI ethics, and AI attitudes. The second source of qualitative data was derived from semi-structured interviews, which were designed to delve deeper into the students’ AI attitudes, AI ethics, and their perception of the activities of SAILD through open-ended questions. Sample questions included: “Which activity do you like/unlike most and why?” and “How do you think the design work can or cannot help with your understanding of AI, and why?.”
A deductive coding approach was employed to analyze the qualitative data. This approach, grounded in the preexisting theory, guided the initial stages of the analysis (Bradley et al., [Citation2007](#); Burnard et al., [Citation2008](#)). This method involves applying a predetermined set of codes, often referred to as a start list (Miles & Huberman, [Citation1994](#)), to identify certain key aspects of the data that directly relate to the research goals (Bryman, [Citation2014](#)). In this study, deductive coding was conducted using a start list derived from the AI literacy categories (Table 4). We utilized the research goal as a lens to assign emerging codes to the portfolios and interview data, exploring the impacts of SAILD on students’ AI literacy (Elo & Kyngäs, [Citation2008](#)). We further analyzed the coded data using the thematic analysis framework described by Braun and Clarke ([Citation2006](#)). This involves a systematic process of coding and identifying patterns within the data, thereby enabling us to construct meaningful themes related to AI literacy. Through this comprehensive qualitative analysis, we aim to construct a holistic depiction of Grade-5 students’ AI literacy development and contribute valuable insights into the effectiveness of SAILD in AI education, offering a nuanced perspective on pedagogical outcomes.

## Results
### Acquisition of AI knowledge
Paired-sample t-test indicates that students had significant improvement in the KT after the intervention (\*M\* = 13.77, SD = 3.38) compared to the pretest (\*M\* = 9.52, SD = 2.58), with a large effect size (\*p\* < .001, Cohen’s \*d\* = 1.67).
For AI knowledge, we are most interested in students’ conceptual understanding of the term “AI.” Students were asked to answer the question on the worksheet: “What do you think AI is?” at the beginning of the first lesson and at the end of the last lesson. Table 5 summarizes the themes before and after the intervention, with multiple expressions coded independently. Before the lesson, the most common conceptual understanding of AI held by students was “robots,” followed by “interactive features.” For example, S23 wrote in the worksheet, “AI is a robot.” S12 answered, “AI is a machine that can communicate with humans.” The remaining students used AI-powered products they had been using to represent AI. For example, S07 stated that “AI is Siri.” After the intervention, more themes were summarized regarding the same question. The first is “AI is a simulation of humans.” Sample answers included “AI can simulate the thinking process of a human” and “AI can make decisions like humans.” “Machine learning” stands out as the second common theme, such as “AI needs learning supervised by humans” and “The difference between AI and non-AI is the ability to learn.” Around one-fifth of the answers mentioned “solve a real-world problem.” For example, “AI can solve complicated problems such as recognizing people.” However, about a quarter of the answers still focused on robots and interactive features, which is identical to the pre-lesson perception.

Notably, while the overall score of AI knowledge among students increased, as evidenced by the results of the KT, there remain significant gaps in their conceptual understanding. A concerning observation is that 25% of students continued to hold a rudimentary view of AI after the lesson, equating it with specific robots or other AI-powered products. Furthermore, approximately one-third of the students exhibited a partially correct understanding of AI, seeing it as a simulation of human intelligence. This viewpoint, while closer to the reality of AI functions, still misses the nuanced differences between AI processing and human cognition.
### Development of AI skills
Regarding AI skills, students demonstrate their competencies in two dimensions: determining the problems that can be solved by AI and training a supervised learning model. In the first lesson, students were required to answer the question, “When do you think we should use AI to solve a problem and when not to?” In the design task, the guiding question was “Describe in general your plan to protect the target animals; which part will you use AI and which part will use the non-AI technique?” Table 6 summarizes the themes before and after the intervention. Before the intervention, most students either used real-life products such as auto-drive and Siri to represent what problem to be solved by AI (3 groups), or believed that AI was used to solve problems beyond human ability (4 groups). However, none of the students explicitly expressed which problems were more appropriately addressed by AI and which were better suited for non-AI solutions. In the design solutions after the intervention, all groups were able to successfully identify problems that could be solved using AI, and distinguish other procedures that might employ non-AI techniques in their design solutions. Specifically, students identified two types of problems suitable for AI: using AI to find the target (8 groups) and using AI to make decisions (2 groups), with actions carried out by non-AI systems to solve the problem. For example, Group E answered, “To protect polar bears, I will build many small robots and put them on the ice. They will search and monitor the polar bear using AI. Then the robots will send the information to humans automatically; no need to use AI (the part of sending a message).”

In addition to the design solution, the post-interview also shed light on the impacts of SAILD on student development of AI skills. Students acknowledged the utility of AI in problem-solving, as evidenced by responses such as “The design project is helpful (to learn AI) because I can experience AI functions and how AI can be used to solve a real-life problem” (Group A). ### Understanding of AI ethics
As for AI ethics, the paired-sample t-test also shows a significant improvement in the post-test (\*M\* = 3.38, SD = 1.06) compared to the pretest (\*M\* = 2.44, SD = 1.17), with a large effect size (\*p\* < .001, Cohen’s \*d\* = 0.98). Students demonstrated their ethical consideration of AI in the DS. Most groups (9 out of 10) considered the accuracy of AI-generated results, highlighting it either as a potential weakness of their plan or as a facet requiring human verification. Additionally, some students (4 out of 10) acknowledged the potential risks associated with their designed products being used for malicious purposes. For example, when answering the guiding question “What potential problems do you think your solution will have, and how to solve it?” most groups considered the accuracy of AI decision-making, such as “It needs a human to manually review the pictures (of targeted animals)” (Group E), as well as the potential misuse of AI. For example, “While AI can find the whale, it might also provide information to bad people, maybe poachers, and they will use it to hunt them” (Group B).
During the interview, two themes related to AI ethics emerged: critical consideration of the consequences of using AI and the social impact of using AI, listed in Table 8. For critical reflection on the consequences of using AI, students recognized the need for human oversight in AI applications, particularly in scenarios involving image recognition, noting that “it is not always accurate.” The design projects further enabled students to comprehend the potential risks of AI technology and underscored the importance of addressing these risks. Regarding social considerations, the design activities fostered an understanding of the wider societal implications of AI, as evidenced by responses such as “I realize that using AI for problem-solving can benefit individuals and society” and “I am interested in using AI to protect animals in my future career,” illustrating the perceived societal advantages of employing AI.

### Attitudes toward AI
Regarding AI attitudes, the survey achieves a reliability of 0.72 for positive attitudes and 0.71 for negative attitudes, which is acceptable for further analysis. Paired-sample t-test indicates that students held fewer positive attitudes in the post-test (\*M\* = 3.58, SD = 0.52) than pretest (\*M\* = 3.82, SD = 0.69) in general (\*p\* < .01). Especially for the item “Most of the society will benefit from a future filled with AI” (from 3.96 to 3.67) and “AI system can outperform human” (from 3.50 to 3.02). Regarding the items of negative attitudes, the results reveal a less negative attitude in the post-test (\*M\* = 3.14, SD = 0.82) than in the pretest (\*M\* = 3.36, SD = 0.74) in general (\*p\* < .05), especially on the item: “I shiver with discomfort when I think about future uses of AI” (from 3.10 to 2.67). To conclude, the changes in positive and negative attitudes suggest that students had more rational and neutral standpoints toward AI. They tended not to believe that AI was omnipotent, nor did they fear the use of AI in the future. The survey on motivation for learning AI demonstrates high reliability overall (0.93) and in each factor—interest (0.85), confidence (0.89), and utility (0.77). The results reveal an upward trend in students’ motivation. Specifically, in the dimension of perceived interest, the mean increased from 3.51 (SD = 0.90) in the pretest to 3.95 (SD = 0.62) in the post-test (\*p\* < .01). Similarly, in the dimension of utility, the mean rose from 3.67 (SD = 0.74) in the pretest to 4.00 (SD = 0.56) in the post-test (\*p\* < .01). No significant differences in confidence are observed. These results suggest that students are increasingly recognizing both the engaging nature of AI and its practical value.
Students also expressed their attitudes toward AI in the interview. When asked the question, “Do you think AI is good?,” most groups had double-sided answers. For example, Group F mentioned, “I think there are both good and bad aspects of AI. If the AI is designed with a bad purpose, then it is not a good AI.” Group E stated, “AI is good, but it might make mistakes. When it makes mistakes, it will become a problem.”
## Discussions
### Students’ development of AI literacy: strengths and weakness
The learning outcomes in students’ AI literacy present a nuanced picture. Despite the overall increase in AI knowledge as indicated by the KT change, there were considerable gaps in their conceptual understanding. Notably, 25% of the students continued to hold a simplistic view of AI, equating it with robots capable of human-like interactions. This misconception underscores the teaching challenges of AI, as students often develop a preconceived understanding of AI based on its everyday applications, which impedes their ability to grasp more nuanced concepts (Dai, [Citation2024](#)). Students’ prior knowledge primarily focused on AI applications such as robotic vacuums, Siri/Alexa, and YouTube, without a deep understanding of the underlying concepts and mechanisms, such as how computers collected and processed data for representation and reasoning (Ottenbreit-Leftwich et al., [Citation2023](#)). Additionally, about one-third of the students showed a partially correct understanding of AI, interpreting it as a simulation of human intelligence. Although this perspective was closer to the actual functions of AI, it overlooked the crucial differences between AI processes and human cognition, illustrating the challenge students faced in comprehending abstract and complex AI concepts. It suggests that considerable efforts have been devoted to decreasing the cognitive load for students learning about AI. However, some risks have emerged, such as the potential oversimplification of the complex and diverse nature of AI technologies (Aung et al., [Citation2022](#); Vartiainen et al., [Citation2021](#)). According to Dai et al. ([Citation2024](#)), employing analogies can be helpful but might also result in overly simplistic comparisons between AI operations and human thought processes.
The intervention has notably improved students’ AI skills and their awareness of the ethical and social implications of AI, marking a vital step forward. This progress suggests that students were not only successfully solving problems with AI, but also beginning to contemplate the broader impacts of AI on society and recognize the ethical dilemmas and societal challenges it poses—a crucial aspect of holistic AI literacy. The inclusion of societal and ethical considerations in AI education across K-12 levels is crucial (Sanusi et al., [Citation2023](#)). Ali et al. ([Citation2019](#)) emphasize the importance of educating the next generation of technologists on both the ethical and societal impacts of technology, encouraging them to view AI not merely as a tool. Furthermore, Skirpan et al. ([Citation2018](#)) showed that integrating ethical dilemmas into the computer science curriculum can significantly enhance student interest. SAILD responds to these calls to cultivate ethical awareness and social responsibility among young students regarding AI technologies they encounter daily.
Another significant finding relates to the shift in students’ attitudes toward AI following the intervention. Despite the rapid advancement of AI, public comprehension has lagged, fostering misconceptions about its capabilities and associated risks (Brauner et al., [Citation2023](#)). The media often blur the lines between realistic AI portrayals and speculative, human-centric visions of intelligence enhancement (Hansen, [Citation2022](#)). Consequently, there is a discrepancy between the public’s view of AI as a universal solution and its actual, more constrained utility in addressing specific, clearly defined problems (Upshall, [Citation2019](#)). This gap leads to unrealistic expectations and misunderstandings about the current capabilities of AI among the public, including young learners. However, this study reveals a decline in both overly optimistic and pessimistic attitudes among the participating students, indicating the development of a more balanced and realistic understanding of AI. This shift suggests a maturing view of AI, transitioning from a universal remedy or a menace to a nuanced tool with both possibilities and constraints.
### Insights from design projects
The design project in our study provides substantial insights into the educational dynamics of AI literacy. Significantly, students exhibited marked improvements in their practical AI skills, ethical understanding, and overall attitudes toward AI, indicating that the hands-on, project-based approach successfully engaged them and enriched their perceptions of AI’s real-world applications and ethical considerations. This is further illustrated by the findings of Huang et al.’s study ([Citation2024](#)), which indicate that design projects provide opportunities for students to switch their perspectives among designers, users, and evaluators, thereby sensing multidimensional needs. The perspective switching encourages students to ponder the benefits and risks of AI and consider its societal impacts to negotiate the needs of different stakeholders.
Nevertheless, the design activity did not notably advance the conceptual understanding of AI. This discrepancy might stem from DBL’s focus on practical skills and applications rather than theoretical comprehension. Although SAILD emphasizes the research part to align the taught concepts with the design process, which aims to resolve the gaps in conveying in-depth content knowledge of DBL (Kafai, [Citation2005](#); Kolodner et al., [Citation2003](#)), it lacks the theory-based scaffolding necessary to effectively unpack sophisticated AI concepts for students. Consequently, while students become proficient in utilizing AI tools and evaluating their impacts, their understanding of the fundamental principles of AI remains rudimentary. This situation highlights a potential shortfall in the current educational model for AI literacy. To bridge this gap, an integrated pedagogical framework that merges the hands-on, project-oriented approach of DBL with theory-based direct instruction could be beneficial. This approach aligns with the research findings of Henry et al. ([Citation2021](#)), which indicate that elementary students require more concrete and descriptive explanations in their learning activities. The work of Samuelsson ([Citation2024](#)) also provided empirical evidence supporting the significance of combining teacher-led instruction with child-centered learning in programming robotics education. Such a comprehensive framework might enable students to not only practically apply AI but also grasp the core concepts and working processes underlying AI technologies.
Moreover, the design activities emphasize the educational value of constructionism and the creation process in AI, reflecting the transition from traditional, physical artifact creation to digital innovation (e.g. Ng & Cui, [Citation2021](#); Rich et al., [Citation2024](#); Weng et al., [Citation2023](#)). This study introduces an alternative perspective, highlighting problem-solving with digital examples over the production of physical artifacts. By utilizing accessible and open AI platforms like Teachable Machine, students could bring their solution proposals to life in a virtual setting. The elimination of expensive tangible AI resources, such as AI-enabled robots, significantly reduces the barriers to AI education, allowing students from varied socioeconomic backgrounds to engage and learn AI.
### Implications and limitations
The results of our research have several implications for the evolution of AI literacy education. Firstly, the persistence of AI misconceptions among students—such as the tendency to associate AI primarily with robots or other AI-based products—underscores the necessity for a more refined educational approach to scaffold students in constructing a deeper understanding of the underlying principles of AI. The prevalence of oversimplified perceptions of AI indicates that current educational strategies may not adequately address the sophisticated nature of AI technologies. As a result, there is a significant need for educators to devise and implement curriculum enhancements aimed at enriching students’ conceptual understanding of AI. This would involve transcending mere surface-level applications to delve into the intricacies and foundational principles of AI systems.
The notable enhancement in students’ skills and comprehension of the ethical and societal consequences of AI marks a commendable progression toward more responsible and enlightened interactions with technology. This development attests to the effectiveness of incorporating ethical considerations within AI practices, resonating with the broader educational objectives of promoting critical thinking and moral discernment (Zhou et al., [Citation2020](#)). It is imperative for educational policymakers and curriculum developers to acknowledge these findings, emphasizing the vital role of ethics in technology education to prepare students not only as proficient users and creators of AI but also as conscientious members of a technologically advanced society.
Moreover, the varied outcomes from the design project underscore both the strengths and constraints of DBL in AI educational contexts. The combination of AI with social good as a design project topic encourages students’ active participation in the learning activities, which is consistent with the research findings of Huang et al.’s ([Citation2024](#)) work. Although DBL has been shown to be effective in fostering practical abilities and ethical insights, its influence on enhancing conceptual understanding appears to be constrained. This necessitates a reconsideration of how to effectively employ a student-centered constructionist learning approach. We suggest integrating DBL with conventional educational methods to create a more comprehensive AI learning experience that endows students with both the practical competencies required to deploy AI and the foundational knowledge necessary to comprehend its mechanisms.
A notable limitation of this study is the short-term interventions and evaluations, which may restrict the potential impacts of SAILD on AI literacy. For example, the limited time for students to process abstract concepts, coupled with their exposure only to Google Teachable Machine and Google Quick Draw, may constrain their understanding and perception of AI. Long-term investigations remain needed to gauge the enduring effects of SAILD and to track the progression of students’ comprehension and attitudes toward AI over time. Furthermore, another possible challenge to this study is the sample size and research site. This study involved 50 students from a single school in Hong Kong, where the socioeconomic status (SES) of students was relatively high compared to other developing countries. Consequently, there is uncertainty about whether the pedagogical design of SAILD can be effective for a larger and more diverse student population with varying SES backgrounds.
## Conclusion
Although the integration of AI education into the K-12 context has garnered significant global attention, there is a dearth of empirical research exploring effective methods to support students in acquiring comprehensive AI literacy, particularly in upper elementary schools. The present work developed and empirically evaluated SAILD. It also highlighted the design component as the driving force, operating in parallel with the research component to support simultaneous knowledge construction and application by students. The results indicated overall positive impacts on students’ AI literacy development. The intervention significantly improved students’ AI skills and awareness of AI’s ethical and social implications, while fostering more rational and neutral standpoints toward AI. Further efforts could focus on providing sufficient scaffolding to support students’ conceptual understanding of AI in DBL. Our work pioneers the integration of DBL into AI literacy education and informs the development of AI-focused pedagogy in elementary education. SAILD can be further extended and adapted to K-12 education to offer school-age students an accessible and engaging pedagogical approach.
