Abstract
--------

*“The intelligence that was formed on the Enterprise didn’t just come out of the ship’s systems. It came from us. From our mission records*, *personal logs*, *holodeck programs*, *our fantasies.”* This fictitious quote from Captain Jean-Luc Picard of the Starship Enterprise in the 1990s, though predating the advent of generative AI (GenAI) technologies, reflects a key truth: the efficacy of artificial intelligence is fundamentally tied to the quality of human input and interaction. GenAI tools are most valuable when they augment rather than replace human cognition. Guided by this principle, our study investigates the potential of GenAI tools as an adjunct or assistant to student learning in ICT education at universities. Using structured focus groups conducted across three institutions, we explored student perspectives on GenAI’s utility, challenges, learning outcomes and skills development. Participants generally expressed positive attitudes towards GenAI, recognising its time-saving and problem-solving capabilities, but also highlighted concerns about accuracy, ethical usage, and the necessity for guidance on effective utilisation. The findings of the thematic analysis informed the development of the GROW-AI framework, a holistic strategy for integrating GenAI tools into educational practices, addressing components that included guidelines, resources, oversight, workforce preparation, and awareness. This framework provides actionable insights for institutions seeking to harness the potential of GenAI while mitigating its risks, fostering a balanced approach to AI in education.

1 Introduction
--------------

The rapid growth of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) has emerged as one of the fastest technology adoption phenomena in human history (Giannakos et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR31)). As a subset of artificial intelligence (AI), GenAI is capable of generating diverse types of human-like content, including text, images, music, code, video and 3D assets (Kanbach et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR38); Zhang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR73)). Its applications span a wide range of domains, transforming industries such as education by enhancing personalised learning, adaptive assessment, learning outcomes, and intelligent tutoring systems (Bahroun et al., [2023a](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR7); Borah et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR13)). In the education sector, educators and academic leaders recognise GenAI’s potential to reduce administrative burdens and offer valuable support in areas such as brainstorming, language assistance, immediate feedback, professional development, and improving writing and communication skills (Kutty et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR44)).

Within the domain of Information and Communication Technology (ICT) education, GenAI tools have gained rapid acceptance among students as a valuable learning resource for improving their learning performance, surpassing the capabilities of traditional search engines (Elkhodr et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR26)). There is also a growing need for the effective integration of GenAI to support diverse aspects of ICT education, including programming, networking, cybersecurity, research and project management. With increasing calls to transform higher education by integrating GenAI tools to equip students with the skills required for an AI-powered workforce (Chiu, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR21)), it is essential to investigate how GenAI tools can be used as a learning assistant in ICT education.

Educators and students in computer education highlight several benefits of GenAI, such as offering alternative perspectives on programming concepts and providing a convenient source of assistance for learners (Zastudil et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR70)). GenAI enables students to develop a deeper understanding of complex concepts through personalised assistance, potentially transforming educational methods by fostering more flexible and tailored learning experiences.

However, while students generally view GenAI positively in higher education, they also raise significant concerns about its accuracy, privacy, over-reliance, equity and ethical implications (Chan & Hu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR18); Farrelly & Baker, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR28)). Addressing these complexities, this study investigates the effectiveness of GenAI as a learning assistant in ICT education, focusing on its ability to deliver personalised learning, guidance, and feedback.

Specifically, this study examines the utility, challenges, ethical considerations, learning behaviours, skills development, workforce preparedness, and academic outcomes associated with the integration of GenAI tools as learning assistants in ICT education. In doing so, the study also seeks to provide an understanding of how these tools influence students’ educational development and readiness for professional environments.

To achieve these aims, this study addresses the following research questions (RQ):

* RQ1: How do students perceive the integration of GenAI tools as a learning assistant in their ICT education, and how does this impact their learning experiences and attitudes towards AI?
* RQ2: What are the challenges and ethical considerations associated with the use of GenAI tools as a learning assistant in ICT education, and how can these be addressed?
* RQ3: How do GenAI tools influence students’ learning behaviours, skills development, and preparedness for the workforce?
* RQ4: How does the use of GenAI tools as a learning assistant impact student academic performance and learning outcomes in ICT subjects?

Considered holistically, these four research questions form a cohesive inquiry that moves from students’ current experiences with GenAI, through perceived benefits and risks, to their views on its potential role and benefits in enhancing student performance. By capturing student perspectives, the findings of this study aim to develop a framework for integrating GenAI tools in ways that enhance learning while addressing potential concerns.

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows: the next section presents the literature review, followed by the methodology. Subsequently, the results and analysis are detailed, leading to a discussion of the findings. Following this, we present the GROW AI framework, developed directly from empirical findings with ICT students, and justify it as an effective model for integrating GenAI into educational delivery structures. The paper concludes with a summary and limitations.

2 Literature review
-------------------

As GenAI continues to revolutionise education, its integration as a learning assistant in ICT education presents both significant opportunities and challenges. This section examines existing research on GenAI’s role in enhancing teaching and learning, user experiences, ethical considerations, and its capacity to support personalised learning, skill development, and trust. Given the relatively recent emergence of GenAI tools in universities, this review also incorporates insights beyond ICT education to inform the study.

GenAI has gained significant attention in education due to its capability to produce human-like text, images, and other content, offering valuable resources for educators and students alike (Chan & Lee, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR19); Farrelly & Baker, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR28)). For example, Haroud and Saqri ([2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR36)) emphasise that while GenAI enhances access and flexibility, it may hinder students’ critical thinking without structured support, underscoring the need for digital literacy and careful pedagogical framing. Recent research highlights the diverse roles of GenAI in education, which presents both opportunities and challenges. Recent scholarship has proposed several frameworks to guide the effective and ethical integration of AI into higher education. Zhou and Schofield ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR74)) present an AI literacy framework for educators, emphasising the importance of AI competence for student employability and global citizenship while also acknowledging the barriers educators face in adopting AI tools. Complementing this, Chan ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR17)) introduces the AI Ecological Education Policy Framework, which outlines pedagogical, governance, and operational dimensions for managing AI’s integration in universities. These frameworks provide essential context for the development of future models, which build on these principles through a student-informed lens, capturing the lived experiences, expectations, and ethical concerns of learners engaging with GenAI in academic settings. Moreover, in a global policy scan, Jin et al. ([2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR37)) reveal the wide variance in how institutions approach GenAI integration, recommending ethical guidelines, curriculum redesign, and professional development as foundational for effective use.

GenAI tools can reshape education by providing personalised, real-time assistance(Kanont et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR39); Nyaaba et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR51)). For instance, Nyaaba et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR51)) investigated the use of GenAI applications among pre-service teachers’ and found these tools served as effective “learning buddies”, providing access to reading materials, detailed explanations, and practical examples. Similarly, Yilmaz and Karaoglan Yilmaz ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR69)) examined the impact of using ChatGPT on programming education and reported significant improvements in computational thinking skills, programming self-efficacy, and motivation among students using the tool. GenAI tools have also been shown to enhance educators’ self-efficacy and AI literacy, fostering innovative teaching practices and aiding tasks like content creation and assessment preparation (Kanont et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR39); Meli et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR47)). Chugh et al. ([2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR22)) emphasise that while students in ICT disciplines found GenAI valuable for debugging and ideation, they also highlighted the need for digital literacy training and guided usage to maximise its pedagogical value.

Despite these benefits, the use of GenAI in education raises concerns regarding accuracy, trustworthiness, and ethical implications. While educators generally hold positive attitudes towards GenAI tools (Kanont et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR39); Nyaaba et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR51)), there are persistent challenges related to the reliability of AI-generated content. For example, incorrect or biased outputs can mislead students, necessitating better training for educators and students on effectively using these tools.

Ethical concerns, such as academic integrity, are particularly pressing. The potential for students to submit AI-generated work as their own raises issues around plagiarism and undermines the development of critical thinking and problem-solving skills (Farrelly & Baker, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR28); Susnjak & McIntosh, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR62); Wood & Moss, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR68)). Over-reliance on GenAI tools may discourage students from engaging deeply with learning materials, thereby hindering their intellectual growth. There is a need for clear institutional guidelines and educator-led interventions to curb misuse (Khlaif et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR42)).

Beyond ethics, GenAI is seen as a bridge to future workforce readiness. Studies demonstrate that ChatGPT enhances university students’ creative problem-solving capabilities (Urban et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR66)) and critical thinking skills (Almazrou et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR6); García-Alonso et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR29)). Furthermore, research by Rathnayake and Gunawardana ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR57)) explores how GenAI supports human resource management by streamlining processes such as hiring, training, and performance evaluations. Brynjolfsson et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR15)) note that while GenAI can increase productivity, its impact varies widely among individuals, underscoring the need for tailored applications in different educational and professional contexts. Batista et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR10)) echo this, noting that successful preparation for AI-driven futures depends on fostering equitable access, digital fluency, and flexible skills across student cohorts.

The integration of GenAI tools into curricula requires strategic planning to enhance, rather than replace, essential educational processes. Effective implementation involves teaching students prompt-writing skills, improving educators’ AI literacy, and addressing broader ethical concerns such as data privacy and algorithmic bias (Kanont et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR39); So et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR61); Yilmaz & Karaoglan Yilmaz, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR69)). Without such measures, the full potential of GenAI in education may remain untapped.

Questions remain about using GenAI effectively to enhance academic performance and who should take responsibility for ensuring its ethical use in education. To optimise the benefits of new technology, GenAI in ICT requires strategic integration rather than just adoption. Through a methodical approach and strategic choice of integration tactics, the technology discipline can successfully leverage the potential of GenAI to drive innovation (Dwivedi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR25)). Embracing GenAI requires constant learning and adaptation, but those who do so with a clear plan and clear vision will be well-positioned to lead in the digital age (Reznikov, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR58)). Although it is disappointing that most national AI policy strategies have not yet addressed AI in education and its ethical implications, the AI community still has the chance to become more involved with policymakers and influence policy discourse, regulation, and the ethics of AI in education for the greater good (Kenthapadi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR41); Schiff, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR60)). The AI Act and related regulatory frameworks must consider the unique characteristics of trained models to address their implications in educational contexts (Hacker et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR34)).

3 Methodology
-------------

This study adopted a qualitative research methodology, which is well-suited for exploring complex phenomena and capturing detailed participant perspectives (Tenny et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR63)). Qualitative methods facilitate identifying emergent themes, support participant-focused exploration, and address ethical considerations (Nowell et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR48)). To achieve the study’s objectives, focus groups were selected as the primary data collection method, providing a structured and facilitated setting for small groups of individuals to discuss specific topics (Kitzinger, [1995](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR43)).

Three universities with a strong focus on information and communication technology education were purposefully selected to ensure that participant experiences were drawn from institutions where GenAI integration is particularly relevant. These activities incorporated GenAI tools such as Microsoft Copilot and OpenAI ChatGPT into the learning process across five ICT subjects: Business Process Management, Cybersecurity, ICT Project Management, Networking, and Research Projects involving software development.

The in-class activities were designed to enhance students’ understanding of ICT concepts, enable them to generate answers to subject-related questions, and provide personalised learning experiences through the use of a GenAI tool. For instance, in the Research Project, the activities focused on helping students understand Python programming code and convert and validate the output, whereas in Cybersecurity, they centred on identifying active devices within a private network, analysing open ports and services, explaining the function of these services, and assessing potential vulnerabilities. These activities aimed to provide students with practical, hands-on exposure to GenAI tools within the context of their ICT education, thereby preparing them for the subsequent focus group discussions.

Following the completion of the in-class activities, five subject-based focus group discussions were conducted. Each session lasted up to 90 min and included a total of 19 student participants who volunteered after completing the activities. The number of focus groups and participants was informed by the empirical findings of Guest et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR33)), which indicate that more than 90% of all themes can typically be identified within three to six focus groups.

The discussions were guided by semi-structured questions directly aligned with the study’s research questions. This format provided participants with a forum to share their experiences, discuss diverse perspectives, and engage in meaningful dialogue (Wilkinson, [1998](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR67)). Focus groups were particularly advantageous for this study as they captured the complexity of participants’ responses and context-specific challenges, offering deeper insights into their views and enabling the researcher to explore emerging themes more thoroughly (Gibbs, [1997](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR32)).

Ethical clearance for the study was obtained from the lead researcher’s university Human Research Ethics Committee. All participants provided informed consent, ensuring their voluntary participation and the safeguarding of their privacy. Participants and institutional identities were anonymised during data analysis and reporting to maintain confidentiality. Personal details were removed or replaced with generic descriptors to protect the privacy of individuals and institutions. These procedures were conducted in accordance with ethical research standards approved by the Ethics Committee, ensuring participant confidentiality was maintained throughout the study.

The qualitative data collected from the focus groups were analysed using thematic analysis, a widely used method for identifying, analysing, and reporting patterns (themes) within qualitative data. The thematic analysis process involved a structured process comprising several stages, which included initial familiarisation through transcript readings, coding by classifying relevant data segments against focus group questions, grouping codes into preliminary themes, refining and naming themes to accurately reflect the data, and, finally, preparing a detailed analysis with identified patterns and supporting participant quotes (Nowell et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR48)). The results and analysis are presented in the next section.

4 Results and analysis
----------------------

This section presents the findings from the focus group discussions and thematic analysis conducted using NVivo software. NVivo provided a comprehensive environment for analysing qualitative data (Nvivo, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR50)). NVivo cases were created for each participant across the three institutions. These participant codes are referenced in the discussion section where necessary.

The initial phase of the thematic analysis involved coding the overarching research questions and sub-questions from the focus groups into NVivo. This deductive approach provided a hierarchical structure of codes (Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#Fig1)), which formed the basis for allocating participant statements. Since the focus group questions were closely aligned with the study’s research questions, most responses corresponded directly to the workshop facilitator’s questions. An additional inductive analysis of the data was performed but did not reveal insights beyond those captured in the deductive coding process.

### 4.1 Sentiment analysis

To gauge participant attitudes towards the adoption of GenAI tools, sentiment analysis was conducted using NVivo’s auto-coding feature. This tool analysed individual sentences within the coded data, extracting keywords to categorise sentiment as positive, mixed, neutral, or negative. Figure [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#Fig2) summarises the sentiment distribution across responses to the focus group questions. Although this analysis is not intended to provide a precise measure of sentiment strength regarding the research sub-questions, it offers valuable insights into areas of strength and potential challenges for AI adoption from a participant perspective. For instance, responses to Research Sub-Question 2-a—which examined the ease of use of GenAI tools—were generally positive. However, sentiments regarding the trustworthiness of AI-generated outputs were more mixed, with participants expressing reservations about the reliability and dependability of these tools.

5 Discussion
------------

This section discusses the findings, integrating quotes (structured for better readability) from participants in relation to each of the 16 detailed focus group questions. Participant quotes are tagged with a reference ID in the format: [Focus\_Group\_Number]\_[Participant\_Number]\_[Occurrence\_Count]. For instance, the fourth quote from Participant 5 in Focus Group 1 would be referenced as (1\_5\_c4). The discussion is organised according to the sequence of research sub-questions outlined in Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#Tab1).

### 5.1 Student perceptions of GenAI integration (RQ1)

#### 5.1.1 Initial thoughts and changing attitudes (RQ1\_a)

Participants generally expressed enthusiasm for the integration of GenAI tools in their academic studies, particularly highlighting gains in task efficiency. The immediacy of response and capacity to provide useful prompts were seen as valuable in facilitating learning. One participant commented:*“Generative AI has been very helpful for me… it improves efficiency and helped a lot with problem-solving”* (5\_P1\_c1).

These benefits were often qualified by recognition of the tools’ limitations, especially around precision and reliability. As another participant explained: *“Using an AI tool in my academics… it’s quite efficient and not too accurate… because I have to dig deeper on some details. If the question is not precise*, *it gives different answers”* (1\_P1\_c1).

This tension between usefulness and caution aligns with observations by Bozkurt ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR14)), who emphasises that GenAI’s effectiveness depends heavily on the user’s ability to craft precise and contextually relevant prompts.

Students reported using the tools to support specific ICT tasks, such as system design and technical troubleshooting: *“For example*, *if I want to make a network design… I ask how to add the devices… for a basic understanding”* (4\_P1\_c1). Another noted:

However, alongside the functional advantages, students voiced a consistent concern about the absence of structured institutional guidance on ethical boundaries and acceptable use. One noted: *“I was kind of excited… but I wanted better-defined guidelines on where AI can assist and where it breaches academic integrity”* (3\_P4\_c1).

This gap in instructional support reflects a wider issue reported in recent studies. Chan and Hu ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR18)) emphasise that students desire clear institutional guidance and boundaries when using GenAI tools, particularly to avoid breaching academic integrity policies. Without such scaffolding, students may resort to ad hoc approaches that risk misuse or reinforce misconceptions.

Overall, while GenAI was seen to boost confidence and learning efficiency, students’ initial impressions also emphasised the need for critical oversight and structured support. These findings emphasise the importance of institutional interventions that equip students with prompt engineering skills and raise awareness of ethical constraints (Bozkurt, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR14); Al Ali et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR3)).

#### 5.1.2 Interaction and engagement (RQ1\_b)

Participants expressed varied views on whether interacting with GenAI tools enhanced their engagement in the learning process. Some reported limited impact, suggesting that AI tools functioned more as passive aids than active contributors to engagement: *“Personally*, *I do not think it really affected my engagement… I use it just like an aid”* (2\_P2\_c3).

Others noted that the impact on engagement depended significantly on subject matter and task complexity: *“If it is a subject that would traditionally be considered easier*, *it will engage me more… it saves the effort of researching everything yourself”* (2\_P1\_c1).

A recurring theme was that GenAI tools were largely viewed as supplementary to traditional learning resources. One participant remarked: *“In terms of using AI to help get a better understanding… it works as an addition to textbooks*, *lectures*, *and tutorials”* (3\_P4\_c2).

These insights are consistent with Triberti et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR64)), who found that AI tools can improve engagement when they function as instructional companions rather than replacements. In particular, students appreciated the exploratory value of GenAI, which could introduce related concepts and expand the scope of inquiry. Several participants described how GenAI offered step-by-step support, acting as an interactive guide.

The effectiveness of GenAI in fostering engagement was closely tied to prompt formulation. As one participant observed: *“It is really good at providing information*, *but only as good as the prompts you provide”* (3\_P3\_c2).

This supports Bozkurt’s ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR14)) assertion that prompt engineering is a critical literacy in GenAI-supported learning. Students who crafted clearer prompts were able to obtain more meaningful and tailored responses, reinforcing the importance of training learners in this skill.

Moreover, Kanont et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR39)) found that higher education students’ perceived enjoyment and engagement with GenAI tools are significantly influenced by their interactivity and perceived usefulness, suggesting that responsiveness to learner input fosters more sustained engagement. However, this benefit is contingent on learners’ engagement with the tool, not merely its availability. Thus, GenAI’s potential to enhance engagement appears to depend on its alignment with learners’ goals, prompt quality, and contextual integration into teaching design.

#### 5.1.3 Dependence on AI (RQ1\_c)

Participants expressed diverse views on whether GenAI tools affected their independence in learning. While some students regarded them as helpful yet supplementary, others acknowledged a growing reliance due to the tools’ efficiency and integration into routine academic activities.

Several participants emphasised their continued autonomy, describing GenAI as a support mechanism rather than a primary source: *“I don’t really… just depend on AI itself. Although it’s helpful*, *I want to make sure what I’m passing to my tutor is not all about AI”* (1\_P1\_c2), and *“I don’t think right now I’m very dependent on AI myself… but someone new to AI could definitely get very dependent on it*, *especially if they’re not told to second guess it”* (2\_P2\_c4).

Conversely, others acknowledged increasing dependence, especially in contexts where GenAI helped them save time or overcome difficult programming tasks: *“Yes*, *it definitely made me more dependent. Before I had it*, *I had to actively try… especially with coding”* (2\_P1\_c2). Another compared it to everyday tools: *“It’s a good tool and I rely on it every time… it’s just like using a car or a bicycle”* (5\_P3\_c1).

A nuanced view emerged from participants who described GenAI as a starting point or fallback tool. These students valued its foundational support but still engaged with alternative sources for verification or deeper understanding: *“You can feel independent while using the tool… but also in understanding what it’s doing for you*, *not just inputting”* (3\_P7\_c1), and.

*“If I want to know about something*, *I usually search it up first*, *and if those results don’t yield what I’m after*, *I might lean back to ChatGPT”* (3\_P4\_c3).

Despite these benefits, concerns about over-reliance and the passive acceptance of GenAI outputs were evident: *“We shouldn’t be very dependent on AI… it’s right most of the time*, *but it can be wrong because it’s not human”* (4\_P2\_c2).

These findings reflect what Zhai et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR72)) argue that over-reliance on AI dialogue systems can compromise students’ critical cognitive capabilities, reducing opportunities for independent reasoning and reflection. Maleki et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR45)) clarify the phenomenon of AI hallucinations—plausible yet incorrect or fabricated outputs—warning that such issues may lead to misinformation and undermine trust if users lack the skills to detect and challenge these inaccuracies.

In sum, GenAI tools have the potential to support autonomous learning, especially when used as scaffolding rather than solution engines. Their effect on student independence is contingent on the user’s critical thinking skills and the presence of institutional guidance. Embedding digital literacy and critical evaluation skills into higher education curricula can help mitigate the risks of dependency by enabling students to use GenAI tools responsibly and reflectively (Chugh et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR22); Zhai et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR72)).

#### 5.1.4 Sense of achievement (RQ1\_d)

Participants expressed diverse views on whether GenAI tools contributed to or detracted from their sense of achievement. For some, these tools enhanced accomplishment by enabling rapid resolution of complex tasks: *“I had to spend a lot of time on an assessment… stuck on one task for three hours. Using ChatGPT*, *within a few minutes I figured out what I had to do”* (3\_P5\_c1).

Conversely, others felt that using GenAI diminished their satisfaction, as it reduced the intellectual effort involved in problem-solving: *“The sense of satisfaction is a bit lower when using GenAI compared to traditional methods”* (5\_P2\_c1).

Another noted that usefulness was closely tied to subject area, indicating GenAI’s value varied across disciplines: *“For me*, *it depends on the subject. I’ve had subjects where I literally couldn’t use GenAI”* (2\_P1\_c3).

Some participants also likened GenAI to a collaborative peer or ‘study buddy’, describing it as a supportive tool that offered personalised help in ways comparable to peer assistance: *“It’s like a study buddy… if you ask another programmer for help*, *‘Hey*, *I’m having trouble with this’”* (3\_P4).

GenAI’s utility was further reflected in how it simplified complex academic instructions, helping students interpret assessment criteria: *“I copy a paragraph and paste it in ChatGPT to make it simpler*, *to understand what the assessment criteria is asking for”* (3\_P8\_c1).

These findings highlight that the effect of GenAI on students’ sense of achievement is multifaceted and context-dependent. For some, the tool fosters accomplishment by reducing barriers to understanding and providing immediate feedback. Others, however, associate a higher sense of achievement with independent cognitive effort.

AlAli et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR3)) note that when GenAI is used to support rather than replace student thinking, it can promote engagement with higher-order cognitive processes. Moreover, subject matter plays a crucial role in shaping perceptions of utility. Qu et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR56)) found that students in applied disciplines such as engineering and business demonstrated higher engagement with GenAI tools than those in pure disciplines, suggesting that subject-specific relevance significantly influences both usage and satisfaction.

Equity concerns also emerge when different subscription tiers affect access to advanced features such as more accurate responses or deeper contextual understanding. This raises the issue of fairness and consistent learner experience.

Ultimately, institutions must prepare students to use GenAI tools strategically, balancing efficiency with academic integrity. Training should focus not only on tool functionality but also on cultivating reflective judgement to ensure students derive both academic value and a genuine sense of achievement.

### 5.2 Challenges and ethics of GenAI tool use (RQ2)

#### 5.2.1 Ease of use (RQ2\_a)

Participants widely agreed that GenAI tools were user-friendly and intuitive. Many appreciated the simplicity of the interface and the natural, conversational design that resembled human communication. As one participant noted: *“It’s like you want to have a conversation with it*, *almost just like texting someone”* (3\_P4\_c4).

This ease of interaction was supported by the accessible layout, which made it easy to input questions and receive usable responses. Several participants commented that the tools’ responsiveness and ability to streamline searches reduced the time and effort typically associated with online research. One explained: *“It is very user-friendly because it’s quite simple. The layout is just there; you just need to type in the question”* (1\_P2\_c1).

Some participants also commented on the evolving functionality of these tools, particularly the inclusion of features like visual content generation. However, limitations were acknowledged, specifically in areas requiring more complex outputs such as diagram creation. One participant observed: *“It’s user-friendly for giving text answers*, *but for diagrams*, *it’s not”* (4\_P3\_c1).

A recurring theme was the importance of crafting clear and specific prompts. Participants reported that imprecise or vague questions often led to irrelevant or confusing responses, indicating that successful use of GenAI is contingent on users’ skills in prompt engineering. As one noted: *“You have to ask the question in the right way to get the best response… and then understand what the response actually means”* (2\_P2\_c5).

These findings reinforce that ease of use is a central strength of GenAI tools, particularly their conversational interfaces, simple layouts, and quick outputs. This aligns with previous research emphasising functionality, response time, and interactivity as key determinants of user satisfaction in ICT education (Perera et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR53)).

However, effective use remains highly dependent on user skill in prompt engineering. Poorly framed queries can produce suboptimal responses, echoing Bozkurt’s ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR14)) argument that prompt literacy is an essential component of GenAI literacy.

While the addition of image generation features expands the potential of these tools, current limitations in areas like diagram creation suggest the need for further development. Addressing these gaps may broaden GenAI’s applicability across more technical or design-focused disciplines.

#### 5.2.2 Challenges encountered (RQ2\_b)

Participants reported a range of challenges and barriers when using GenAI tools.

While one participant encountered no significant issues due to the structured and straightforward nature of the tools, others described key difficulties, particularly related to ambiguity and inaccuracy in GenAI outputs. As one participant reflected: *“Sometimes it gives me a struggle to get full context of a query… leading to irrelevant or inappropriate responses”* (1\_P1\_c5).

These inaccuracies often required additional effort to interpret or verify responses:*“Part of it is wrong*, *and you have to figure out how to fix that or get it to say the right answer”* (2\_P2\_c7).

To overcome such limitations, participants commonly turned to supplementary resources. One noted: *“Broken links*, *broken information… having to go back to the website to find that information because it wasn’t available in ChatGPT”* (3\_P8\_c2).

Prompt refinement emerged as a key strategy to resolve confusion: *“I need to refine it and retype*, *explaining*, *‘This is not what I wanted; I meant this’”* (5\_P2\_c2).

Some also noted the tools’ limitations in dealing with specialised queries: *“If something is too niche… it just doesn’t know. That’s sometimes a challenge I face”* (3\_P4\_c5).

Plagiarism was another concern, with some participants pointing out the risks of relying heavily on GenAI tools: *“The only challenges I faced were like plagiarism—AI plagiarism”* (4\_P3\_c2).

The challenges identified by participants include ambiguity, inaccuracy and limitations in specialised knowledge, which highlight the reliance of GenAI tools on the quality of their training data and their inability to fully contextualise specific queries. These findings align with observations by Baidoo-Anu and Ansah ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR9)), who note that GenAI tools often lack the depth required to handle niche knowledge effectively.

Ambiguity in outputs, often linked to poorly constructed prompts (Dwivedi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR25)), reinforces the importance of developing user competencies in prompt engineering. Similarly, reliance on GenAI tools for high-stakes or complex tasks necessitates critical evaluation and verification of outputs to avoid inaccuracies or misinformation.

Concerns about plagiarism further emphasise the need for ethical guidelines and user training in academic settings (Bhullar et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR12)). Such training can help students understand how to appropriately credit AI-generated content and use it as a learning aid rather than a shortcut that bypasses original thought.

Implementing a clearly formulated plan that dynamically adapts the use of these technologies in response to changing circumstances can have benefits (Reznikov, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR58)) and help mitigate the problem of inaccurate outputs. Such an approach would involve regular updates to the tools’ capabilities, ongoing user education, and the integration of real-time feedback mechanisms to improve their reliability.

#### 5.2.3 Ethical considerations (RQ2\_c)

Participants identified a range of ethical and data privacy concerns associated with the use of GenAI tools in education. While one participant expressed a fundamentally negative view of AI technologies, dismissing concerns as dystopian (*“I genuinely don’t care… I hate this stuff on a fundamental level”* [2\_P1\_c4]), this perspective was not representative of the broader consensus.

Most participants acknowledged key ethical risks, including data security, intellectual property, and biases in AI-generated content. As one participant put it: *“Ethical concerns including data privacy*, *potential bias in AI responses… I think all of them are important”* (5\_P2\_c3).

Concerns about plagiarism were widespread, with a clear emphasis on transparency in acknowledging AI-assisted work:*“In the assignment*, *we need to acknowledge whether we used AI or not to assist us”* (1\_P2\_c2).

Participants also raised the issue of intellectual property, particularly regarding how AI is trained on copyrighted materials: *“The biggest concern would be in terms of images*, *artists… where AI has just been scanning it”* (2\_P2\_c8).

Data privacy emerged as a recurring theme. Participants cautioned against inputting sensitive data: *“I would never put in… employees’ information*, *or data from your company that is protected… like driver information or birthdays. I typically don’t enter any of that”* (3\_P7\_c3).

Some also worried about AI inadvertently leaking sensitive information used in training: *“AI tools may use or leak sensitive personal information that was part of its training data”* (4\_P3\_c3).

Participants called for greater transparency from AI developers regarding data collection and usage. They suggested that platforms should clearly inform users about what data is collected and seek explicit consent: *“They should confirm consent and fully inform us on what data is being collected about me”* (1\_P1\_c6).

These concerns align with literature highlighting ethical risks such as plagiarism, intellectual property violations, and data misuse (Al-kfairy et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR2)). The reliance of these tools on training data introduces additional risks, as such datasets may inadvertently include sensitive or biased information, leading to privacy breaches or systemic inaccuracies (Phattanaviroj et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR54)). Bias and misinformation further complicate ethical considerations, as the reliability of GenAI tools is inherently linked to the quality of their training datasets and the transparency of their outputs (Tsamados et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR65)).

To address these issues, scholars such as Bahroun et al. ([2023b](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR8)); Dwivedi et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR25)) advocate for ethics education in university curricula, including covering plagiarism avoidance, data privacy, and intellectual property. They also emphasise the importance of educating students about consent and responsible AI use. Ultimately, institutional policies must go beyond technical training and engage students in reflective discussions about the ethical implications of AI, helping ensure not just competent but conscientious usage.

#### 5.2.4 Societal implications (RQ2\_d)

Participants reflected on the broader societal implications of integrating GenAI into education, identifying both long-term benefits and challenges. A common view was that GenAI’s role in society is inevitable and accelerating. As one participant observed: *“You can’t put the genie back in the bottle. ChatGPT*, *generative AI*, *it is a part of our world*, *and it is becoming a part of our world faster and faster”* (3\_P7\_c4).

Time-saving and productivity-enhancing benefits were widely recognised, particularly in both academic and workplace settings. Participants remarked: *“I think it’s very useful… it actually saves our time”* (4\_P3\_c4), and *“It can boost our performance way*, *way faster for the white-collar worker”* (5\_P3\_c2).

Despite these advantages, participants voiced concerns about the impact of GenAI on learning quality. There was a shared worry that excessive reliance on AI could hinder deep engagement, reduce conceptual understanding, and compromise fundamental learning. As one participant explained: *“One of the bigger challenges is just people getting an answer and not understanding it”* (3\_P1\_c2).

This reflects broader concerns about superficial learning, where AI-generated outputs may be accepted uncritically, bypassing reflection or comprehension. The risk of an emerging “AI divide” also emerged, where differences in AI literacy and access could widen educational inequalities. One participant captured this tension: *“Some people not using it… suffer from not being able to understand it. And some people using it… suffer from not having it properly integrated to a point where they’re no longer learning”* (2\_P1\_c5).

Ethical concerns were raised about the outsourcing of cognitive effort to machines, and the risk that this could normalise intellectual passivity: *“Outsourcing thought is not something that should be done willy-nilly”* (3\_P8\_c3).

These findings highlight a central tension in GenAI’s societal integration. While it can increase efficiency and productivity, there is a real risk of undermining users’ critical thinking, problem-solving, and independent learning. Gerlich ([2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR30)) warns of long-term erosion of cognitive autonomy when users rely on AI outputs without reflection or evaluation.

The notion of an “AI divide” echoes equity challenges previously noted by Dwivedi et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR25)), who stress that differential access to AI tools and training can entrench existing disparities. Addressing this requires more than access; it demands an education system that builds AI literacy and cultivates responsible use.

Finally, the ethical dimensions of delegating intellectual tasks to machines require attention. Dakakni and Safa ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR23)) argue that ethical awareness and human agency must remain at the centre of educational practice, especially when integrating tools that can so easily supplant human reasoning.

### 5.3 GenAI tool influence on behaviour and learning (RQ3)

#### 5.3.1 Understanding complex concepts (RQ3\_a)

Participants widely regarded GenAI tools as effective aids for understanding complex ICT concepts, often likening them to supportive peers or mentors, resources that complemented traditional support networks such as tutors or classmates. As one participant remarked:*“I think I would have struggled to learn a lot of these concepts without external help anyway*, *like a friend who already understands it or reaching out to my tutor or lecturer”* (2\_P1\_c6).

A key benefit cited was GenAI’s usefulness in programming contexts. Participants described using the tools to verify code, resolve syntax errors, and interpret unfamiliar programming logic, with one participant noting: *“GenAI helped me verify my answers*, *especially in coding… even though it gave me different answers*, *it was still really helpful”* (1\_P1\_c7).

GenAI was also valued for simplifying technical terminology and unpacking new conceptual content. Several participants reported that its ability to provide definitions and explain jargon made it easier to approach unfamiliar topics. One participant stated: *“In research and study*, *GenAI helped me a lot… when getting into new concepts*, *I normally do not know where to navigate or about new ideas and glossary terms”* (5\_P3\_c3).

A common approach involved breaking down large problems into smaller, step-by-step tasks, with GenAI acting as a guide throughout the process: *“I try to break down those complex problems into smaller*, *simpler problems. If it’s something big*, *I ask: How do I do the first part? The second part? The third part?”* (2\_P2\_c10).

Underlying these experiences was the importance of prompt clarity. Participants frequently acknowledged that the usefulness of GenAI depended heavily on how well the question was framed. One noted: *“As long as I give it a really good prompt… there’s probably an 80% chance it will give me the answer I am after”* (3\_P4\_c6).

Collectively, these findings support the view that GenAI tools act as valuable supplementary resources in ICT education, rather than replacements for formal instruction. This reflects the early assertions by Ohsuga ([2001](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR52)), who proposed that AI learning systems should be autonomous, practical, and general to be effective learning partners.

However, the extent to which GenAI can support increasingly specialised knowledge areas remains uncertain and warrants further exploration. The participants’ experiences highlight the need for students to develop skills in prompt engineering and critical evaluation of AI-generated responses. These competencies are essential to collaborating effectively with GenAI tools, ensuring they are used as a bridge to deeper understanding rather than a substitute for critical engagement.

#### 5.3.2 Personalised learning (RQ3\_b)

Participants highlighted several aspects of GenAI tools that facilitated personalised learning. One of the most frequently cited advantages was 24/7 availability, which allowed students to access support beyond conventional learning hours: *“It’s a 24/7 available software… providing us with learning assistance whenever we need*, *without the limitations of school or tutor schedules”* (4\_P3\_c6).[Footnote 1](#Fn1)

The provision of immediate feedback was also seen as critical, enabling real-time responses to learning challenges and improving the efficiency of self-guided study: *“It provided me a lot of resources and also gave instant feedback”* (5\_P1\_c3).

Despite these benefits, some participants questioned whether GenAI tools offered true personalisation, noting that the tools operated more like generic assistants than tailored educational solutions: *“There’s no personalisation… it’s not like a personal education product for me. It’s just a thing that I use”* (2\_P1\_c7).

However, others emphasised the user’s role in shaping the learning experience. By crafting precise prompts and controlling the structure of the interaction, participants reported that they could direct the tool to better suit their learning styles. For example: *“In terms of personalised learning*, *it’s pretty good… because you’re the one fully asking the questions and mostly in control of the conversation*, *you can tailor it to how you like to work and learn”* (3\_P4\_c7).

These findings suggest that while GenAI systems may not intrinsically personalise content, they enable quasi-personalised learning when users proactively guide the interaction. This reflects broader insights from Chen et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR20)), who argue that large language models contribute to individualised learning through their responsive reasoning and broad informational access.

However, the level of personalisation is reliant on user engagement and input. This finding aligns with Ziebell and Skeat ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR75)), who noted that GenAI tools can serve as effective learning companions, but their effectiveness depends on how actively users interact with the technology. Students who refine their interactions and leverage the flexibility of these tools can create tailored learning experiences that better meet their unique needs and preferences.

#### 5.3.3 Skill development (RQ3\_c)

Participants reported that using GenAI tools enhanced several non-ICT skills, including communication, problem-solving, critical thinking, and research capabilities. These tools were viewed as supportive in both academic and professional contexts, with particular emphasis on improving written communication. One participant noted: *“First*, *the communication and the collaboration is very helpful for my communication*, *especially with grammar and stuff like that.”* (1\_P1\_c8).

Others cited GenAI’s utility in improving the tone, structure, and clarity of formal correspondence, such as emails: *“I can just tell ChatGPT or any software what I want to say in basic English*, *and it provides very effective and formal sentences with proper indentation… this enhanced my email-writing skills.”* (4\_P2\_c4).

Participants also described using GenAI to support analytical reasoning. For instance, some applied it to evaluate design decisions and critique software architectures: *“If I design the architecture myself and ask [the tool] to analyse it—for example*, *the pros and cons—I think it’s pretty okay for that.”* (5\_P3\_c4).

The development of critical thinking and problem-solving skills was frequently reported. GenAI helped participants interpret complex information, refine queries, and reflect on the quality of AI-generated responses. As one participant explained: *“It enhanced my critical thinking*, *analytical skills*, *and ability to gather data or interpret information or answers that I needed to understand.”* (1\_P2\_c3).

Some participants stressed that effective GenAI use presupposed foundational competencies in these areas. Engaging with the tool, they argued, helped refine such skills further: *“You should really have those critical thinking and researching skills before using AI*, *and then you can improve them during the use of AI.”* (2\_P2\_c12).

The feedback accentuates the alignment of GenAI tools with Bloom’s taxonomy of learning, which encompasses knowledge, comprehension, analysis, application, synthesis, and evaluation (Martin & Nesbit, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR46)). Participants’ experiences suggest that GenAI tools can engage multiple levels of this taxonomy by by enabling learners to apply concepts, analyse outputs, and evaluate information accuracy.

However, the development of communication skills was seen as double-edged. While GenAI offered grammatically refined outputs, participants expressed concern that these polished responses might lack originality, potentially diluting personal voice and reducing authenticity. This reflects a broader pedagogical challenge: leveraging AI-generated support without sacrificing creativity or critical self-expression.

To maximise the developmental potential of GenAI tools, educators should encourage intentional use, prompting students to reflect on when and how to engage AI effectively while maintaining their unique contributions. Structured guidance in this area could ensure that cognitive and interpersonal skill development is not compromised by over-reliance on generic AI outputs. As Roe and Perkins ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR59)) caution, poorly integrated AI may reduce learner agency and active engagement. Similarly, Fan et al. ([2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR27)) warn that overuse of GenAI may lead to metacognitive laziness and undermine critical thinking and deeper learning.

#### 5.3.4 Preparation for the workforce (RQ3\_d)

Participants expressed mixed views on whether GenAI tools effectively prepare students for the workforce. While some were sceptical, viewing GenAI as more beneficial in academic contexts, others recognised its potential to support workplace tasks if used strategically. One participant described GenAI as “*really great at helping you do assignments*” but “*useless in the workforce*” (2\_P1\_c9), while another questioned how technical tasks would integrate GenAI meaningfully in professional settings (1\_P2\_c4).

In contrast, other participants framed GenAI as a supportive aid rather than a substitute for professional skills. They highlighted its potential for easing workflows and reducing stress:

*“With ChatGPT*, *I think it’s very easy.”* (4\_P3\_c7). However, the consensus emphasised the need for domain-specific training and professional adaptation. As one participant observed, the effectiveness of GenAI depends on job type and the extent to which educational experiences align with workplace expectations (2\_P2\_c13).

Some participants viewed GenAI positively for continuous learning and upskilling, particularly in rapidly evolving industries. They cited its ability to support mastery of core competencies and awareness of current trends, especially when used intentionally to complement professional development (1\_P1\_c9).

These findings align with Noy and Zhang ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR49)), who reported that GenAI tools, such as ChatGPT, improved worker efficiency by reducing task completion times and enhancing the quality of outputs. Nonetheless, the gap between academic utility and real-world applicability persists. As Ali and Aysan ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR4)) note, effective GenAI integration in the workforce requires tailored applications, particularly in regulated fields like healthcare.

Bridging this divide requires rethinking how GenAI is embedded into educational contexts. Institutions should implement targeted training focused on industry-relevant applications, critical thinking, and ethical use. Emphasising GenAI as a complement to human expertise can enhance adaptability, digital fluency, and professional readiness, thereby preparing students to navigate AI-augmented work environments.

### 5.4 GenAI impact on ICT learning outcomes (RQ4)

#### 5.4.1 Impact on academic performance (RQ4\_a)

Participants reported mixed experiences regarding the impact of GenAI tools on their academic performance and learning outcomes in ICT subjects. Many highlighted significant benefits, particularly in terms of efficiency, comprehension, and task management.

One participant noted a clear improvement in their academic results after using GenAI tools: *“Yeah, it helped me in a previous task that we mentioned before, so in the end for the result I saw the improvement before using AI and after using generative AI.”* (5\_P3\_c5).

Another participant praised the tools’ ability to streamline the learning process, enabling them to manage their workload across multiple subjects: *“Yes*, *it’s probably made them better. I don’t know. It actually takes a lot of time away from doing assignments*, *so that is good and lets me focus more on multiple classes rather than just a couple that are hard.”* (2\_P1\_c10).

The time-saving benefits of GenAI tools were frequently mentioned. One participant noted their utility in simplifying concepts and troubleshooting: *“It’s definitely benefited… It’s been a big time saver*, *at the minimum*, *for me to understand concepts and stuff like that. Maybe the troubleshooting*, *that sort of thing.”* (3\_P4\_c10).

However, concerns about over-reliance on GenAI tools emerged as a recurring theme. One participant observed that dependency on these tools could hinder the development of critical problem-solving skills: *“For example*, *they are addicted to using it*, *so they do not think or independently find the answers to their questions. They are very addicted to ChatGPT*, *so they have very limited problem-solving skills because of that. They do not know how to solve it manually if someone asks—they are only dependent on the ChatGPT answer.”* (4\_P3\_c8).

GenAI tools demonstrate strong potential to enhance academic performance by fostering creativity, simplifying complex concepts, and improving task efficiency. These tools can also assist in multitasking and managing the demands of multiple subjects. Such findings align with existing literature, highlighting the role of GenAI in supporting knowledge acquisition and boosting academic and job performance (Ahn, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR1)).

Nonetheless, the risk of over-reliance on GenAI tools warrants attention. An over-reliance on these tools has the potential to undermine critical thinking skills, as it may discourage learners from engaging deeply with problem-solving and independent analysis (Ali et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR5)).

As participants indicated in their responses, there are positive benefits to using GenAI in learning, such as saving time and breaking down complex topics into manageable chunks. However, these advantages must be tempered by its drawbacks, including its potentially addictive nature, which may detract from developing essential cognitive and analytical skills. To address these concerns, universities could incorporate this topic into preliminary AI training sessions, equipping students with the knowledge and strategies needed to use GenAI effectively while mitigating its potential drawbacks.

#### 5.4.2 Trust in AI (RQ4\_b)

Participants shared mixed views on the trustworthiness of GenAI tools, with many expressing concerns about their reliability and accuracy. One participant commented: *“Usually at the core it’s telling you what to do or it’s giving you something to go on but as a whole usually it comes out with the wrong thing.”* (2\_P2\_c14).

Another participant highlighted the inherent limitations of GenAI in addressing complex or nuanced issues: *“You have to understand that you can’t trust it like it’s some freaking master at the subject. It’s not. It’s just giving you the most likely answer that you’re expecting.”* (2\_P1\_c11).

The accuracy of responses was also seen to depend on the clarity of user inputs. Poorly constructed prompts often resulted in vague or irrelevant outputs: *“Because sometimes if you ask like a vague question it will also give us a vague answer.”* (1\_P1\_c11).

To mitigate these limitations, participants emphasised the need for independent research to validate AI-generated information. One participant described their approach: *“I have a bit of limited trust with AI because I find when I’ve prompted or asked questions*, *it’s generally misleading*, *which plays into the fact that you have to do your own research.”* (3\_P3\_c5).

Cross-referencing AI outputs with other sources was frequently suggested as a strategy to ensure reliability: *“If I’m not sure of it*, *I will get the answer from Generative AI and compare it against what we can actually get from traditional Google Search and supporting sources.”* (1\_P2\_c5).

Participants highlighted the limited suitability of GenAI for specialised or technical queries: *“If it’s a niche thing*, *maybe something like finding a part number for a car*, *I don’t really trust it.”* (3\_P4\_c11).

Despite these concerns, participants recognised the value of GenAI for brainstorming and idea generation: *“For tasks like brainstorming or receiving explanations on topics we are learning*, *ChatGPT can be a helpful guide.”* (4\_P3\_c9).

These findings align with existing research, which highlights trust as a critical factor in the adoption of GenAI tools (Kanont et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR39); Nyaaba et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR51)). Trust is influenced by factors, including user competency, the tool’s design features, and the context in which it is applied (Kaplan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR40)). Participants’ feedback highlights that the effectiveness of GenAI depends not only on the technology itself but also on users’ ability to critically evaluate its outputs. Poorly constructed prompts and over-reliance on GenAI tools were seen as significant contributors to inaccurate or misleading responses.

Further, as emphasised by Zawacki-Richter et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR71)) and Popenici and Kerr ([2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR55)), cultivating digital literacy and fostering users’ epistemic vigilance, defined as the ability to evaluate information credibility, can enhance trust calibration. Encouraging users to adopt reflective practices and verify AI responses against authoritative sources is critical to ensuring GenAI’s effective and responsible use in education.

#### 5.4.3 Responsibility and accountability (RQ4\_c)

Participants widely agreed that ensuring the ethical use of GenAI tools in education requires shared accountability among various stakeholders, including students, educators, policymakers, and developers. One participant captured the essence of this consensus: *“I think it’s everyone. I think everyone has a part of accountability to play when it comes to AI.”* (3\_P7\_c5).

Many participants emphasised that students bear significant responsibility for ethical AI use, particularly in avoiding plagiarism and over-reliance on AI-generated outputs: *“We should use AI responsibly to avoid plagiarism and misuse of AI. Because a lot of students think that it will be easy for us to just use AI*, *just copy and paste that one.”* (1\_P1\_c12).

Students were also viewed as being at the “endpoint” of ethical responsibility: *“Ultimately*, *it is the user who is asking the question and getting that information… It’s their responsibility to use it properly.”* (3\_P8\_c4).

Participants stressed that educators must guide students on ethical AI usage by providing clear boundaries and training: *“The educators could give us a little guidance or support on the ethical and effective ways of using AI.”* (1\_P1\_c13). Institutions, too, were seen as key players in regulating and facilitating ethical AI practices: *“In educational institutes*, *I think the educational commissions are the ones who should introduce these kinds of things and implement them.”* (4\_P2\_c5).

Some participants attributed the greatest responsibility to policymakers and governments, calling for clear regulations to ensure responsible AI use: *“The government needs to be on this. The government and policymakers need to make sure– I don’t know if it’s such a big problem right now*, *but they need to act.”* (2\_P1\_c12). Another participant suggested that government intervention is essential for protecting younger generations: *“The government must have a policy or rules to limit AI use for the young generation because it will highly affect them.”* (4\_P3\_c10). Developers were also seen as integral to ethical AI use, particularly in ensuring the reliability and fairness of the tools they create: *“I think it should be developers who are responsible for this.”* (5\_P1\_c4).

The findings suggest a consensus that responsibility for ethical AI use cannot rest solely on one group. This view is consistent with prior research emphasising the critical role of the IT community in influencing the development of policies, regulations, and the ethical integration of GenAI tools into education systems (Kenthapadi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR41); Schiff, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR60)). Delgado et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR24)) emphasise the importance of cultivating dialogue among academics, governments, and private-sector actors to ensure the equitable and responsible integration of GenAI tools into educational systems.

Key principles such as transparency, equity, responsibility, and beneficence should underpin these collaborations. Policymakers, educators, and developers must work together to create regulatory frameworks that minimise risks of marginalisation and harm while ensuring the equitable distribution of AI’s benefits.

#### 5.4.4 Future use and recommendations (RQ4\_d)

Participants broadly supported the continued use of GenAI tools in ICT education, acknowledging their value while highlighting areas for improvement. Many noted that the tools could significantly enhance learning outcomes when coupled with an understanding of their functionalities: *“In terms of IT*, *I think the way we’re going is fine because we have that general understanding of how to use generative AI.”* (2\_P2\_c16). Another participant echoed this view but stressed responsible use: *“Yes*, *definitely*, *especially in the IT sector. It’s very useful*, *and I really do recommend using AI tools across various things. However*, *we should be mindful of how we use it.”* (1\_P1\_c14).

Participants called for clear boundaries and guidelines to regulate GenAI use, recognising the potential for misuse if left unchecked: *“I definitely think that it should continue to be… Maybe not entirely encouraged*, *but as someone said*, *that barrier is set. You’re more than welcome to use it*, *as long as you keep this*, *and this*, *and this in mind.”* (3\_P4\_c12). One participant advocated for higher education policies that clearly outline permissible applications: *“My recommendation for ICT education is that there should be some sort of higher-up guideline or policy on how to use AI. My university already has that*, *and it should be crystal clear what exactly you can use it for.”* (2\_P1\_c13).

Participants suggested several improvements to enhance the tools’ effectiveness, such as better support for image-based inputs and clearer distinctions between free and paid feature sets. One participant explained: *“The only feature I wanted was the picture one. They recently updated it so we can upload pictures as well. For other features*, *I’m not sure.”* (4\_P3\_c11). Another participant highlighted the complexity of subscription-based features, which may restrict access to advanced capabilities: *“There’s ChatGPT 4.0*, *4.1… All these different versions*, *and one of them you have to pay for to get.”* (3\_P7\_c8).

They also emphasised the importance of improving accuracy and reducing bias in AI-generated outputs:*“AI tools should integrate real-time fact-checking to ensure the responses provided are accurate.”* (4\_P3\_c12) and*"AI tools should be regularly updated to ensure accuracy and minimise biases.”* (5\_P1\_c5).

While participants showed enthusiasm for GenAI tools, they stressed the need for continuous improvement and robust regulatory frameworks. These concerns align with existing research advocating for plans and policies that address ethical and practical challenges in AI use in education (Bhullar et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR12); Reznikov, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR58)).

To maximise the benefits of GenAI, educational institutions should collaborate with policymakers and developers to establish ethical standards and guidelines. This includes addressing issues of accessibility and ensuring that all students can benefit from advanced features regardless of pricing models. Additionally, institutions should implement training programs to teach students how to critically evaluate AI outputs and use these tools responsibly. As Kenthapadi et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR41)) and Delgado et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR24)) argue, multi-stakeholder collaboration, encompassing educators, policymakers, and developers, is essential to establishing ethical standards and guidelines for AI integration.

The findings of this study emphasise the potential of GenAI tools in ICT education while highlighting the complexities of their adoption. Balancing benefits such as enhanced efficiency and personalised learning with challenges like ethical concerns and reliance on accuracy requires a deliberate and informed approach. Moreover, this emphasises the importance of moving beyond isolated implementations towards a more structured, comprehensive framework that can address these multifaceted needs.

Moving forward, it is also important to consider broader systemic implications. From a technological perspective, the biases inherent in GenAI training data (Hanna et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR35)) may influence the content and quality of support students receive, potentially reinforcing existing inequities. Educationally, there is a risk that sustained reliance on AI tools for guidance, feedback, or content generation could inhibit the development of students’ independent critical thinking and metacognitive skills (Çela et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR16)). At a societal level, the integration of GenAI may exacerbate the digital divide, disproportionately benefiting students with consistent access to advanced technologies and institutional support (Bentley et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13686-3#ref-CR11)). These concerns underscore the importance of ethical foresight and inclusive planning in the implementation of GenAI within higher education.

6 The GROW-AI framework
-----------------------

The GROW-AI framework was developed through the thematic analysis of qualitative data gathered from structured focus group discussions. Recurring student concerns and aspirations were synthesised into five core components: Guidelines, Resources, Oversight, Workforce Preparation, and Awareness. These components reflect students’ expectations for ethical, practical, and future-oriented use of GenAI in education. GROW-AI framework

### 6.1 Guidelines - establishing standards and policies

Clear institutional guidelines and policies are essential to ensure the ethical and effective use of GenAI tools. These policies should delineate acceptable practices, particularly in the context of academic integrity, by providing boundaries around plagiarism prevention and the use of AI in assessments. Participants in the study expressed a strong desire for such clarity, noting confusion about when and how GenAI use constitutes a breach of academic conduct. Institutional guidelines should operationalise these policies at the course level, while national frameworks can promote consistency across sectors and address overarching concerns such as intellectual property, data privacy, and bias mitigation. Ethical principles such as transparency, accountability, and fairness should underpin these policies, ensuring that the integration of GenAI aligns with broader societal and educational goals.

### 6.2 Resources - providing support for effective use

This component focuses on educational support during formal learning. It includes instructional materials, skills training (e.g. prompt engineering), and digital literacy initiatives aimed at improving students’ use of GenAI for academic tasks. Resources are institutional enablers, designed to optimise GenAI use within the curriculum. Comprehensive training programs must be designed to equip both students and educators with the necessary skills to engage with these tools effectively. Training should focus on areas such as prompt engineering, critical thinking, and ethical considerations, enabling users to optimise GenAI’s potential. Institutions should also address subscription-based inequities, as some students noted that advanced features are locked behind paywalls, creating an uneven playing field. Students also reported that task complexity affects how satisfied they feel when using GenAI tools. To support effective learning, resources should be scaffolded to match varying levels of complexity, with worked examples, tutorials, and troubleshooting guides that contextualise GenAI use across both routine and cognitively demanding tasks. For example, resources might include a prompt-engineering workshop to help students deconstruct a networking concept or a quick-reference guide to using GenAI in project planning. Investing in technical enhancements, such as retrieval-augmented responses with verifiable source citations and expanded functionalities like AI-assisted diagram generation, would further increase the pedagogical value of these tools across diverse educational contexts.

### 6.3 Oversight - monitoring and continuous improvement

Regular oversight and monitoring mechanisms should be established to evaluate the effectiveness of GenAI tools in improving learning outcomes, fostering engagement, and supporting skill development. Such oversight could include periodic reviews of AI usage data, educator-led checks of student interactions with GenAI tools, and formalised feedback channels for students to report concerns or limitations. Feedback systems can capture insights from students and educators, allowing for iterative refinement of the tools and their implementation strategies. Participants in the study highlighted the need for shared responsibility and structured guidance to ensure ethical and pedagogically sound use of GenAI. Institutions should also periodically assess the impact of these tools on academic performance, ensuring that their integration aligns with broader educational objectives. This iterative process will ensure that GenAI tools remain relevant, effective, and aligned with the evolving needs of students and educators.

### 6.4 Workforce preparation - bridging academic and professional applications

This component focuses on aligning GenAI use with professional practice, ensuring students are ready to engage with AI in real-world work environments. While ‘Resources’ support learning within the university, Workforce preparation ensures students can transfer those capabilities to industry-relevant scenarios, reflecting employer expectations and evolving professional norms. Tailored training programs should reflect real-world scenarios and address industry-specific needs, enabling students to transition seamlessly into AI-driven workplaces. The focus should extend beyond technical proficiency to include the development of lifelong learning, adaptability, and critical thinking skills. Collaboration with industry stakeholders is essential to align training with professional standards and to ensure that students are equipped to meet the demands of an AI-enabled workforce. For example, integrating GenAI into industry-based projects or offering simulation exercises that mirror workplace uses (e.g. writing code explanations for clients using GenAI) prepares students to use such tools appropriately in the workforce.

### 6.5 Awareness - promoting ethical and responsible use

Raising awareness of the ethical and societal implications of GenAI is critical to fostering its responsible use in education. Educational programs should include workshops and discussions on topics such as plagiarism, data privacy, bias, and the potential risks of over-reliance. Encouraging a balanced approach, where GenAI tools complement traditional pedagogies rather than replace them, can help students engage critically with these technologies. Awareness campaigns should also include a discussion of access disparities and the implications of differential access to GenAI tools (free vs. paid tiers). Addressing these issues transparently helps build a culture of fairness and inclusion. Moreover, institutions should emphasise the importance of validating AI-generated outputs to avoid uncritical dependence, ensuring that students develop the skills necessary for informed and ethical engagement with AI tools.

7 Conclusion
------------

This study explored the integration of GenAI tools as learning assistants in ICT education, addressing their potential benefits, associated challenges, and ethical considerations. The findings amplify the potential of GenAI tools in enhancing learning outcomes, personalising educational experiences, and equipping students with skills for an AI-driven workforce. However, the study also reveals critical complexities, including accuracy limitations, risks of over-reliance, and ethical implications such as privacy concerns and academic integrity challenges.

This study engaged students through focus groups and employed thematic analysis to interpret their perceptions, behaviours, and experiences with GenAI tools. The results highlight the need for a structured and multidimensional approach to AI integration, culminating in the proposed GROW-AI Framework. This framework offers practical guidance by emphasising the importance of clear guidelines, robust resources, continuous oversight, workforce-aligned preparation, and heightened ethical awareness to ensure the effective and responsible adoption of GenAI tools.

While this study offers meaningful insights into the integration of GenAI in ICT education, several limitations should be acknowledged. The focus group sample, comprising ICT students from three Australian universities, was relatively small, which may limit the breadth of perspectives captured. Consequently, the generalisability of the findings may be constrained not only by disciplinary and institutional context but also by the limited sample size. Caution should, therefore, be exercised when applying these findings to other disciplines, regions, or educational settings. Additionally, the qualitative methodology, grounded in focus group discussions, relies on subjective self-reported data, which, while rich in detail and contextually insightful, may not capture the full diversity of student experiences across broader populations. The inclusion of complementary quantitative data could have enhanced the breadth of the findings by offering a broader cross-sectional perspective and enabling comparisons across demographic or institutional variables.

Practically, the findings call for targeted interventions, such as tailored training programs to develop critical skills in prompt engineering and ethical decision-making, alongside the establishment of institutional policies to mitigate risks. Moreover, they reinforce the importance of collaboration among stakeholders, including students, educators, policymakers, and AI developers, to ensure GenAI tools serve as enablers of meaningful learning rather than substitutes for intellectual engagement.

Future research should build on these findings to explore the longitudinal impact of GenAI tools on student outcomes, including their readiness for dynamic professional environments. Investigating domain-specific applications, diverse educational contexts, and emerging AI functionalities will further deepen our understanding of the evolving role of GenAI in education.

In conclusion, while GenAI tools hold immense potential to transform ICT education, their effective integration requires a balanced, ethical, and inclusive approach. The GROW-AI framework provides a roadmap to guide this process, ensuring that AI technologies fulfil their promise of enhancing learning while preparing students to navigate the complexities of a GenAI-powered world.
