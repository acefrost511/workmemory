# Human versus machine: The effectiveness of ChatGPT in automated essay scoring

## ABSTRACT
As ChatGPT continues to reshape student engagement and instructional design, it is crucial to examine its practical implications. This study aims to evaluate the effectiveness of ChatGPT3.5 and ChatGPT4 as potential automated essay scoring (AES) systems. Fifty authentic, student-written annotated bibliographies were evaluated by three human raters (HRs), ChatGPT3.5, and ChatGPT4, each performing three rounds of grading. Statistical analyses were conducted to determine if the AI evaluations were comparable to the evaluations of HRs in terms of accuracy, reliability, and consistency. The findings reveal that although AI-generated evaluations occasionally aligned with more lenient evaluations by certain individual HRs, overall, the performance of the GPT models did not align with that of HRs.

## Introduction
The rise of artificial intelligence (AI) technologies and natural language processing (NLP) chatbots has had a substantial impact on education. The most widely used NLP chatbot service, ChatGPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer), has over 100 million weekly users as of November 2023 (Altman, [Citation2023](#)), with researchers reporting 50% (Pallivathukal et al., [Citation2024](#)) to over 70% (Castillo et al., [Citation2023](#)) of higher education (HE) students actively using ChatGPT. Along with student adoption, there is growing interest in the use of such services by instructors. The sentiment, ‘If my students are going to use ChatGPT to write their papers, we might as well use ChatGPT to grade their papers’, may reflect a common perspective among HE instructors. While this might reveal a limited understanding of GPT capabilities and a fear of its impact on pedagogy, it warrants exploration into the extent this emerging technology could assist in assessing students’ written work.
The integration of ChatGPT in HE may not only be inevitable but has the potential to enhance efficiency in teaching across disciplines (Meyer et al., [Citation2023](#)). Tools like ChatGPT are increasingly recognised for supporting personalised learning and aiding in the development of instructional content, positioning them as valuable assets in educational settings (Dai et al., [Citation2023](#)). As the academic community becomes more familiar with these technologies, their use in assessments may lead to more accurate and efficient evaluations, potentially transforming grading methods (Altamimi, [Citation2023](#)). However, there is limited research on GPT use from the instructor’s perspective, particularly in assessment. This project aims to investigate the potential of GPTs as a practical tool for instructors in the evaluation process.
### Literature review
#### Automated essay scoring
In pursuit of delivering effective and consistent assessments, the incorporation of technological tools is not a novel phenomenon. AES has been used as far back as the 1960s, when Ellis Page created the Project Essay Grader (PEG) using teacher-graded student essays (Hearst, [Citation2000](#)). This program showed a high correlation to teacher scores but was limited in the textual features assessed, such as general semantics, word length, essay length, commas, and use of various vocabulary. As this program used indirect methods, it was susceptible to inaccurate assessments as longer texts would receive higher scores regardless of quality (Dikli, [Citation2006](#)). Nevertheless, Page’s initiative served as a precursor, leading to advances in AES development.
In the 1990s, AES took a significant leap forward with programs such as the Intelligent Essay Assessor (IEA) and e-rater. IEA assesses texts based on conceptual and semantic content, instead of merely vocabulary and word count (Hearst, [Citation2000](#)). The e-rater program was developed by the Educational Testing Service (ETS), a provider of several international standardised tests that incorporate writing components (ETS, [Citation2024](#)). E-rater evaluates writing by identifying linguistic features using NLP techniques according to predetermined criteria (Dikli, [Citation2006](#)). Modern AES systems are learning-based, employing supervised or reinforcement learning algorithms, and focus on dimension-specific scoring, targeting aspects such as organisation, thesis clarity, argument persuasiveness, and relevance (Ke & Ng, [Citation2019](#); Ramesh & Sanampudi, [Citation2021](#)). However, evidence of their ability to consistently assess relevance and coherence remains limited (Ramesh & Sanampudi, [Citation2021](#)), leading to criticism of their practical applications. In this context, some standardised tests continue to rely on human evaluators alongside AES programs.
### ChatGPT as an AES
Recent research has explored the potential of ChatGPT in text analysis. Altamimi ([Citation2023](#)) analysed the grading consistency of ChatGPT3, ChatGPT3.5, and ChatGPT4 on student texts, reporting that ChatGPT4 demonstrated superior performance. However, the study did not compare these GPT models with HRs, leaving questions about their practical reliability in authentic contexts. Moreover, Altamimi emphasises the need for comprehensive methods that address AI-specific challenges, particularly in mitigating biases inherent in automated assessment. Mizumoto and Eguchi ([Citation2023](#)) assessed ChatGPT3.5’s ability to grade 12,100 essays from the ETS Corpus of Non-Native Written English (TOEFL11). Although ChatGPT3.5 generally reflected the essays’ assigned levels, it did not achieve perfect agreement with HRs, prompting the authors to recommend using GPTs alongside human evaluations.
A key challenge in using GPTs for assessment is prompt engineering, or designing prompts to elicit desired results (Giray, [Citation2023](#)). Dai et al. ([Citation2023](#)) found that while ChatGPT could generate consistent feedback, reliability improved through prompt manipulation. Similarly, Xia et al. ([Citation2024](#)) suggested the importance of prompt engineering, showing that ChatGPT3.5’s accuracy in assessing TOEFL essays depended heavily on prompt elements. Research has widely supported prompt engineering as essential for controlling ChatGPT output (Choi et al., [Citation2023](#)), though effective prompt engineering exceeds typical instructor training.
### Availability
AES, once primarily available through institutions or organisations, are now increasingly accessible in educational settings. Massive Open Online Courses (MOOCs), for example, have adopted AES programs for efficient writing evaluations. However, essay-grading programs have been cost-prohibitive to the average instructor. This project, conducted by English language teaching (ELT) practitioners, considers the expense of such tools. Free GPT models, such as OpenAI’s ChatGPT, are now publicly available, offering cost-effective options (Dai et al., [Citation2023](#)). These models have sparked interest in their pedagogical implications, with researchers suggesting this technology could benefit educational stakeholders (Nazari et al., [Citation2021](#)).
### The present study
This study is conducted within an ELT context, where exploration of GPT applications is still emerging. While GPTs are increasingly acknowledged for their roles in communication, language exercises, and virtual tutoring (Yan, [Citation2023](#)), research on their application in assessment is limited. Some studies (Nazari et al., [Citation2021](#)) have explored AI writing tools, with little emphasis on evaluating writing. Although this study is situated within an ELT setting, the findings have broader implications for educators across disciplines, shedding light on the potential benefits and limitations of integrating AI-driven grading systems. Insights from this analysis could inform future research on AI in educational assessment and guide best practices for implementation in diverse contexts. This study aims to investigate the use of GPTs by instructors for assessment, answering the question: How do the assessments generated by AI GPTs compare to those of HRs in terms of accuracy, reliability, and consistency?
## Methods
### Participants and context
Fifty annotated bibliographies written by graduate students attending English language courses at two STEM universities in South Korea were collected. The texts were gathered from three courses focusing on academic rhetoric for scientific purposes. Students consented to the use of their submissions for research. Each course included an assignment requiring students to write annotated bibliographies for their research. The texts were submitted during the 2021–2022 academic year. The curriculum emphasised key aspects of annotated bibliographies, including content, academic tone and vocabulary, structure, and grammar.
### Study design
The rubric used in this study was based on existing rubrics from the courses (see Appendix). While minor adjustments were implemented in format and language to enhance usability across HRs, ChatGPT3.5, and ChatGPT4, the fundamental criteria remained consistent. The rubric included four criteria: content demonstrating understanding of the topic, academic tone/vocabulary appropriate for graduate-level writing, structure appropriate for academic writing, and grammar/sentence structure showing variety and accuracy. Each criterion was worth up to 2.5 points in 0.5 increments, totalling a maximum score of 10.
### Procedure
Three instructors, the researchers-one from each of the two participating universities and an additional instructor from an unrelated university – served as HRs. HRs, aged 35 to 40, share similar backgrounds in ELT with approximately a decade of experience in STEM HE institutions. Each HR independently evaluated the texts using the rubric, replicating the grading behaviour typical of assessing student submissions in a classroom setting. Upon completion of the evaluations, the scores were recorded on a shared document.
### Measurement
To collect data on the use of ChatGPT as an assessment tool, the researchers independently entered the rubric and student texts into the two GPT models, simulating the use of GPTs by a typical instructor. This resulted in three datasets per model, as displayed in Figure 1. During data collection, anomalous scoring patterns were observed. When inputting student texts consecutively without re-entering the rubric, the GPTs deviated from the rubric instructions. Deviations included non-standard scoring increments, missing scores for specific criteria, or scores outside the given range. In response, the rubrics and texts were re-entered to ensure accurate scoring within the prescribed criteria. These adaptations were regarded as indicative of behaviours that would be typically employed by instructors without prior background utilising GPTs for AES.

### Analysis
To assess accuracy, reliability, and consistency of assessments, statistical analyses were conducted using SPSS. Descriptive statistics, including mean, standard deviation (SD), range, and median, provided an overview of the ratings’ distribution. Normality was tested using Q–Q plots and the Shapiro–Wilk test. Due to the non-normal distribution in the GPT datasets, parametric tests (e.g. paired t-tests, Pearson’s correlation, and linear regression) were not appropriate. Consequently, non-parametric analyses were implemented. The Mean of Absolute Deviation (MAD) was used to evaluate the agreement and accuracy among the raters. Furthermore, the Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC 3) was calculated using a two-way random effects model with absolute agreement to quantify the reliability between the raters (Koo & Li, [Citation2016](#)). Spearman’s correlation coefficient was utilised to assess the strength of correlation and significance (p-value) of relationships between raters (Akoglu, [Citation2018](#)).
## Results
The table below summarises descriptive statistics for assessments by HRs and ChatGPT models. While the study assessed ChatGPT3.5, the label of GPT3 was adopted for the Results section to denote the 3.5 model. The metrics provided an overview of how each rater evaluated student texts across various criteria, including group averages. Subsequent sections provide statistical analyses to compare accuracy, reliability, and consistency among raters.
### Means
The HRs’ mean scores were similar, with slight variation, compared to those of GPT3 and GPT4. Although Table 1 shows that HRA is slightly more generous compared to HRB and HRC, consistency among the HRs is relatively strong. In comparison, GPT3 and GPT4 consistently gave higher scores than the HRs. However, the scores among GPT3A, GPT3B, and GPT3C were very similar, indicating consistency. GPT4 showed more variability compared to GPT3 and the HRs. GPT4A’s scores were higher than GPT4B and GPT4C. Overall, the GPTs had higher means, both individually and averaged, compared to the HRs, suggesting GPTs were more generous in their evaluations.

### Standard deviations
The individual SDs among the GPTs covered a broader range compared to the HRs. The difference of HRs’ SD was 0.23, compared to GPT3’s SD with a difference of 0.34, and GPT4 with a difference of 0.32. When averaging, GPT3 and GPT4 had narrower deviations than the HRs.
### Median
By comparing median scores, a tendency towards higher scores in GPT datasets implied skewed evaluations. GPT3 showed its data centres around 9.5 and GPT4 centres around 9.5, 8, and 8. Medians of GPTs were markedly higher, except for GPT4B and GPT4C, in comparison to the HRs with medians of 8, 7.5, and 7.5.
Normality was assessed using the Shapiro–Wilk test and Q–Q plots. Table 2 shows the results of the Shapiro–Wilk test: HR scores appeared normally distributed (all \*p\* > .05), while GPT3 and GPT4 were not normally distributed (all \*p\* < .05), apart from GPT4C (\*p\* = .159). The results of the HRs indicated consistent, predictable, and standardised rating behaviour, suggesting similar judgement criteria. In contrast, GPT scores, apart from GPT4C, showed varied rating behaviour and were skewed. Q–Q plots revealed a ceiling effect in the GPT datasets, further indicating deviation from HRs.

Inter-rater variability was calculated using the MAD, shown in Table 3, between pairs of raters and categorised into four ranges: <0.5 (excellent), 0.5–1.0 (good), 1.0–1.5 (fair), and >1.5 (weak). Values were colour-coded: green for excellent and good, yellow for fair. Although no excellent values were observed, all MAD values between HRs were less than 1.0, indicating good alignment. Among GPTs, only GPT 4B showed good alignment with all HRs, achieving a MAD of 0.74 with HRA, within the threshold for good HR alignment. GPT4B’s alignment with HRB and HRC was slightly above this threshold. GPT4C also showed good alignment with HRA but slightly higher than values between HRs.

Table 4 displays the ICC3 by two-way random and absolute agreement between raters. Based on the 95% confidence interval of the ICC estimate: <0.5 indicates poor reliability, 0.5–0.75 moderate, 0.75–0.9 good, and >0.9 excellent (Koo & Li, [Citation2016](#)). HRs demonstrated a statistically significant high level of agreement, suggesting evaluations were based on differences in annotated bibliographies rather than random errors. GPT3 showed moderate agreement, while GPT 4 had poor agreement, indicating inconsistent evaluations. Low ICC values between HRs and GPTs suggest GPTs do not align with HR assessment standards.

Table 5 shows the strength of correlation and significance between raters using Spearman’s Correlation Coefficients. According to Akoglu ([Citation2018](#)) guidelines, the strength of the correlation values were interpreted as follows: 0.7 and above indicated a strong correlation, 0.4 to 0.6 indicated a moderate correlation, 0.3 to 0.1 indicated a weak correlation, and 0 indicated zero correlation. All three HRs demonstrated strong correlation and significance when compared to one another indicating a high level of agreement in their assessments. Moreover, GPT4B showed strong and significant correlations with each of the HRs. Comparing HR averages and GPT4 averages revealed a statistically significant and strong correlation coefficient of 0.739. Overall, these findings suggested that the HRs exhibit consistently high levels of agreement among themselves and demonstrated strong agreement with specific GPT datasets. However, most correlations between GPTs and HRs were weaker, indicating that the GPTs were ineffective in generating evaluations that correlate with human assessments.

## Observations
An unexpected finding was that raters observed occasional deviations in the scores generated by GPT models, which did not align with the rubric’s instructions, as outlined in the Methods section. This variation, which occurred during the evaluation process, reflects instances of misalignment between the scores produced by the models and the established criteria. Similar challenges regarding the accurate comprehension of input by AI systems have been reported in other studies (Luo et al., [Citation2023](#)). These results emphasise that, while accuracy, reliability, and consistency are critical for the deployment of GPTs as AES, the model’s ability to comprehend and interpret user-provided information is equally vital. If GPTs struggle to process instructions accurately, this could undermine user trust and result in decreased adoption.
## Discussion
This research aimed to assess the capabilities of using GPTs as AES by conducting statistical analyses of scores given to 50 authentic student texts from three HRs, ChatGPT3.5, and ChatGPT4. The study compared the performance of GPT utilisation against that of HRs, employing descriptive statistics and metrics of accuracy, reliability, and consistency.
### Descriptive statistics and metrics
The descriptive statistics indicated significant disparities between AI and HR evaluations. Similar to Jackaria et al. ([Citation2024](#)), mean scores showed that both ChatGPT3.5 and ChatGPT4 consistently gave higher scores than the HRs, suggesting a tendency towards more generous evaluations. Additionally, the wider range of SDs among AI raters suggested greater variability scoring. These discrepancies indicate that AI evaluations may not fully capture the nuanced judgement criteria applied by HRs, potentially overestimating quality.
The normality analyses revealed inconsistencies between HRs and AI raters. HRs’ scores were normally distributed, indicating a predictable and standardised approach to evaluations. This normality suggested that the HRs applied similar standards and judgement criteria, leading to homogenous evaluations. In contrast, the non-normal distribution of ChatGPT3 and ChatGPT4, with the exception of ChatGPT4C, displayed significant variability in the AI’s rating behaviour. Skewed distributions and ceiling effects in the GPT datasets suggested that AI models do not demonstrate the consistency of HRs, leading to questionable reliability.
### Accuracy
To assess accuracy, MAD was calculated. The scores from three HRs were used to establish a benchmark for comparison. Neither ChatGPT3.5 nor ChatGPT4 were able to replicate the alignment seen among the HRs. ChatGPT3.5 had weak alignment across all three datasets. ChatGPT4 presented slightly better alignment than ChatGPT3.5 and even managed to achieve good alignment with one HR. Therefore, while stronger than ChatGPT3.5, ChatGPT4 was unable to consistently replicate the accuracy of HRs. These findings were consistent with similar work by Mizumoto and Eguchi ([Citation2023](#)), who also found that while using a GPT as an AES could produce a certain level of accuracy, it was not able to achieve the level of agreement between HRs.
### Reliability
Reliability, or the ability of raters to maintain a level of quality over multiple evaluations, was assessed using ICC3. HRs demonstrated high values, indicating strong agreement. In contrast, ChatGPT3.5 showed moderate reliability, suggesting consistent but less robust evaluations. ChatGPT4 exhibited significantly lower values, indicating poor consistency even within its own evaluations. Despite being more advanced, ChatGPT4’s reliability as an AES tool was lower than its predecessor, ChatGPT3.5. When compared directly with HRs, both ChatGPT3.5 and ChatGPT4 showed notably lower values, underscoring their limited reliability in evaluating writing. This suggests AI-generated writing assessments lack validity and reliability, echoing Dai et al. ([Citation2023](#)) and Jackaria et al. ([Citation2024](#)) regarding the unreliability of GPT assessments.
### Consistency
When compared with HRs, the majority of GPT datasets (excluding ChatGPT4B) exhibited weaker correlations, indicating challenges for AI models in replicating the consistent judgement observed among HRs, which supported the results by Shin and Lee ([Citation2024](#)). The significant correlation between the average ratings of HRs and ChatGPT4 suggests there may be potential in certain AI evaluations. However, the overall inconsistency underscores that GPTs have yet to achieve evaluations that align with human standards. These findings suggest limitations in the ability of GPT models to deliver consistent assessments.
### Educational implications
These findings suggest that AI could serve as a supplementary tool in assessment but cannot replace the human element. Instead, they can function as complementary tools, augmenting rather than replacing the nuanced evaluative capacities of human instructors. A promising avenue for integration involves hybrid models, wherein instructor assessments are supplemented by AES-generated feedback or, conversely, AES assessments are contextualised through instructor insights and feedback (Chen & Pan, [Citation2022](#)). Furthermore, studies suggest that GPT-based systems can provide more detailed, individualised feedback on students’ writing within specific disciplines (Dai et al., [Citation2023](#)). However, other research indicates that students demonstrate a clear preference for instructor feedback in language courses (Chen & Pan, [Citation2022](#)). This further highlights the potential of AI to enhance assessment practices, along with the indispensable role of human judgement in ensuring pedagogical quality.
The need for specialised prompt engineering further underscores the necessity for professional development for educators, enabling them to use AI tools effectively. In the context of using ChatGPT to evaluate writing, instructors could input past graded examples as a pre-training dataset in ChatGPT giving more weight to their personal grading preferences than the GPT’s corpus. Such methods could reduce the inaccuracies found in this study’s GPT-based evaluations. However, properly designing the prompt for this type of specialised task would require more training than the average teacher currently has. Thus, training programmes could be developed to help instructors understand how to craft prompts that optimise AI output and minimise inconsistencies.
Additionally, educators must be vigilant regarding the potential ethical implications of using AI in grading, including the transparency of AI systems, the possibility of bias, and the fairness of grading practices (Meyer et al., [Citation2023](#)). Instructors both within and beyond the field of ELT could benefit from guiding students to perceive imperfect output given by GPTs and teach how to use these tools as a writing assistant rather than a writing replacement, focusing on critical thinking skills while engaging with this technology. In sum, AI tools can play a vital role in enhancing educational practices, but they should be integrated in ways that preserve the human-centric nature of teaching and learning.
### Limitations
A primary limitation of this study is the use of the authors as the three HRs, all of whom share similar backgrounds and experience in ELT. Despite efforts to mitigate bias, such as anonymising student texts and using a standardised rubric, the homogeneity of the raters likely introduced scoring uniformities. This could affect the reliability of the results, as grading by raters with similar profiles may not fully represent how unrelated raters with more diverse backgrounds might score the same texts. Similar studies address this issue by training raters to enhance reliability and reduce potential biases (Chen & Pan, [Citation2022](#)).
The researchers’ limited prompt engineering skills likely impacted the output of scores. However, this project focused on instructor use of GPTs’ to grade texts using prompts typical of instructor-created course assignments, hence a straightforward prompt was chosen. A grading rubric was employed that resembles a rubric an instructor with limited expertise in prompt engineering might develop (Choi et al., [Citation2023](#); Dai et al., [Citation2023](#); Giray, [Citation2023](#); Xia et al., [Citation2024](#)). Future research may more actively incorporate prompt engineering.
Another limitation is the evolving nature of GPT models, which may present challenges in replication of this study. ChatGPT3.5, no longer receiving updates, functions as a relatively static program, though output may vary with modifications to creativity settings. This cannot be asserted for ChatGPT4, which undergoes ongoing updates that potentially enhance ChatGPT-4’s AES capabilities over time, introducing a variable that complicates the reproducibility and comparability of its performance in future studies.
## Conclusion
The research aimed to determine the ability to use ChatGPT3.5 and ChatGPT4 as an affordable alternative to cost-prohibitive AES software. This study found these programs currently cannot replicate human assessments. The GPTs consistently assigned higher grades than the HRs and failed to distinguish between high- and low-level texts. While this project focused on graduate-level academic texts, future research may explore ChatGPT3.5 or ChatGPT4’s ability to assess a broader range of texts.
ChatGPT3.5 and ChatGPT4 exhibit potential for diverse applications across various educational settings and disciplines. Their capabilities for providing automated feedback, assisting in grading, and supporting personalised learning experiences can enhance instructional practices in fields such as STEM, humanities, and social sciences. However, this study advises against employing ChatGPT3.5 as an AES. While ChatGPT4 demonstrates promise, it should be approached cautiously and implemented under heavy instructor oversight. Future developments may enhance the accuracy, consistency, and reliability of ChatGPT4 for AES, warranting further investigation.
