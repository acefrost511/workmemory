Evaluating the impact of an AI-integrated learning platform on student performance: a quasi-experimental study

Abstract
--------

The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into higher education has intensified interest in adaptive learning environments that support personalized instruction and self-regulated learning. However, empirical evidence regarding the pedagogical effectiveness of fully integrated AI-based platforms in practice-oriented, skill-intensive university courses remains limited. This study examines the effect of an AI-integrated learning platform on academic performance, cognitive engagement, and self-regulated learning, employing a quasi-experimental design. In this study, cognitive engagement is conceptualized as a key behavioral and cognitive manifestation of self-regulated learning within an AI-supported instructional environment. 120 undergraduate students enrolled in a university-level 3D modeling course participated in the study, forming an experimental group (*n* = 62) and a control group (*n* = 58). The intervention lasted 8 weeks. Data were collected through pre- and post-tests, rubric-based practical assignments, final course grades, and a mixed-format satisfaction questionnaire. Quantitative analyses included paired *t*-tests, Bowker’s test of symmetry, effect size calculations, and confidence intervals. Qualitative data were examined using thematic analysis. The results reveal statistically significant improvements in post-test performance and practical task proficiency in the experimental group (*p* < 0.05), accompanied by higher levels of perceived autonomy, adaptive support, and self-regulated learning. The findings suggest that a fully integrated AI learning ecosystem can function as a metacognitive scaffold, enhancing strategic learning behaviors and cognitive engagement in applied digital skills education. The study contributes empirical evidence to ongoing discussions on the pedagogical role of AI in higher education and outlines implications for instructional design and future research.

1 Introduction
--------------

In recent years, the integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into higher education has become a central topic of academic discourse, driven by the rapid expansion of adaptive learning systems, automated assessment tools, and intelligent tutoring technologies. These tools have been shown to personalize learning trajectories, enhance instructional efficiency, and improve student engagement by providing real-time analytics and individualized feedback ([Alotaibi, 2024](#B1); [Mojolou et al., 2024](#B10)). AI-powered systems can process large volumes of educational data to predict learning difficulties, optimize task sequencing, and adapt the complexity of instructional materials to students’ cognitive needs. Such mechanisms align with key principles of self-regulated learning theory, which emphasizes goal-setting, progress monitoring, and metacognitive control ([Peng et al., 2019](#B12)). At the same time, recent research also highlights several challenges associated with AI implementation in education. Some studies report potential negative consequences, such as a decline in educational quality when AI tools are misused or poorly integrated ([Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019](#B16)). A review of over 150 publications reveals that many AI tools prioritize technical capabilities over pedagogical or ethical considerations, leaving questions of instructional value underexplored ([Holmes et al., 2019](#B7)). Systems such as AIIA illustrate that while AI tools can enhance personalization, they must be implemented within a clear pedagogical framework to avoid unintended consequences ([Sajja et al., 2024](#B13)). Despite growing interest in AI-enhanced learning, a clear research gap remains: relatively few empirical studies have evaluated the pedagogical effectiveness of AI-integrated platforms in practical, skill-oriented university courses, particularly in fields that require hands-on digital competencies, such as 3D modeling. Existing studies often focus on technical aspects rather than measurable learning outcomes, student autonomy, or user experience in real academic environments. Within this theoretical context, cognitive engagement is conceptualized as a key behavioral and cognitive manifestation of self-regulated learning, reflecting learners’ active involvement in planning, monitoring, and regulating their learning processes in AI-supported instructional environments.

Recent years have witnessed an unprecedented expansion of research on AI-integrated learning environments, particularly with the emergence of generative AI and large language models in higher education. Recent studies highlight the growing role of AI in supporting personalized learning, automated feedback, and adaptive instructional design in university contexts ([Kasneci et al., 2023](#B9); [Zawacki-Richter et al., 2024](#B15); [Crompton and Burke, 2023](#B5)). These developments indicate a shift toward fully integrated AI-supported learning ecosystems rather than isolated digital tools.

The AI-integrated platform examined in this study was designed as a supplementary instructional module intended to support instructors, provide students with opportunities for self-paced practice, enhance time-management flexibility, and foster autonomous learning through adaptive feedback and personalized task sequencing. These design principles guided both the implementation of the platform and the evaluation framework adopted in the study.

### 1.1 Research problem, research questions, and hypotheses

Despite growing interest in AI-enhanced learning environments, current research lacks empirical evidence on the pedagogical effectiveness of AI-integrated platforms in practice-oriented courses. Existing studies predominantly focus on technical capabilities, while the impact of adaptive algorithms, automated assessment, and intelligent feedback on measurable academic performance, student motivation, and self-regulated learning remains insufficiently explored. This gap is especially evident in disciplines requiring complex cognitive processing and hands-on mastery, such as 3D modeling.

Research problem: There is insufficient empirical understanding of how AI-integrated educational platforms influence academic performance, motivation, and self-regulated learning in practice-oriented higher education courses. Therefore, an AI-integrated educational platform was developed to enhance students’ academic performance, learning motivation, engagement, and self-regulation skills.

Research objective: to evaluate the effectiveness of an AI-integrated educational platform in improving academic performance, motivation, engagement, and self-regulated learning skills in a university-level 3D modeling course.

Research questions (RQs):

> *RQ1*: Does the use of an AI-integrated educational platform significantly improve students’ academic performance compared to traditional instruction?

> *RQ2*: How does the platform affect students’ motivation and engagement in learning?

> *RQ3*: Does the platform support the development of self-regulated learning skills (goal-setting, progress monitoring, metacognition)?

> *RQ4*: How do students perceive the usability, adaptive, and overall learning experience of the AI-enhanced platform?

Research hypotheses (H):

> *H1*: Students using the AI-integrated platform will demonstrate significantly higher post-test performance than students in the control group.

> *H2*: The AI-integrated platform will have a positive effect on students’ learning motivation and engagement.

> *H3*: The platform will enhance students’ self-regulated learning skills.

> *H4*: Students in the experimental group will report higher satisfaction and more positive perceptions of usability and adaptivity.

These research questions and hypotheses provide a clear analytical direction and are aligned with the theoretical foundation discussed in the Introduction, namely, self-regulated learning theory, with cognitive engagement considered as its central operational component.

The educational activity examined in this study is grounded in self-regulated learning (SRL) theory, which provides the primary conceptual framework for the platform’s design. The platform emphasizes goal-setting, progress monitoring, adaptive feedback, and metacognitive reflection, which are operationalized through AI-driven recommendations and learning analytics. Within this framework, cognitive engagement is treated as an observable indicator of learners’ self-regulatory processes in practice-oriented digital learning tasks. Self-regulated learning provides the conceptual foundation for the platform’s emphasis on goal-setting, progress monitoring, adaptive feedback, and metacognitive reflection, which are operationalized through AI-driven recommendations and learning analytics. Cognitive load theory informs the adaptive sequencing and personalization of learning tasks, ensuring that instructional complexity is dynamically aligned with learners’ prior knowledge to support effective cognitive processing. Activity-based learning principles guide the integration of practice-oriented tasks that require active engagement and the application of knowledge in authentic digital contexts.

2 Literature review
-------------------

### 2.1 AI-integrated learning platforms: global developments

Several large-scale adaptive learning platforms have been introduced worldwide. Smart Sparrow and Squirrel AI provide adaptive feedback and analytics dashboards; however, research indicates that the effectiveness of adaptive learning technologies is strongly influenced by factors such as teacher preparation, instructional design quality, and implementation facilitators ([Simon and Zeng, 2024](#B14)). These studies demonstrate that AI platforms can support differentiated instruction but require alignment with pedagogical models and learner characteristics.

Recent studies also highlight the pedagogical effectiveness of intelligent educational systems in higher education. AI-powered learning platforms have been shown to enhance student engagement, personalize learning paths, and support instructors in monitoring academic progress, thereby improving learning outcomes ([Ashimbekova and Bondarenko, 2025](#B3)). These studies underscore the potential of AI-driven educational technologies to improve teaching and learning processes while aligning academic content with professional requirements.

Recent research also emphasizes the transformative impact of generative AI and large language models on higher education. Studies published in 2023–2025 demonstrate that AI-supported learning platforms can enhance personalization, automate feedback processes, and support data-driven

instructional decision-making ([Kasneci et al., 2023](#B9); [Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019](#B16)). These findings suggest that AI-integrated ecosystems are increasingly viewed as comprehensive pedagogical infrastructures rather than supplementary technological tools.

### 2.2 AI for assessment and feedback

Recent studies explore the use of large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 and related models for evaluating open-ended student responses and programming tasks; research has also highlighted concerns about consistency, bias, and explainability in these automated assessments ([Jauhiainen and Garagorry Guerra, 2024](#B8)). Automated essay scoring systems, including those based on large language models, have been shown in multiple studies to achieve high agreement with human raters, but their effectiveness varies across contexts and highlights ongoing challenges such as consistency, bias, and the need for domain-specific adaptation ([Emirtekin, 2025](#B6)). This study investigates the impact of AI in educational settings on academic outcomes, including changes in grades, engagement, and personalized learning assessments as measurable results of AI implementation in learning environments ([Ateeq et al., 2024](#B4)). Overall, existing systems effectively reduce grading workload but vary in precision, transparency, and contextual understanding.

### 2.3 AI-enhanced personalized learning and student behavior modeling

Research indicates that adaptive algorithms can enhance motivation and engagement when aligned with cognitive and motivational learning principles; adaptive learning platform features have been shown to influence self-regulated learning and engagement, supporting overall educational quality ([Ouyang, 2025](#B11)). The research also considers how artificial intelligence can be used to transform assessment methods and personalize learning paths through adaptive learning, with implications for student performance and the analysis of large student data sets, emphasizing enhancements in academic outcomes via AI-driven adaptive systems ([Arwa, 2025](#B2)). However, most studies emphasize technical performance rather than pedagogical value, leaving questions about student agency, metacognition, and long-term learning habits underexplored. Emerging literature further highlights the pedagogical potential of generative AI systems for supporting metacognition, adaptive feedback, and student engagement in higher education contexts. However, empirical research examining the implementation of fully integrated AI learning environments in practice-oriented courses remains limited, particularly in applied digital skills education.

### 2.4 Gaps identified in previous research

A critical examination of the existing literature reveals several unresolved issues that limit the generalizability and practical relevance of current research on AI-driven educational platforms. First, empirical evidence from practice-oriented and skill-based courses remains limited. Most studies concentrate on domains such as mathematics, language learning, and other theoretical subjects, while creative and applied fields-including 3D modeling, engineering design, and other hands-on disciplines- are still underrepresented. This imbalance constrains our understanding of how adaptive and AI-based systems function in learning contexts that require procedural knowledge, creativity, and iterative problem-solving.

In addition, the influence of AI-supported personalization on self-regulated learning remains insufficiently explored. Although personalization is frequently cited as a key advantage of adaptive learning technologies, relatively few studies systematically examine its effects on learners’ metacognitive processes, such as planning, monitoring, and self-evaluation. As a result, it remains unclear whether adaptive systems genuinely foster self-regulation or merely optimize short-term performance outcomes. Methodological limitations further compound these gaps. Moreover, mixed-methods approaches that combine objective performance data with qualitative insights into user experience remain relatively rare, limiting a comprehensive understanding of learning processes and outcomes.

Finally, current research often provides insufficient attention to students’ subjective perceptions and experiences. While system efficiency and performance gains are frequently emphasized, learners’ perceptions of usability, adaptivity, motivation, and trust in AI-driven systems receive comparatively less scrutiny. Contexts where learners and instructors need to understand and trust adaptive interventions. Although the body of research on AI in education is rapidly expanding, many studies remain conceptual or exploratory, focusing on perceptions, technical capabilities, or ethical considerations. Empirical evaluations of fully integrated AI learning ecosystems embedded within authentic university courses are still relatively scarce. Addressing this gap is therefore essential for advancing evidence-based implementation of AI in higher education.

### 2.5 Contribution of the present study

This study addresses the identified gaps by: conducting a controlled experiment (experimental vs. control group) in a practice-oriented 3D modeling course; evaluating both quantitative outcomes and qualitative perceptions of adaptivity, usability, and motivation; analyzing the effects of AI-generated feedback, automated assessment, and personalized recommendations on self-regulation; applying a mixed-methods design, strengthening methodological rigor; situating the results within established theoretical frameworks, primarily self-regulated learning theory, while using cognitive load theory as a complementary lens to interpret how adaptive learning design supports cognitive engagement and effective task performance.. Thus, this work contributes new empirical evidence on the pedagogical value of AI-integrated educational platforms in applied digital disciplines.

3 Research design and methods
-----------------------------

The study employed a mixed-methods pretest-posttest design with experimental and control groups. This approach enabled the integration of quantitative measures of learning outcomes with qualitative insights into students’ perceptions of the AI-integrated learning environment ([Peng et al., 2019](#B12)).

The experimental component of the study analyzes the organizational features, implementation methodology, and key outcomes of piloting the AI-integrated educational platform, which was embedded into the academic process at Astana IT University. The platform is designed to support the development of a personalized, adaptive digital learning environment that enhances instruction quality and improves student performance. Employing multiple AI models with differentiated roles allowed the study to align specific model capabilities with distinct pedagogical and assessment objectives, thereby strengthening methodological rigor.

### 3.1 Educational platform overview

The developed platform is designed to host digital courses, interactive assignments, assessment materials, and tools for monitoring learning outcomes. Its structure includes two access levels: one for teachers and one for students. Each has differentiated permissions to ensure flexibility in managing the learning process and protecting personal data.

### 3.2 Integration of artificial intelligence functionality

The educational platform incorporates several modules based on artificial intelligence (AI), each designed to address specific problems within the learning process.

The platform leverages state-of-the-art language models, including GPT-3.5, GPT-4, and Gemini, to generate content and assess learner responses. Each model is configured with specific parameters, including temperature, max token length, and response diversity, to balance creativity and accuracy. The use of two different large language models was a deliberate design choice aimed at functional specialization rather than redundancy. ChatGPT (GPT-3.5/GPT-4) was selected for tasks requiring natural language generation, formative feedback, and interactive question generation due to its strengths in dialogic interaction and pedagogical explainability. In contrast, the Gemini model was employed for rubric-based assessment and final grading, where consistency, structured output, and alignment with predefined evaluation criteria were prioritized. This separation of functions reduced the risk of systematic bias associated with relying on a single model and increased the robustness of the AI-supported instructional workflow.

Assessments are automatically generated based on predefined learning objectives and evaluation criteria, ensuring consistency and alignment with educational standards. Protecting learner privacy is a core principle. Personal data is safeguarded through encryption and secure access protocols. The subsequent section examines the integration of artificial intelligence across the platform’s various functional modules.

#### 3.2.1 Automatic question generation

To improve the effectiveness of formative assessment and active knowledge retrieval, the platform employs the large-scale GPT-3.5 language model (OpenAI). ChatGPT was selected for this functionality due to its demonstrated effectiveness in generating pedagogically coherent open-ended questions and providing adaptive, context-sensitive feedback in educational settings. The model processes lecture materials and automatically generates randomized open-ended questions, except for the option to rewind lecture content. Learners answer the given question in the designated window; if the answer is incorrect, the model allows multiple attempts and, in some cases, provides hints to guide them toward the correct answer. This functionality prevents mechanical memorization of fixed test banks and promotes a deeper understanding of the learning material ([Figure 2](#F2)).

#### 3.2.2 Assessment of practical assignments and final tests

The Gemini model (Google) is integrated into the system to assess students’ completion of practical assignments. This model was chosen for assessment-related tasks because of its capacity to produce stable, structured evaluations aligned with analytic rubrics, which is essential for ensuring consistency and objectivity in summative assessment. This module analyzes the correctness and completeness of submitted solutions using predefined rubrics aligned with the course objectives. In addition, Gemini automatically grades final tests and calculates students’ final overall grades. This approach ensures objectivity, consistency, and efficiency of the assessment process. [Figure 3](#F3) demonstrates the results of students’ practical assignments assessment as well as their final assessments. For methodological consistency, the final practical assignments and tests of both the experimental and control groups were evaluated using the same rubric-based criteria. The Gemini model was employed as a support tool for structured assessment and grading; however, course instructors retained primary responsibility for verifying and approving final grades. In the experimental group, Gemini-assisted assessment was fully integrated into the platform workflow, whereas in the control group, the model was used only during the final evaluation stage to ensure comparability of grading criteria across groups. This approach ensured objectivity, consistency, and alignment between automated and instructor-led evaluations. To ensure methodological consistency and fairness, the final practical assignments and tests of both the experimental and control groups were evaluated using identical rubric-based criteria. The Gemini model was employed for structured assessment and preliminary grading in both groups. However, course instructors retained primary responsibility for reviewing, verifying, and approving all final grades. This combined approach ensured consistency of evaluation standards while maintaining pedagogical oversight and minimizing potential grading bias.

### 3.3 Experimental setup

As part of the research, a comprehensive set of activities was conducted, including an analysis of relevant national and international academic and methodological literature, testing, monitoring, registering academic performance, and conducting both pilot and pedagogical experiments.

A quasi-experimental, non-randomized pretest-posttest control-group design was used. The sample included 120 second-year undergraduate students enrolled in the “3D modeling” course within the “Media Technologies” program at the School of Creative Industries of Astana IT University. Inclusion criteria:

* enrolment in the program;
* no prior professional experience with Blender (exclusion: portfolio or professional courses). Sampling followed a cluster allocation based on existing academic cohorts.

Two intact cohorts were assigned as follows: one control group (*n* = 58) and one experimental group (*n* = 62). Although randomization was not feasible, the groups were equivalent in terms of gender and age. and pretest performance. The experimental group completed the “3D Modeling” course using the AI-integrated educational platform, while the control group followed the standard curriculum, including:

* **–**
  
  lecture-based instruction;
* **–**
  
  supervised practical exercises;
* **–**
  
  a final LMS-based test.

The control group did not complete additional applied or scenario-based assignments, but total classroom time was identical across groups.

To ensure methodological consistency, both groups used the same lecture materials, course structure, and baseline practical tasks. The only difference was the AI-enhanced adaptive practice available exclusively to the experimental group.

The pedagogical experiment lasted for one academic module (8 weeks). Each week consisted of a 90-min lecture and a 120-min practical session. Students in the experimental group additionally completed 2–3 AI-enhanced tasks per week.

#### 3.3.1 Measurement instructions

A comprehensive set of measurement instruments was employed to assess the effectiveness of the instructional intervention, including:

* **–**
  
  pre-and post-tests comprising 40 items designed to measure competencies in navigation, modeling, texturing, animation, and rendering in Blender;
* **–**
  
  practical assignments evaluated using standardized analytic rubrics;
* **–**
  
  the final course grade represents an aggregate indicator of students’ theoretical and practical performance.

To ensure fairness and comparability between groups, the final performance evaluation followed identical rubric-based criteria. AI-assisted grading served as a decision-support mechanism, while final validation of results remained under instructor supervision.

#### 3.3.2 Validity and reliability

To ensure the psychometric soundness of the instruments, the following procedures were undertaken:

* **–**
  
  content validation of test items by two domain experts specializing in 3D modeling;
* **–**
  
  departmental approval of the rubrics used to evaluate practical assignments, ensuring standardized assessment criteria.
* **–**
  
  assessment of internal consistency of the questionnaire, which demonstrated high reliability (Cronbach’s α = 0.87).

To maintain comparability between groups, the final performance evaluation was conducted using the same rubric-based criteria. AI-assisted grading served as a structured support tool, while final validation and approval of grades remained under instructor supervision.

#### 3.3.3 Statistical analysis

The statistical analysis involved:

* **–**
  
  assumption checks for parametric testing, including the Shapiro—Wilk test for normality and Levene’s test for homogeneity of variances;
* **–**
  
  paired *t*-tests to compare pre- and post-test performance;
* **–**
  
  Bowker’s test of symmetry to examine transitions between categorical proficiency levels.
* **–**
  
  computation of effect sizes (Cohen’s *d*) and 95% confidence intervals;
* **–**
  
  qualitative analysis of open-ended questionnaire responses using thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke).

### 3.4 Data collection and evaluation

The purpose of the testing phase was to assess students’ initial knowledge in the “3D Modeling” course, with a particular focus on their proficiency with Blender. A diagnostic pre-test consisting of 40 questions was administered, covering core thematic areas: navigation and interface, 3D object modeling, texturing, basic animation, and rendering processes.

To interpret the results, a performance scale was used to categorize the level of skill proficiency:

High level (75–100%)—demonstrates a strong understanding of Blender’s principles, confident use of both basic and advanced features, and practical experience in 3D modeling.

Above average (60–74%)—indicates confident use of core tools, though some difficulties may arise with advanced or non-standard tasks.

Average level (40–59%) — reflects familiarity with the interface and basic functions, but lacks consistent and structured use of the software at a more advanced level.

Low level (20–39%)—suggests a limited understanding of the software’s functionality and difficulty performing even standard operations.

Very low level (0–19%)—points to minimal or no prior knowledge or experience with Blender.

The analysis of the initial test results provided insight into the students’ baseline preparedness, forming a reference point for comparison with post-course performance indicators following completion of the adaptive AI-integrated program.

### 3.5 Data analysis

The results of pre- and post-course testing, assignment completion, final course grades, and student feedback collected via questionnaires were analyzed to assess the effectiveness of the implemented AI-integrated educational platform. The combination of rubric-based automated assessment and instructor verification ensured reliability and transparency of the evaluation process.

Quantitative analysis enabled comparison of the academic progress of students in the experimental group, who used the platform, with that of students in the control group, who followed a traditional instructional format.

Qualitative data from both open- and closed-ended questionnaire responses were analyzed to understand student satisfaction, level of their motivation, perceived ease of use of the platform’s interface, accessibility of materials, and perceptions of the personalization of the learning process.

This comprehensive approach to data collection and interpretation enabled a well-rounded evaluation of the platform’s impact on the learning process.

### 4.4 Analysis of students’ subjective evaluations regarding the usability and impact of the platform on the learning process

To obtain qualitative feedback from students in the experimental group, a survey was conducted to assess key aspects of the educational platform developed using artificial intelligence technologies. All 62 students from the experimental group participated in the survey.

The evaluation criteria included the following:

* Usability and navigation of the interface
* Clarity and accessibility of learning materials
* Effectiveness, adaptability to students’ level of knowledge
* Quality of automated feedback and assignment assessment
* Impact on learning motivation
* Overall satisfaction with the learning process

The analysis of student responses revealed the following results:

Interface usability: 87% of students reported that the platform’s interface was intuitive and easy to navigate. Most participants positively noted the logical structure of the sections and the ease of access to course materials.

Content accessibility: 82% of respondents indicated that the 3D modeling course materials were clearly presented, included illustrative examples, and were accompanied by helpful video instructions.

Platform adaptability: 76% of students stated that the system’s ability to adjust to their individual knowledge levels, through task customization, recommendations, and hints, contributed to a deeper understanding of complex topics.

AI-based feedback: 79% of students emphasized the usefulness of automated comments and prompt grading of assignments, which enabled them to address mistakes promptly.

Motivation: 74% of students reported that the platform encouraged more active engagement in the learning process compared to traditional methods.

Overall satisfaction: 85% of students expressed satisfaction with the platform’s learning experience and would recommend it for other courses.

A comparison of objective learning outcomes and students’ subjective evaluations reveals a consistent pattern. The observed improvement in post-course performance and knowledge distribution corresponds with students’ positive perceptions of platform usability, adaptive support, and feedback quality. In particular, students who reported higher levels of perceived adaptivity and timely feedback also demonstrated notable progress in practical and theoretical performance. Although the study

did not aim to establish direct causal correlations between individual survey responses and performance scores, the convergence of objective and subjective indicators strengthens the overall interpretation of the platform’s effectiveness.

Thus, the AI-integrated platform demonstrated its effectiveness not only in improving academic outcomes but also in enhancing the quality of educational interaction, personalizing the learning experience, and increasing student motivation.

5 Findings and discussion
-------------------------

This study examined the effectiveness of an AI-integrated educational platform in terms of students’ academic performance, learning motivation and engagement, development of self-regulated learning skills, and learners’ perceptions of usability and overall learning experience.

In response to RQ1, the findings indicate that the use of the AI-integrated educational platform had a statistically significant positive effect on students’ academic performance compared to traditional instruction. Students in the experimental group achieved higher post-test scores than those in the control group. These findings are consistent with previous research indicating that

AI-supported adaptive learning environments can improve academic performance through personalized feedback and task sequencing ([Peng et al., 2019](#B12); [Ashimbekova and Bondarenko, 2025](#B3)). Similar positive effects of AI-integrated platforms on measurable learning outcomes have been reported in recent higher education studies ([Ateeq et al., 2024](#B4)), supporting the interpretation that adaptive AI tools can enhance both instructional efficiency and student achievement. This improvement can be attributed to adaptive task sequencing, automated feedback, and personalized learning support provided by the platform’s AI components. From the perspective of cognitive load theory, these adaptive mechanisms may have reduced extraneous cognitive load by aligning task complexity with learners’ prior knowledge. This likely enabled students to devote greater cognitive resources to meaningful learning activities, thereby supporting improved performance. Therefore, Hypothesis H1 is supported.

Regarding RQ2, the results demonstrate that the platform positively influenced students’ learning motivation and engagement. Interactive learning activities, timely AI-generated feedback, and personalized recommendations encouraged more active participation and sustained interest in the learning process. This result aligns with studies demonstrating that AI-driven adaptive learning systems can strengthen student engagement and motivation when feedback is timely and personalized ([Ouyang, 2025](#B11)). Previous research has shown that intelligent tutoring and adaptive recommendation systems can foster sustained learner involvement by supporting autonomy and self-monitoring, which is consistent with the patterns observed in the present study. These findings confirm that Hypothesis H2 is supported.

With respect to RQ3, the study shows that the AI-enhanced platform supported the development of self-regulated learning skills, including goal-setting, progress monitoring, and metacognitive awareness. Learning analytics dashboards and continuous performance feedback enabled students to better reflect on their learning progress and adjust their learning strategies accordingly. The observed development of self-regulated learning skills corresponds with existing literature emphasizing the role of adaptive learning analytics and AI-generated feedback in supporting metacognitive processes ([Ouyang, 2025](#B11); [Peng et al., 2019](#B12)). These studies highlight that personalized feedback and progress monitoring tools can function as scaffolds for planning, monitoring, and self-evaluation, reinforcing the interpretation of the current findings. Thus, Hypothesis H3 is confirmed.

In answer to RQ4, students in the experimental group reported generally positive perceptions of the platform’s usability, adaptivity, and overall learning experience. The platform was perceived as user-friendly, adaptive to individual learning needs, and supportive of effective learning. Positive student perceptions of usability and adaptive support are also consistent with prior research on AI-enhanced learning environments, which indicates that perceived usefulness and ease of use are critical factors influencing acceptance of educational technologies ([Holmes et al., 2019](#B7); [Simon and Zeng, 2024](#B14)). The present findings support these conclusions by demonstrating that students not only benefited academically but also reported a favorable learning experience. These results indicate higher student satisfaction with the AI-integrated platform, thereby supporting Hypothesis H4.

Overall, the findings suggest that AI-integrated educational platforms have the potential to support learning outcomes, student engagement, and self-regulated learning when implemented as supplementary instructional tools within an existing course structure. These results correspond with a growing body of research demonstrating the pedagogical effectiveness of AI-supported adaptive learning environments in higher education ([Holmes et al., 2019](#B7); [Ateeq et al., 2024](#B4)). Previous studies have shown that personalized feedback, learning analytics, and intelligent tutoring systems can significantly improve student performance, engagement, and self- regulated learning, which aligns with the patterns observed in the present study.

It should be noted that the AI-integrated platform in this study was implemented as a supplementary instructional module embedded within the existing course structure. The control group followed the standard curriculum without additional supplementary interventions. Therefore, the findings reflect a comparison with baseline instructional practice rather than with all possible forms of alternative supplementary instructional support.

The study did not include comparisons with alternative forms of supplementary instruction, such as additional tutoring sessions, instructor-led extra assignments, or other traditional support mechanisms. Therefore, the results should be interpreted as reflecting the contribution of the AI-integrated module relative to baseline instructional practice rather than as evidence of superiority over all other supplementary approaches. Future research may explore comparative effects between AI-based and other traditional support mechanisms.

Interpreting these results through the lens of self-regulated learning (SRL) theory suggests that automated feedback, adaptive recommendations, and access to progress analytics strengthened students’ metacognitive processes. The platform supported strategic planning, monitoring, and adjustment of learning behaviors, aligning with established SRL frameworks ([Ouyang, 2025](#B11)). Moreover, the experimental group’s improved practical performance aligns with increased cognitive engagement, which can be interpreted as an outcome of effective self-regulation and optimized cognitive load rather than as an independent theoretical framework.

Comparison with prior research indicates consistency with studies reporting positive effects of AI tools on student engagement and perceived usefulness in learning contexts ([Holmes et al., 2019](#B7)); research on AI-powered learning assistants ([Sajja et al., 2024](#B13)). Unlike many prior works focusing on isolated AI functions, the present study examines a fully integrated AI ecosystem—combining automated question generation, adaptive guidance, intelligent tutoring, and AI-driven assessment—embedded directly into course pedagogy. This design suggests that AI can function as a coherent instructional component rather than a collection of disconnected add-ons. By providing empirical evidence from a practice-oriented digital skills course, this study extends existing literature beyond predominantly theoretical contexts and contributes to a more comprehensive understanding of AI-integrated learning environments.

Accordingly, the present study primarily evaluates the functional role of the AI-integrated supplementary module in supporting student engagement, self-regulated learning processes, and user experience within the course context, rather than making broad claims about comprehensive pedagogical transformation.

An additional observation concerns students in the low and very low performance groups, whose learning gains were uneven and in some cases limited. This pattern may reflect the additional adaptation required to engage effectively with AI-supported and self-directed learning environments, particularly for learners with lower prior knowledge or weaker self-regulation skills. These findings suggest that AI-supported supplementary modules may not benefit all learners equally without additional scaffolding and instructor support. For some lower-performing students, the transition to adaptive and self-directed learning formats may require additional scaffolding and instructional support. Further research is needed to explore how AI-supported modules can be optimized to support learners with lower initial proficiency levels.

6 Limitation
------------

The study is subject to several limitations. First, random assignment was not feasible because intact class groups were used. Second, the sample was constrained to a single institution, a single cohort, and a single disciplinary context, limiting external validity. Third, the experimental and control groups differed in the amount of practice-oriented learning activities, potentially influencing performance outcomes. Fourth, the duration of the intervention was relatively short (8 weeks), restricting the ability to observe longer-term learning effects. Finally, part of the data relied on self-reported questionnaire responses, which may introduce response bias.

These factors may affect the generalizability of the findings and should be taken into account when interpreting the results.

7 Implications
--------------

Theoretical implications: the study extends current understanding of how integrated AI ecosystems activate self-regulated learning processes and manage cognitive load, thereby fostering higher levels of cognitive engagement in practice-oriented learning contexts. It suggests that AI can serve as a metacognitive scaffold, supporting learners’ strategic behavior rather than merely automating assessment.

Practical implications: the platform demonstrates potential to personalize instruction, enhance feedback quality, and support adaptive learning trajectories. The findings can inform institution-level strategies for scaling AI-supported learning tools across disciplines and programs. The consistency between objective performance indicators and subjective student perceptions suggests that AI-supported personalization not only improves measurable outcomes but is also experienced as pedagogically meaningful by learners.

8 Contribution of the paper
---------------------------

Despite methodological constraints, this study makes a meaningful contribution to the field of digital education and applied AI research. Its contribution can be articulated across several dimensions.

First, the paper provides an empirical evaluation of a fully integrated AI ecosystem that combines automated question generation, adaptive recommendations, AI-driven assessment, and intelligent tutoring within a unified instructional architecture. Unlike most prior work, which examines isolated AI tools, this study investigates the effectiveness of a holistic AI-enhanced environment embedded in course pedagogy.

Second, the findings extend existing knowledge on how AI-enabled systems support components of SRL. The results indicate that AI-generated feedback, learning analytics, and adaptive guidance strengthen key SRL mechanisms, including planning, monitoring, and self-correction.

Third, the study contributes to understanding how AI tools enhance cognitive engagement by supporting self-regulated learning processes and reducing extraneous cognitive load through adaptive instructional design.

Fourth, the paper offers a practical implementation model of AI integration within a professional digital-skills course (3D modeling), adding empirical data to a relatively underexplored domain where complex skill learning is required.

Finally, the study establishes a foundation for future theoretical and empirical research, including the development of a conceptual model for pedagogically grounded AI integration and broader multi-institutional studies to improve generalizability.

9 Conclusion and future research
--------------------------------

This study suggests that integrating a comprehensive AI-enhanced learning ecosystem into a 3D-modeling course may contribute to improvements in students’ academic performance, cognitive engagement, and self-regulated learning behaviors within the specific instructional context examined. The observed performance gains, strengthened metacognitive processes, and positive student perceptions highlight the potential of AI as a supportive and supplementary component of instructional design rather than as a comprehensive pedagogical replacement. The practical implications indicate that educators and institutions can use AI ecosystems to enhance personalization, assessment transparency, and instructional efficiency. Theoretically, the study provides a foundation for conceptualizing how AI can be embedded into holistic pedagogical models.

Future research should explore:

* **–**
  
  broader, more diverse samples across disciplines, institutions, and learner populations;
* **–**
  
  longitudinal designs to investigate the long-term development of SRL and cognitive competencies;
* **–**
  
  comparative evaluations of different AI-tool configurations;
* **–**
  
  richer qualitative methods to capture learners’ experiences in depth.

Overall, the study underscores the potential of integrated AI-supported environments to enhance digital skill development and contributes to the ongoing conversation on the pedagogical role of AI in higher education.
