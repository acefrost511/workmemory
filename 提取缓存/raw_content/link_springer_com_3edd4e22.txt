Abstract
--------

This study investigates the evolving landscape of Artificial Intelligence (AI) literacy, acknowledging AI's transformative impact across various sectors in the twenty-first century. Starting from AI's inception to its current pervasive role in education, everyday life, and beyond, this paper explores the relevance and complexity of AI literacy in the modern world. To evaluate the current state of the literature on AI literacy, a systematic literature review was conducted with the objective of identifying thematic and recent research trends. Through a rigorous selection process involving 323 records from databases such as Web of Science, SCOPUS, ERIC, and IEEE Xplore, 87 high-quality studies have been analysed to identify central themes and definitions related to AI literacy. Our findings reveal that AI literacy extends beyond technical proficiency to encompass ethical considerations, societal impacts, and practical applications. Key themes identified include the ethical and social implications of AI, AI literacy in K-12 education, AI literacy curriculum development, and the integration of AI in education and workplaces. The study also highlights the importance of AI literacy models and frameworks for structuring education across diverse learning environments, as well as the significance of AI and digital interaction literacy. Additionally, our analysis of publication trends indicates a strong growth in AI literacy research, particularly in China and the United States, reflecting the global urgency of addressing AI literacy in policy and education. Conclusively, the research underscores the importance of an adaptable, comprehensive educational paradigm that incorporates AI literacy, reflecting its diverse interpretations and the dynamic nature of AI. The study advocates for interdisciplinary collaboration in developing AI literacy programs, emphasizing the need to equip future generations with the knowledge, skills, and ethical discernment to navigate an increasingly AI-driven world.

Introduction
------------

From its inception in the mid-twentieth century, AI has journeyed from being a novel concept to becoming a pervasive technology that is intricately woven into the fabric of our society (Chen et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR16); Hwang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR38)). The roots of AI can be traced back to the 1940s, when Alan Turing, a British mathematician, laid the theoretical groundwork for intelligent machines with his pioneering work on the Bombe, an early electromechanical computer designed to crack the German Enigma code during World War II. Turing’s work inspired his famous 1950 paper *Computing Machinery and Intelligence*, which introduced the Turing Test as a way to assess a machine’s intelligence. Around the same time, science fiction writer Isaac Asimov, in his 1942 short story *Runaround*, articulated the Three Laws of Robotics, marking the cultural and ethical dimensions of AI's impact on society (Ranieri et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR101)).

The term "Artificial Intelligence" was officially coined in 1956 at the Dartmouth Conference, where John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, and others convened to discuss the possibility of machines simulating human intelligence (McCarthy, [2007](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR75)). This period, often referred to as the first AI spring, saw optimism and significant funding for AI research, leading to early successes such as Joseph Weizenbaum’s ([1966](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR129)) natural language processing program *ELIZA* and Herbert Simon's *General Problem Solver (*1959*)*. However, limitations in computing power and overly ambitious expectations led to the first AI winter in the 1970s, when both the U.S. and U.K. governments reduced their funding for AI research after a series of underwhelming results.

Despite these setbacks, the development of AI continued through the 1980s and 1990s, albeit at a slower pace, driven by advancements in Expert Systems—rule-based systems capable of performing complex tasks within limited domains, such as IBM's Deep Blue, which famously defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov in 1997. Yet, these systems lacked the adaptability and learning capabilities of true AI. The breakthrough came with the resurgence of artificial neural networks and the advent of deep learning in the 2000s (Boden, [2018](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR6)). By 2015, Google’s AlphaGo demonstrated the power of these techniques by defeating the world champion in Go, a game far more complex than chess, marking a new era of AI development.

Today, AI is not just a tool but an integral part of a continually evolving digital ecosystem. Modern AI technologies, such as machine learning, sensory perception, natural language processing, and computer vision, rely on vast amounts of data and computational power, enabled by the rise of cloud computing, big data analytics, and advanced hardware architectures (Russell & Norvig, [2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR104); Wang et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR126); Zawacki-Richter et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR143)). The shift from rule-based systems to adaptive, data-driven AI has transformed how AI interacts with the world, allowing machines to learn from data, recognize patterns, and make decisions with minimal human intervention. This evolution has positioned AI at the heart of industries such as healthcare, education, marketing, and autonomous systems, where its applications continue to expand (Chen et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR16); Hwang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR38)).

The rapid integration of AI into these fields has generated the emergence of AI literacy as a critical aspect of education. Scholars posit that AI education should be regarded with the same level of importance as traditional literacy skills like reading and writing, due to its broad interdisciplinary nature and growing influence (Kandlhofer et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR41)). AI literacy encompasses a broad set of competencies, including not only technical knowledge of AI mechanisms but also the ability to critically evaluate its societal impacts, ethical considerations, and applications in lifelong learning (Long & Magerko, [2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR70); Miao et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR78)). These skills are essential for effectively engaging with AI systems and for making informed decisions about their governance and application within the broader digital landscape.

As AI continues to evolve, the World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report suggests that by 2025, AI will significantly reshape the job market, displacing many roles while also creating new ones (World Economic Forum, [2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR135)). However, most individuals interact with AI-driven technologies without fully understanding the underlying principles or the ethical challenges, such as privacy concerns, biases, and surveillance, that accompany these innovations (Burgsteiner et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR7); Ghallab, [2019](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR30)). Given these profound impacts, there's a rising emphasis on being "AI literate"—a competence allowing individuals to critically engage with AI systems and concepts, facilitating effective collaboration and communication with AI (Long & Magerko, [2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR70); Ng et al., [2021a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR82), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR88)).

As a modern counterpart to foundational literacies like digital and media literacy (Calvani et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR8); Gilster, [1997](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR31); Livingstone, [2004](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR68)), AI literacy underscores the importance of understanding and interacting with AI in the context of the broader, rapidly evolving digital environment. Especially for non-experts, increasing AI literacy is as crucial as training AI experts, since this group will likely use, collaborate with, and coexist alongside AI technologies in daily life (Ng et al., [2021a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR82), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR88)).

### Rationale and Research Questions

As mentioned in the previous paragraph, the applications of AI are becoming increasingly numerous and pervasive. It can be difficult for both the typical user and the expert to detect the line between their cognitive talents and those provided by an AI program, due to the inherent "mimetic" character of human behaviour. The aforementioned factor can readily lead to ethical dilemmas, even without considering futuristic scenarios (Mökander et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR80)). These problems highlight how critical the issue of AI literacy (and related competences) is. However, there are several factors making it far from easy to define the concept of AI literacy. The challenge is primarily caused by the absence of a generally accepted definition of AI, or the subject matter for which literacy should be practiced. There are various research traditions within the subject of artificial intelligence as well as various interpretations of AI that vary in strength.

As an example of the increasing interest in AI literacy, the provided graphs illustrate the growth in the number of articles per year on this topic, as indexed by Scopus and Google Scholar (Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#Fig1)). The Scopus graph shows a consistent rise from 2 articles in 2016 to 62 articles in 2023, suggesting a steady growth in scholarly attention within the Scopus-indexed literature. The Google Scholar graph presents even a more pronounced surge, from 14 articles in 2016 to 1630 articles in 2023. The exponential growth reflected in the Google Scholar data points to a broader and more rapid increase in interest, likely capturing a wider array of publications, including those outside of traditional academic journals. Both graphs collectively signal that AI literacy is gaining momentum as a field of study, reflecting the escalating relevance of AI in various aspects of society and the recognition of the importance for individuals to be educated and proficient in AI concepts and applications.

Although interest in the field has grown, as we have observed, the systematic research in AI literacy still remains somewhat limited. Some scholars, such as Ng et al. ([2021a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR82), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR88)) and Long and Magerko ([2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR70)), have attempted to organize and assess this fragmented body of research literature through comprehensive explorative literature reviews. These efforts include approaching AI literacy from a broad perspective and striving to establish a universally acceptable definition of the concept. While the pioneering efforts of Ng and colleagues, as well as Long and Magerko, laid the foundational understanding of 'AI literacy' and initiated discussions on methodologies for its acquisition and associated considerations, they did not approach the literature systematically. Moreover, since their publications were made in 2020 and 2021, the academic discourse on AI literacy has advanced significantly. While other systematic literature reviews exist (Casal-Otero et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR12)), they are devoted to specific aspects of AI literacy (e.g., Ethical) and/or application context (e.g., K-12). Therefore, grounding on previous seminal works in the area, the current study aims to advance in the field by the systematic examination of the evolving definitions of AI literacy, the exploration of the variety of educational methodologies for its acquisition, and providing an in-depth examination of the critical issues, including the ethical ones, intertwined with it. Through this comprehensive analysis, we seek to offer a less fragmented and more updated representation of the conceptual components of AI literacy and its critical importance in today's digital landscape. Furthermore, understanding the geographical distribution of AI literacy research is crucial because it highlights the diverse educational, technological, and policy approaches taken by different countries. This insight is vital for educators and policymakers as it helps identify global trends, regional challenges, and the need for localized strategies in the development and implementation of AI literacy programs. Countries with robust AI policies and investments, such as China (State Council of China, [2017](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR17)) and the US (National AI Initiative Act, [2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR118)), offer valuable lessons in integrating AI literacy into national education systems, while highlighting the importance of adapting these efforts to fit local contexts and needs.

Specifically, the present study investigates the following three research questions:

* **RQ 1**: How have publications on AI developed over time, where are they coming from in terms of geographical distribution and what audience are they referring to?
* **RQ 2**: How does the research literature conceptualize and differentiate the definitions of AI literacy?
* **RQ 3**: What are the key themes dealt with in research studies related to AI literacy?

Methods
-------

### The Search Strategy and Manuscript Selection Process

The approach utilized for this study was a systematic literature review (SLR), adhering to the framework established by Cooper and colleagues ([2019](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR19)). The subsequent sections provide a concise overview and record the principal procedures applied within this research method.

The methodology conformed to the PRISMA guidelines (Moher et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR79)), employing a systematic and repeatable approach to analyse and synthesize existing studies, aiming to reduce mistakes and bias and thus, yielding more trustworthy outcomes (Cooper et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR19)). The process of the SLR was divided into five distinct phases: a) defining the problem and research queries, b) identifying relevant literature, c) evaluating the search outcomes, d) categorizing, decoding, and summarizing the findings, and e) disseminating the review findings. The search encompassed several academic databases, including Web of Science (Clarivate), ERIC (Institute of Education Sciences), IEEE Xplore (IEEE), and SCOPUS (Elsevier), conducted in March 2023. For a detailed account of the records obtained from each database, see Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#Fig2). Eventually, this resulted in the following complete search string: TITLE-ABS-KEY (((" artificial intelligence literac\*" OR "AI literac\*" OR "AI awareness" OR "AI readiness")) AND NOT ("machine learning") AND NOT ("computer-based learning") AND NOT ("intelligent tutoring system")). A subsequent search was conducted in September 2023, filtering out non-conforming articles based on the established exclusion criteria.

Five inclusion – exclusion criteria were formulated and applied during the abstract and full-text report reviews (refer to Table [1](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#Tab1)). The criteria were as follows: works that utilized the term AI literacy but concentrated on a different subject were omitted (criterion 1). Studies concerning other types of literacy, specifically reading and writing skills, were not considered (criterion 2). Editorials and books were not included due to their non-peer-reviewed nature (criterion 3). Articles that mentioned “AI literacy” but pertained to AI's application in specific fields unrelated to education were disregarded (criterion 4). Finally, publications not in English or Italian were excluded to prevent misinterpretation due to language barriers, as these were the languages the authors were proficient in (criterion 5).

The journey of study selection begins with the identification of studies from several databases: 211 from Web of Science, 293 from SCOPUS, 102 from ERIC, and 26 from IEEE Xplore, totalling 632 records. From this pool, records are pre-screened, resulting in the removal of 309 duplicate records, which is about 48% of the initial pool. This leaves 323 records to be screened, about 51% of the initial number. After screening, 199 records are excluded, which is approximately 61% of the records. This results in 124 reports (38,4%) being sought for retrieval. However, 4 reports cannot be retrieved, representing about 3.6% of those sought for retrieval. Of the 120 reports that are retrieved and assessed for eligibility, a number are excluded for various reasons: 15 for the first criterion (13.9% of reports assessed), 10 for the second (9.3%), and so forth, leaving 87 studies that are included in the review based on this assessment. Figure [2](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#Fig2) contains the details of the process.

Two researchers then independently examined the selected articles to verify their relevance to the aims of the current study with the support of Rayyan web application (Ouzzani et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR94); rayyan.ai), which is specifically designed to assist in the execution of literature reviews. In particular, on the basis of the predefined inclusion criteria, the two researchers analysed the abstracts, and for the most dubious studies even the full text, employing a method of blind selection. Rayyan was used to facilitate the screening process by highlighting main keywords from inclusion and exclusion criteria. The individual selections were compared and the level of agreement between the raters, quantified using Cohen’s kappa coefficient, was κ = 0.78 (Cohen, [1960](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR18)). This value indicates a substantial level of agreement between the researchers’ decisions, as classified by Landis and Koch ([1977](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR57)). The final number of the papers included in the systematic review was N = 87.

### Data Extraction and Coding

The identified articles were examined and discussed through a QUAN/QUAL research approach, addressing three research questions that explore the development, definition, and thematic content of AI literacy in academic publications. Starting with RQ1, our investigation charted the chronological progression and geographical dispersion of AI literacy literature and its targeted audience demographics. A systematic review underpinned our methodology, during which metadata extraction provided the necessary quantitative scaffolding. The extracted metadata with the help of descriptive statistic culminated in the creation of Table [2](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#Tab2), which lays out the foundational information of the surveyed articles, shedding light on the trajectory of AI literacy publications over time, their global genesis, and their analysis subjects.

For RQ3, a dual-strategy approach was implemented for the analysis. On one hand, a bibliometric network analysis was conducted to explore the major thematic clusters and their interrelationships, highlighting the comprehensive mapping of the research field. On the other hand, the analysis was simultaneously complemented by a thematic analysis, which involved coding the data to identify prevalent themes. While the selection of papers was performed by two independent researchers, the validity of the analyses was ensured by triangulating the results of both the bibliometric and thematic analyses. The comprehensive results have been encapsulated in Table [4](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#Tab4).

[Full size table](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w/tables/4)

The bibliometric network analysis elucidated the thematic skeleton of the field via a co-occurrence analysis (Van Eck & Waltman, [2007](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR123)) of the most frequent keywords (100) within the 87 identified papers. The co-occurrence of most common words assumes that keywords in the same context have close semantic relationships, allowing the discovery of key terms and their composition of communities. In observing complex networks, it is said that a network has a community structure if the nodes of the network can be easily grouped into sets of nodes in such a way that each set of nodes is densely connected internally (Fortunato, [2010](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR28)). The corpus was built by an automatically created dataset containing the metadata and the entire article text of selected papers, using a python script developed by the author. Then, using Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques (e.g., stop words removal, stemming), specifically applied by the authors, the dataset was cleaned of words not useful for the analysis (pronouns, prepositions, conjunctions). Finally, the bibliometric network analysis was conducted on this dataset with the help of VOSviewer software. VOSviewer is a program developed to build and visualize bibliometric maps in detail. It can, for example, be used to build maps of authors or journals based on co-citation data or to build keyword maps based on co-occurrence data. In our case, this approach was used to identify key concepts and terminologies of the examined articles (Van Eck & Waltman, [2010](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR121)). The co-occurrence network still contains rich information in its topological structure (such as topics constituted by a group of nearby nodes and relationships between these topics). Such information becomes more meaningful as the scale of the data increases. To avoid uninformative clusters, the threshold of presence was set at 60 mentions. The data were then visualized in VOSviewer to detect related communities (Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#Fig3)).

This quantitative analysis was complemented by a qualitative inductive thematic analysis through which each paper underwent meticulous coding and classification within Atlas.ti software (Atlas.ti, [2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR3)). The dual analysis yielded a conceptual map of keyword interrelationships and a synthesized tableau of primary themes, presented in Table [4](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#Tab4). Finally, a data charting table was developed to extract relevant content from the studies. The fields identified attempted to capture: 1) the research identity (Authors, Title, Year), 2) the research focus (a 'Short Summary' was drafted for each paper to encapsulate its core contributions and findings, ensuring a understanding of the work's essence), 3) the target of the article (to identify the primary audience or subject group each study aimed to address, revealing the scope and applicability of the research), 4) the main themes addressed (identified across the literature were coded to uncover recurrent patterns, concerns, and perspectives within the domain of AI literacy). The output of this process was a table for charting data created in order to capture pertinent information from the studies (see Annex).

Results
-------

### RQ1 How have Publications on AI Developed Over Time, Where are they Coming from in Terms of Geographical Distribution and what Audience are they Referring To?

In this section, we describe the background information (i.e., publication year, country, target audience) of the 87 selected studies (Table [3](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#Tab3)). The table categorizes the AI literacy papers based on their country of origin, showing that China leads with 27 publications, accounting for 31% of the total. This is closely followed by the US with 20 publications (23%), and Germany with 12 (14%). Other countries like Korea, Japan, Taiwan, Belgium, Italy, Austria, England, Spain, and Greece also contribute to the body of research, with each country accounting for 2% to 5% of the total publications. Furthermore, a diverse group of countries including Australia, Canada, Finland, Iran, Norway, Romania, and Türkiye contribute one article each, accounting for 1% of the total. In terms of publication years, notably, the year 2023 has seen a significant increase, accounting for 59% of the publications. The year 2022 also holds a notable share with 39%, additionally, the year 2021 remains significant, contributing 30% to the total. The earlier years such as 2020 and 2019, each with 3%, along with 2016 at 2%.

Regarding the target audience, the articles predominantly cater to “researchers” and “educators”, with 30 and 28 papers respectively. “Professionals” are the next most common target with 15 papers. “University students”, with 14 publications, and “K-12 students”, with 12, are also significant targets. The “general audience” and “K-5 students” are less frequently addressed but still notable targets. Finally, we have “non-experts” and “parents” with 6 and 4 papers respectively. ### RQ2 How Does the Research Literature Conceptualize and Differentiate the Definitions of AI Literacy?

The findings reveal a diverse range of interpretations, underscoring the complexity and multifaceted nature of AI literacy. While some scholars (Aoun, [2017](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR2); Kandlhofer et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR41)) emphasize foundational AI knowledge and applications, others (Druga et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR23); Ng et al., [2021a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR82), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR88)) integrate ethical considerations, societal impact, and the necessity of responsible AI engagement. A comparative analysis highlights two primary perspectives: (1) a technical proficiency-based model, which focuses on coding, computational thinking, and AI mechanics, and (2) a socio-ethical framework, which prioritizes AI’s implications on individuals and society. By examining these varying definitions, our study provides a structured understanding of how AI literacy has been conceptualized over time, shedding light on gaps and emerging trends in the literature.

Building on this comparison, it becomes evident that AI literacy is not only about understanding how AI systems function but also about comprehending their ethical, societal, and professional implications. The interplay between these perspectives highlights the need for a balanced approach that incorporates both technical and ethical dimensions, ensuring that individuals are equipped to engage critically with AI in multiple contexts.

The ethical dimension of AI literacy, as brought forth by authors like Druga et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR23), [2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR24)) and Wong et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR132)), underscores the growing consciousness of AI's broader societal impact. This aspect is critical in guiding responsible development and use of AI technologies, addressing concerns such as data privacy, algorithmic bias, and ethical AI deployment. The inclusion of ethical considerations reflects an acknowledgment of AI’s potential far-reaching effects on society, necessitating a well-rounded and responsible approach to AI literacy. The review also highlights the importance of AI literacy in the context of education and career development. Long and Magerko ([2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR70)) define AI literacy in terms of competences that enable individuals to critically evaluate and effectively collaborate with AI technologies. Liu and Xie ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR66)) and UNICEF ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR119)) broaden the scope by including digital literacy and computational thinking as part of AI literacy. Kim et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR46)) offers a three-pronged approach, encompassing knowledge (defined as the ability to comprehend the core concepts of AI. i.e. ‘definitions and types of AI’, ‘data and machine learning’, ‘applications’), skill (computational thinking or programming skills), and attitude (the ability to consider all aspects of AI in society collectively like ‘social impact’ and ‘collaborate with AI’), while Kong and Zhang ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR52)) and Cetindamar et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR14)) discuss AI literacy in the context of career readiness, emphasizing the need for understanding AI's impact on future job markets and the skills required to thrive in an AI-driven world. Ng et al. ([2021a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR82), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR88)) present a structured approach, organizing AI literacy into four distinct concepts, Know & understand AI (basic functions of AI and how to use AI applications), Use & Apply AI (applying AI knowledge, concepts and applications in different scenarios), Evaluate & create AI (evaluate, appraise, predict, design), AI ethics (fairness, accountability, transparency, safety). Furthermore, the literature review points out the cultural (understanding the characteristics of AI and the society to which it is applied) and subjective aspects of AI literacy (metacognition as the “competence” for AI literacy), as discussed by Yi ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR141)). This perspective is unique in linking AI literacy to personal and cultural adaptability, suggesting that AI literacy is not just a set of technical skills but also involves the capacity to navigate and shape one’s life amidst the transformations brought by AI. Deuze and Beckett ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR21)) and Hermann ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR36)) add a normative and practical lens, respectively, to AI literacy, focusing on its application and impact. Laupichler et al. ([2022a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR58), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR59)) and Wang et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR126)) stress the importance of informed decision-making regarding AI's use and implications. Lastly, UNESCO ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR120)) and Weber et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR128)) contribute a comprehensive view, encompassing data and algorithm literacy as key components. However, alongside AI literacy, data literacy has emerged as a critical component, enabling individuals to interpret, analyse, and make informed decisions based on AI-driven data (OECD, [2019](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR91); UNESCO, [2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR120)). Recognizing this, UNESCO ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR120)) emphasizes the role of data literacy as an essential subset of AI literacy, particularly in relation to understanding the mechanics of AI-driven decision-making. Furthermore, the OECD Learning Compass framework positions data literacy as one of the foundational competencies for 21st-century learners, particularly in a world where AI is reshaping industries and educational paradigms. By integrating both perspectives earlier in this review, we establish a stronger theoretical foundation for understanding the broader implications of AI literacy within education and beyond. This qualitative inquiry delves deeper into the textual corpus, surfacing themes that, while resonating with the established clusters, also bring to light additional insights.

Developing AI Literacy Curriculum and Interventions: Kong et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR49)); Laupichler et al. ([2022a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR58), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR59)); Park et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR97)); Zhao et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR145)), and others concentrate on creating and implementing curricula and interventions for AI literacy. These researchers embark on a critical endeavour to delineate the boundaries of AI literacy itself. In their efforts to design comprehensive curricula and teaching methodologies, as well as to evaluate AI literacy programs, they engage deeply with the essential task of defining what AI literacy encompasses and, importantly, what it excludes. Even if these articles do not address a particular thematic focus, in order to operationalise the AI literacy contents to teach it they provide some interesting information on the components of the literacy itself (e.g., what topic should be taught in the Critical dimension?).

AI Literacy Models and Frameworks: In this theme, Ng et al. ([2021a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR82), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR88)); Carolus et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR10)); Cuomo et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR20)); Panciroli et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR96)) and Pinski and Benlian ([2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR100)) propose theoretical models and frameworks for understanding AI literacy. These models serve as foundational guides for educators and policymakers in structuring AI education. Specifically, they dissect AI literacy into distinct but interrelated components such as the cognitive (e.g. detailing the knowledge necessary for understanding AI technologies, including algorithmic logic, data analysis, and machine learning principles and so on), affective (e.g. addressing the emotional and ethical considerations that AI raises, such as privacy concerns, ethical use of AI, and fostering a responsible mindset towards AI impacts on society), and sociocultural (e.g. exploring how AI literacy intersects with cultural and social contexts, emphasizing inclusivity, accessibility, and the societal implications of AI, including issues of bias and equity) dimensions of AI literacy, offering a structured approach to incorporating AI education across diverse learning environments.

Ethical and Social Implications of AI: Researchers like Hermann ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR36)); Yi ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR141)); Long et al. ([2023a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR72), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR73)); Lee ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR64)); Sakai ([2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR105)) and others delve into the complex ethical and social dimensions of AI. Their work examines how AI influences societal norms, individual behaviours, and ethical considerations. These studies does not provide an entire framework for interpreting AI literacy instead they propose guidelines for responsible AI usage, discussing issues like privacy, bias, and the broader societal impact of widespread AI adoption. They also explore how AI literacy can include an understanding of these ethical and social aspects.

AI Literacy in K-12 Education: Addressed by researchers such as Van Brummelen et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR122)); Su et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR112)); Steinbauer et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR109)); Lee et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR63)) and others, this theme emphasizes the importance of embedding AI literacy in the K-12 education system. These studies explore various approaches to introducing AI concepts to younger students, focusing on curriculum development, teaching methodologies, and the impact of AI education on children's cognitive and social development. The authors advocate for early exposure to AI, preparing students for a future where AI plays a significant role in many aspects of life.

AI Literacy Assessment Tools: This area, explored by Laupichler et al. ([2022a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR58), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR59)); Wang et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR126)) and Pinski and Benlian ([2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR100)) involves the development of tools and methodologies to assess AI literacy. These tools aim to evaluate the effectiveness of AI education programs and measure individuals’ understanding of AI concepts. The research in this field contributes to establishing benchmarks and standards for AI literacy, providing educators and policymakers with valuable insights into the strengths and gaps in current AI educational initiatives.

Integration of AI in Education and Workplaces: This theme, explored by authors like Cardon et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR9)); Cetindamar et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR14)); Hamburg et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR33)) and Laupichler et al. ([2022a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR58), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR59)) focuses on how artificial intelligence is becoming an integral part of both educational systems and workplaces. These studies examine how AI technologies are reshaping learning environments, teaching methodologies, and workplace dynamics. They highlight the need for new skills and competencies to effectively integrate AI into these settings, discussing strategies for educators and employers to adapt to this technological evolution.

Family and Informal Learning Environments: Investigated by Druga et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR24)) and Long et al. ([2023a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR72), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR73))., this theme explores AI literacy in non-traditional learning environments like family settings. These studies highlight the role of parents and informal educational activities in fostering AI literacy. They discuss how families can engage with AI technologies, the importance of parental guidance in children's AI education, and the potential of informal learning experiences in developing a foundational understanding of AI.

AI Literacy for Early Childhood Education: Researchers like Su et al. Their work emphasizes the importance of introducing AI concepts to young learners in an engaging and developmentally appropriate manner. This includes exploring pedagogical strategies for teaching basic AI concepts, the use of AI-powered educational tools, and the impact of early AI education on children's learning and development.

Their work explores how AI technologies are transforming medical diagnostics, treatment planning, and patient care. These studies also discuss the integration of AI education into medical training, emphasizing the need for healthcare professionals to be proficient in using and understanding AI tools in clinical settings.

Their research addresses the skills and knowledge needed to navigate and utilize AI-driven technologies and platforms. Discussion
----------

Our study embarked on an extensive journey to select relevant studies, starting with the identification of 323 records from various databases including Web of Science, SCOPUS, ERIC, and IEEE Xplore. This initial pool underwent rigorous pre-screening, leading to 87 studies that were included in our review. This thorough selection process ensured the inclusion of the most pertinent and high-quality studies in our analysis.

### A Polarized World

The results to the first research question show a dominance of publications from China and the US which can be due to the strong government support and significant investments in AI research and education in both countries. China's push for AI dominance, as outlined in its "Next Generation AI Development Plan" (State Council of China, [2017](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR17)), has fostered an important focus on AI education and literacy as a strategic priority, resulting in a growing body of research aimed at improving AI literacy across various sectors (Chen et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR16)). Similarly, the United States, home to leading tech companies and research institutions, has prioritized AI education, particularly in higher education and professional development, to ensure that the workforce is prepared for an AI-driven future (National AI Initiative Act, [2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR118)).

Cultural and institutional factors also play a role in shaping AI literacy research. In China, the government's centralized approach to education and policy allows for large-scale implementation of AI literacy programs, while in the US, the emphasis is more on innovation and private sector-driven initiatives. These differences influence not only the volume of research but also the focus areas. For example, Chinese publications often emphasize AI literacy in K-12 education as part of a broader national strategy to cultivate technological expertise from an early age (Chen et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR16)), whereas US publications tend to focus on higher education and professional skills development (Long & Magerko, [2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR70)).

In addition to China and the US, European countries such as Germany and the UK also contribute significantly to AI literacy research. However, the distribution is more fragmented, reflecting the decentralized nature of European education systems and the diverse approaches to AI education across different countries. While European initiatives, such as those led by UNESCO ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR120)), stress the importance of ethical AI practices and data privacy, research from the US and China often prioritizes technological proficiency and workforce readiness (UNESCO, [2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR120)).

### Defining AI Literacy: A Spectrum of Perspectives

The second research question focus into the literature's definitions of AI literacy, uncovering a rich mosaic of interpretations that mimic the evolving nature of AI itself since we can find references that emphasize the concept's breadth and depth. The emphasis by Kandlhofer et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR41)) on basic AI techniques and applications underscores a crucial entry point into AI literacy, highlighting the necessity of a solid grasp of AI fundamentals across diverse products and services. This foundational perspective is vital for developing further competencies in AI literacy, including those required for effective utilization in various sectors, as indicated by Aoun ([2017](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR2)). Moreover, Aoun's work accentuates the practical dimension of AI literacy, stressing the importance of not just understanding but actively applying AI principles in real-world scenarios. The ethical considerations brought forth by Druga et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR23), [2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR24)) and Wong et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR132)) reflect an expanding consciousness towards AI's societal impacts, underscoring the critical need for responsible AI development and usage. This growing awareness addresses pivotal concerns such as data privacy, algorithmic bias, and the ethical deployment of AI technologies, suggesting a holistic approach to AI literacy that encompasses ethical discernment alongside technical proficiency. Furthermore, the comprehensive set of competencies described by Long and Magerko ([2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR70)) reveals AI literacy's profound relevance in educational and professional development contexts. This approach underscores the integration of AI literacy into curricula and career planning, preparing individuals not just for current technological landscapes but for future advancements as well. The incorporation of digital literacy and computational thinking by Liu and Xie ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR66)) expands AI literacy beyond traditional boundaries, weaving it into the broader tapestry of digital competences essential for the 21st century. Significantly, the structured approach presented by Ng et al. ([2021a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR82), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR88)), organizing AI literacy into four distinct concepts (knowing and understanding AI, using and applying AI, evaluating and creating AI, and considering AI ethics), offers a comprehensive framework for understanding and teaching AI literacy. The discussions on cultural and subjective aspects of AI literacy, as highlighted by Yi ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR141)), propose AI literacy as a capability that transcends mere technical skills, advocating for the development of a capacity to adapt and thrive amidst societal transformations. Finally, the normative and practical perspectives offered by Deuze and Beckett ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR21)) and Hermann ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR36)) emphasize the application and impact of AI literacy in decision-making processes. The synthesis of AI literacy definitions from the literature underscores the need for an educational paradigm that is adaptable, comprehensive, and forward-thinking. It also highlights the importance for AI literacy to be integrated into curricular frameworks across educational levels, equipping future generations with the knowledge, skills, and ethical understanding required to navigate and shape a world increasingly influenced by AI. Furthermore, the variations in the conceptualization of AI literacy reflect the diverse needs and contexts of different societies, calling for context-specific approaches to AI education. This diversity also implies the importance of interdisciplinary collaboration in developing AI literacy programs, combining insights from education, technology, ethics, and social sciences to create well-rounded, effective educational experiences. Based on the diverse perspectives explored in this review, we propose the following working definition of AI literacy based on our framework and related works (Biagini et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR4), [2024](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR5); Cuomo et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR20)): AI literacy refers to the knowledge required to understand, the operative skills to engage with, the awareness needed to critically evaluate AI technologies, including their ethical, societal, and practical implications. It encompasses both the minimum technical proficiency and the ability to apply AI responsibly across various contexts, ensuring individuals are equipped to navigate and influence an AI-imbued society as informed citizens.

### Towards a Comprehensive AI Literacy Roadmap

The results of our systematic review highlight the diverse and multifaceted nature of AI literacy, revealing the complexity of this concept across different educational and societal contexts. The analysis underscores the importance of developing robust frameworks and curricula tailored to different educational levels and societal needs, reflecting the varied approaches to AI literacy that have emerged in recent years. Central to this effort is the delineation of AI literacy models, which form the backbone of how AI literacy is understood, taught, and assessed. These frameworks can be broadly categorized into two main orientations: technical frameworks, which align closely with STEM education, and holistic frameworks, which are better suited to interdisciplinary approaches.

Technical frameworks, such as those proposed by Ng et al. ([2021a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR82), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR88)) and Su and Zhong ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR111)), emphasize computational thinking and technical skills (i.e. neural networks, or deep learning). These models focus on the cognitive and operational dimensions of AI literacy, providing the foundation for understanding how AI technologies work at a technical level. Such frameworks are particularly effective in structured learning environments where technical proficiency is paramount, such as STEM subjects. Here, students develop the necessary competencies to interact with AI systems, including knowledge of algorithms, machine learning, and data analysis.

In contrast, holistic frameworks—as discussed by Yi ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR141)); Cuomo et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR20)) and Hermann ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR36))—offer a broader perspective, integrating ethical, social, and cultural considerations into AI literacy. These models align more closely with interdisciplinary curricula, where AI literacy is not only about technical skills but also about understanding the societal impacts of AI technologies. This approach fits well also within subjects such as social studies or humanities, where students are encouraged to reflect on the ethical implications of AI, including issues like privacy, bias, and transparency. By fostering critical thinking, holistic frameworks prepare students to engage with AI in ways that consider its broader effects on society.

### Curriculum Development and the Role of UNESCO Guidelines

Moving from frameworks to curriculum development, the key challenge is how to operationalize these different approaches to AI literacy. As pointed out by Kong et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR49)) and Laupichler et al. ([2022a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR58), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR59)), curriculum development efforts must be flexible enough to accommodate both technical and holistic frameworks, depending on the learning context. Some curricula, particularly those aligned with UNESCO ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR120)) guidelines, emphasize the need for AI literacy to include both ethical considerations and technical competencies. For instance, UNESCO stresses the importance of digital literacy as part of AI literacy, ensuring that learners are not only capable of using AI technologies but also critically aware of the ethical issues these technologies raise.

One of the main questions in curriculum development is how to balance these different components. For example, technical frameworks are well suited to STEM-oriented curricula, where the focus is on developing technical skills such as coding, data manipulation, and machine learning (Ng et al., [2021a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR82), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR88); Su & Zhong, [2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR111)). Here, the curriculum might include hands-on projects that allow students to design and implement simple AI systems, fostering a deep understanding of the mechanisms behind AI technologies. In contrast, more interdisciplinary curricula might integrate discussions on AI ethics into social studies courses, where students explore how AI technologies influence privacy, governance, and equality (Cuomo et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR20); Long & Magerko, [2020](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR70)). This approach is in line with UNESCO’s emphasis on responsible AI use and the need to develop a well-rounded understanding of AI’s societal impacts (UNESCO, [2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR120)).

### K-12 Education and Early Exposure to AI Concepts

The integration of AI literacy into K-12 education is a critical aspect of curriculum development. As noted by Van Brummelen et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR122)) and Su et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR112)), early exposure to AI concepts can help students develop the foundational skills they will need in a future increasingly shaped by AI. However, the approach to AI literacy in K-12 education varies significantly depending on whether the focus is on technical proficiency or broader ethical and societal issues.

For instance, in Finland, AI literacy is embedded in STEM programs through project-based learning, where students are encouraged to apply AI concepts to solve real-world problems (Mertala et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR77)). This hands-on approach aligns well with technical frameworks, helping students develop computational skills while fostering creativity and problem-solving. In contrast, South Korea has integrated AI literacy into its national K-12 curriculum with a strong focus on ethics (Kim et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR46)). Here, students engage in discussions about the societal implications of AI, such as the use of AI in surveillance or its role in social media algorithms, encouraging them to reflect critically on the influence of AI in everyday life.

Despite the progress in integrating AI literacy into education, one of the major gaps remains the lack of practical guidance on how to teach AI ethics. While there is widespread recognition of the importance of ethical considerations, as discussed by Druga et al. ([2019](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR23), [2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR24)), many curricula still focus primarily on technical skills, with less emphasis on operational strategies for integrating ethics into the classroom. This imbalance highlights the need for further research and development of pedagogical tools that can help teachers address ethical questions alongside technical training.

In addition to formal education, AI literacy is also cultivated in informal learning environments, such as family settings. Research by Druga et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR24)) and Long et al. ([2023a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR72), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR73)) highlights the importance of parents and caregivers in fostering a basic understanding of AI technologies outside of the classroom. These informal learning experiences are crucial for developing early AI literacy, particularly in younger children, and offer a complementary approach to formal education.

### AI Literacy in the Workplace and Specialized Fields

Finally, the increasing integration of AI technologies into the workplace presents a significant challenge for AI literacy. As noted by Cardon et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR9)) and Cetindamar et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR14)), AI literacy is becoming an essential skill for navigating modern work environments, where AI is used for data analysis, decision-making, and automation. Workers must not only be proficient in using AI tools but also understand the broader implications of AI for their roles and industries. Employers and educators need to collaborate in developing training programs that ensure workers can effectively engage with AI technologies, while also considering the ethical and social dimensions of AI in the workplace.

In specialized fields such as healthcare, AI literacy is particularly critical. Studies by Laupichler et al. ([2022a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR58), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR59)) and Perchik et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR99)) explore how AI is transforming medical diagnostics and treatment planning, highlighting the need for healthcare professionals to be proficient in AI technologies. However, as with education, there is still a gap in the integration of ethical considerations into training programs for medical professionals. Addressing this gap is essential to ensure that AI technologies are used responsibly in clinical settings.

### AI Literacy Assessment Tools

Finally, the development of assessment tools is crucial for measuring the effectiveness of AI literacy programs. Research by Laupichler et al. ([2022a](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR58), [b](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR59)) and Wang et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00466-w#ref-CR126)) has shown that while efforts have been made to create tools to evaluate AI literacy, there is still no consensus on what constitutes a comprehensive evaluation. Effective assessment tools must capture both the technical competencies and the ethical, critical-thinking aspects of AI literacy, providing educators with the data needed to refine and improve AI literacy curricula.

Ultimately, the development of AI literacy is a multifaceted process that requires the integration of various frameworks, models, and pedagogical strategies. From early childhood education to professional training, AI literacy must encompass both technical skills and ethical considerations to prepare individuals for the challenges of an AI-driven world. As AI continues to reshape industries and society, it is essential that AI literacy programs evolve in tandem, ensuring that learners are equipped not only to use AI technologies but also to understand and critique their broader implications. By focusing on comprehensive curriculum development, effective assessment tools, and the integration of AI literacy into both formal and informal learning environments, we can create a roadmap for AI literacy that ensures future generations are prepared to navigate an increasingly AI-driven landscape.

Conclusion
----------

Our comprehensive study highlights the transformative role of Artificial Intelligence (AI) in shaping the 21st-century digital landscape, influencing domains from education to healthcare, and necessitating AI literacy as a fundamental component of modern education. Through a rigorous selection process, we analysed 87 high-quality studies to offer a panoramic view of the evolving landscape of AI literacy. Our findings reveal that AI literacy extends beyond technical proficiency, encompassing a broad spectrum of skills and knowledge, including ethical considerations, societal impacts, and practical applications.

Key themes such as the ethical and social implications of AI, AI literacy in K-12 education, and the development of AI literacy curricula and interventions underscore the importance of a holistic educational approach. This approach addresses the need for responsible AI practices, early integration of AI concepts in education, and strategic curricular frameworks to prepare individuals for an AI-driven future. Additionally, AI literacy models and frameworks offer a structured way to teach and understand AI and are crucial for guiding the development of assessment tools. The integration of AI into education and workplaces highlights the importance of developing new competencies to navigate AI-influenced sectors effectively.

Our study also underscores the significance of AI and digital interaction literacy, emphasizing the skills required for engaging with AI-driven technologies. The exploration of informal learning environments, such as family settings, adds another layer to the conversation, demonstrating the role of non-traditional educational spaces in fostering foundational AI understanding.

Conclusively, the diverse interpretations and evolving definitions of AI literacy reflect the dynamic nature of AI itself, emphasizing the need for an adaptable, comprehensive educational paradigm. While computational thinking remains a key concept for understanding and interacting with AI technologies, it is important to recognize that frameworks such as the OECD Learning Compass also emphasize *data literacy* as an essential skill in today's digital society. A balanced approach to AI literacy should integrate computational thinking, data literacy, and digital literacy, providing individuals with a comprehensive toolkit for engaging with AI responsibly and effectively.

Despite the growing body of literature on AI literacy, several critical areas require further exploration. Future research should focus on how AI literacy can be effectively tailored to different age groups and learning contexts, adapting to the diverse cognitive and social development needs of learners. Additionally, understanding the role of cultural context in shaping AI literacy education is crucial, as research is currently concentrated in regions like China and the United States. Exploring how AI literacy can be contextualized in underrepresented areas, such as Africa and Latin America, is essential for creating inclusive educational strategies.

Another important area is the long-term impact of AI literacy on individuals and society. While much attention has been given to the immediate outcomes of AI literacy programs, longitudinal studies are needed to examine how AI literacy influences career development, civic engagement, and societal attitudes towards AI technologies over time. Furthermore, the development of standardized assessment tools remains a challenge. Future work should focus on creating comprehensive frameworks that measure both technical skills and the ethical and critical thinking aspects of AI literacy across diverse learning environments.

Additionally, while our study has analyzed AI literacy research based on thematic focus and target populations, the methodological categorization of the selected studies was not explicitly coded. Future research should consider incorporating a systematic classification of methodologies to provide further insights into the research approaches employed in this field. This would enhance the understanding of prevailing research methods and support the development of best practices in AI literacy studies.

In conclusion, addressing these challenges requires interdisciplinary collaboration between educators, technologists, ethicists, and policymakers. As AI continues to shape our world, the pursuit of AI literacy emerges as a societal imperative, ensuring that future generations are equipped with the knowledge, skills, and ethical discernment necessary to navigate and thrive in an increasingly AI-driven society.
