## Abstract
Developing Artificial Intelligence (AI) education in K-12 contexts, i.e., teaching students about AI, is critical to promote students' AI literacy. However, the state-of-the-art of AI education is not clear enough. To this end, this study reviewed 45 high-quality empirical studies on K-12 AI education over the past decade from both research and instruction perspectives. Regarding the research design, this study revealed the relationship between publication year, sample size, learning stage, educational setting, research method, research focus and duration. Regarding the instruction design, this study revealed the relationship between learning stage, pedagogical strategy, learning tool, learning activity, learning content, assessment method and learning effect. Besides, this study also derived recommendations for research (i.e., time allocation, [samples selection](/topics/social-sciences/sample-selection), longitudinal design, rigorous methodology and technical democracy) and instruction (i.e., group learning, authentic context, teacher involvement, triangular evidence and learning scaffolding). Overall, the main findings indicate that K-12 AI education has the potential to develop students’ AI literacy, which contains AI knowledge, AI affectivity, and AI thinking. However, deficiencies in research and instructional design still remain, including short durations, small sample sizes, non-standardized research methods, lack of long-term and cross-age AI curriculum, etc. This study also discussed several critical topics for future research and instruction.
## Introduction
Nowadays, the rapid development of Artificial Intelligence (AI) is fundamentally transforming the industries and human lifestyles. This is evidenced by the increasing use of AI to address complex problems in fields such as healthcare, engineering, economics, agriculture, shopping, transportation and psychology (Su et al., 2023a). The ubiquity of AI applications requires future generations to acquire sufficient AI literacy to most effectively use such technologies (Chiu, 2021). In this context, K-12 AI education has become increasingly essential to preparing children early for social and technological changes (Ng et al., 2023a). This section will clarify the definition of AI education, elucidate the development of K-12 AI education, and provide an overview of existing relevant reviews.
According to the diverse AI roles in education, there are multiple interpretations of AI education. Through a systematic literature review, Xu and Ouyang (2022) summarized three AI roles in education: (1) AI as a direct mediator (a platform or a tool); (2) AI as a supplementary assistant (data mining, learning analysis and predictive ability); and (3) AI as a new subject (an instructor or a student). Currently, AI plays the former two roles, which are dedicated to meeting demands, assessing performance or providing feedback in various educational scenarios (Marr, 2018). AIED (AI in Education) has captured the interest of educators who are devoted to enhancing the application of AI technologies in teaching, learning, evaluation, and management (e.g., Moore et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023). On this basis, AI education can be broadly defined as the use of AI technology to accelerate the reform of talent training modes.
However, the possibilities for AI in education exist beyond simple online platforms, tools or applications. Especially, the emergence of ChatGPT highlights the potential for AI as a new subject to replace the original subjects (Jeon & Lee, 2023). In the future, AI may work as a tutor to transform the instructor-student relationship, a tutee to transform the student-self relationship, and a peer to transform the student-student relationship (Xu & Ouyang, 2022). In this context, a growing body of studies emphasize that future generations should acquire sufficient AI literacy to better adapt to human-machine collaboration (Chiu et al., 2021; Long & Magerko, 2020). With the rapid advances in technology, the concept of literacy has transcended its original meaning associated with books and printing (Buckingham & Burn, 2007). As an emerging technology that simulates and extends human intelligence, AI involves multiple disciplines such as neurophysiology, psychology, computer science, and mathematics, etc. The cross-disciplinary nature of AI requires both domain-specific and domain-general attributes of AI literacy (Authors, 2024a). The former enables students to master scientific, technical and ethics foundations of AI, while the latter equips students with the higher-order thinking required to address unfamiliar problems, uncertainties, or dilemmas in an AI-powered society.
Education should not only take the present situation into consideration but also be future-oriented (Huang, 2021). The escalating significance of AI literacy necessitates a shift in AI education from “AI in education” to “education about AI”. From a domain-specific perspective, this type of AI education focuses on designing learning contents within the AI discipline, such as “knowledge in AI," "impact of AI," and "process in AI " (Chiu, 2021). From a domain-general perspective, this type of AI education focuses on developing higher-order thinking activities (e.g., craft AI-based artifacts, design AI applications) that involve real-world problems (Ng et al., 2021). These two aspects form the meaning of "AI education" in this paper, which aims to cultivate students' AI literacy by teaching students about AI through higher-order thinking activities in real-world contexts.
Various governmental initiatives, university, school and enterprise efforts have been expended to promote K-12 AI education. For instance, UNESCO (2020) announced an education policy titled “Al in Education: Change at the Speed of Learning” to describe the current practices of Al curriculum in K-12 schools. Besides, AAAI and CSTA (2019) proposed the “five big ideas” to elucidate essential AI concepts for K-12 students in the United States in terms of perception, representation and reasoning, learning, natural interaction, and societal impact of AI. Furthermore, ISTE (2022) provided a set of guidelines for teachers who are seeking AI instructional and curriculum resources for all grades and disciplines in K-12. In alignment with the national trend, Macquarie University's School of Education (MUSE) collaborated with IBM to propose an inclusive AI curriculum framework for middle schools (Sabuncuoglu, 2020). Besides, East China Normal University (ECNU) collaborated with SenseTime to publish the inaugural series of high school textbooks titled “Fundamentals of Artificial Intelligence” (Tang et al., 2018). Moreover, the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) focused on creating AI tools for kindergarten children - robots, and examined their uses and effects (Williams et al., 2019). Therefore, the development of AI education guidelines, curriculum, and tools has become a national strategy pushed by public and private sectors, which reflects a concerted commitment to systematically advancing educational frameworks aligned with the evolving landscape of AI technologies. Nevertheless, these curriculum and tools still require rigorous examination to better understand global guidelines and develop effective instructional design in K-12 education.
With a development of AI education, researchers have initiated empirical studies to report on AI education in K-12 schools. Several exploratory reviews have started to summarize the effective practices of AI education across different levels. For instance, Ng et al. (2023d) performed a thematic analysis of 50 studies from 2016 to 2022 to evaluate pedagogy, instructional tools, learning contents, and assessment methods of AI education in secondary school. However, their studies did not focus on the entire range of K-12 education.
Von Wangenheim et al. (2021) presented a ten-year systematic mapping of emerging visual tools from 24 artifacts to support ML teaching in K-12 settings in terms of educational characteristics, deployment, and evaluation of these tools. Additionally, Ng et al. (2023a) conducted a thematic and content analysis of 49 empirical studies from 2000 to 2020 to summarize the teaching tools and pedagogical strategies of AI education in K-12 levels. However, these reviews have not revealed what is being taught about AI and how it affects student learning in K-12 education.
Su et al. (2023a) synthesized 21 empirical studies from 1984 to 2021 to show how AI curriculum have been designed and implemented in K-12 classrooms in terms of learning goals, contents, teaching methods, assessments, and learning effects. Yue et al. (2022) analyzed 32 empirical studies from 2010 to 2022 both descriptively and thematically to provide the research status, pedagogical design, assessments and outcomes of the AI teaching programs. Sanusi et al. (2023c) reviewed 43 conference and journal papers to identify three focus areas for K-12 ML teaching: curriculum development, technology development, pedagogical development, and teacher training/professional development. These reviews summarize learning contents, tools, and pedagogy in K-12 AI education while assessing students’ learning effects, which provides valuable insights for this study. However, they involve several limitations: (1) did not focus on studies within a refined time span; (2) did not ensure the quality of reviewed papers, as the selected papers included many conference papers; (3) did not utilize multiple search strategies to retrieve sufficient papers; (4) did not cover more AI topics such as neural networks, natural language processing, etc. (5) did not examine the research design of empirical studies; (6) did not examine the relationships between multiple coded elements. The term “AI literacy” was first introduced in 2016, which signaled the rise of AI education for non-technical students (Kandlhofer et al., 2019). Thus, the last decade is a suitable time span to explore K-12 AI education.
Considering aforementioned limitations, the purpose of this review is twofold: (1) to systematically review high-quality empirical studies from January 2014 to April 2024 on K-12 AI education from both research and instruction perspectives, and (2) to explore future directions for research and instruction in K-12 AI education based on the reviewed papers. The term “empirical study” refers to field-based research which gathers data from real-world observations or instructional experiments, such as action research, design-based research, experimental research, and case study (Flynn et al., 1990). Therefore, this review aims to examine the empirical evidence on K-12 AI education to answer three research questions.
\* (1)
What research has been conducted on K-12 AI education?
\* (2)
How has K-12 AI education been designed and implemented?
\* (3)
What recommendations for K-12 AI education are addressed in these empirical studies?

\* ### [Integrating generative Artificial Intelligence into student learning: A systematic review from a TPACK perspective](/science/article/pii/S1747938X25000788)
2025, Educational Research Review
Show abstract
Advances in Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) have sparked widespread debate in education. According to a survey by the Digital Education Council (DEC), 86 percent of students use AI in their learning, with ChatGPT being the most widely used tool. Given that students constitute a considerable proportion of GenAI users, concerns arise regarding the potential misuse of this technology by students themselves. Consequently, researchers and educators should rethink how GenAI tools interacts with curriculum needs and pedagogical strategies, particularly through frameworks such as TPACK, to enhance student learning while mitigating potential risks. However, the state-of-the-art of integrating GenAI into student learning remains unclear. To address this gap, this study systematically reviewed high-quality empirical studies published after 30 November 2022 (release date of ChatGPT) on integrating GenAI into student learning from a TPACK perspective. The findings aim to provide an evidence-based foundation for future research and instructional design. For the former, we analyzed the 71 studies from the perspectives of publishing year, country/region of corresponding author, authorship type, sample size, learning stage, educational setting, duration, research method and focus, as well as the moderating effect of key research design variables on learning outcomes. For the latter, we analyzed the 71 studies from the perspectives of learning content, learning tool, pedagogical strategy, assessment method, and learning outcome, as well as the moderating effect of key instructional design variables on learning outcomes. Moreover, we also summarized the recommendations of these studies for future research and instruction. The empirical evidence suggested that GenAI has the potential to enhance student learning in cognitive, affective and behavioral domains. However, deficiencies in research and instructional design still remain, including short duration, small sample sizes, and incomplete pedagogical scaffolding, etc. Based on the review's findings, this study also identifies two critical priorities for future research and instruction.
\* ### [Fostering responsible AI literacy: A systematic review of K-12 AI ethics education](/science/article/pii/S2666920X25000621)
2025, Computers and Education Artificial Intelligence
Show abstract
AI ethics education remains significantly underprioritized in classroom practice, despite the global push for AI literacy in K-12 curricula. This systematic review analyzes 68 peer-reviewed publications (from January 2014 to March 2025) to map the research landscape of K-12 AI ethics education. The results reveal global trends in the current research landscape, pedagogical designs addressing major responsible AI (RAI) principles, and various learning assessment methods, and students’ ethical learning outcomes manifested across cognitive, affective, and behavioral domains. By further synthesizing findings, we identify disparities of eastern and western contexts, gaps in addressing RAI principles and emerging ethical issues, limitations of methodological methods, and issues in assessing ethical learning outcomes. Building on this synthesis, we propose a competency-based responsible AI literacy framework that reconceptualizes AI ethics as a transformative learning dimension that progressively permeates all the learning dimensions of AI literacy development. The framework also provides actionable insights to empower K-12 educators and policymakers in fostering students' responsible AI literacy. The review concludes with three future research directions to advance this critical field.
\* ### [Evaluating AI literacy of secondary students: Framework and scale development](/science/article/pii/S0360131524002446)
2025, Computers and Education
Show abstract
K-12 AI education not only equips students with AI literacy but also encourages underrepresented groups to pursue further studies or careers in this field. Secondary students were particularly well-suited for comprehensive AI education due to their cognitive characteristics and developmental readiness. While most studies have focused on developing pedagogy, curriculum, and tools for secondary AI education, they have prioritized measuring students' learning outcomes over literacy development. Referring to the empirical research on secondary AI education as well as Piaget's Epistemology and Bloom's Taxonomy, this study figured out a KAT framework that constitutes AI literacy: (1) AI Knowledge (AI fundamentals, elements of AI technology, application of AI technology), (2) AI Affectivity (AI and human, AI and society), and (3) AI Thinking (engineering design thinking, computational thinking). Based on this, a 57-item AI literacy scale (AILS) was developed, and 56 items were retained after expert judgement. Then, a large sample of Chinese secondary students was surveyed, resulting in 1392 valid samples, which were randomly divided into two sub-samples: 720 samples were used for item reduction through Rasch Analysis and Exploratory Factor Analysis; 672 samples were used for model validation and comparison through Confirmatory Factor Analysis. Results indicated the AILS with three-factor structure of 48 items has a good reliability and validity. Moreover, gender differences in AI literacy among secondary students were examined. Results indicated that boys had significantly higher AI Knowledge than girls, whereas girls had significantly higher AI Affectivity than boys. The implications, limitations and future research were also discussed.
