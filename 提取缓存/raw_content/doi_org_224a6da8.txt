## Abstract
The rapid advancement of GenAI has created unprecedented opportunities to enhance student learning through large language models (LLMs). While LLM-based conversational agents have demonstrated potential in improving accessibility and instructional support, evidence of their impact on higher-order thinking remains limited, especially within the context of Chinese higher education. This study introduces the Socratic Intelligent Conversational Agent (S-ICA), which integrates Socratic questioning strategies with semantic generation mechanisms to promote student learning and reflective thinking in disciplinary contexts. A total of 94 university students from China participated in the study, with random assignment to an experimental group (EG) using the S-ICA and a control group (CG) using a non-Socratic conversational agent (nS-ICA). The results showed that the EG outperformed the CG in both academic achievement and reflective thinking, particularly in the dimensions of “reflection” and “critical reflection”. Cognitive network analysis revealed that the EG students activated more advanced reflective pathways, linking understanding, reflection, and critical reflection processes. Although no significant differences were found in learning motivation between the groups, interviews with students indicated that the S-ICA facilitated more effective learning and deeper reflective engagement. These findings contribute to research on integrating classical pedagogical strategies into GenAI-based systems, offering insights into how such technologies can foster higher-order thinking and guide the design of future collaborative human–AI learning systems.
## Introduction
Reflective thinking, as a critical higher-order thinking ability, has been widely recognized as fundamental for solving complex problems, fostering deep learning, and promoting innovation (Bauer, 1991; Dewey, 1922). It involves an active and sustained process through which individuals critically examine and evaluate their own experiences and beliefs, allowing cognitive restructuring and knowledge reconstruction (Schön, 1995). An increasing emphasis within higher education highlights the importance of cultivating students’ ability to reflect to better prepare them for increasingly dynamic and uncertain environments (Harvey et al., 2016). Empirical evidence has shown that learners with well-developed reflective thinking skills achieve deeper conceptual understanding, greater transfer, and improved problem solving in novel contexts (Askell-Williams et al., 2007). To this end, diverse pedagogical strategies, such as dialogic classroom discourse (Cui & Teo, 2023), reflective journals or blogs (Chu et al., 2012), and collaborative group work (Lee et al., 2016)—have been employed to cultivate students’ reflective awareness and critical dispositions (Cloude et al., 2021). Nevertheless, while digital technologies—and more recently advanced GenAI systems—offer promising opportunities to scale and scaffold reflective practices, their systematic integration into authentic classroom settings remains limited.
Recent advances in GenAI, particularly large language models (LLMs), offer new opportunities to address educational challenges (Yan et al., 2024; Zirar, 2023). Intelligent conversational agents (ICAs powered by LLM) can produce fluent and contextually relevant dialogue, provide immediate feedback, and deliver personalized and adaptive learning support (Kasneci et al., 2023). Empirical studies have reported encouraging results, showing that such agents can improve conceptual understanding, problem solving abilities, and knowledge transfer (Wang et al., 2025). (Liu et al., 2024). However, concerns have also emerged that excessive reliance on AI-generated answers can reduce students’ critical reflection, self-regulation, and long-term engagement (Darvishi et al., 2024; Zhai et al., 2024). These concerns underscore the importance of examining not only cognitive outcomes, but also affective dimensions such as motivation and perceptions, areas that remain not adequately explored in authentic higher education contexts (Yu, 2023). Therefore, the integration of GenAI into education must address both cognitive and affective dimensions to fully understand its impact on student learning (Liu et al., 2024).
The integration of advanced AI technologies into the pedagogy requires careful alignment with robust instructional principles (Barrot, 2023; Hu et al., 2025). Researchers are increasingly focusing on the fact that learners should be guided through exploratory questioning and scaffolded instructions, rather than provided with ready-made solutions (Borchers et al., 2025). Among such approaches, the Socratic method, rooted in probing questions that encourage learners to articulate reasoning, justify claims, and confront inconsistencies, has long been recognized as effective for cultivating critical and reflective thinking (Hsu et al., 2022). Recent work has begun to explore how Socratic strategies can be implemented through agents powered by LLM. For example, Kuo et al. (2026) documented the application of the TALPer system in fifth-grade mathematics education, where it employed the Socratic dialogue method and carefully designed questioning strategies to foster reflective thinking and enhance mathematical understanding among students. However, most studies have focused on knowledge acquisition, with limited attention to how Socratic agents foster higher-order cognitive processes such as reflection and metacognitive awareness. Moreover, while Socratic dialogue was designed for one-to-one interactions, adapting it to group settings is essential for ecological validity. Although group dynamics may pose challenges like unequal participation, they also facilitate collaborative reflection and critical thinking (Fiock, 2020). Our design mitigated these risks by incorporating structured worksheets and turn-taking scaffolds to ensure active participation in small groups, thus supporting personalized and collaborative learning.
Against this backdrop, the present study introduced a Socratic Intelligent Conversational Agent (S-ICA) that integrated Socratic questioning strategies with LLM technologies to support inquiry-based learning in university classrooms. To rigorously evaluate its effectiveness, we contrasted the S-ICA with a carefully defined non-Socratic ICA (nS-ICA). Both agents operated under identical conditions in terms of tasks, time allocation, and interface; the only intended pedagogical difference was that the S-ICA employed Socratic questioning while the nS-ICA provided direct answers and clarifications without reflective prompts. We emphasize that the nS-ICA refers specifically to the control condition in this study and is not intended to be generalized to all non-Socratic agents. This design isolates the contribution of Socratic questioning while minimizing potential confounding.
The study assesses both cognitive and affective outcomes. At the cognitive level, we investigate whether the S-ICA enhances academic performance and reflective thinking. At the affective level, we explore how the S-ICA influences students’ motivation and learning perceptions—critical yet underexplored dimensions in AI in education. Epistemic Network Analysis (ENA) was employed to model the co-occurrence patterns of cognitive categories in dialogue, a widely recognized method in educational research for examining learning processes and knowledge construction. By analyzing connections among reflective categories such as understanding, reflection, and critical reflection, ENA provides insights into how students’ cognitive engagement evolves during collaborative tasks. In this study, we apply Epistemic Network Analysis (ENA) to reveal distinct cognitive patterns and the underlying mechanisms through which ICAs integrating Socratic prompting promote deeper reflective engagement, shedding light on how these cognitive pathways differ between the experimental and control groups. These outcomes are critical to understanding how personalized and adaptive learning support in GenAI-powered agents can promote sustained engagement and deeper learning. To this end, the following research questions were addressed:
- 1.
Does the S-ICA approach improve learners’ academic performance compared to the nS-ICA?
- 2.
Does the S-ICA approach foster learners’ reflective thinking compared to the nS-ICA?
- 3.
Does the S-ICA approach enhance learners’ learning motivation compared to the nS-ICA?
- 4.
How do students perceive their learning experience when interacting with the two types of ICAs?

- ### [Generative AI in higher education: A bibliometric review of emerging trends, power dynamics, and global research landscapes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X26000056)
2026, Computers and Education Artificial Intelligence
Show abstract
The rapid evolution of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) is reshaping higher education (HE), offering transformative opportunities for academic engagement while posing significant challenges to academic integrity, ethical frameworks, and global research power dynamics. This study maps the recent (2022–2025) research landscape of GenAI in HE through a bibliometric analysis of 2762 articles from the Web of Science Core Collection. Employing multipolarity as an analytical lens, this study examines the power dynamics within this research domain reflected by publication records from different countries (or regions). Findings highlight surging global interest in GenAI in HE, with contributions led by the US, China, and the UK, alongside rising participation from non-Western scholars and institutions. By identifying the major topics, this study uncovers a more nuanced trajectory of GenAI-related discourse in HE. By examining publication status, contributors, and research topics, this study provides insights for stakeholders navigating the complexities of GenAI integration into HE and suggests trajectories for future research in this rapidly evolving field.
