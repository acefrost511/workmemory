## Abstract
Fostering preservice teachers’ (PSTs) design thinking (DT) is essential for addressing complex instructional challenges, yet remains difficult to achieve in teacher education. Although generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) and analytics-supported reflective assessment (ASRA) show promise for supporting DT, how they can be pedagogically integrated within collaborative inquiry remains relatively underexplored. The study aimed to explore the effects of the combined use of GenAI and ASRA within a collaborative inquiry Knowledge Building (KB) environment on PSTs’ DT, and how their DT competencies evolves with this integrative design. Participants were 110 PSTs enrolled in a semester-long Modern Educational Technology Applications course. We proposed a conceptual framework integrating collaborative inquiry, strategically delayed GenAI use, and ASRA, and operationalized it through a pedagogical model that positions GenAI as a cognitive and reflective design partner rather than a solution provider. Multiple data sources were analyzed, including a DT competency scale, successive instructional design artifacts, and 1305 online discourse notes. Results showed significant gains in problem discovery and definition, along with a developmental shift from stage-bound engagement toward more integrated and iterative practices, particularly in prototyping and testing. GenAI was most effective when introduced after human-only design efforts, while reflective analytics supported monitoring of collective progress and sustained iteration. This study contributes to learning sciences by theorizing how GenAI and ASRA can be integrated within a KB environment to support DT, and offers an empirically grounded approach for preparing PSTs for AI-rich teaching contexts.
## Introduction
Design thinking (DT) is increasingly viewed as a key competence of higher-order thinking, enabling learners to tackle complex, real-world problems through creative and iterative approaches (Calavia et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2025; Lee & Kim, 2025). DT is commonly defined as an iterative, human-centered process that involves addressing ill-structured problems through cycles of problem framing, ideation, prototyping, testing, and reflection (Buchanan, 1992; Razzouk & Shute, 2012). Recent studies in teacher education show that DT-oriented activities help PSTs frame authentic pedagogical problems, make principled instructional decisions, and design more adaptive learning environments (Calavia et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2025; Lee & Kim, 2025). Beyond technical skill development, DT fosters dispositions such as resilience, tolerance and metacognitive awareness for ambiguity—qualities crucial for navigating complex, ill-structured problems (Koh, Chai, Wong, & Hong, 2015a, 2015b; Thuan and Antunes, 2024). It also promotes creative, innovative, and critical thinking—competencies central to 21st-century education (Koh et al., 2015a, 2015b; Lin et al., 2020). Without well-developed DT, PSTs often struggle to design meaningful, learner-centered lessons aligned with contemporary educational demands (Avsec & Jagiełło-Kowalczyk, 2021; Baran & AlZoubi, 2024; Yüksel, 2025). Consequently, helping PSTs develop DT competencies is crucially important.
However, developing PSTs’ DT remains challenging. Many PSTs rely heavily on existing templates or online resources, lacking the ability to independently design instruction (Ananda et al., 2023; Baran & AlZoubi, 2024). Even in design-oriented coursework, PSTs often struggle to sustain iterative cycles of framing, ideation, prototyping, and testing, particularly when design work requires coordination, justification, and revision over time (Calavia et al., 2023; Lee & Kim, 2025). Prior research indicates that teacher education often emphasizes final lesson products over process-oriented idea improvement and reflection (Ohly et al., 2017; Panke & Harth, 2019). In addition, PSTs frequently lack tools that make design processes visible and support assessment of idea connections and reasoning gaps during collaboration (Rodríguez-Chirino et al., 2025). These challenges are closely tied to the nature of instructional design, a core professional practice through which PSTs are expected to enact DT. Instructional design involves analyzing learner needs, aligning goals with instructional strategies, designing assessments, iterating instructional prototypes, and evaluating learning outcomes (Branch & Kopcha, 2013; Merrill, 2002). Effective instructional design therefore requires PSTs to justify design decisions, integrate theory, and revise instruction based on evidence, rather than follow fixed templates. In this sense, instructional design provides an authentic context for DT development, while DT represents the mode of thinking that enables PSTs to engage productively in such design work.
The rapid growth of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) offers new opportunities to support instructional design, but also raises pedagogical concerns. Studies show that GenAI can facilitate idea generation, provide alternative design options, and support early lesson drafting in teacher education (Moorhouse et al., 2024; Moorhouse & Kohnke, 2024). At the same time, concerns persist regarding accuracy, over-reliance, and teacher agency (Ko et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025). Research on GenAI-supported knowledge building further suggests that GenAI can promote elaboration and synthesis when embedded in collaborative inquiry rather than used as a stand-alone tool (Chen et al., 2023). However, most existing studies focus on PSTs’ attitudes or intentions, leaving underexplored how GenAI can be pedagogically integrated into structured, collaborative DT processes for instructional design.
Parallel research on Knowledge Building (KB) and analytics-supported reflective assessment (ASRA) provides promising foundations for addressing this gap. KB is a collaborative inquiry framework in which learners work as a community to advance shared knowledge through sustained idea improvement (Scardamalia & Bereiter, 2014, Scardamalia & Bereiter, 2022). Prior studies show that KB environments support progressive discourse and design-oriented inquiry in teacher education (Chen & Hong, 2016; Hong et al., 2019). ASRA complements KB by helping learners monitor, evaluate, and regulate inquiry processes over time (Borge et al., 2018; Rodríguez-Chirino et al., 2025). Reflective assessment refers to a learning-oriented approach in which learners using rubric or data about learning to monitor, evaluate, and adjust their learning strategies in order to improve inquiry and learning process and outcomes (Lei & Chan, 2018; Scardamalia, 2002; White & Frederiksen, 1998), while learning analytics make inquiry processes visible and interpretable (Tao & Zhang, 2021; Tong et al., 2024). Such support is especially important in GenAI-augmented contexts, where idea generation may be accelerated but reflective judgment and principled revision remain demanding. Despite these advances, how GenAI and ASRA can be jointly integrated within KB environments to support PSTs’ DT in instructional design remains insufficiently understood.
Building on these lines of research, this study designed a KB collaborative inquiry environment enriched with GenAI and ASRA to support PSTs’ DT through instructional design. The study aimed to examine the effects of the combined use of GenAI and ASRA on PSTs’ DT, and how PSTs’ DT competencies evolved over time within this integrative design. Participants were 110 third-year PSTs enrolled in a compulsory Modern Educational Technology Applications course at a teacher education university in central China. GenAI tools (e.g., Kimi, DeepSeek, iFlytek Spark, Doubao-Seed) were used as cognitive and reflective partners to support ideation, elaboration, and critique, while ASRA scaffolded monitoring, reflection, and action planning in KB inquiry. Reflective assessment was supported by the Knowledge-Building Discourse Explorer (KBDeX; Oshima et al., 2012), a learning analytics tool that provides social network–based visualizations of learners’ notes, enabling PSTs to see how ideas connect, how contributions develop, and how their work advances collective progress. These visualizations offer actionable feedback that helps learners track their inquiry, identify gaps, and deepen their inquiry (Wise et al., 2021). In this study, KBDeX functioned as a process-oriented scaffold aligned with the goal of fostering PSTs’ DT within a KB environment.
