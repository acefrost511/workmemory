## Abstract
This study examined the impact of integrating the Taiwan Adaptive Learning Platform (TALP) with generative AI-based learning companion (TALPers) on fifth-grade students' mathematics learning performance. The integration was applied to both self-learning and remedial instruction, with both contexts incorporating Socratic dialogue and Pólya's problem-solving strategy. Results showed that video-based self-learning with TALPer support yielded the highest learning gains. In the remedial setting, students receiving TALPer support also demonstrated significantly better performance. Both approaches were particularly effective for low-achieving students. Lag Sequential Analysis (LSA) further revealed that high-achieving students engaged in more complex interaction patterns with TALPers, sustaining feedback cycles, whereas low-achieving students exhibited simpler interaction patterns. These findings provide empirical support for implementing AI-augmented learning systems in mathematics education and suggest that structured AI support can promote differentiated instruction and student engagement to help bridge achievement gaps.
## Introduction
While traditional elementary mathematics instruction often relies on teacher-centered lectures, such approaches often fail to address individual differences in students’ prior knowledge, learning pace, and cognitive needs. Prior research has shown that video-based learning can mitigate some of these shortcomings, particularly by enhancing mathematics learning and achievement compared to conventional classroom instruction (Al Farra et al., 2024; Lai & Hwang, 2016). However, such approaches often lack sufficient interactivity and adaptivity, which may limit deeper engagement and personalized support (Merkt et al., 2011; Yousef et al., 2014). Recent research has shown that AI-powered systems can provide personalized scaffolding and adaptive feedback, thereby enhancing learning outcomes and motivation (Létourneau et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025; Pai et al., 2021). Advances in AI technologies therefore offer opportunities to deliver scalable, personalized, and adaptive learning experiences that complement traditional instruction and address its shortcomings.
Large language models (LLMs) have transformed education through personalized learning experiences and improved educational resource accessibility (Baek & Kim, 2023; Williams, 2024). These systems offer real-time feedback and interactive dialogue support while enhancing teaching efficiency (Laak & Aru, 2024). A systematic review revealed that while language education dominates LLM research (31.88 % of studies), there is growing potential for broader educational applications (Deng et al., 2025).
In mathematics education, LLMs have also been applied as personalized tutors, offering multidimensional instructional support through hint generation, comprehensive solution provision, and exercise creation, thereby effectively facilitating student autonomous learning (Ramanathan & Palaniappan, 2024). LLMs have also been investigated in collaborative learning contexts, where they can support group discussions and enhance the quality of peer interactions (Sixu et al., 2024). Furthermore, prompt engineering optimization has been shown to enhance LLM efficacy, particularly in mathematical concept instruction and assessment (Zhang et al., 2024a, Zhang et al., 2024b).
Despite LLMs' potential in mathematics education, several challenges persist. Studies reveal limitations in AI feedback; research with Finnish high school students highlights difficulties in guiding appropriate mathematical methods. Additionally, integration challenges require careful planning and teacher training to ensure AI promotes learning rather than mere answer-seeking (Setälä et al., 2025).
The Taiwan Adaptive Learning Platform (TALP), developed by the Taiwan Ministry of Education (MoE), serves over 2.8 million registered users and is distinguished by its integration of artificial intelligence technology to deliver personalized learning paths for students. To provide well-rounded learning support, TALP incorporates a variety of modules, including instructional videos, exercises, diagnostic assessments, unit tests, cross-grade assessments, learning packages, and AI tutors. Large-scale studies have demonstrated the platform's effectiveness in enhancing students' academic performance and fostering self-regulated learning (Kuo & Chang, 2025).
Although TALP has demonstrated great potential in mathematics education without generative AI (Wu et al., 2012, 2017), there remains a pressing need to understand how different AI tutoring approaches influence students’ learning outcomes and engagement. Prior research has shown that video-based learning generally yields positive effects across contexts (Yousef et al., 2014) and can sometimes outperform text-based learning (Al Farra et al., 2024; Kiili et al., 2025; Tani et al., 2022), yet few empirical studies have simultaneously compared video-based self-learning, video-based self-learning with generative AI companion support, and textbook-based self-learning with such support. To address this gap, the present study investigates the relative effectiveness of these approaches in both pre-class self-learning and post-class remedial instruction contexts.
Therefore, this study investigates three self-learning conditions (video-based, video-based with TALPer support, and textbook-based with TALPer support) and two remedial instruction approaches (video-based and TALPer-supported) to evaluate their impacts on students’ mathematical learning outcomes. Accordingly, RQ1 and RQ2 address the self-learning context, whereas RQ3 and RQ4 focus on remedial instruction.
RQ1: How do different self-learning approaches affect students' mathematical learning outcomes?
RQ2: How do students' interaction patterns with TALPer influence their self-learning processes and outcomes, particularly between high-achieving and low-achieving groups?
RQ3: How do video-based and TALPer-supported remedial instruction approaches differ in improving students' understanding of non-mastered mathematical concepts?
RQ4: How do students' interaction patterns with TALPer influence their remedial learning processes and outcomes, and what are the differential characteristics across student proficiency levels?

- ### [STEM education: Understanding secondary students’ epistemic cognition in the design process with the support of a personalized multi-agent system](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0360131525002726)
2026, Computers and Education
Show abstract
The engineering design process is a vital component of integrated STEM education, where students' design practices both shape and are shaped by their epistemic cognition (EC). Nonetheless, research on secondary students' EC in STEM contexts is limited, and implementing personalized STEM instruction that accommodates diverse students' interests and needs poses significant challenges. Harnessing a Multi-Agent System (MAS) afforded by Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) offers a promising pathway to address this gap. Grounded in the design thinking model of \_E\_ DIPT (Empathize-Define-Ideate-Prototype-Test), this study developed a \_P\_ ersonalized \_MAS\_, namely EPMAS, to support secondary students' STEM project-based design process. Twenty students, grouped into dyads, participated in a five-day STEM winter camp. We sought to explore students' EC within the EPMAS-supported design process and their perceptions of the system. Data were collected through in-depth group interviews, supplemented with student artifacts and interaction log data, and analyzed using theoretical thematic analysis. Findings revealed students' engagement with epistemic aims, ideals, and reliable processes, reflecting their developed understanding of engineering design. Contrary to common concerns about over-reliance on GAI, students demonstrated high epistemic agency, critically employing EPMAS's stepwise guidance as part of their epistemic repertoire to pursue creative solutions. These results suggest that EPMAS-supported authentic STEM learning holds promise for broader school adoption.
- ### [Investigating the effects of an LLM-based Socratic conversational agent on students’ academic performance and reflective thinking in higher education](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0360131525002623)
2026, Computers and Education
Show abstract
The rapid advancement of GenAI has created unprecedented opportunities to enhance student learning through large language models (LLMs). While LLM-based conversational agents have demonstrated potential in improving accessibility and instructional support, evidence of their impact on higher-order thinking remains limited, especially within the context of Chinese higher education. This study introduces the Socratic Intelligent Conversational Agent (S-ICA), which integrates Socratic questioning strategies with semantic generation mechanisms to promote student learning and reflective thinking in disciplinary contexts. A total of 94 university students from China participated in the study, with random assignment to an experimental group (EG) using the S-ICA and a control group (CG) using a non-Socratic conversational agent (nS-ICA). The results showed that the EG outperformed the CG in both academic achievement and reflective thinking, particularly in the dimensions of “reflection” and “critical reflection”. Cognitive network analysis revealed that the EG students activated more advanced reflective pathways, linking understanding, reflection, and critical reflection processes. Although no significant differences were found in learning motivation between the groups, interviews with students indicated that the S-ICA facilitated more effective learning and deeper reflective engagement. These findings contribute to research on integrating classical pedagogical strategies into GenAI-based systems, offering insights into how such technologies can foster higher-order thinking and guide the design of future collaborative human–AI learning systems.
