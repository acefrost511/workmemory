Abstract
--------

The use of artificial intelligence (AI) applications, including ChatGPT, within the educational field opens disruptive potentials of personalized education and improved administration. Nonetheless, complex ethical and governance concerns are also involved and need to be studied systematically. This paper is based on a systematic literature review (SLR) based on the PRISMA 2020 framework, in which the conceptualization and operationalization of ethical principles and governance mechanisms in the context of the educational setting are examined through a thematic synthesis of 42 peer-reviewed studies. Results show that there are five prevailing ethical and governance areas, namely privacy and data protection, algorithmic fairness and bias, transparency and explainability, student well-being, and accountability through human oversight. The gap in the current literature, recognized in the review, is the absence of combined ethical frameworks and unequal global representation, especially by low- and middle-income nations. This review was carried out in order to unify scattered knowledge adhocracy and offer a logical basis of responsible implementation of AI in education. It is unique in tracing the application of ethical principles in governance practices in the various learning environments. This research finds that it is essential to promote transparency, equity, and stakeholder involvement, and, therefore, to make sure that AI systems can support inclusive educational purposes. Such results can be used in policy design as well as future research in responsible AI innovation.

1 Introduction
--------------

The technologies of artificial intelligence (AI), and conversational models in particular, like ChatGPT have quickly become popular as a tool in the educational setting [[29](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR29), [34](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR34), [42](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR42)]. The tools have the potential to enable tailored learning opportunities, increase student involvement, and promote the effective sharing of knowledge [[1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR1), [19](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR19)]. However, together with these possibilities, the implementation of AI in the classroom also presents a mixed range of ethical and governance issues that have to be thoroughly analysed in order to achieve responsible implementation [[21](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR21), [65](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR65)]. Ethical problems are defined as issues related to fairness, responsibility, and privacy, while governance problems refer to the challenges posed by institutional policies and control mechanisms that ensure the responsible use of AI [[9](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR9), [68](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR68)].

Use of ChatGPT in schools and universities has brought about both advantages and disadvantages. On the one hand, it is provided with innovative applications (adaptive tutoring, assessment support, accessible learning resources, etc.). Conversely, it highlights issues related to academic integrity, excessive reliance on artificial feedback, and unequal accessibility across socio-economic and cultural backgrounds [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR2), [25](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR25), [45](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR45)]. The potential of AI-mediated learning as a learning platform and the ethical dilemma are both stressed by scholars [[22](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR22), [29](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR29), [59](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR59)]. This ambivalence is evident in the institutional reactions taking place across the globe, with some universities having tried guided use and the rest having limited access as a result of plagiarism and the so-called syndrome of academic dependency, as described by some scholars.

With the increase in the popularity of ChatGPT in education, the existing body of literature on the topic is not comprehensive enough and has a disproportional representation. The majority of research is focused on high-income nations and in the realm of higher education, and primary and secondary situations remain largely unexplored [[32](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR32)]. More so, current systems of governance tend to be abstract and provide general principles, including fairness and transparency, and accountability, without specific, practical guidelines that would direct the classroom-based application [[4](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR4), [58](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR58)]. This creates a major loophole in the synthesized frameworks that connect ethical values to practice in various educational institutions. Such an omission reflects broader anxieties over AI ethics scholarship regarding the inadequacy of operational models for responsible deployment [[44](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR44), [65](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR65)]. As a result, policymakers, educators and developers do not have tangible policies to work around real-world issues like mitigation of bias, protection of privacy, and responsible ways of assessment design. Therefore, systematic synthesis is urgently needed, which would bring the ethical and governance systems together into practice through education.

To address these gaps, the proposed study will conduct a systematic literature review (SLR) of the existing literature on ChatGPT in the field of education, following the PRISMA 2020 guidelines [[48](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR48)]. In particular, it looks at four areas that are mutually dependent: (1) ethical factors, (2) governance systems, (3) effects on outcomes in learning, and (4) responsible implementation of mitigation approaches. One of the earliest systematic reviews to map the translation of ethical principles into generative AI governance practices in education. The combination of knowledge in these areas could help the study to offer evidence-based information to educators, policymakers, and developers on how to strike the right balance between innovation and ethical considerations.

The paper follows the following structure. Following this introduction, the methodology provides the systematic review approach. The findings are synthesized literature results and the discussion gives meaning to the synthesized results in terms of highlighting important ethical themes, conflicts that are not resolved, global and equity considerations, and governance implications. Lastly, the conclusion will summarize contributions, determine limitations and suggest future research directions. In such a way, the review offers a holistic basis to the responsible and context-sensitive integration of ChatGPT in the education sector [[54](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR54)], which will translate into the scholarly knowledge as well as institutional policy-making regarding the responsible use of AI.

The utilization of ChatGPT in education provokes urgent ethical and governance challenges that have to be addressed to prevent the safe, fair, and efficient use of technology. The core issues of these arguments are student welfare, data security, bias, transparency, and human intervention.

Data protection and privacy are always listed as the key points of concern. ChatGPT is exposed to sensitive data during the interaction with students and needs to be offered informed consent and anonymity, and comply with the laws of data protection [[29](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR29), [43](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR43)]. But the higher level of personalization usually involves the need to keep data, which puts a strain on innovation and privacy. Lack of strong protective measures can destroy the confidence between students and teachers and between students and institutions.

Prejudice and impartiality are another issue. The AI models are trained on large sets of data, which can recreate cultural or language bias, resulting in discriminatory or unfair results. Bias audit and anti-discrimination protection are critical mitigation measures that need to be implemented to make sure inclusivity and equity are maintained (Dwivedi & Kshetri, 2023). The pilot projects in education have empirical experience that unchecked algorithmic bias may increase disparities, especially in situations with more than one language or limited resources.

It is also important with transparency and explainability. Both students and educators need to understand the way ChatGPT is used to address outputs critically to prevent agreeing blindly with machine-generated outputs [[29](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR29)]. Clear instructions and interpretable results make it possible to interact meaningfully and create AI literacy, yet little research evaluates their pedagogical power.

Teachers have to maintain some form of supervision, on the one hand, through automated assistance, and on the other hand, through professional judgment [[39](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR39)]. The pedagogs must take action only when needed, check the performance of a system, and make sure that ChatGPT does not substitute human pedagogy. The approach is consistent with co-regulation models that put particular focus on the shared responsibility of human educators and AI systems.

There is also a need to be continuously evaluated and governed. Institutions should evaluate the effects of ChatGPT on learning outcomes, integrity, and well-being on a regular basis and revise the policies and practices to address the issues that arise [[54](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR54)]. Governance standards that focus on transparency, reduction of bias, accountability, and protection of students may facilitate responsible adoption and promote conformity to ethical standards. Most existing frameworks are, however, normative as opposed to being evidence-based,thus, there is a need to design policies based on empirical validation.

To conclude, ChatGPT has a lot of potential, but the implementation process in the educational field should be supported by well-defined ethical principles and regulatory frameworks. By following the principles of transparency, fairness, the protection of privacy, and human control, the institutions will be able to exploit the advantages of AI and protect the educational values and well-being of students.

2 Research questions
--------------------

This review is guided by the following research questions, framed through the lens of responsible AI and educational governance theories:

1. 1.
   
   What ethical principles and dilemmas underpin the integration of ChatGPT in education, and how are they addressed across contexts?
2. 2.
   
   How do existing governance frameworks operationalize these ethical principles to ensure responsible and equitable AI use in education?
3. 3.
   
   What impacts—positive or negative—does ChatGPT have on learning processes, assessment integrity, and student autonomy?
4. 4.
   
   What strategies have demonstrated effectiveness in mitigating ethical and governance challenges, and how can these inform future educational policy and practice?

3 Methodology
-------------

### 3.1 Approach

The article was based on a systematic literature review (SLR), which was conducted to identify, analyze, and summarize current information on the ethics and regulation of ChatGPT in the field of education. The review met the PRISMA 2020 criteria [[48](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR48)] of transparency, reproducibility, and rigor of the methodology. The four PRISMA steps, which include identification, screening, eligibility, and inclusion, were clearly adhered to in order to organize the review process.

This review attempted to generate an evidence-based basis on behalf of informing how to responsibly incorporate ChatGPT in education by systematically analyzing peer-reviewed and credible sources. The qualitative thematic synthesis integrated with the aspects of narrative integration was used in the analytical process to allow conceptual mapping as well as finding the empirical patterns across studies.

### 3.2 Search strategy

A detailed and repeatable search strategy was formulated using Boolean operators (AND, OR) and truncated terms in order to address variation of key concepts. One of the search strings was representative.

#### 3.2.1 ChatGPT or generative AI and (education or learning or teaching or pedagogy) and (ethic or governance or policy or responsible AI).

The searches were conducted in multidisciplinary databases, including Scopus, Web of Science, and IEEE Xplore, as well as online repositories such as Google Scholar and ResearchGate. The choice of these databases was based on the fact that they cover technical, ethical, and educational research. Although ERIC, an important database in the field of education, was also previewed during pilot testing, it was excluded to focus on more multidisciplinary relevance as a representation of ChatGPT cross-sectoral uses in education.

Each database had its own syntax requirements, and search strings were formulated to meet these requirements and tested using pilot searches to ensure the search was complete and relevant. Policy briefs, institutional reports, and white papers (e.g., [[41](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR41), [56](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR56)]) were not excluded as grey literature, as they can reflect the emergent discourse and non-peer-reviewed evidence.

The initial search yielded a total of 80 records, with 32 in Scopus, 25 in Web of Science, 15 in IEEE Xplore, and 8 in Google Scholar and ResearchGate combined. After screening, deduplication, and eligibility evaluation, 42 studies were qualified to undergo full-text analysis and participate in qualitative synthesis. The flow diagram, represented in Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#Fig1), was used to record screening procedures and results following the PRISMA 2020 guidelines. Excel and Rayyan were used to streamline the management of the reference and screening process to allow tracking, documentation of exclusion, and methodological reproducibility.

### 3.3 Inclusion and exclusion criteria

Studies were included if they:

* Directly addressed the ethics or governance of ChatGPT or generative AI in education.
* Demonstrated methodological soundness and conceptual relevance.
* Were published in peer-reviewed journals, indexed conference proceedings, or institutional reports from reputable outlets**,** which are defined as those known for rigorous editorial and peer-review standards.
* Were published in English between 2022 and 2025, ensuring contemporaneity with the post-ChatGPT era.

The English-language restriction was applied to ensure conceptual precision and reliable translation of ethical terminology, although this may introduce linguistic bias.

> Exclusion criteria eliminated papers that:

1. (a)
   
   lacked relevance to education or the ethical/governance dimensions of AI;
2. (b)
   
   presented serious methodological flaws (e.g., absence of data, unclear design);
3. (c)
   
   were non-peer-reviewed opinion pieces, preprints, or editorials;
4. (d)
   
   were duplicates or inaccessible; or
5. (e)
   
   focused exclusively on technical performance without engaging with ethical, pedagogical, or governance issues [[26](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR26)].

All decisions regarding exclusions were reported in a PRISMA 2020 flow diagram and recorded in Excel and Rayyan throughout the screening phase to enhance transparency and reproducibility.

### 3.4 Screening and data extraction

The screening was done in two stages. The first stage involved the screening of the titles and abstracts to the objectives of the review. Shortlisted articles were in the second stage where full-text assessments were made.

Two independent reviewers, both with expertise in educational technology and AI ethics, performed the screening. Inter-rater reliability was assessed using Cohen’s kappa (κ = 0.86), indicating strong agreement. Discrepancies—occurring in less than 10% of cases—were resolved through discussion until consensus was reached [23].

Although no automated text-mining tools were employed, manual management and cross-referencing were facilitated through Rayyan and Microsoft Excel to ensure consistency in record tracking and decision documentation.

A structured data extraction framework was developed and pilot-tested on five studies to ensure clarity and consistency. It captured the following elements:

* Bibliographic information (author, year, country, and education level).
* Study design and methodological approach.
* Ethical concerns (e.g., privacy, bias, transparency, accountability, oversight, and well-being).
* Governance frameworks and policy recommendations.
* Reported impacts on learning outcomes.
* Proposed mitigation and implementation strategies.

Quality appraisal was conducted using study-design–appropriate checklists, specifically the Critical Appraisal Skills Program (CASP) and the Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool (MMAT). The studies were assessed depending on methodological rigor as high, moderate, or low. The resulting dataset comprised 42 studies, which were summarized in Appendix A and included information about the author, year, region, educational context, ethical or governance focus, and main findings, providing complete transparency and reproducibility.

### 3.5 Data synthesis

Considering the variety of the study designs and contexts, the narrative synthesis was performed in accordance with the Popay et al. [[51](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR51)] as to combine conceptual and empirical evidence by means of coding and thematic clustering. Recurring themes of ethical and governance codes were determined by inductive coding, further deductively applied to existing frameworks, such as the AI Ethics Guidelines provided by UNESCO and the EU AI Act. The concluding synthesis was structured the four areas that conformed to the research questions, which include ethics, governance, the impact of pedagogies, and mitigation strategies.

To determine the disparities and possible bias, heterogeneity was examined in three major dimensions, including geographical representation, methodology, and institutional context. Conflicting views were also identified, and the areas that should be investigated further empirically were mentioned [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR2)].

This synthesis provided an integrated understanding of how ethical principles are operationalized through governance structures in educational AI adoption, forming the analytical foundation for the results and discussion that follow.

### 3.6 Quality and bias assessment

All studies were critically appraised for reliability, validity, and methodological rigor. Factors considered included research design, data sources, and source credibility [[6](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR6)]. Rather than excluding weaker studies, relative weight was assigned: empirical classroom studies were prioritized in synthesis, while conceptual or opinion pieces were used for contextual framing.

To reduce reporting bias, multiple strategies were employed:

* cross-checking results across databases,
* including both peer-reviewed and grey literature,
* documenting exclusion reasons,
* conducting sensitivity comparisons before and after including lower-rigor studies [[7](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR7), [57](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR57)].

Despite these safeguards, limitations remain. The review is constrained by the modest number of qualifying studies and their concentration in high-income contexts, which may limit generalizability.

4 Results
---------

### 4.1 RQ1: key ethical considerations for ChatGPT integration

Four main ethical dilemmas include privacy, bias, transparency and accountability, which are always found in the literature. All these concepts are linked in theory, as privacy is the foundation of trust, prejudice affects justice, transparency allows control, and responsibility ensures adherence to rules, as well as moral responsibility.

Privacy and data protection are two of the most frequently emphasized aspects, as ChatGPT collects conversational data, user profiles, and behavioral information. Researchers believe that institutions should implement strong protections, such as encryption, access controls, anonymization, and informed consent in addition to making sure that third-party providers do not violate it [[38](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR38), [46](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR46), [49](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR49)]. In addition to compliance, various authors emphasize the importance of conducting continuous privacy impact assessments and maintaining open communication with students to foster trust [[33](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR33)]. It is an indication of an increasing agreement that privacy is not a regulatory necessitate but an operational process of ethical stewardship.

Prejudice and impartiality are still issues. Aydin and Karaarslan [[6](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR6)] warn that stereotypes or cultural injustices may be replicated by biases that are a part of the training data, thus benefiting certain groups of learners at the expense of others. Suggested mitigation measures would involve curating more representative datasets, regular bias audits and getting different stakeholders involved in model validation. Disclosure of residual bias is also considered as required to avoid the false impression of impartiality or objectivity.

Explainability and transparency are considered to be critical to responsible use. Oviedo-Trespalacios et al. [[47](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR47)] and Parray et al. [[50](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR50)] say that students and educators should be aware of the artificial character of ChatGPT, the scope of its functionality, and the logic of its generation. Some of the measures proposed include user-friendly interfaces, explanatory cues that are easy to access, and documentation that is not technical. Some of the current studies draw on the necessity of explainability mechanisms that fall somewhere between pedagogical and technical accuracy.

Ethical governance is based on accountability and responsibility. Rao [[53](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR53)], Sison [[58](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR58)], and Malik et al. [[36](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR36)] support the adoption of institutional governance frameworks that clarify the distinct oversight positions, risk-monitoring framework, and incident-response practices. Such practices as the creation of ethics committees, risk registers, and redress mechanisms towards students are often suggested. Notably, accountability is not limited to compliance it also includes academic integrity protections and educator professionalization, so that teachers would still be at the center of agency in AI-mediated learning settings.

### 4.2 RQ 2 Governance frameworks and policies

The literature indicates that the current AI governance principles of transparency, fairness, accountability, and privacy are still applicable but need to be modified significantly to fit the context of ChatGPT because it provides a conversation experience and is real-time. The international frameworks like those offered by UNESCO and the European Union offer good normative guidance but do not offer operational specifics that can be used in the classroom [[14](#ref-CR14),[15](#ref-CR15),[16](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR16), [58](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR58)].

Three endemic governance loopholes are singled out:

1. 1.
   
   Specificity: Existing frameworks do not sufficiently tackle issues that are specific to conversational AI, including the real-time monitoring and assessment abuse or adaptive content generation.
2. 2.
   
   Accessibility: Technical or legal vocabulary is common in many policies, which restrict their usability to those educators and administrators not familiar with AI.
3. 3.
   
   Empirical validation: Not many frameworks are empirically validated, and it is unclear how effective and scalable they are [[40](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR40), [57](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR57)].

Solutions proposed are focused on context-sensitive governance: a compromise between general moral principles and local response. Researchers suggest placing ChatGPT as a pedagogical aid but not a substitute for educators, conducting periodic bias and privacy audits, and establishing open communication of AI potentials and risks. It is also important in role derivation among the stakeholders (teachers, developers, administrators and policymakers) and feedback systems to provide regular monitoring. In general, governance has been regarded as not a singular regulation, but a participatory process that responds to the changing realities of education.

### 4.3 RQ3: Effects on learning and academic integrity

The literature provides an objective picture of the role of ChatGPT in education, as it has both a positive and negative side, but the disadvantages differ depending on the pedagogical setting.

On the positive side, ChatGPT promotes individualized and differentiated learning with adaptive explanations that are relevant to the pace and styles of learning of an individual. Research indicates an increase in student engagement, access, and efficiency to the delivery of content across disciplines [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR2)]. Also, ChatGPT can fill the learning gaps of students who lack access to human tutors or learning materials [[1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR1)].

Risks are, on the other hand, eminent. Researchers fear that more chances to commit academic dishonesty, such as plagiarism, unauthorized help, and false references, can be created [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR2), [24](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR24)]. It is sometimes stated that dependence on AI can destroy critical thinking and self-directed research, resulting in what has been called lazy student syndrome. Furthermore, educational inequities can be aggravated by digital inequalities and infrastructure since students in technologically capable or better-funded schools have more privileges. The issue of copyright violation and ownership of contents also remains a constant problem that makes institutional governance even more complicated.

These tensions are reflected both in institutional responses. A number of universities and school systems such as Australia public schools have temporarily banned ChatGPT over ethical issues, and some are restructuring their assessment systems to focus more on process-based assessment and artificial intelligence detection systems. In all settings, researchers issue warnings that inequitably-resourced institutions and underserved students are disproportionately impacted, which implies that unless the policy is designed with equity in mind, it may only enhance the existing gaps instead of reducing them.

### 4.4 RQ4: Mitigation measures and recommendations

This is reflected in the literature as they all come to a common 4-category of mitigation strategies: framework development, capacity building, technical and pedagogical adaptations and regulatory compliance.

1. 1.
   
   Framework development: A broadly held belief by many authors is the complete governance framework, which incorporates honesty aspects like prejudice, privacy, transparency and responsibility. These frameworks must also have a continuous monitoring system, feedback loop, and participatory designs to identify and remedy the emerging risks [[1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR1)]. Active involvement of teachers and learners is stated to be a key to legitimacy and situational appropriateness.
2. 2.
   
   Capacity building: One suggestion that is repeated is that AI literacy needs to be increased amongst educators and learners. Its use is suggested to be supported with training programs, professional training workshops, and curricula that would teach users to be critical in their evaluation of outputs and ensure academic integrity [[66](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR66)]. Capacity building is perceived as a core of sustainability, ethical integration.
3. 3.
   
   Technical and pedagogical adaptations: Adaptation: the successful application of technology necessitates a cyclic synchronization of technological design and pedagogical purpose. Through the joint efforts of teachers and AI programmers, it is possible to guarantee that ChatGPT tools would complement but not replace human instruction [[37](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR37)]. Pedagogical adaptations will involve AI-resilient tests, including oral tests, reflective journals, project-based projects, which will promote creativity, reasoning, and genuine learning.
4. 4.
   
   Compliance, and policy creation: The compliance with data protection frameworks (e.g. GDPR) is always noted as a requirement that cannot be negotiable [[24](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR24)]. Global ethical principles are then subjected to local adaptation so as to make them culturally and legally relevant. Another critical aspect highlighted by the authors is the role of grievance and redress mechanisms where users are able to report about harm or misuse [[53](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR53)],Temsah et al., 2023).

All these measures show that responsible implementation of AI in educational settings stands as a technical and social issue that needs constant review, cooperation among all stakeholders, and evidence-based governance. The authors also note that the effectiveness of these strategies lies in the fact that policy should be developed and refined through the iterative method, with strong monitoring because of the changing nature of technologies and learning practices [[66](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR66)],Daz-Rodríguez, 2023).

5 Discussion
------------

### 5.1 Maintenance of ethical and governance issues

In accordance with the review, the most ongoing challenges of the educational integration of ChatGPT are privacy, bias, transparency, and accountability [[29](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR29), [31](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR31), [54](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR54)]. These dimensions also relate well to each other, as privacy forms the basis of trust, fairness forms the basis of inclusion, transparency forms the basis of oversight, and accountability forms the basis of good governance.

The concepts of privacy and data protection become the foundation of issues since student data can be stored, processed, or reused in a way that jeopardizes the confidentiality [[30](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR30)]. Although Li [[33](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR33)] focuses on adherence to legal frameworks and informed consent, Kasneci et al. [[29](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR29)] argue that compliance is not enough and students, teachers, and institutions should constantly build the trust without exception. This highlights a more general trend, in which compliance-based governance is replaced by trust-based governance in which ethical responsibility is more than a legal duty.

Prejudice and impartiality are still hotly debated. As Aydin and Karaarslan [[6](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR6)] show, generative models can reproduce cultural and linguistic biases of training data and contribute to the increase in inequity of learning outcomes. Dwivedi and Kshetri [[23](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR23)] state that policy measures and equity audits should be implemented alongside technical interventions like bias audits, in order to make an impact. In the same vein, transparency and explainability are the key but complicated ones: on the one hand, Chan [[14](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR14)] and Lund and Wang [[35](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR35)] focus on transparent explanations as the driving force of critical interaction, on the other hand, Ariffin [[5](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR5)] warns about the simplified disclosures that create false confidence in AI outputs.

Lastly, there is the aspect of accountability that is a necessary yet underdeveloped aspect. Ariffin [[5](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR5)] recommends a clear definition of roles between the educators, policymakers, and developers, whereas Currie [[20](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR20)] cautions that the frameworks that lack implementation tools can be more symbolic than practical. Together, these observations suggest that ethical adoption must be governed in layers, i.e. with or through legal compliance, technical protection, institutional responsibility, and cultural responsiveness.

### 5.2 Opportunities and positive effects

Irrespective of these threats, the literature reiterates that ChatGPT has the potential to revolutionize human existence when used in a responsible manner. Adiguzel et al. [[1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR1)] and Zhu [[67](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR67)] show that ChatGPT is capable of offering personal advice, supporting various learning styles, and providing instant feedback, thus improving interaction and accessibility. On the same note, Calonge, Smail, and Kamalov [[12](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR12)] find better conceptual understanding of mathematics and statistics, whereas Bassiri-Tehrani and Cress [[10](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR10)] mention more broadly applicable applications, including medical preparation courses and creative industries.

ChatGPT is also promising in the elimination of resource-based educational inequality in resource-limited settings. Alfiras et al. [[3](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR3)] discovered that AI-enabled platforms assisted with the gaps in teaching during the COVID-19 pandemic, especially at the locations where the shortage of teachers was critical. Nonetheless, as warned by Halaweh [[27](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR27)], these advantages could only be sustained when equity guarantees are incorporated into institutional governance. This two-sidedness of accessibility and possible marginalization shows that ethical supervision and pedagogical planning cannot be detached when it comes to practice.

### 5.3 Another problem of conflicting perspectives of literature is present

The literature presents a range of attitudes over control, independence and disclosure. Regarding human supervision, there are scholars who recommend a rigid educator control, so that there appears a compliance with ethics and curriculum integrity [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR2), [41](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR41)]. Some others, like Biswas [[11](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR11)], indicate that creative thinking and self-regulation can be encouraged through semi-autonomous use due to the adaptive feedback. According to Sullivan et al. [[61](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR61)], a task-contingent model of autonomy is examined in which the freedom granted to ChatGPT is determined by the stakes of the assessment, i.e., freedom to experiment in low-stakes situations and human oversight of high-stakes tasks.

The same is true of transparency arguments. Full disclosure of model limitations, as proposed by Dwivedi et al. [[23](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR23)] to improve AI literacy, could also provide excessive information that could overload learners, as cautioned by Ariffin [[5](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR5)]. Graduated transparency as a pragmatic solution is based on simplified cues that learners can understand and more detailed documentation, which can be used by educators and policy makers [[66](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR66)].

Other tensions include:

* Academic integrity vs. authentic learning Currie [[20](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR20)] advocates the measures to prevent misconduct, and Sullivan et al. [[61](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR61)] believe that bans can suppress the honest support of learning. They both get to the point of redesigning assessments, including oral defenses or process portfolios, to strike a balance between integrity and genuine skill development.
* Equity and access: Adiguzel et al. [[1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR1)] emphasize the advantages of ChatGPT in the case of multilinguals and neurodiversified students, whereas Kasneci et al. [[29](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR29)] discuss the danger of digital illiteracy maintaining the idleness without specific measures.
* Governance locus: There are those who propose centralized institutional policies in accordance with global standards [[32](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR32), [58](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR58)] and others suggest decentralized, discipline-specific ones that react to local pedagogy [[4](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR4)].
* Open vs. closed ecosystems Ray [[54](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR54)] favors open models due to transparency but admits that they are costly to maintain, and Yang et al. [[66](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR66)] mention that closed systems are more compliant than auditables.
* Disciplinary variation: Arif et al. [[4](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR4)], Cheng et al. [[18](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR18)], and Sallam [[57](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR57)] propose harsher regulation in high-stakes areas (medicine and accounting) whereas Lund and Wang [[35](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR35)] propose more flexible and reflective application in fields-based on creativity and writing.

All these conflicts, taken together, underscore the importance of governance being context-sensitive to strike a balance between innovation and accountability. The human-in-the-loop (HITL) model is a viable approach because it involves AI as a proposal system, with educators serving as the final decision-makers [[61](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR61)]. To make this operational, researchers suggest: (1) risk-tiering AI use cases [[5](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR5), [53](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR53)], (2) progressive transparency [[23](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR23), [66](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR66)], (3) redesigned assessments to guarantee authenticity [[20](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR20), [61](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR61)], (4) equity protection measures [[29](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR29)],Adiguzel et al.

The next steps to research should be empirical assessment of these tactics by comparative classroom research [[25](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR25)], learning and well-being longitudinal studies [[36](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR36)], and policy implementation assessment of governance models [[56](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR56)].

### 5.4 International and shareholding principles

One of the factor constraints of the present research is the geographic and institutional focus of the research on high-income, higher-education settings [[25](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR25), [36](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR36)]. Such imbalance creates gaps in the knowledge about the functioning of ChatGPT in primary school, vocational education, or in under-resourced environments. According to Kasneci et al. [[29](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR29)], this tunnel vision may further sustain digital hierarchies, favoring more networked people and those that are more AI-literate.

However, a number of articles describe the possibility of ChatGPT to lessen the gap in education. The benefits of multilingual and underserved learners are recorded in Adiguzel et al. [[1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR1)] and Zhu [[67](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR67)], and its contribution to maintaining learning continuity during a crisis like the COVID-19 pandemic is demonstrated in Alfiras et al. [[3](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR3)]. However, these advantages still depend on the condition of infrastructures, the capacity of teachers, and the policies of digital inclusion [[27](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR27)].

Regulatory diversity further makes equitable adoption more difficult. Strict systems such as the GDPR provide higher data security, and other legal frameworks have a weak legal system, which exposes students to cybercrime [[32](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR32), [49](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR49)]. Halaweh [[27](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR27)] and Sabzalieva [[56](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR56)] emphasize that achieving success in governance requires a context-related and adaptive approach, which entails adjusting international ethical standards to local pedagogical conditions.

Some other authors also propose participatory governance where the voices of students and communities are heard and are included in the decision-making process to make it inclusive and legitimate [[8](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR8), [15](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR15)]. This model aligns with the capability approach and digital-divide models, where learners are viewed as co-creators of responsible AI use rather than passive users.

Ultimately, addressing equity requires a multi-level strategy. Policymakers must invest in infrastructure and training, developers must design inclusive systems, and educators must advocate for student-centered governance. Without these interventions, ChatGPT risks entrenching inequities; with them, it could become a tool for educational justice.

### 5.5 Practical policy implications to governance

One of the common conclusions is that current governance frameworks are still largely normative and abstract, focusing on high-level ethical principles but lacking operational guidance [[4](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR4), [58](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR58)]. Institutions then need practical policies that can put values into practice- like establish the role of ChatGPT in formative and summative assessment, integrate AI ethics in teacher education programs and develop easily accessible reporting and feedback programs to users.

Multi-stakeholder collaboration is also crucial to the good governance. Calton [[13](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR13)] emphasizes that, through stakeholder networks, there is a sense of mutually accountable and mutually beneficial, and BaHammam [[7](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR7)] emphasizes the need to have adaptive redress mechanisms to address emerging ethical issues. All these views serve to refer to the necessity of dynamic and iterative governance, as opposed to the rule-based models.

Context sensitivity is as well important. Halaweh [[27](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR27)] and Sabzalieva [[56](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR56)] caution against the wholesale adoption of international AI models, which may overlook local-level realities, pedagogical conventions, or infrastructural limitations. Indicatively, a model of governance that suits higher education may not be replicable in primary schools, and schools in resource-limited settings may need to establish basic digital infrastructure first, and only then implement more sophisticated controls.

To operationalize accountability, organizations are advised to establish risk registers, incident reporting systems, and conduct regular audits for bias [[5](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR5), [29](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR29)]. A human-in-the-loop (HITL) oversight [[61](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR61)] can be further embedded to enable AI systems to assist human judgment instead of replacing it.

In short, the principles of governance should be turned into practice. Educational institutions can protect ethical standards and harness the power of ChatGPT in teaching and learning by adopting flexible, context-sensitive, and collaborative structures.

### 5.6 Pedagogical implications

To be responsible in adopting ChatGPT would entail specific strategies that would be directed at the main stakeholder groups, namely, educators, policymakers, and developers, and would be supported by the principles of fairness, transparency, and accountability.

As a teacher and educator, it is crucial to focus on developing clear policies in the classroom to define precisely the circumstances under which ChatGPT can be applied to maintain academic integrity. Students should be motivated to be more critical by ensuring that their teachers train them on how to question the reliability, bias, and sources of AI outputs. Overreliance on automation can be reduced with the help of assignments where creativity, oral defense, and collaborative problem-solving are being emphasized. Adjustments of classroom practices ought to be guided by constant feedback gathering and reflective follow-ups. In addition, the teaching of ethical awareness (that is, privacy, plagiarism and bias) must be included in the regular learning to enhance responsible digital citizenship.

To policy makers and administrators, AI application should be standardized by institutional guidelines in the various levels of education. They must be consistent with the ethical principles of fairness, accountability, and transparency with strong data governance policies, including informed consent, minimalizing data, and by adhering to privacy policies such as the GDPR. The implementation should be followed by oversight committees and regular ethics audits, as equity measures have to make the implementation of AI not worsen the digital divide. The policymakers need to also insist on continuous analyzing the effects of AI on learning, well-being, and inclusion to facilitate evidence-based decision-making.

In the case of developers and technology providers, it is ethical-by-design innovation. This involves the use of heterogeneous and representative training data, creation of explainability and traceable outputs, and educator-friendly features like age-filtering, plagiarism detection and classroom features. The process of designing AI systems can be coordinated with the pedagogical requirements by collaborating with educators and students. Moreover, developers should be in line with the new international laws, including the EU AI Act and UNESCO AI Ethics Framework, to make them accountable to jurisdictions.

Lastly, there is the need to have cross-stakeholder collaboration. The long-term communication between teachers, policymakers, and developers can ensure that governance systems are not rigid in relation to classroom realities. The introduction of digital literacy and AI ethics in the curriculum will also prepare students to use ChatGPT in a critical and responsible way. Additional longitudinal and comparative studies are required to assess the long-term effects of ChatGPT on learning, equity, and cognitive growth.

By adopting such collaborative, evidence-based strategies, institutions can strike a balance between innovation and responsibility, as ChatGPT improves learning while also protecting the rights, fairness, and well-being of students.

6 Conclusion
------------

The incorporation of artificial intelligence into learning, specifically conversational applications such as ChatGPT, holds a radical potential of improving the learning experience, interaction and individualization. However, the benefits can be achieved under the condition of effective ethical governance, situational adaptation, and institutional responsibility [[14](#ref-CR14),[15](#ref-CR15),[16](#ref-CR16),[17](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR17), [20](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR20), [27](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR27), [28](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR28), [31](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR31), [33](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR33), [52](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR52)].

Five major ethical pillars identified in this review as being critical to the responsible use of AI in education include privacy, fairness, transparency, accountability, and inclusivity. Protecting privacy and data security helps foster trust and meet regulatory requirements [[33](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR33), [52](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR52), [60](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR60)]. Fairness will help avoid bias and inequity in AI-generated interactions [[6](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR6)], whereas transparency will encourage critical thinking and digital literacy [[14](#ref-CR14),[15](#ref-CR15),[16](#ref-CR16),[17](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR17), [33](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR33)]. Ethical integrity is maintained at institutional levels through accountability mechanisms, including oversight roles, audits, and redress systems [[4](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR4), [20](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR20), [31](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR31)].

Finally, the integration of the responsible AI is a socio-technical project, i.e., it needs to be governed properly, as well as aligned pedagogically, culturally, and organizationally. The results indicate a comprehensive model in which the design of technology, human control, and institutional policy support each other to govern the use of AI in education, focusing on ethicality, fairness, and effectiveness.

7 Limitations and future research
---------------------------------

While offering valuable insights, this review faces several limitations. First, the existing literature remains heavily conceptual and theoretical, with limited empirical validation in classroom contexts. This constrains understanding of ChatGPT’s practical performance and real-world pedagogical value. Second, current studies are predominantly situated in high-income and higher-education settings, limiting the generalizability of findings to diverse socioeconomic and educational contexts. Third, many identified governance frameworks remain broad and aspirational, lacking operational specificity to guide teachers, administrators, or policymakers in implementation.

Future research should address these limitations through:

* Longitudinal studies examining ChatGPT’s long-term impacts on learning outcomes, critical thinking, academic integrity, and socio-emotional well-being.
* Empirical classroom research capturing the lived experiences of students, educators, and administrators to ground ethical and governance debates in practice.
* Cross-cultural and multi-level investigations exploring ChatGPT’s application across primary, secondary, and tertiary education in both high- and low-resource environments.
* Policy-focused analyses assessing the effectiveness of adaptive governance models, generating evidence-based and context-sensitive guidelines for responsible AI integration.

Such research will provide the empirical foundation necessary to refine governance frameworks and inform data-driven policymaking in educational AI adoption.

8 Practical recommendations
---------------------------

Practical recommendations emerging from this review emphasize translating ethical principles into operational strategies across institutional and policy levels. Key priorities include:

* Fostering digital and AI literacy among students and educators to cultivate critical engagement and informed use of generative AI tools.
* Encouraging collaborative governance between policymakers, developers, and educational institutions to ensure shared accountability and adaptive oversight.
* Aligning institutional policies with international ethical standards such as the GDPR, UNESCO’s Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence, and the EU AI Act [[10](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR10), [58](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR58), [62](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR62)].
* Developing actionable, context-specific policies that operationalize fairness, transparency, and privacy through teacher training, risk assessment, and regular audits.

Ultimately, the path forward requires balancing innovation with ethics. When guided by adaptive policy frameworks, continuous research, and cross-stakeholder collaboration, ChatGPT can enhance learning, empower students, and promote equitable access to education. By embedding a culture of accountability and ethical reflexivity, educational institutions can shape an AI-enabled future that prioritizes student welfare, academic integrity, and inclusive educational advancement [[27](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR27), [55](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR55), [64](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-01051-y#ref-CR64)].
