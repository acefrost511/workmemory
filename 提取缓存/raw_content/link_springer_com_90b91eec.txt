Abstract
--------

The emergence of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) marks a fundamental transformation in education, mirroring wider technological changes across society. This conceptual article positions GenAI not merely as a tool, but as a relational paradigm shift that redefines pedagogy. Drawing on posthumanist and phenomenological perspectives, we explore how AI reshapes educational practice, focusing on shifts in pedagogy, teacher identity and student engagement. Posthumanism challenges anthropocentric assumptions, inviting new collaborations between human and machine. Phenomenology foregrounds the lived experiences of learners and educators in AI-mediated environments, emphasising the affective and relational dimensions of technological integration. By synthesising these perspectives through the interconnected dimensions of Positionality, Relationality and Functionality, we outline the educational implications of GenAI. Teacher positionality shifts from authoritative knowledge-holder to facilitator; relationality reframes AI as a co-creative presence; and functionality opens new modes of assessment, feedback and creative production. These principles inform the development of the ‘Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogy Model’ for reimagining classroom practice and teacher education. A fictional classroom narrative is offered to illustrate the complexity of GenAI integration, showing the tensions and possibilities educators navigate. This work contributes to emerging scholarship on ethical and effective AI use in education, advocating for approaches that respect human experience while embracing the agency of intelligent technologies.

Introduction
------------

The emergence of Artificial Intelligence (AI), and more recently Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI), has prompted renewed interest in the relationship between human and nonhuman forms of knowledge production and process. These developments bring new questions about collaboration, creativity and pedagogy in educational settings (Chen et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR15)). As GenAI becomes increasingly embedded in both classroom practices and broader social systems, it is crucial to examine how it might reshape teaching, learning and curriculum (Giannakos et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR27); Hall, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR30)).

This article explores the pedagogical shifts emerging from GenAI integration, using three ontological dimensions—positionality, relationality and functionality—to understand how roles and practices are changing. Our conceptual framework draws on phenomenology, particularly post-intentional phenomenology, to highlight the lived experience of these changes, and on posthumanism to attend to the agency and co-presence of AI systems in education.

The positionality of teachers is undergoing a marked transformation. Traditionally seen as central knowledge holders and curriculum leaders, teachers are now increasingly positioned as facilitators and co-learners in classrooms where AI systems contribute content, feedback and even pedagogical design (Teles & Moreira, [2025](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR62); Zhai, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR71)). This shift demands new approaches to teacher education, equipping educators with critical AI literacies and adaptive pedagogical skills (Evmenova et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR21)).

Relationality captures the interdependencies between human and nonhuman agents. In a posthumanist framing, AI is no longer a passive instrument but a relational actor within the learning ecology. Students, teachers and AI systems engage in dynamic meaning making processes, requiring a shift from top-down instruction to more collaborative pedagogies (Lan & Chen, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR37)). Educators must now consider how relationships with AI influence classroom dynamics, learning outcomes and the ethics of shared agency.

Functionality refers to the practical implications of GenAI in learning design and assessment. AI can generate personalised feedback, scaffold differentiated instruction and create novel forms of engagement tailored to student needs (Bearman et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR10); Shah, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR54)). Traditional tasks may give way to more fluid, AI supported learning experiences that promote exploration, responsiveness and creative output (Kaldaras et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR35)).

To bring these dimensions together, we propose the Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogy Model that reimagines teaching and learning in the context of GenAI. The model positions GenAI not as a supplementary tool but as an active pedagogical presence that reshapes educator positionality, enhances and problematises relational dynamics, and diversifies classroom functionality. This framework encourages educators, researchers and policy makers to move beyond instrumentalist approaches, embracing the complexity and potential of human machine entanglements in education.

Our fictional classroom narrative serves as a thought experiment and offers a grounded illustration of this model in action, showing how teachers negotiate these shifting dynamics in practice. Ultimately, the Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogy Model provides a foundation for ethical, inclusive and forward-looking educational innovation.

Literature review
-----------------

The emergence of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) in educational contexts presents both opportunities and challenges that necessitate a re-examination and shifting of pedagogical practices. A responsive pedagogy emphasises the fluidity of relational aspects that operate between teachers, students, and learning objects. The integration of GenAI into classrooms introduces new dimensions to these relationships. The literature review considers existing conceptions of digitally mediated pedagogical practices in classrooms and argues that GenAI has the potential to significantly reshape pedagogical approaches by transforming content creation, learning interactions and the very nature of knowledge construction.

### Pedagogy, relationality and learning

Pedagogy involves the decisions and judgements teachers make about how students learn most effectively within specific contexts, and how these choices are implemented through classroom practice (Shulman, 1986; 1987). These pedagogical decisions draw upon multiple knowledge domains: content expertise, curriculum understanding, learning theory, technologies, student characteristics, and the educational context (Forkosh-Baruch et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR24); Shavelson & Stern, [1981](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR55)). However, as noted by Van Manen ([1992](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR67)) pedagogy extends beyond mere teacher-directed actions and decisions. Instead, it emerges from the dynamic liminal spaces created through relational and contextual interactions among teachers, students, and learning objects. Phenomenologists such as Van Manen ([1992](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR67), [2015a](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR39), [2015b](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR40)), Sumara (1996) and Vagle ([2010](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR65)) emphasise these deeply contextual, cultural and relational aspects of pedagogy that are connected to human experience and learning. This perspective reveals pedagogy as a fluid, ever-evolving process that is deeply relational and embedded into contexts rather than a fixed set of practices that can be applied.

The concept of relationality in learning is well-established, particularly within social constructivist theory (Vygotsky, 1978; Wood et al., [1976](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR70)). Vygotsky’s concept of the More Knowledgeable Other (MKO) is especially significant, recognising that learning support can come from various sources: teachers, peers, parents, artefacts such as books and videos, digital technologies (Wood, [2018](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR69)), and more recently, GenAI (Stojanov, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR59)). Wood et al.’s ([1976](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR70)) definition of scaffolding as ‘a process that enables a child or novice to solve a task or achieve a goal that would be beyond his unassisted efforts’ (p. 90) further reinforces this understanding, emphasising the social constructivist view that learning emerges through and within relationships.

### Learning in the digital age

Digital learning can potentially extend educational opportunities by making resources and expertise globally accessible, yet implementing technology does not automatically improve learning outcomes. Its effectiveness depends on the complex relationship between digital tools and learners (Fawns, [2022](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR22); Selwyn, [2022](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR53)). While technology in education is not new, today’s exponential technological growth creates unique challenges. Online and blended learning have become commonplace, particularly after COVID-19, which propelled teachers and learners into online platforms and disrupted treasured approaches to teaching and learning (McLaren et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR43)).

Learning has become more ubiquitous, offering flexibility not contingent on time and space, nor reliant on formal education during school hours. This ubiquitous learning refers to educational experiences occurring anywhere and anytime, facilitated by mobile devices and wireless connectivity, allowing learning to extend beyond traditional boundaries and spaces. However, challenges persist with learner engagement and access to digital tools. Significant barriers exist in the form of the digital divide and digital exclusion (Park, [2022](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR50)), including inequitable access to tools and Internet infrastructure, gaps in critical literacy skills needed to evaluate truth and bias, and variations in digital competency levels (Lythreatis et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR38); Van Dijk, [2020](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR66)).

The digital divide represents not only disparities in physical access to technology but also inequalities in how effectively different populations can utilise digital resources based on socioeconomic, geographic and demographic factors. While the success of online learning platforms such as Massive Open Online Courses (MOOCs) and e-learning remains debated (Ellis et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR20); Shu et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR56)), Selwyn ([2022](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR53)) argues that teachers are needed in these spaces to mediate and extend learning as a social practice. This social mediation is grounded in Vygotskian perspectives, where knowledge construction occurs through guided interactions with more capable peers or adults.

Another proposed advantage of digitally mediated learning is personalising education to learners’ needs and preferences, though its effectiveness remains contested (Bartolome et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR7); Selwyn, [2022](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR53); Van Schoors et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR68)). Personalised learning involves tailoring educational content, pace and approach to individual characteristics, rather than applying a standardised curriculum uniformly. This approach allows learners to progress at their own pace with more choice while receiving customised feedback. Fitzgerald et al. ([2018](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR23)) propose that personalisation through digital mediation involves considering content personalisation, who performs the personalisation, how learning is personalised, and who benefits. This multidimensional framework highlights that personalisation encompasses questions of agency, pedagogy and power relationships in educational settings.

Access to digital technology has enabled learners to engage with information, expertise and resources beyond physical classroom confines. Learning can be globalised across countries and cultures, creating distributed learning experiences that leverage networks of people, tools and contexts to create authentic and diverse opportunities. Resources including virtual guest speakers, excursions, online tutoring, webinars and courses are abundant (Bell et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR11); Haleem et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR29)). Social learning can also flourish in online environments through peer-to-peer interaction, with McKnight et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR42)) noting increased student engagement outside school hours. This extended social learning transforms and disrupts not only where and when learning happens but also the relational dynamics between learners and with educators, creating communities that transcend traditional classroom boundaries. This disruption is likely to be even more pronounced with the advent of generative AI.

### Emergence of GenAI

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a product of computer science that aims to generate machines that mimic human intelligence and replicate human productivity in certain realms of output. Its evolution, which began in the 1950s, was designed to respond to inputs and produce intelligent responses based on predictions using existing databases. For example, the voice assistant ‘SIRI’ is designed to intelligently follow and analyse instructions and produce an output. While AI is not necessarily new, the introduction of widely available generative AI (GenAI) based on large language models (LLMs) has been described as the next wave of artificial intelligence. UNESCO ([2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR64)) defines GenAI as:

Technology that automatically generates content in response to prompts written in natural language conversational interfaces. Rather than simply curating existing web pages, by drawing on existing content, GenAI actually produces new content. The content can appear in formats that comprise all symbolic representations of human thinking: texts written in natural language, images (including photographs, digital paintings and cartoons), videos, music and software code. (Miao & Holmes, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR45), p. 8)

What is important to note in this definition is that GenAI is conversational and interactional and capable of creating new content using a range of communication modes including written, audio, images and multimedia. Increasingly, a wide range of GenAI embedded software applications are rapidly emerging. For example, social networking company Meta ([2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR44)) has embedded AI into products such as Facebook, Instagram, Messenger and WhatsApp as part of the daily and often invisible functionality of the app. Copilot, which works with Microsoft products, is a generative AI tool that ‘works beside you’ (Spataro, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR58), para 4). The use of the copilot metaphor is indicative of the relationality that GenAI is known for. Knowledge is generated between the machine and the human, working together with synchronicity. The educational impact of generative AI represents a rapidly evolving field of scholarly inquiry.

### Conceptions about GenAI, pedagogy and classroom practice

The positioning of GenAI in educational contexts often presents as a dichotomy (Grassini, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR28); Sok & Heng, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR57)). On the one hand, it has been heralded as a transformative revolution in teaching and learning, offering individualised and differentiated support through real time tutoring (Almufareh et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR3); Furini et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR25); Grassini, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR28)) and teacher support (Celik, [2022](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR14)). On the other hand, it is viewed as a significant threat to education, particularly concerning ethical, responsible and inclusive use, especially given its ability to generate content (writing and graphical materials) in mere seconds (Akgun & Greenhow, [2022](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR2); Sullivan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR61)).

Bearman et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR9)) and Bearman and Ajjawi ([2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR8)) add nuance to this dichotomy, arguing that changes in classroom practice due to GenAI will be mediated through shifting power relationships between students, teachers, technologies and educational institutions. They advocate for pedagogical approaches that position generative AI within broader social and literacy practices, shifting focus from technical instruction to supporting students as they navigate increasingly complex and ambiguous AI-mediated environments.

When GenAI pedagogy is viewed as inherently relational, knowledge and learning become distributed in the spaces between GenAI, teachers and students, shaped by broader social and contextual forces (Creely & Janssen, [2025](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR17); Mishra et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR46); Pratschke, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR52)). Teachers are no longer the sole holders of content knowledge; rather, knowledge is generated discursively between people and the objects with which they interact, necessitating different approaches to learning design and pedagogical enactment.

This perspective has historical foundations. Scholars such as Ihde ([1998](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR33)), drawing on Heidegger ([1977](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR31)), have long explored the relational aspects of human-technology interactions. What is new is how this relationality with GenAI is shaping these interactions? Mishra et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR46)) argues that GenAI possesses a type of agency that affects our actions and modifies learning environments: ‘We’re not just users or operators, we’re co creators, shaping and being shaped by these technologies in a continuous and dynamic process of co constitution’ (p. 245).

The influence of GenAI on classroom pedagogy remains an emerging field. In 2022, only seven countries had AI frameworks to guide teachers on implementing GenAI in learning (UNESCO, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR64)), though educational departments worldwide are now developing guidelines to support education (ACARA, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR1); Australian Government Department of Education, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR4); Department for Education, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR18); U.S. Department of Education, Office of Educational Technology, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR63)). However, how these guidelines translate to practice remains largely unexplored.

### Implications for pedagogy

How GenAI is being used in schools as a pedagogical tool is an emerging and important research area (Australian Education Research Association, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR5)) and one that is currently under researched (Chen et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR15); Ng et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR47)). Klopher ([2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR36)) highlights the sporadic and often vastly different approaches to how individual schools and districts are thinking about and using AI pedagogically. These contextual differences may stem from a research gap: studies have yet to bridge pedagogical theory with classroom applications of generative AI (Diaz & Nussbaum, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR19)). Selwyn ([2022](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR53)) argues that ‘AI is not a straightforwardly good thing for education’ (p. 628), rather it is a complex endeavour in an GenAI environment that is constantly shifting. Educational use of GenAI is relatively new and educational institutions are still grappling with potential changes. Generative AI is likely to reshape educational futures through three critical dimensions: repositioning teacher-student relationships, transforming task design, and determining which AI applications meaningfully enhance learning outcomes (Niemi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR48)).

Theoretical perspectives
------------------------

In this section we discuss the two key theoretical lenses–posthumanism and phenomenology– that are deployed in this article to understand GenAI and pedagogical change and construct the ontological categories of *Positionality*, *Relationality*, and *Functionality* offered as ontological categories for the examination of pedagogical change.

### Posthumanism

Posthumanism is a philosophical approach to knowledge and being that challenges human centric views and instead focuses on the interconnectedness of humans and nonhuman entities and their shared agency. One of the leading scholars in posthumanist thought is Karen Barad. Her notion of agential realism positions both human and nonhuman agents as actively participating in the ongoing process of meaning making and reality formation. Barad’s work extends agency beyond humans to include all matter, highlighting the entanglement of humans and the environment in creating phenomena (Barad, [2007](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR100)). She locates agency not within individuals but across networks of complex interactions. Her work, best described as onto-epistemological, dissolves the boundary between ontology and epistemology to demonstrate how existence and knowledge mutually constitute each other: being and knowing as one unified process.

Alfred North Whitehead’s early twentieth century work laid foundations for posthumanist ideas with his concept of creative impulse in the universe. Whitehead argued that creativity is intrinsic to all existence, suggesting that all entities, human and nonhuman alike, participate in the creative process of ‘becoming’ (Whitehead, 1978). This notion aligns with posthumanism and rejects strict separation between humans and other forms of matter, advocating for integrated, relational understanding and a holistic sense of being in the world.

Other posthumanist scholars have expanded these ideas. Haraway’s concept of the cyborg disrupts boundaries between human and machine, advocating for an inclusive, interconnected view of all beings (Haraway, 1991). Braidotti’s research highlights identity mutability and ecological implications of human existence with the material world (Braidotti, [2013](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR99)). These scholars suggest an onto-epistemological architecture of interconnection that acknowledges the co-constitutive nature of human and nonhuman entities, encouraging reassessment of anthropocentric perspectives and promoting an inclusive view of agency, knowledge making and creativity.

For education, posthumanist thinking reconceptualises relationships among teachers, students and learning materials, including technologies, viewing them not as separate entities but as co-constituents of educational processes that emerge meaningfully through pedagogical encounters. This leads to considerations of mutual agency and co-construction of meaning, distinct from traditional teacher-centred approaches. In educational contexts, onto-epistemological thinking helps us reconsider relationships between teachers, students, texts, cultures and learning materials as co-contributors within the educational process (Abdi, 2022). Learning thus transcends Western education’s formal boundaries to become onto-epistemological: where embodied being, sentient, feeling, thinking, merges inseparably with knowing the world (Kaiser & Thiele, [2014](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR97)).

### Phenomenology

Phenomenology, originating with Edmund Husserl in the early twentieth century, offers valuable insights for understanding the integration of GenAI technologies into educational practice. This philosophical approach examines consciousness, perception, embodiment and the essence of experience within the context of the lifeworld (*Lebenswelt*) where lived experience unfolds (Husserl, 1970).

For educators navigating AI integration, phenomenology provides crucial perspectives on how technological change affects teaching and learning experiences. Hermeneutical phenomenologists like Heidegger (1962) illuminate how educators interpret their lived experiences with AI technologies, acknowledging both opportunities and uncertainties. This becomes particularly relevant as teachers incorporate AI into established practices while maintaining authentic human connections in education.

Derrida’s (1982, 1973) critique of phenomenology’s tendency to view human experience as stable offers important insights for understanding AI integration. His perspective on the shifting nature of meaning making, always mediated through language and culture, suggests that educators’ experiences with AI will continually evolve rather than remain fixed. This aligns with Vagle’s ([2018](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR95)) post-intentional phenomenology, which views human experience as a complex assemblage of entanglements between humans and their environment. For educators, this suggests that their relationship with GenAI technologies remains dynamic and contextual.

Phenomenology provides a valuable lens for investigating how both educators and students experience technological integration (Creely, [2018](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR98); Dall’Alba, 2009). Each educator’s journey with AI is unique, shaped by personal context, beliefs and prior experiences, necessitating flexible approaches to professional development. Through this lens, knowledge emerges experientially through relationships between people, technologies, materials, spaces and time. AI becomes a co-participant in the educational process, influencing how meaning is created within learning environments. This challenges traditional teacher-centred approaches, suggesting the need to conceptualise a more collaborative relationship between educators, students and AI technologies, potentially transforming the educational experience itself.

### Posthumanism, phenomenology and GenAI

The intersection of phenomenology and posthumanism provides a robust theoretical foundation for understanding and implementing GenAI in educational settings, particularly as human and machine intelligence become increasingly entangled. Phenomenology’s focus on lived experience and consciousness complements posthumanism’s emphasis on the distributed nature of agency and the interconnectedness of human and non-human entities. Together, these perspectives illuminate how educators and learners might meaningfully engage in human–machine partnerships while maintaining authentic pedagogical relationships.

Through this combined theoretical lens, GenAI emerges not merely as a disembodied tool but as an entangled co-participant in the educational process, where human and machine capabilities merge and transform one another (Creely & Blannin, [2025](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR16)). Phenomenology helps educators understand their lived experiences and those of their students within these human–machine entanglements, while posthumanism challenges traditional anthropocentric views of teaching and learning. This synthesis suggests that effective AI integration requires attention to both the experiential dimensions of teaching and learning and the ontological shifts that occur when AI becomes intrinsically woven into the educational assemblage.

The integration of these theoretical perspectives supports a transformation in educational practice that acknowledges both human experience and technological agency within an entangled relationship. This understanding encourages pedagogical approaches that embrace the dynamic, evolving nature of human–machine co-evolution while maintaining awareness of the embodied, relational aspects of learning. Such a theoretical foundation offers educators a nuanced framework for navigating the complexities of human–machine interactions while fostering meaningful educational experiences in which the human connection to creation and learning is not lost.

Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogy Model
-------------------------------------

Figure [1](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#Fig1) illustrates a pedagogical model that reimagines classroom teaching with generative AI through two theoretical lenses: posthumanism, which decentres human perspectives to include technological actors, and phenomenology, which foregrounds learners’ embodied experiences. The model is represented by a Venn diagram, which brings together three interlaced pedagogical ideas: Positionality of the Teacher, Relationality in Pedagogy, and Functionality of GenAI in Learning. These three ideas converge to suggest a transformative approach to education that is both inclusive of non-human agency and responsive to the complexities of contemporary teaching and learning environments that involve humans learning.

### Circle 1: positionality of the teacher

This element of the diagram is about the role of the teacher and the prospect of shifting from a position of knowledge authority to one of a facilitator towards enhancing knowledge and educational experiences. Conventionally, teachers have been viewed as the primary source of knowledge, directing the learning process, even if the approach is student-centred. However, in the age of GenAI, this positionality is likely to shift or be more complex, along with the locus of control. Teachers may now be positioned as guides who help students navigate complex learning environments augmented by AI. This shift necessitates a re-evaluation of teacher education and professional learning, which would require new skills that enable teachers to effectively integrate AI into their teaching practices. The teacher’s role becomes less about controlling the flow of information and more about curating and facilitating a learning journey that is increasingly co-developed with AI and accounts for what works for individual students in this new technological space.

### Circle 2: relationality in pedagogy

In this circle, the diagram represents the concept of relationality, where AI is positioned not merely as a useful tool, but as a companion, tutor and co-producer in the learning process. This perspective aligns with posthumanist thinking, which sees non-human entities as possessing agency and contributing to knowledge creation. In this model, AI and teachers collaboratively shape the learning experience, moving away from a hierarchical, top-down approach to a more dynamic, relational pedagogy. The interplay between human and AI agents leads to emergent interactions, where teaching and learning outcomes are not predetermined but evolve through ongoing, reciprocal engagement, and AI is integral to the planning process.

### Circle 3: functionality in learning

The third element focuses on how GenAI changes and transforms classroom tasks, mechanisms of learning, creative production and assessments. AI introduces personalised learning experiences, providing immediate feedback and enabling differentiated instruction tailored to individual student needs. This transformation shifts classroom activities from static, uniform tasks to dynamic, student-centred engagements where the teacher’s role is decentred. Moreover, the design of learning environments must now consider both physical and relational dimensions, integrating GenAI functionality seamlessly into the structures of everyday teaching practices.

### Intersections and integration

The convergence of teacher positionality and relational pedagogy reshapes the educator’s role: facilitating spaces where human-AI collaboration transforms both learning processes and knowledge production into fundamentally collective endeavours. The overlap between Relationality in Pedagogy and Functionality of GenAI in Learning highlights the emergence of new pedagogies of posthuman interaction that incorporate AI as a co-creator with presence in the learning process, intricately embodying the role of AI in learning and making. Finally, the convergence of Positionality of the Teacher and Functionality of GenAI in Learning stresses the need for teachers to adapt to AI-driven functionalities, requiring ongoing skill development and a shift in taken-for-granted teaching paradigms and educational power structures.

At the centre of this model, where all three circles intersect, lies the concept of an Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogy that will emerge in everyday teaching practice. This central idea encapsulates a dynamic, adaptive, and collaborative learning process where teachers, AI, and students co-engage in the creation of knowledge, embodying a posthumanist approach that transcends the conventional boundaries of pedagogy.

Fictional practice narrative
----------------------------

### Introduction and rationale

Having established the theoretical framework for the Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogy Model, we now present a fictional practice narrative contextualised in an Australian upper primary classroom. This narrative serves as a tool for exploring the pedagogical complexity of integrating GenAI into classrooms. Mar and Oatley ([2008](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR41)) argue that fictional narratives provide valid simulations and insights into the real social world. These fictional practice narratives share similarities with thought experiments (Galili, [2009](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR26); Stuart, [2020](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR96); Stuart et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR60)), which are imaginative scenarios used to explore ideas based on ‘theory and experience’ (Bancong, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR6), p. 1). This approach has long been a common practice in both science and philosophy (Binini et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR12)).

We position this narrative as such a tool for thinking, rather than as a generalisation based on empirical research or a claim to truth. We have intentionally constructed it as a thought experiment, carefully crafted to evoke reflections about the complexities, tensions and pedagogical decisions that teachers may encounter when using GenAI in the classroom. The narrative serves two purposes: 1) it invites reflection, dialogue and theoretical engagement, whilst 2) exploring and illuminating aspects of the Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogy Model (Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#Fig1)). While this narrative draws on both authors’ experiences as former school teachers, we recognise that this fictional account cannot reflect the full breadth of experience found in primary classrooms. To strengthen the narrative’s foundation, we consulted several published empirical studies during its construction (Calleja & Camilleri, [2025](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR13); Picton & Clark, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR51); Zhai, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR71)). These sources provided valuable background that informed our approach, ensuring the narrative remains grounded in contemporary educational research whilst maintaining its primary function as a theoretical exploration tool.

This practice narrative follows Alicia, a Grade 6 teacher, as she navigates the integration of GenAI in her classroom, demonstrating how the three interconnected dimensions of our model—teacher Positionality, pedagogical Relationality, and AI Functionality—manifest in authentic teaching practice.

### The narrative

Alicia is a Grade 6 teacher (students are aged 10–12 years) at a multicultural suburban primary school. Her class has 26 students, and one-third are from EAL/D backgrounds with rich and diverse linguistic and cultural experiences, thus students from these backgrounds come with a strong linguistic and cultural repertoire. Alicia has always been a keen adopter of digital technology and uses an assortment of tools within her daily teaching pedagogy. Her school is a 1:1 iPad school, meaning every student has their own iPad connected to the school servers. Although the iPads have access to the Internet through Google, the school blocks many websites, including YouTube. She is glad that in 2024, the Australian Education Ministers have backed a framework on GenAI in schools, which means that her students can access the software.

Alicia arrives at school feeling a mix of excited anticipation and a little nervousness about the upcoming day. The grade six teaching team has planned a unit of work on natural disasters, and using GenAI seems like a natural fit. The students and herself use the Google search function all the time in the classroom, especially for research, and Alicia thinks that maybe there is not much of a difference. She has recently attended a professional development session focusing on using GenAI in the classroom and is keen to try it. Although she has spent the past few weeks researching GenAI tools and how they could support learning, she is somewhat apprehensive. There has been so much in the news about cheating and issues with the credibility of information; how will she navigate it all and ensure that her students can use it ethically but as a way to support their learning? There is so much to think about, and although she feels confident and believes she is able to navigate this new technology successfully, this is all new.

The first task that Alicia wants her students to do is create a video for their parents explaining the inquiry topic and why they are using GenAI. Alicia thinks that maybe they can use AI software so that the videos can be exported into multiple languages, but firstly she needs to get the students to think about GenAI, its challenges and opportunities. Alicia settles the class down after lunch and asks them to grab their iPads. She announces to the class, ‘Okay, today, we will learn about GenAI.’ There is a ripple of excitement throughout the room, and Alicia smiles at the excited looks on the students’ faces. ‘Now, I have had a play with the software, but this is going to be about us learning together,’ says Alicia.

With a sense of anticipation, Alicia opens up GenAI using the whiteboard at the front of the room and types in a prompt she has carefully crafted: ‘Can you explain how GenAI can support learning, including opportunities and challenges?’ Of course she has some doubts; this is normal when experiencing change. What if she’s opening Pandora’s box? What if the information is incorrect? What if she can not support the students? What if inappropriate answers appear? But in mere seconds, a message appears on the screen. Alicia hears gasps around the room. ‘Wow, that’s so fast’, ‘That’s cool’, and ‘We no longer have to write’, comes a cacophony of voices.

Alicia reads the answer out loud to her students. ‘What do you think?’ ‘Do you agree with the answer?’ ‘What other questions do you have about this?’ As hands shoot up in the air, students excitedly start to talk amongst each other. ‘My mum said that she heard that GenAI is cheating,’ one student says, and this comment is followed by, ‘This is great, we no longer need to come to school’. As Alicia listens to these impromptu comments from the students, she realises the complexity of this work with AI. It will not only be about the technicalities of the technology but how to guide them practically and ethically in its use.

Building from her strong pedagogical content knowledge and her previous work with technologies, Alicia puts her students into small groups and asks them to write briefly about the opportunities and challenges of using GenAI to research natural disasters. At first, it is silent as students begin to type and read, but the conversation soon abounds as they ask each other questions: ‘What do I ask?’ ‘What does that word mean?’ Alicia sits with small groups, guiding them in refining their questions and prompts further, and confidently showing them how to use GenAI as a conversational and writing tool. She realises that this is going to be about us learning together. Alicia hears laughing at one of the other groups, and as she walks over, she can see that the group has gone off task and is instead asking GenAI about the best video games and how to beat the dragon in level six. Bryce, one of the grade six boys, looks up and says, ‘Ms. Alicia, if we can find out all of this using the Internet, is this our new teacher? What will you teach us?’.

That’s a good question, thinks Alicia, and she reflects deeply on Bryce’s seemingly simple question. What will be her role now? Drawing from her previous experiences with new learning technologies and her emerging understandings about what GenAI will mean for the classroom, she begins to conceptualise possible changes to her role. Certainly she will be teaching students how to use GenAI as a responsive learning technology. In that, she will need to support students in how to craft appropriate questions and prompts, how to be critical about the output of GenAI and how this information can be employed. Will her role be more about supporting students to learn, rather than just be a deliverer of content? That’s not unlike the approach I have always taken, thinks Alica, feeling more confident as she moves back to Bryce’s table to support the student with the learning task.

### Analysis using the Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogy Model

The fictional narrative illustrates the three dimensions of the Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogy Model. Regarding Positionality, Alicia experiences a shift from knowledge authority to facilitator, evidenced by her acknowledgment that ‘this is going to be about us learning together’ and her growing recognition that her role involves teaching students how to critically engage with AI generated content.

The Relationality dimension emerges as students interact with GenAI as a conversational partner, with their excited responses showing how AI functions not merely as a tool but as an active participant in knowledge construction. The Functionality aspect manifests in Alicia’s plan to use GenAI for creating multilingual videos and researching natural disasters, demonstrating AI’s capacity to transform learning tasks and enable personalised, culturally responsive education. Bryce’s provocative question about teachers becoming redundant highlights the tensions at the intersection of these dimensions, prompting Alicia’s realisation that her evolving role centres on guiding critical engagement with AI, reinforcing the model’s central purpose of providing an integrated, collaborative approach to AI enhanced learning.

Discussion
----------

The rapid evolution of generative AI (GenAI) in education is reshaping pedagogical practices and classroom dynamics. Analysed through posthumanist and phenomenological lenses, this transformation reveals a reconfiguration of teacher-student-knowledge relationships. Focusing on the concepts of Positionality, Relationality and Functionality, in this article we have explored how AI invites a re-imagination of pedagogy that acknowledges the co-agency of human and non-human actors.

### Changing positionality: from authority to co-learner

The notion of Positionality highlights the evolving status of teachers in an AI-enriched educational context. Traditionally, teachers served as the central knowledge holders, guiding students through pre-determined curricula. However, GenAI technologies such as ChatGPT, Claude, and DALL.E now offer avenues for personalised content generation, shifting the teacher’s role from a provider of knowledge to a facilitator of inquiry and critical engagement (Chen et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR15); Holmes et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR32); Zhai, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR71)).

Alicia’s case study illustrates this shift. Facing her Year 6 class, Alicia announces, ‘this is going to be about us learning together,’ signalling a transition to shared and collaborative learning. This move potentially disrupts hierarchical authority and introduces a co-learning paradigm. Her responsibility increasingly lies in helping students frame productive prompts, evaluate AI responses critically, and explore ethical implications. In this way, the teacher supports students’ development of AI literacy, echoing the call of Nemorin et al. (2022) for teacher preparation that transcends technical competence to include ethical reflection and pedagogical transformation.

### Relationality: co-creation with AI

From a posthumanist stance, AI is not a passive instrument but an active collaborator in educational settings. Theorists such as Barad ([2007](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR100)) and Braidotti ([2013](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR99)) argue that agency is not the sole domain of humans but distributed across a network of human and non-human actors and within complex systems. In the classroom, GenAI co-produces knowledge through dialogue with learners, becoming a relational presence within the learning environment.

Alicia’s students respond to AI outputs with awe and curiosity. Their questions and observations suggest that GenAI is becoming a co-agent in their learning. Bryce’s comment, ‘Is this our new teacher?’ underscores the relational tension: as AI takes on responsive roles, the identity and function of the teacher shifts. Teachers are thus invited to move beyond instrumental uses of AI, engaging with it as a dynamic interlocutor. This reframing challenges educators to foster spaces where learners negotiate meaning collaboratively with AI, rather than receiving static knowledge.

### Functionality: enabling new pedagogical practices

GenAI’s functional capabilities extend the learning space to new areas of discursive practice. It facilitates personalised learning, immediate feedback, and context-sensitive support (Miao & Holmes, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR45)). Alicia plans for her students to use AI to produce multilingual videos and explore natural disasters, showing how AI enables differentiated and culturally responsive learning. Yet with this potential comes complexity. AI may decentralise the teacher’s control and provoke uncertainty about pedagogical authority.

Bryce’s challenging question triggers a moment of existential reflection for Alicia. However, she regains her footing, realising that her role is to scaffold inquiry, guide ethical reasoning, and support students to ask better questions. AI becomes a partner, but not a replacement. As Alicia sits with students, refining prompts and modelling critical thinking, she exemplifies the functional integration of AI in ways that enrich rather than erode human pedagogical relationships.

### Theoretical and practical integration

The fusion of posthumanism and phenomenology provides a nuanced understanding of AI’s educational implications. Barad’s ([2007](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR100)) agential realism and Vagle’s ([2018](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR95)) post-intentional phenomenology both suggest that learning environments are entangled, emergent, and affective. Teachers must therefore be attuned to how students experience AI not just as a tool, but as a co-learner and co-creator. Alicia’s uncertainty, curiosity, and ethical awareness model the reflective stance required to navigate these entangled spaces.

Phenomenological insights into students’ lived experiences reinforce the importance of educator attentiveness to learners’ emotional and interpretive responses to AI. The shifting atmosphere in Alicia’s classroom—from excitement to uncertainty to engagement—demonstrates that successful integration of AI depends not just on access, but on intentional relational and reflective pedagogy.

### Ethical considerations and future directions

As GenAI becomes embedded in education, ethical concerns around bias, data privacy and equity must remain central. UNESCO ([2024](/article/10.1007/s13384-025-00913-6#ref-CR64)) and OECD (2021) have emphasised the importance of ethical AI frameworks in education. Teachers, as Alicia’s story illustrates, are key to implementing these frameworks in practical ways. Alicia fosters critical awareness, supports inclusive practices, and navigates ethical dilemmas, positioning herself as a mediator between technology and learning.

The Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogy Model captures this dynamic through three intersecting dimensions: Positionality (teacher as facilitator), Relationality (AI as co-agent), and Functionality (AI as transformative tool). Alicia’s journey embodies all three. She resists reductive narratives of replacement, embracing instead a vision of coevolution. In this, the teacher’s role is not diminished but redefined, moving towards guiding ethical inquiry, supporting learner agency, and enabling AI-informed creativity.

GenAI invites a rethinking of what it means to teach and learn in the digital age. Rather than fearing obsolescence, educators like Alicia show how embracing uncertainty and co-agency can lead to richer, more inclusive pedagogical practices. Through ethical reflexivity and relational engagement, teachers can reposition themselves not outside AI, but alongside it, shaping the future of education in partnership with both students and intelligent technologies.

Conclusion
----------

The emergence of generative AI has triggered a profound transformation across educational landscapes—a shift that continues to unfold. Reflecting broader changes in how digital technologies reshape work, creativity, communication and learning, this article explored the implications of AI integration for classroom pedagogy. Through the lenses of posthumanism and phenomenology, we examined how AI disrupts traditional paradigms, revealing new dynamics between human and nonhuman agents in learning.

Integrating GenAI into teaching, demands a rethinking of entrenched anthropocentric pedagogies. Posthumanism highlights the potential for co agency between teachers, students and intelligent systems, requiring educators to develop more adaptive, relational strategies. Phenomenology foregrounds the lived experience of students and teachers, prompting reflection on how AI influences the emotional, ethical and embodied dimensions of learning. Together, these perspectives point to a pedagogical recalibration attuned both to shifting educational ontologies and classroom realities.

We propose the Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogy Model as a guiding framework. This model positions AI as agential and present in teaching and learning. It encourages educators to move beyond surface uses of technology, instead, engaging with deeper questions of what it means to teach and learn in an AI-infused world. The model integrates Positionality, Relationality and Functionality, offering a structure through which educators can explore how GenAI reshapes roles, relationships and ultimately their pedagogy.

While these theoretical insights are critical, they must be complemented by empirical inquiry, and the use of the fictional narrative might be employed to point to areas for classroom-based research and also could act as a provocation for what is or is not happening in classrooms. Future research should investigate how teachers and learners navigate the practical and ethical complexities of AI across diverse contexts, along with the role of parents and their connections with students learning with AI. It is vital to understand the long term implications of AI on learning, teacher identity and practice. As educational settings vary, locally informed approaches will be essential to ensure that generative AI is applied effectively and equitably.

Education in the age of GenAI calls for thoughtful experimentation, informed by theory and grounded in classroom realities. The Integrated AI-Oriented Pedagogy Model offers a foundation for this reimagining, embracing co-creation between human and machine, while remaining grounded in the ethical and experiential core of teaching and learning.
