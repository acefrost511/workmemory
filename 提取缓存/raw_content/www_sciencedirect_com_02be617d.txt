# Predicting learner autonomy through AI-supported self-regulated learning: A social cognitive theory approach

## Abstract
As artificial intelligence (AI) becomes increasingly integrated into education, its influence on learner autonomy through self-regulated learning warrants investigation. This study examined the predictive role of AI tool usage on learner autonomy, mediated by self-efficacy, metacognitive strategies, and self-monitoring, among university students. Grounded in Social Cognitive Theory, structural equation modeling was used to analyze data from validated self-report questionnaires. Results showed that AI tool use significantly influenced learner autonomy, both directly and indirectly through psychological resources. The findings suggest that effective AI integration should not only provide technological support but also foster students’ self-regulatory capacities, contributing to the design of educational environments that encourage autonomous learning.
## Introduction
In today’s educational context, artificial intelligence (AI) has evolved from a supplementary resource into a powerful driver of pedagogical transformation (Huang & Derakhshan, 2025; Kasneci et al., 2023; Wang, Derakhshan & Ghiasvand, 2025; Wu & Wang, 2025; Xu et al., 2025; Zhi & Wang, 2024). Its integration into learning settings has facilitated the development of interactive, customized, and adaptive learning systems that can adjust in real time to meet individual learner needs (Derakhshan, 2025, Saint et al., 2022, Chiu, 2024a). This advancement holds particular promise for language education, where the provision of immediate feedback, tailored instructional content, and adaptable learning pace is crucial for maintaining learner engagement, improving their thinking skills, and enhancing language proficiency (Derakhshan and Taghizadeh, 2025, Vázquez-Cano et al., 2021, Wang et al., 2025, Wang and Yan, 2025). AI-powered applications, such as chatbots for conversational practice, machine translation tools, grammar checkers, pronunciation trainers, and intelligent learning platforms, are increasingly being incorporated into language classrooms and self-study programs (Chiu et al., 2023; de Mooij et al., 2025; Wang, Derakhshan & Ghiasvand, 2025; Zhang & Yan, 2025). These tools empower learners by offering opportunities for autonomous exploration, self-paced learning, and immediate correction of errors, thereby expanding the scope for independent learning outside traditional classroom boundaries (Kelly et al., 2024, Lo, 2024, Shi et al., 2025, Zhou, 2025).
While AI offers substantial potential for enhancing education, its effectiveness depends not merely on the sophistication of technological features but critically on how learners interact with these tools (Guan et al., 2024, Chiu, 2024b, Zhou and Ma, 2025). Previous studies have documented the promise of AI to scaffold learning and provide personalized feedback (Liu et al., 2025, Liu and Fan, 2025, Saint et al., 2022); however, much of this research treats AI as a static or uniform intervention, often overlooking how individual differences in motivation, strategic competence, and engagement shape outcomes (Aldosari and Alsager, 2023, Li et al., 2025, Qi and Derakhshan, 2025). This gap is particularly salient in the context of learner autonomy—a construct characterized by the ability to plan, monitor, and evaluate one’s learning independently—which is increasingly recognized as a necessary competence rather than a desirable trait in rapidly evolving educational and technological landscapes (Little, as cited in Järvelä & Hadwin, 2024; Cerolini et al., 2022; Taub et al., 2022). Although AI tools offer opportunities for personalized and adaptive learning, evidence suggests that their benefits are contingent upon learners’ internal capacities, including self-regulation, motivation, and strategic engagement (Saint et al., 2022, Zhang et al., 2025).
To address these complexities, Social Cognitive Theory (SCT) provides a robust framework for understanding the mechanisms through which AI can support autonomy (Bandura, referenced in Järvelä & Hadwin, 2024; Derakhshan & Ghiasvand, 2024). Unlike prior research that primarily documents tool effectiveness, SCT emphasizes the dynamic triadic interactions among personal factors, behaviors, and environmental influences, highlighting the reciprocal causation that shapes learning outcomes (Sailer et al., 2024; Vázquez-Cano et al., 2021; Wang, Gao et al., 2025). In AI-enhanced learning environments, the “environment” extends beyond the technology itself to include adaptive content, automated feedback, and intelligent system features, which interact with learners’ beliefs, strategies, and actions in complex ways (Wang and Wang, 2024, Wang and Reynolds, 2024, Xia et al., 2022). This perspective moves beyond descriptive accounts of AI usage and positions learner engagement and psychological processes at the center of educational impact, offering a more nuanced rationale for the present study.
Three central constructs of SCT,self-efficacy, metacognitive strategy use, and self-monitoring, are particularly relevant in understanding learner behavior in AI-enhanced settings (Saint et al., 2022; Ahmed Abdel-Al Ibrahim​​​​​​​ & Hashemifardnia, 2024). Self-efficacy, or learners’ belief in their ability to accomplish specific tasks, plays a critical role in determining how learners approach challenges, persist in difficulty, and take initiative in learning (Chiu, 2024c, Derakhshan, 2025). In AI-supported environments where learners often work independently and make decisions about their learning trajectories, high self-efficacy can foster deeper engagement with tools (Chiu, 2024a, de Mooij et al., 2025). Metacognitive strategy use, encompassing planning, goal-setting, and monitoring one’s learning process, is essential for navigating adaptive AI tools effectively (Järvelä and Hadwin, 2024, Sailer et al., 2024). Self-monitoring, the ability to track and evaluate progress, is critical for learners to make adjustments based on AI feedback (Guan et al., 2025, Xia et al., 2023). SCT thus provides a comprehensive framework for understanding how internal personal factors mediate the influence of technological tools on outcomes like learner autonomy.
A growing body of empirical research has examined the intersection of AI technologies and learner-centered pedagogies, especially within self-regulated learning (SRL) (Kasneci et al., 2023, Wang and Derakhshan, 2025, Xu et al., 2025). Yet a critical review of this literature reveals that many studies adopt fragmented or tool-centric perspectives. For instance, research frequently evaluates AI applications (e.g., tutoring systems, grammar tools) based on immediate language outcomes such as test scores or vocabulary retention, while overlooking learners’ internal cognitive and affective processes, like goal setting, motivation, and reflection, which are essential for long-term autonomy (Hu, 2021, Rospigliosi, 2023, Xia et al., 2022). Other studies treat AI-supported contexts as static or uniform, ignoring the wide variability in learners’ engagement and readiness (Ahmed Abdel-Al Ibrahim and Hashemifardnia, 2024, Yang et al., 2025). Some acknowledge SRL but rely on narrow proxies such as usage logs or module completion, without capturing deeper regulatory strategies or beliefs (Chiu, 2024a). Others emphasize learner autonomy but do not theorize how it emerges from interaction with AI tools (Chiu et al., 2023, Aldosari and Alsager, 2023).
In summary, the literature underscores the critical roles of self-efficacy, metacognitive strategies, and self-monitoring in mediating learners’ engagement and autonomy within AI-enhanced environments. While existing studies highlight the potential of AI tools to support self-regulated learning, much of the research remains fragmented, tool-centric, or limited to short-term outcomes. There is a clear gap in integrating psychological constructs with dynamic learner-AI interactions to understand how autonomy develops over time. This study, therefore, highlights the need for a holistic, theory-driven approach that considers both learners’ internal regulatory capacities and their interactions with adaptive AI systems, setting the stage for the present study.
Few efforts have integrated SRL with established learning theories like SCT to provide a coherent account of how self-belief and regulation interact with technological affordances (Taub et al., 2022, Guan et al., 2024). Even among those that reference SCT constructs, they often treat self-efficacy as a static, background variable rather than a dynamic construct influenced by feedback and performance (Chiu, 2024b). Similarly, metacognitive strategy use is often described descriptively without examining its evolution over AI engagement or its contribution to autonomy (Chiu, 2024c, Lo, 2024, Sun et al., 2025). Overall, the literature lacks cohesive, theory-driven models that link AI tool usage with learner characteristics and outcomes through defined psychological mechanisms (Derakhshan & Ghiasvand, 2024). This gap highlights the need for comprehensive research that integrates cognitive, behavioral, and environmental variables to explain how learner autonomy develops in AI-supported learning contexts.
Building on these gaps, the present study introduces several key innovations. First, it adopts a theory-driven approach by integrating Social Cognitive Theory with self-regulated learning constructs to examine how AI tool usage influences learner autonomy. Second, it simultaneously investigates multiple psychological mediators, including self-efficacy, metacognitive strategies, and self-monitoring, providing a more comprehensive understanding of learners’ internal regulatory processes in AI-enhanced settings. Third, unlike prior research that often relies on static usage data or short-term performance metrics, this study examines dynamic interactions between learners and AI tools, capturing both behavioral engagement and cognitive-affective mechanisms. By addressing these dimensions, the study not only advances theoretical models of autonomous learning in digital environments but also offers practical insights for designing AI-integrated educational interventions that actively cultivate self-directed, strategic learners.
In summary, while prior research highlights the promise of AI to support learner autonomy, existing studies often overlook how individual psychological and regulatory factors shape engagement and outcomes (Chiu, 2024a, Järvelä and Hadwin, 2024, Cerolini et al., 2022). The literature suggests that access to AI tools alone is insufficient; learners’ self-efficacy beliefs, metacognitive strategy use, and self-monitoring behaviors critically determine whether AI facilitates meaningful autonomous learning (Xu et al., 2025, Guan et al., 2025, Saint et al., 2022, Kelly et al., 2024). However, there remains a lack of theory-driven research that explicitly models these mechanisms within AI-supported learning environments, particularly in language education. This gap underscores the need for studies that integrate learner characteristics, regulatory processes, and AI interactions to clarify how autonomy develops, thereby providing a clear rationale for the present research questions.
