## Abstract
Effective teachers should be equipped to solve complex problems across diverse instructional and learning contexts. However, many [teacher training](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/computer-science/teacher-training) programs struggle to bridge the gap between theoretical knowledge to real-world applications. The current study tackles this challenge by developing a generative artificial intelligence \*\*(\*\* GenAI)-enhanced simulation to improve preservice teachers’ problem-solving abilities. Using design-based research (DBR), we created a virtual environment that integrates problem-based learning (PBL) with GenAI technology. The simulation was rigorously refined through expert review and usability testing before being implemented in a teacher training program. We evaluated its effectiveness by comparing three groups: (1) a text-based scenario, (2) a rule-based simulation, and (3) a GenAI-enhanced simulation. Pre- and post-test results showed significant improvements in problem-solving skills for both the rule-based and GenAI-enhanced simulation groups compared to the text-based scenario group. Notably, qualitative findings revealed that students reported heightened realism and immersion in the GenAI-enhanced simulation, attributing this to more dynamic interactions with [AI](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/computer-science/artificial-intelligence) agents that helped them better contextualize PBL and increased their motivation. Our study findings contribute design principles for developing GenAI-enhanced simulations in teacher education, offering promising insights into leveraging AI technology to create more engaging and effective training experiences.
## Introduction
Teachers often encounter intricate and unexpected problems during their teaching endeavors. They need to be decision makers and problem solvers in various instructional and learning situations. However, previous research has suggested that teacher training programs have constraints in bridging theory and practice (Hennissen et al., 2017; Hursen, 2021; Zeichner, 2012). Consequently, novice teachers face challenges in applying theoretical knowledge acquired in their teacher training programs to address complex real-world instructional problems they encounter in their classrooms.
To address this theory-practice gap, researchers have stressed the importance of authentic learning (Darling-Hammond and Snyder, 2000; Herrington & Oliver, 2000) to improve preservice teachers’ abilities to transfer instructional knowledge and skills to real situations (Dawson & Lignugaris-Kraft, 2017). While teacher training programs provide practicum opportunities for preservice teachers to be exposed to complex and multi-dimensional problems in real teaching and prepare them for unforeseen issues that may exist in the real world (Ballantyne et al., 2020), they may not always have ample opportunities for practical teaching experience before they teach their future students (Cheong, 2010; Dalgarno et al., 2016; Ward et al., 2018).
Virtual reality (VR) simulation can serve as a safe and effective alternative for preservice teachers to experience authentic problem situations and practice their teaching skills without the potential risk of negatively impacting students due to their inexperience (Dalinger et al., 2020; Delamarre et al., 2021). Many studies have demonstrated the efficacy of VR simulations in teacher training, highlighting their effects on enhancing motivation, interaction, actual practice, and embodiment of abstract concepts in concrete experiences through their advantages in visualization fluidity, interactivity, and immersion (Lamb et al., 2018; Lamb & Etopio, 2020; Psotka, 2013). For instance, Lamb and Etopio (2020) indicated that there are no significant differences in preservice teachers’ physiological (e.g., pulse, respiration, eye tracking, and galvanic skin response) and affective responses between VR simulation and face-to-face environments for preservice teacher education.
VR simulations can effectively immerse preservice teachers in a contextualized learning environment, enabling them to apply their teaching knowledge to solve authentic problems (Ke et al., 2021). However, some researchers have identified limitations in VR simulations, such as low realism and limited interaction with non-player characters (Correia et al., 2016; Lovreglio et al., 2022) as well as difficulties in accurately reflecting the complexity and unpredictability inherent in real-world problems (Schwarze et al., 2019; Wang, 2012), which could potentially undermine the learning outcomes of VR simulations.
Recently, incorporating generative AI (GenAI) into virtual simulations has been attracting attention for its potential to offer students more realistic and dynamic learning experiences, with its capability to yield human-like responses and adapt to user inputs (Jeong, Lee, Byun, & Moon, 2024). In particular, some researchers have highlighted the capability of GenAI to empower virtual agents to replicate intricate and dynamic human cognition and interaction. This advancement could lead to more genuine and engaging problem-solving scenarios for learners. However, empirical studies on the application of GenAI in virtual simulations are still nascent as GenAI debuted in 2022. This lack of empirical research presents a significant gap in our understanding of how to effectively design and integrate GenAI into educational simulations. Therefore, this research aims to explore the effective design and development of GenAI-enhanced virtual simulations to improve the problem-solving abilities of preservice teachers employing a design-based research (DBR) methodology. While some studies have suggested the potential of GenAI to enhance simulation immersion and authenticity (Park et al., 2023, pp. 1–22; Qian et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023), its application in teacher education remains underexplored. To our knowledge, this is the first study to integrate GenAI into teacher training simulations using a DBR approach. Although Lee (2023) introduced a GenAI-enhanced simulation, the study was limited to prototype development and small-scale usability testing. Compared to previous studies, our research seeks to contribute to the literature on educational simulation by extracting key design principles that guide the systematic development of GenAI-based simulations through an iterative design-execution-revision process, rather than focusing solely on one-time usability testing or demonstrating effectiveness.

- ### [Harnessing artificial intelligence for preservice teachers’ development: A scoping review of applications, benefits, and challenges](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666557326000017)
2026, Computers and Education Open
Show abstract
The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into teacher education is profoundly reshaping the professional development of preservice teachers. This scoping review examines the application of AI in this context, aiming to delineate its characteristics, benefits, and challenges. Following the PRISMA-ScR guidelines, this study conducted a systematic search of three academic databases (Web of Science, ScienceDirect, and EBSCOhost) for empirical research published between 2020 and 2025. After screening an initial pool of literature, 55 studies were included for analysis. The findings reveal a diverse landscape of AI applications, technologies, and subject distributions in preservice teacher development. The analysis indicates that AI can significantly enhance various competencies, including instructional design, subject-specific instruction, practical teaching skills, evaluation efficiency, reflective practice, critical thinking, technology integration, and pedagogical innovation. However, several challenges were identified, encompassing technical limitations, the risk of over-reliance, ethical and social concerns, and barriers to implementation. This review advocates for a proactive approach to leveraging AI’s innovative potential while reducing its associated risks. Future research should focus on the continual evaluation and adaptation of AI tools to ensure they effectively support and advance the professional development of preservice teachers.
- ### [The double-edged sword of technology: Investigating technostress and techno-eustress in academic burnout through digital literacy, internet self-efficacy, and cognitive flexibility](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0360131525003094)
2026, Computers and Education
Show abstract
The swift advancement of technology in educational contexts introduces a range of challenges alongside significant benefits. Technostress and techno-eustress have been identified as significant determinants of academic achievement in higher education, influencing students’ well-being, productivity, and levels of burnout. However, there is limited research on how psychological mechanisms play a mediating role in the relationship between technostress, techno-eustress, and academic burnout. To fill this gap, two serial mediation models with three mediators were proposed to explore the associations among technostress, techno-eustress, digital literacy, internet self-efficacy, cognitive flexibility, and academic burnout in a random sample of university students from different education faculties in Türkiye ( \_N\_ = 677). The total effect of technostress on academic burnout was significant. This first model, with an additional three mediators, accounted for 16 % of the explained variance in academic burnout, and then 5 % without mediators. In the second model, the total effect of techno-eustress on academic burnout was nonsignificant (p = 0.602), while the direct effect had a small impact (p = 0.029). However, the total indirect effect of techno-eustress on academic burnout was significant and serially mediated by digital literacy, internet self-efficacy, and cognitive flexibility, accounting for 13 % of the explained variance. Triple serial mediation analyses indicated that digital literacy enhances internet self-efficacy, which in turn improves cognitive flexibility. This sequence ultimately promotes techno-eustress and mitigates technostress, resulting in a reduction of academic burnout. Focusing on these mediators as protective resources against technostress may enhance psychological and behavioral outcomes in higher education students.
