Abstract
--------

This longitudinal study, grounded in Dynamic Systems Theory (DST), explores how language teachers’ integration of AI tools evolves over an 18-week period, revealing AI adoption as a complex pedagogical transformation rather than a simple technological shift. Drawing on these findings, the research introduces two models: (1) the DST-informed Pedagogical Content Knowledge (PCK) model, which specifies AI-empowered PCK by detailing the five domains of knowledge teachers draw upon, and how these inform teaching practice and scaffolding strategies for personalised student learning; and (2) the AI-in-PCK stage framework, which maps the trajectory of AI adoption, illustrating how teachers’ concerns and practices evolve from initial exploration and experimentation to strategic integration and ongoing learning, while responding to classroom realities and student feedback. Together, these models illuminate adaptive, multifaceted changes in PCK and teaching practice, highlighting how AI integration shapes decision-making and professional growth. The findings underscore critical implications for designing flexible, context-responsive professional learning and systemic support strategies, particularly in under-resourced rural contexts, and provide a foundation for future AI-in-PCK research.

Introduction
------------

It is acknowledged that teachers’ Pedagogical Content Knowledge (PCK) has impacted students’ development and academic performance positively (e.g., Baumert et al., [2010](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR6); Gess-Newsome et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR23); Holtsch et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR30); Kulgemeyer & Riese, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR36)), and it is widely accepted as an essential part of teacher quality (Jiang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR32); Park et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR56), [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR55)). Teachers’ PCK is a unique knowledge that teachers have to transform content knowledge into knowledge students can understand. PCK involves teachers’ understanding of their students’ prior knowledge, learning interests, development status and diverse backgrounds. It also enables teachers to provide appropriate teaching and scaffolding strategies to suit the various abilities of students (Kumar & Wiseman, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR37); Nurulsari et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR54); Park et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR55)).

Due to persistent inequalities between various educational settings (Goundar, [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR27); Miao et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR45); Zulu et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR79)), many teachers often face unique challenges related to PCK. For example, these challenges can stem from disparities in teacher qualifications (Huang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR31); Zulu et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR79)). In particular, rural teachers often lack access to adequate teaching resources and professional development opportunities, making it difficult to apply effective PCK strategies, such as scaffolding, to meet students’ diverse academic needs.

Generative Artificial Intelligence (AI), particularly language models like ChatGPT, has been acknowledged to start transforming the landscape of education, including teachers’ PCK development and the inequalities concerns (e.g., Ning et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR53)). Its ability to generate human-like text, provide instant feedback, and support personalised learning offers new opportunities for both teachers and students in diverse contexts. For example, since the emergence of AI, the generative AI serves as a powerful assistant in enhance their PCK particularly in lesson planning, assessment design and content development. For example, language teachers can use AI to generate reading passages, vocabulary lists, quizzes, and writing prompts tailored to different proficiency levels (Shi & Aryadoust, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR64); Suharsono et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR71)). This not only saves time but also allows educators to adapt materials to specific learner needs, especially in multi-level classrooms.

However, current research is mainly conducted through interviewing or surveying teachers or their own reflections of use of AI in their teaching (e.g., Barnes & Tour, [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR5); Creely et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR14); Ning et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR53)). Very limited research, particularly longitudinal research has been conducted in the field to investigate the impact of rural teachers’ AI use on their own PCK and their students’ learning outcomes. Therefore, this study implemented a longitudinal 18-week study on eight rural language teachers’ AI use in their planning, teaching process, and assessment design and implementation.

Teachers’ PCK
-------------

In 1986, Shulman debated the teachers’ role and highlighted the mismatch between teacher knowledge and the teacher profession. In his work “*Those Who Understand: Knowledge Growth in Teaching*”, he emphasised the importance of subject matter knowledge for teaching and identified an imbalance of teaching competence covering delivering the content of the lesson, answering students’ questions and clarifying confusions. Shulman developed three categories of content knowledge: (a) subject matter content knowledge (CK), (b) pedagogical content knowledge (PCK) and (c) curriculum knowledge. Following this, Shulman and colleagues extended to propose seven categories of knowledge for teaching: (1) content knowledge, (2) general pedagogical knowledge, (3) pedagogical knowledge, (4) knowledge of learners and their characteristics, (5) knowledge of educational purpose and values, (6) curriculum knowledge and (7) pedagogical content knowledge. While Shulman ([1986](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR66)) introduced a new concept of PCK which trigged a movement for teacher knowledge of theorising teaching practices, he argued that “incomplete and trivial definitions of teaching held by the policy community comprise a far greater danger to good education than does a more serious attempt to formulate the knowledge base” (Shulman, [1987](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR67), p. 20). When we consider teachers’ frames of reference when planning for teaching, “pedagogical content knowledge is a special amalgam of content and pedagogy that is uniquely the province of teachers, their own special form of professional understanding” (Shulman, [1987](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR67), p. 8).

PCK is widely recognised as a key component of teacher quality across different educational systems. For example, in the United States, PCK was described as necessary for teaching a subject effectively, and the Framework of the National Research Council ([2012](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR49)) required teachers to develop students’ understanding of subject practices and choose appropriate pedagogical approaches to teach important concepts in disciplinary core areas. In Australia, since 2013, the Australian Institute for Teaching and School Leadership (AITSL) has required all the initial teacher training programs to address the sequence of taught, practiced, and assessed learning across every curriculum area. Park et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR55)) also stated that South Korean national standards and reform documents emphasised PCK as a critical element of teacher quality impacting student learning.

Based on Ning et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR53)), Sri et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR70)), and Paul et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR57)) frameworks, this study highlights the five elements of teachers’ PCK, involving teacher’s scaffolding strategies, their curriculum knowledge, their knowledge of their students and educational contexts and subject content knowledge (see Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#Fig1)).

Five elements of teachers’ PCK

### Teaching practice and scaffolding

The elements of PCK are related to teaching practice and scaffolding (Kulgemeyer & Riese, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR36)) in diverse educational contexts (Bryk et al., [2012](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR8)). Through effective teaching practices and scaffolding such as questioning and formative assessment, teachers can adapt their instruction to meet the diverse needs of learners and ensure that content is taught in a meaningful and accessible way. Moreover, with the further development and application of cultural-historical and scaffolding theories, teaching and learning have shifted from static delivery to more dynamic and flexible approaches. Within this evolving context, effective classroom management becomes integral to scaffolding, as it establishes the structured and responsive environment necessary for differentiated support (Shen et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR63)). At the same time, the integration of technological knowledge into teaching practices further enhances scaffolding by aligning with students’ needs and learning trajectories (Nilsson, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR51); Ning et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR53)).

### Educational contexts

Another key element in teachers’ PCK is their knowledge of educational context and knowledge of purpose of teaching and learning (Polat et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR58)). It is particularly important in understanding teachers’ PCK in rural areas, because the role of teachers has also been changed to align with the complex learners in today’s rural teaching and learning context, and studying teachers’ PCK is crucial for enhancing teaching and learning, particularly in rural contexts. However, many traditional teacher training programs are designed with urban or resource-rich environments in mind, and they may not address the realities of rural schools (Goundar, [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR27); Jing, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR33)). Moreover, limited resources, such as a shortage of trained teachers, textbooks, and technologies make it harder for rural students to access high-quality teaching and learning.

When rural teachers have strong PCK, they are better able to create engaging, supportive environments where their students feel confident to apply their learnt knowledge into practice. However, it requires rural teachers to scaffold learning in ways that respect students’ backgrounds while pushing them toward higher levels of learning achievements (Jiang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR32); Park et al., [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR56), [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR55)). Therefore, recognising and building rural teachers’ PCK knowledge can lead to more equitable educational systems where all students, regardless of location, have access to high-quality education.

### Student diversity

Directly connected to teachers’ understanding of the topics and concepts to be taught, PCK provides teachers with the knowledge to accommodate the needs of diverse students (Li et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR40); Park et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR55); Sadler et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR61)). Teachers’ teaching practices shall also include an understanding of what makes the learning of specific topics easy or difficult based on students’ different ages and backgrounds. They also include the students’ demographic background and characteristics, as well as their achievements in subject learning and other social and emotional achievements. Shulman’s pedagogical content knowledge is most useful when considering the impact of student misconceptions and their influence on subsequent learning.

### Curriculum knowledge

One key element of teachers’ PCK is their understanding of curriculum knowledge, which includes familiarity with the materials and programs that function as essential “tools of the trade” for teaching (Deng et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR17)). Teachers with strong curriculum knowledge are able to make informed decisions about what to teach, in what order, and how to connect content across grade levels and subject areas to support coherent student learning (Gong et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR25)).

### Subject content knowledge

Finally, PCK has been conceptualised as a distinct body of knowledge that is closely intertwined with subject matter expertise. Shulman, cited in Setyaningrum et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR62)), characterises the justification of a teacher’s subject content knowledge as “the amount and organisation of knowledge in the mind of the teacher” (p. 9). He further explains that subject content knowledge encompasses both the substantive structure (the core concepts and principles of a discipline) and the syntactic structure, which involves the rules of evidence and ways in which knowledge is generated and validated within that discipline.

### Integration of the elements

Shulman ([1986](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR66), [1987](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR67)) argued that PCK represents a “special amalgam” of content and pedagogy that enables teachers to transform disciplinary knowledge into teachable forms. Moreover, TPACK model has also been used conceptualise teachers’ PCK and technological knowledge (e.g., Drummond & Sweeney, [2017](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR20); Nilsson, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR51); Ning et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR53)). This integration is critical because effective teaching requires more than knowing subject matter or general pedagogy in isolation; it demands the ability to adapt content to learners’ needs through appropriate instructional strategies, representations, and assessments (Berry et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR7)). Without this synergy, lessons risk becoming either overly theoretical or disconnected from the subject matter, leading to superficial learning (Loewenberg Ball et al., [2008](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR41)). However, studies indicate that teachers often struggle to achieve this coherence, as the interplay among PCK components tends to be fragmented and idiosyncratic, especially in early career stages (Wu et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR76)). In rural and other resource-constrained contexts, these difficulties are compounded by limited access to digital infrastructure and teacher preparation experiences that are frequently designed around urban or well-resourced settings. As a result, teachers may encounter challenges in translating the integrative intent of TPACK into practice in ways that are responsive to local conditions and available resources (Goundar, [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR27); Jing, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR33)). Therefore, this challenge becomes even more pronounced when integrating emerging technologies, which require teachers to not only understand content and pedagogy but also orchestrate technological affordances to create meaningful learning experiences (Ginting et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR24)). Addressing this complexity calls for targeted professional development that foregrounds the dynamic and context-dependent nature of PCK integration (Nilsson & Loughran, [2012](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR52)).

Current AI use in teaching and learning
---------------------------------------

Researchers have also been focused on using technologies in teaching and learning (e.g., Cheng & Li, [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR10); Song et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR68)). AI is increasingly transforming teaching and learning across educational levels (Niemi, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR50)). Recently, AI technologies are being integrated into classrooms (Niemi, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR50); Song, [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR69)), curriculum design (Conrad et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR11)), assessment (Corbin et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR13)), and teacher support (Kim et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR35); Moylan et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR48)), offering both opportunities and challenges.

One of AI’s most significant contributions is the personalisation of learning experiences (Miesenberger et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR46)). AI systems can analyse student performance data to provide tailored feedback and adaptive learning paths. For instance, platforms like Duolingo have utilised generative AI to expand their language courses rapidly, enabling customised content for diverse learners (Shinde, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR65)). These AI systems typically rely on structured algorithms and predefined content to personalise learning experiences. The strength of such approaches lies in their ability to provide immediate feedback and maintain learner engagement through incremental challenges. However, Shinde ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR65)) primarily compares existing learning apps rather than exploring how generative models can enhance adaptive feedback mechanisms. This highlights a gap in both theory and practice: while algorithmic adaptation is well-established, the integration of GenAI for real-time personalisation and formative feedback is still emerging and requires systematic investigation.

Similarly, AI-powered tools offer diagnostic assessments to identify students’ knowledge gaps and deliver individualised learning plans. AI is also revolutionising content delivery through innovative methods. For example, AI-generated avatars capable of delivering lessons in multiple languages are being utilised to provide more personalised and accessible educational experiences (Vallis et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR74)).

Teachers are recognising the importance of preparing students for an AI-integrated future. This involves not only incorporating AI tools into teaching and learning but also teaching students about responsible and ethical AI use (Kajiwara & Kawabata, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR34); Tiribelli et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR73)). By fostering digital literacy and critical thinking skills, teachers aim to equip students to navigate and thrive in a technology-driven world. There are concerns about over-reliance on technology, the potential for inaccurate or culturally inappropriate responses, and data privacy issues (Tiribelli et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR73)).

Moreover, teachers can take teacher professional development (TPD) opportunities to learn how to effectively integrate AI into their teaching practices (Copur-Gencturk et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR12)). Other than Copur-Gencturk et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR12)) work, much research has been devoted into investigating AI in the TPD. For example, Mangione et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR44)) stated AI provided teachers with new opportunities for designing and managing instruction, while also introducing significant challenges. Much other research, such as Ahmed and Ganapathy ([2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR1)), Mijwil et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR47)), and Razia et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR59)), also explored the need to develop specific technical skills, address ethical and social implications, and maintain an active, critical role in the teaching and learning. Within the context of TPD, recent research has increasingly examined how AI reshapes teaching practices and the evolving skill sets required of educators (Al-Zyoud, [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR2); European Union, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR21)). This body of work emphasises the importance of considering both the opportunities and constraints introduced by AI. While AI has been identified as offering significant potential for resource generation and instructional support, particularly in contexts where teachers work with limited materials, these benefits are unevenly realised across settings. Rural educators, in particular, often experience reduced access to AI-focused TPD due to financial constraints, geographic isolation, and limited institutional support, which can restrict opportunities to develop informed and critical AI practices.

Celik et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR9)) conducted a systematic review of AI applications used by teachers and identified several key barriers to adoption. These include the limited applicability of AI systems across diverse educational contexts, insufficient technological proficiency among educators, and a lack of interest or even resistance to AI, often rooted in concerns about disrupting established pedagogical routines. In rural educational settings, these barriers are frequently intensified by contextual factors such as unreliable digital infrastructure, constrained access to ongoing AI-focused professional development, and the need to prioritise immediate teaching demands over experimentation with emerging technologies. Such challenges underscore the need to address not only technical capacity but also the cultural and psychological dimensions of AI integration, particularly in settings where structural inequities shape rural teachers’ engagement with the innovation.

Examples of AI integration reveal how PCK domains interact in practice. A well-resourced school adopted large language models (LLMs) to generate language learning materials, revealing both opportunities and challenges in AI integration. Teachers demonstrated strong subject content knowledge of language structures and curriculum requirements, such as understanding proficiency levels. However, their use of LLMs exposed gaps in PCK. For instance, while teachers asked the LLM to create essay examples, they did not provide scaffolding or formative assessment to support student learning. Simply presenting AI-generated essays without guiding students through analysis or adaptation reflected limited scaffolding and insufficient differentiation to meet individual learners’ needs (Li & Wilson, [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR38); Ma & Chen, [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR43)). This aligns with research showing that LLMs can produce high-quality educational content but require thoughtful pedagogical integration to avoid superficial use (Dong et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR18); Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR75); Xing et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR77)). Furthermore, studies suggest that effective AI integration depends on teachers’ ability to connect technological affordances with pedagogical strategies, highlighting persistent gaps in PCK when scaffolding and personalization are overlooked (Arranz-García et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR3)).

The above literature emphasises that teachers’ PCK cannot simply be enhanced by using AI to generate teaching resources aligned with local curricula. Instead, AI has the potential to move beyond static training models to support continuous, practice-oriented learning, where teachers develop not only technical proficiency but also integrate adaptive expertise in classroom teaching and learning. This perspective provides a strong rationale for developing research questions that explore how AI can be integrated into teacher PCK frameworks and professional learning in ways that are both context-sensitive and pedagogically meaningful. Moreover, longitudinal research is particularly important because it captures how teachers’ understanding and practices evolve over time, revealing whether initial gains in AI integration translate into sustained pedagogical change and improved student outcomes. Therefore, it is essential for teachers to learn how to integrate AI into learning experiences, supported by empirical longitudinal research, in order to guide students in using AI responsibly.

Theoretical framework of dynamic systems theory and development of research questions
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

While frameworks like TPACK have traditionally been used to conceptualise teachers’ technological and pedagogical content knowledge (e.g., Drummond & Sweeney, [2017](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR20); Nilsson, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR51), Ning et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR53)), Dynamic Systems Theory (DST) offers a more flexible and holistic lens (Geng et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR22)) during a longitudinal study. In particular, DST catches and shifts the focus from static categories of knowledge to the emergent, adaptive, and context-sensitive nature of teaching practice. By emphasising non-linear development, constant interaction between internal and external factors, and the dynamic integration of experience, environment, and reflection, DST provides a richer understanding of how teachers’ PCK evolves over time. Therefore, DST was used in this study to acknowledges the complexity and individuality of teacher learning trajectories, making it particularly valuable for examining how educators respond to real-time classroom challenges, professional feedback, and curriculum demands.

Most existing studies on AI in education focus primarily on its general adoption and classroom applications (e.g., Dooly et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR19); You, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR78)), often overlooking how AI influences the deeper, long-term development of teachers’ PCK. These studies tend to explore only a single context of AI use and commonly rely on short-term data collection methods, such as surveys or interviews, to capture teacher reflections. Additionally, while AI enhances teachers’ PCK by generating teaching resources aligned with local curricula (Celik et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR9)), it should not be viewed as a shortcut for training. Effective TPD requires opportunities for teachers to critically evaluate AI-generated content, discuss pedagogical implications, and adapt resources to their students’ needs. Without this reflective dimension, AI risks reinforcing superficial practices rather than fostering deep professional learning.

Therefore, although the use of AI in teaching and learning is expanding, there remains a lack of empirical research investigating its specific impact on the five core elements of teachers’ PCK and how these shifts affect student learning achievement. In addition, research on AI within teacher professional development (TPD) is still emerging, with limited evidence on how AI-supported training influences the development of PCK and the long-term transformation of teaching practices.

Furthermore, in rural or under-resourced environments, where access to qualified teachers and quality instructional materials is often limited, AI presents a potentially scalable solution. For instance, rural teachers can use AI to generate teaching resources aligned with local curricula, thereby enhancing their PCK without requiring extensive training in instructional design (López Costa, [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR42)).

To address this gap, this study employed a longitudinal research design to examine how the sustained integration of AI tools shapes rural teachers’ PCK development and how these changes translate into measurable improvements in student learning outcomes. This approach provides a more nuanced understanding of the evolving relationship between AI use and educational effectiveness in real-world classroom settings.

In light of these considerations, this study addressed the following two research questions:

1. 1.
   
   How does the integration of AI tools over time influence rural teachers’ teaching practices and pedagogical decision-making?
2. 2.
   
   How do rural teachers’ attitudes, confidence, and competencies in using AI evolve through sustained exposure of AI and relevant professional development opportunities?

Methods
-------

To answer the research questions, a longitudinal research approach was used, because it allows for a deeper, more comprehensive understanding of how teachers’ AI use in developing their PCKs and impact on their students’ learning achievements over time. Based upon the DST, a longitudinal design captures the dynamic and often gradual changes (Creswell & Creswell, [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR15)) in the five interconnected elements of PCK. This is particularly important in rural settings, where access to resources, professional development, and technology can vary significantly. By observing rural teachers over 18 weeks (half an academic year) from February to June 2025, this study can identify patterns, challenges, and growth in how AI supports teachers’ decision-making and professional learning. Furthermore, this method enables researchers to examine not only shifts in teacher practice but also the potential long-term impact on student learning outcomes, providing a more holistic view of AI’s role in enhancing educational quality in under-resourced rural educational contexts.

### Demographic information of the participating school and teachers

This study involved a rural community-based school in Anhui Province, China. The school has over 2000 students, including more than 1100 in primary setting (Grades 1–6) and over 900 in junior secondary setting (Grades 7–9). Nine language teachers, eight women and one man, teach English across all junior secondary levels.

Research context
----------------

The Compulsory Education English Curriculum Standards (2022 Edition) positions information technology as a core pathway to reconstruct the ecology of English teaching, fostering a systematic integration of educational digitisation and language learning.

The curriculum standards emphasise the infusion of contemporary relevance and technological elements, requiring the embedding of AI, virtual reality, and other advanced technologies into language-learning scenarios. By leveraging multimodal resources and intelligent tools, it aims to establish blended online-offline teaching models that support personalised learning and precision teaching. The development of digital resources and intelligent platforms serves as a critical driver, with textbooks incorporating dynamic corpora and intelligent assessment systems to transcend the temporal and spatial constraints of traditional classrooms, enhancing the authenticity and interactivity of language input. These standards prioritise teacher competency transformation, advocating for the enhancement of educators’ digital literacy through “online training + AI-powered pedagogical research” models. This shift redefines teachers’ roles from knowledge transmitters to designers and facilitators of learning activities, leveraging technology as a fulcrum to elevate both educational quality and equity. This model systematically reconstructs teaching methodologies, resource formats, and educator-student dynamics through a logical chain of “technological integration—resource development—role transformation.”

Therefore, teachers are tasked with designing and delivering lessons through technology integration, guiding students to formulate learning strategies via adaptive platforms, thereby shifting learners from passive knowledge absorption to active language application for innovative problem-solving.

At the time of this study, the participating teachers had limited familiarity with this new standard and approach. Within the context of TPD, this was particularly significant because the updated requirements emphasized not only curriculum alignment but also the integration of pedagogical strategies that address diverse learner needs. In particular, the key challenge for the language teachers was identifying students’ English proficiency needs aligned with the updated curriculum and providing appropriate scaffolding. Despite these challenges, the teachers demonstrated strong personal motivation, professional reflection, and a commitment to rural education. Therefore, inspired by a neighbouring rural school that had successfully integrated AI into teaching and learning to enhance their students’ learning achievements, the participating school’s leadership supported a pilot initiative to explore AI use among its teachers as part of their ongoing TPD efforts.

### Data collection

With ethical clearance, like most of the rural schools, the teachers began experimenting with AI tools in their classrooms with limited formal guidance or training on AI integration. The data collection of this study also highlights a critical need for systematic and deliberate integration of AI. AI should not serve as a shortcut for training, but rather as a supportive tool embedded within reflective and collaborative learning processes. To ensure meaningful professional growth, teachers must engage in structured reflection with trainers and peers, critically evaluating AI-generated resources and discussing pedagogical implications. Therefore, each team member was routinely expected to prepare for their presentations or reflections using materials such as planning documents, student work, teaching practice videos, in-depth interviews, and students verbal feedback across the half academic year (see Table [1](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#Tab1)). These reflective practices were central to the TPD process, enabling teachers to connect AI use with pedagogical reasoning and classroom realities.

### Use of AI tools

Teachers were given freedom to choose relevant AI tools for their classroom practices, enabling them to make decisions based on their pedagogical goals and local teaching contexts. It was found that three AI tools (DeepSeek, Doubao, and Meta) were most frequently used, all of which are free of charge and therefore accessible in rural schools where resources are often limited. These tools were primarily used to generate teaching resources aligned with local curricula, such as lesson plans, vocabulary lists, and practice exercises. In addition, teachers employed them to create multimedia content that made lessons more engaging and interactive, and to support language practice through AI-generated dialogues and exercises tailored to different proficiency levels.

### Data analysis

All data, including the video recordings of the weekly meetings, were translated into English by the researchers, who are bi-lingual academics. The translations were cross-checked among the researchers to ensure accuracy and prevent any misinterpretation of meaning. Moreover, another bilingual academic was contacted to confirm the accuracy of the translation. The translations were then analysed to categorise by quantifying coding and collecting and grouping common themes that were highly related to the research questions. Translating the data prior to coding enabled a shared analytic language and reduced interpretive drift across coders, supporting consistency and transparency in collaborative analysis while helping preserve nuanced meanings through team-based checking (Regmi et al., [2010](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR60)).

This study employed a hybrid deductive approach to data coding, which integrated theory-driven categorisation with data-informed emergent insights. Firstly, the coding process followed a structured approach, beginning with predefined categories derived from the synthesised framework of PCK. Secondly, the research team remained anchored in the theoretical structure while remaining open to unexpected patterns and contextual nuances.

This analysis was conducted using V-Note (<https://v-note.org>), a video analysis software widely utilised in both qualitative and quantitative video-based research (Goods, [2019](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR26); Li et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR39); Thompson & Illes, [2021](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR72)). Researchers imported and indexed video files relevant segments based on key incidents during students’ learning, and organised these tags into categories. While V-Note facilitated the video analysis, researchers created labels and codes within the software to systematically process and interpret the raw recording data.

Moreover, thematic analysis was used to involve identifying patterns or themes that emerge from the recorded meetings, and other video and materials data, which can help researchers gain insights into participants’ experiences and perspectives (Creswell & Guetterman, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR16)). The process began with familiarising ourselves with the data. Next, the data was coded by highlighting meaningful segments and labelling them with descriptive codes. These codes were then grouped into themes, which are refined through multiple iterations to ensure they accurately represent the data. Finally, each theme was defined and named to illustrate key domains for this study.

In detail, data analysis was organised into three components, being “findings on AI tools use in education and elements of PCK”, “findings on AI integration trends of the elements of the teachers’ PCK”, and “findings on teachers’ AI integration activities”. The first section examined the use of AI tools through a structured approach grounded in the PCK framework. The second section presented quantitative findings, highlighting the relative importance of the five PCK domains across the 18-week period. The final section applied the Concern-Based Adoption Model (Hall, [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR28); Hall & Hord, [2014](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR29)) as a conceptual lens to explore how the teachers’ experiences and concerns evolved during the process of adopting AI innovations. This approach enabled the study to capture shifts in focus from self-oriented considerations to task-related and impact-driven concerns, providing a deeper understanding of the dynamics of change throughout the implementation period.

The analysis revealed a clear progression in teachers’ experiences with AI integration, moving through stages of self, task, and impact concerns. Initially, teachers concentrated on building familiarity with AI tools and understanding their own digital capabilities. This early phase involved experimenting with prompts and identifying where AI could complement teaching while recognizing the continued importance of professional judgment. As confidence developed, teachers began to apply their subject and curriculum knowledge to guide AI-generated content, ensuring alignment with learning objectives and adapting materials to meet diverse student needs.

Task-related concerns emerged as teachers navigated practical challenges such as time management and classroom behaviour during implementation. These issues highlighted the complexity of integrating new technologies into established routines. Over time, attention shifted toward impact concerns, including the need for technical support, strategies for differentiation, and opportunities for collaboration with peers. Teachers increasingly engaged in professional dialogue and shared practices to enhance implementation.

The findings also illustrate a transition from using AI for basic, routine tasks to leveraging it as a tool for innovation. Teachers reported using AI to design creative learning experiences, improve student engagement, and inform reflective practice. This evolution underscores the dynamic nature of technology adoption, where initial apprehension gives way to purposeful and transformative use, guided by pedagogical expertise and collaborative learning.

Moreover, across the 18-week period, teachers’ experiences with AI integration followed a developmental trajectory from self to task and impact concerns, while drawing on their PCK. In the self-concern phase, the teachers built familiarity with AI tools and clarified their own digital capabilities, using subject content knowledge and curriculum knowledge to judge the accuracy, relevance, and alignment of AI-generated materials with learning objectives. As confidence grew, attention shifted to task concerns, where teaching practice and scaffolding strategies became central. Teachers refined prompts, sequenced activities, differentiated inputs and outputs, and managed time and classroom behaviours to embed AI meaningfully within lessons. These practical decisions were informed by student diversity and inclusive education, ensuring adaptations accommodated varied readiness levels, linguistic backgrounds, and accessibility needs. Progressing to impact concerns, teachers evaluated how AI-supported designs affected engagement, understanding, and equity, increasingly leveraging peer collaboration and reflective analysis to iterate designs that were responsive to evidence of learning. Throughout, educational contexts, such as school policies, limited resources and infrastructure, and rural community context, shaped opportunities and constraints, mediating the pace and form of adoption.

The integration of AI into rural teachers’ PCK was also found following a progressive trajectory aligned with the seven concerns-based stages (See Fig. [4](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#Fig4)).

The stages of teachers’ AI-in-PCK based on the concerns-based stages

In detail, in the “Awareness” and “Informational” stages, teachers began exploring AI tools, noting early benefits: *“I found each attempt of using AI tools helped me better understand how to use them to meet my teaching goals”*. It shows teachers engaged in initial exploration, such as experimenting with AI-driven lesson planning tools to understand their potential.

During the “Personal” stage, teachers moved beyond general awareness to consider how AI could align with their own teaching philosophy and subject expertise. This phase emphasised the use of content and curriculum knowledge to plan and create instructional materials, as well as the application of scaffolding strategies to support diverse learners. For example, teachers demonstrated this by guiding AI to produce curriculum-aligned resources: *“I was able to guide the AI to generate content that not only matched learning objectives of the 2022 curriculum standards”*.

At the “Management” stage, teachers confronted the practical realities of embedding AI tools into their teaching, which required not only individual effort but also systemic support. While AI offered opportunities for creating engaging and personalised activities, teachers reported that *“developing engaging activities can be very time-consuming. Additionally, some activities took longer in class than expected, which disrupted the teaching schedule”*. These experiences underscore that effective AI integration demands management-driven support, such as flexible scheduling, and professional learning for technology and pedagogies to alleviate workload pressures and maintain classroom stability. Without such institutional backing, teachers’ risk being overburdened by the dual challenge of designing AI-enhanced lessons and managing dynamic classroom interactions.

The “Consequence” stage is not only about measuring outcomes but also about refining practices to ensure equity, responsiveness, and sustained improvement in student engagement and achievement. Moreover, teachers shifted their attention to the impact of AI integration on student learning and instructional effectiveness. This phase was characterized by a growing awareness that successful implementation required ongoing technical competence and pedagogical precision. As one teacher noted, *“I’ve learned that staying up to date with resolving technical issues is essential”*, highlighting the importance of maintaining operational reliability to avoid disruptions in learning.

At the “Collaboration” stage, teachers moved toward collective engagement, sharing strategies and modelling effective AI practices for colleagues. One teacher reported measurable improvements in student outcomes through shared experimentation: *“*I want to share Teacher x’s strategies helped me gaining insights into how to effectively use AI in helping word recognition for my students and the results were enhanced from 45 to 57% in my second class. This shows peer learning became a critical driver of confidence and competence, as teachers exchanged insights and demonstrated practical applications.

At the “Refocusing” stage, teachers transitioned from using AI for routine tasks to leveraging it as a tool for innovation and reflective practice. This phase was marked by deeper pedagogical insights and a commitment to continuous improvement. Teachers began using AI to analyse their own lesson reflections, identifying patterns and areas for growth. Importantly, teachers’ roles evolved from relying on AI for basic content generation to employing it for creative and higher-order learning experiences: *“I used AI for tasks like reusing texts to practice word recognition and vocabulary application, but now I leverage it to design innovative and creative learning experiences, such as helping students enhance their essay writing by incorporating new vocabulary”*. This stage underscores a mature integration of AI, where technology becomes a catalyst for pedagogical innovation rather than a mere efficiency tool.

Ultimately, this trajectory demonstrates a transformation from tool-oriented experimentation to pedagogically principled applications of AI, where technology serves as a driver for inclusive design, strategic integration, and continuous professional growth.

Discussion
----------

This section used the findings and developed two models to answer the two research questions asked earlier. In detail, the DST-informed PCK model (see Fig. [5](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#Fig5)) specifies the AI-empowered PCK by detailing the knowledge bases teachers draw upon (general pedagogy, technological knowledge, curriculum knowledge, subject content knowledge, knowledge of educational contexts) and how these inform teaching practice and scaffolding strategies for personalised student learning. This design model is coupled with a reflection–evaluation cycle through which teachers iteratively refine AI use. In parallel, the concerns-based stages (see Fig. [4](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#Fig4)) map the AI adoption trajectory (see Fig. [6](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#Fig6)), showing how teachers’ concerns and practices evolve, from initial exploration and experimentation to strategic integration and continuous learning, while responding to classroom realities and student feedback. Together, these models provide insights into how the integration of AI tools influences rural teachers’ teaching practices and pedagogical decision-making (RQ1) and how their attitudes, confidence, and competencies develop through sustained exposure to AI and professional development opportunities (RQ2).

> RQ1. How does the integration of AI tools over time influence rural teachers’ teaching practices and pedagogical decision-making?

**Fig. 6**

![]()[Full size image](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5/figures/6)

Teachers’ Seven Stages in Integrating AI in their PCK

Over the 18-week research period, the integration of AI tools significantly influenced the participating teachers’ instructional strategies, particularly in their scaffolding practices. Different from Shinde ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR65))’s studies, which compared language learning apps and focused primarily on technological components, this paper examines how AI tools integrate into language teachers’ PCK development. This influence was observed across the interrelated elements of PCK, such as curriculum knowledge, subject content knowledge, understanding of student diversity, knowledge of educational contexts, and clarity on the purpose of teaching and learning.

Figure [5](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#Fig5) illustrates the dynamic interplay between AI integration and the elements of teachers’ PCK. Drawing on DST, the study found that these elements do not operate in isolation; instead, they interact fluidly as teachers adapt to classroom realities, student feedback, and evolving professional learning. The integration of AI tools should therefore be understood as a complex, adaptive process shaped by teachers’ evolving understanding of content, context, and learner needs. Importantly, the model underscores that pedagogical change is non-linear and emergent: teachers may revisit earlier stages, combine strategies, or accelerate progression based on contextual demands and professional insights.

Firstly, teachers’ insights into general *pedagogical knowledge* highlight a dynamic balancing act: fostering active engagement with AI while maintaining classroom order and instructional flow (Shen et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR63)). From a DST perspective, student engagement acts like an attractor in the classroom. To keep students engaged, Table [3](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#Tab3) shows teachers need to support this attractor with clear *teaching and scaffolding strategies* that work across different classroom contexts (Kulgemeyer & Riese, [2018](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR36); Ning et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR53)).

However, AI tools were found as effective only when filtered through teacher’s *curriculum knowledge*. Familiarity with the 2022 *curriculum* standards acted as a control parameter in the system: it constrained and guided the generation and adaptation of materials so they were not too advanced or too simplistic for target year levels (Conrad & Hall, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR11); Deng et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR17)). In DST terms, *curriculum knowledge* alignment stabilises the system by anchoring pedagogical decisions to externally validated criteria, while misalignment introduces variability that must be corrected through iterative refinement (see Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#Fig3)).

*Subject content knowledge* was also found as a precision mechanism that improved the fit between prompts and outputs (Setyaningrum et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR62)). Teachers’ ratings of the importance of content knowledge were highest at the start few weeks of the 18-week period (see Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#Fig3)). Moreover, teachers with confident *subject content knowledge* were found producing more specific prompts, yielding outputs better matched to their students’ learning goals.

AI integration also supported *teaching and scaffolding* tasks, such as efficient lesson planning, but efficiency emerged iteratively. Teachers used prompts aligned with learning objectives and assessment standards to design differentiated instruction and tailored scaffolding strategies (Shi & Aryadoust, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR64); Suharsono et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR71)). Each planning cycle generated new classroom feedback (e.g., timing, task difficulty, student uptake), which fed back into subsequent prompt, feedback loops typical of DST that progressively tune the system toward better calibration (see Table [3](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#Tab3)).

Initially in this study, use of AI centred on student engagement, consistent with prior studies (Niemi, [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR50); Song, [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR69)). As the project progressed, teachers’ decisions became increasingly *context*-aware, taking into account resource constraints, language diversity, and cultural backgrounds (Jing, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR33)). This contextual coupling shifted the system’s goals toward tangible learning outcomes (e.g., assessment gains) and drove collaborative development of AI-supported materials targeting English skills (vocabulary, word recognition, reading comprehension, writing) (Corbin et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR13); Shi & Aryadoust, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR64)). Here, knowledge of *educational contexts* acted as a boundary condition: it shaped how AI could be meaningfully integrated, underscoring that solutions are local and adaptive, not one-size-fits-all.

Consistent with Miesenberger et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR46)), teachers found AI can enhance *personalised learning* when student performance data inform adaptive pathways and tailored feedback. This was reflected in the findings on teachers’ assessment design and delivery (see Table [3](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#Tab3)), where AI supported inclusive, responsive evaluation (Vallis et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR74)). Notably, the findings on Meta AI’s transformation of reading texts into visual story formats increased accessibility and cultural relevance, particularly for rural students with limited prior exposure to formal language and diverse cultural references (Tiribelli et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR73)). In DST terms, these innovations represent emergent solutions that arise from the interplay of *student diversity* and *contextual constraints*, solutions that are stabilised through repeated cycles of reflection, implementation, and adjustment.

Therefore, the DST lens clarifies why the trajectory of AI integration is not fully linear: teachers revisit earlier decisions, reweight knowledge bases, and reconfigure practices as new information and constraints emerge. The system self-organises through ongoing feedback yielding AI-empowered PCK that is progressively more aligned to curriculum standards, content accuracy, classroom realities, and student needs. This dynamic, adaptive process transforms AI from a tool for engagement into a catalyst for principled, context-responsive pedagogy.

> RQ2. How do rural teachers’ attitudes, confidence, and competencies in using AI evolve through sustained exposure of AI and relevant professional development opportunities?

To address the relevance of the long-term impact, the participating teachers in this longitudinal study demonstrated growing stages in their use of AI tools. As found earlier, based upon the concerns-based stages, a seven-stage teachers’ AI integration of PCK framework outlines a progression observed during the study: Exploration, Experimentation, Personalised Teaching Practice, Management-driven support demands, Transformation of PCK, Strategic Integration, and Evaluation and Continuous Learning.

In detail, in the Exploration stage, rural teachers were introduced to various AI tools with minimal prior exposure or training (Kim et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR35); Moylan et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR48)). During this stage, the elements of their PCK were largely on basic pedagogical knowledge of *teaching & scaffolding*. As teachers began asking whether outputs were appropriate for specific year levels and subjects, the transition toward deeper engagement was shaped by their *curriculum knowledge* (alignment with standards) and *subject content knowledge* (subject accuracy).

Teachers’ confidence began to build gradually as teachers discovered some successful experiences of AI integration in their teaching during the Experiment stage. However, they have also showed their concerns over the lack of professional learning about how to use the prompts (Tiribelli et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR73)). In this stage, findings show *curriculum* and *subject content knowledges* became more salient, as teachers tested whether AI could reliably produce standards-aligned, accurate materials. The variability in outputs prompted attention to differentiation and readiness levels, and this shift was facilitated by *student diversity* (recognition of differences in students’ prior knowledge, literacy, and engagement), which nudged teachers toward more personalised planning and design choices.

In the Personalised Teaching Practice stage, teachers started integrating AI tools into lesson planning in ways that responded to the unique needs of their classrooms and accommodate the needs of diverse students (Park et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR55); Shi & Aryadoust, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR64)). Teachers’ growing awareness and confidence of AI’s capacity for personalised learning was significant in this stage. This work was also driven by *student diversity* (differentiation) and supported by *educational context* (local constraints and cultural relevance), while *teaching & scaffolding* guided how activities were structured and sequenced. As practical constraints and school routines came into sharper focus, the sustainability of these efforts increasingly depended on contextual realities, marking a transition mediated by educational context toward the need for institutional supports.

With the sustained exposure of AI, participating teachers reflected on their experiences and assessed the impact of AI on student engagement and learning, they sought out opportunities to deepen their understandings and learning experiences in the Management-driven support demand stage. It was because of the limited resources learning environments of rural schools (Goundar, [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR27); Jing, [2024](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR33)). Effective integration required support such as flexible scheduling, technical assistance, and targeted professional development so that *teaching & scaffolding* could be enacted within the constraints of time and behaviour management. As supports were put in place, teachers were able to refocus attention on quality and alignment, with *curriculum and content knowledges* regaining prominence and enabling a deeper shift in their PCK.

The Transformation of PCK stage was a critical stage for teachers. At this stage, teachers demonstrated higher-order competencies, including evaluating AI outputs critically, adapting them to local curriculum requirements, and making informed decisions about when and how to use AI for maximum impact. Attitudes were overwhelmingly positive, reflecting a sense of empowerment and innovation grounded in Shulman’s ([1987](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR67)) broad principles of PCK, including effective classroom management and organisational strategies that extend beyond subject-specific instruction. It is linked with *teaching & scaffolding*, *curriculum* and *content knowledges* while remaining responsive to *student diversity* and *educational context*. New routines, more precise prompts, and redesigned assessments consolidated these changes, with *teaching & scaffolding* serving as the mechanism through which this transformation was embedded in day-to-day practice.

Finally, the stages of Strategic Integration and Evaluation and Continuous Learning, teachers were no longer experimenting. Instead, they strategically selected tools based on their fit with learning outcomes, *student diversity*, and curriculum standards. AI was integrated with proper implementation of *curriculum* and *subject content knowledges*. They are also engaged in evidence-informed decision-making, using individual student performance data *teaching & scaffolding* (Park et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR55)) to guide AI-supported interventions. Finally, teachers’ collaboration provides teacher support (Kim et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR35); Moylan et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR48)) to form a community of practice in AI integration and developed a community of practices involving school leadership in teaching in rural *educational contexts*.

Additionally, applying DST in this model also reveals that this progression was not linear (Geng et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s11423-026-10602-5#ref-CR22)). Teachers did not move through the stages in a fixed order or at the same pace. Instead of progressing in a straight line, many teachers often cycled back to earlier stages, particularly within Stages 2–4, when new challenges or insights emerged, such as refining prompts or addressing classroom management demands. Similarly, Stages 5–7 operated as an adaptive loop, where many teachers iteratively refined AI use based on student data, curriculum alignment, and reflective analysis. This pattern reflects Dynamic Systems Theory’s emphasis on flexibility, feedback loops, and adaptive self-organisation, where development occurs through dynamic interactions among knowledge bases, classroom realities, and evolving goals, rather than through a fixed, linear sequence.

Therefore, rather than a straight path, AI integration unfolded as a complex, iterative system, with teachers continuously recalibrating strategies in response to shifting conditions and emergent opportunities for innovation. The concerns-based stages provide a useful heuristic for understanding broad trends, but DST highlights that AI integration in teachers’ PCK involves oscillation, overlap, and co-evolution, teachers’ subject content knowledge, technology use, and pedagogical strategies mutually shaping each other over time.

Conclusion and implications
---------------------------

This study used a longitudinal study to contribute new insights into how rural teachers’ integration of AI tools unfolds over time, offering an understanding of the complex, adaptive nature of pedagogical change. By applying DST, the research captured the evolving interplay between teachers’ PCK elements and their growing engagement with AI in classroom practice.

Over the 18-week research period, rural teachers’ AI integration was shaped by the interdependent development of curriculum knowledge, subject content knowledge, student diversity awareness, contextual knowledge, and clarity of teaching purpose.

The two models developed in this study provide both practical and theoretical lens for understanding non-linear, adaptive teacher development, offering critical implications for future AI-in-PCK research to design flexible, context-responsive professional learning and systemic support strategies. In particular, the stages model offers a practical heuristic for understanding broad patterns of teacher progression, from initial exploration to strategic integration and continuous learning. Meanwhile, the DST-informed PCK model also provides a theoretical lens that explains why this progression is non-linear and iterative, driven by feedback loops and dynamic interactions among knowledge bases, classroom realities, and evolving goals.

Finally, this study provides additional implications for supporting future TPD for teachers while integrating AI into teaching and learning practices, particularly in rural contexts.

First, future TPD programs must articulate a clear pedagogical purpose. This means moving beyond technical skills for AI tool use to focus on strengthening teachers’ curriculum and subject content knowledge. TPD should also equip teachers to critically assess when and how AI can be used to achieve specific teaching goals.

Second, TPD initiatives should ensure that AI tools are adapted to local needs and cultural contexts. Involving rural communities in these processes is essential for fostering context-responsive practices and supporting teachers’ PCK development in the AI era.

Third, school leadership plays a pivotal role in enabling professional collaboration. Establishing communities of practice within TPD frameworks can promote shared growth and foster the development of AI-informed pedagogy among educators, ensuring sustainable and equitable integration of AI in rural education.
