Abstract
--------

This article proposes a novel conceptual framework that integrates Artificial Intelligence (AI) with Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) and the Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML) to enhance Open Distance eLearning (ODeL) systems. By bridging the gap between traditional cognitive theories and cutting-edge AI technologies, this framework supports adaptive cognitive load management, AI-mediated schema creation, and human-AI collaborative learning. A refined critique of Twabu's (2023) PhD study, based on conceptual analysis of its theoretical scope and limitations, highlights the absence of AI integration. This article presents a unique contribution by synthesizing established cognitive theories with the dynamic potential of AI, a perspective underrepresented in current literature. Practical examples, policy recommendations, and ethical considerations are consolidated to offer a comprehensive path forward. The study proposes an expanded framework incorporating *AI-enhanced cognitive load management, AI-mediated schema creation, and human-AI collaborative learning*. The Literature review and theoretical framework emphasises AI's role in simplifying cognitive processes by dynamically adjusting content presentation based on learner needs and performance. This includes AI-mediated adjustments to manage cognitive load and enhance schema development through personalised feedback and tailored content delivery. Such integration promises to create a more effective learning environment by aligning multimedia content with individual learner profiles, thus addressing the challenge of cognitive overload and improving knowledge retention and understanding.

1 Introduction
--------------

The Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) and Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML) are foundational in understanding human learning and instructional design [[1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR1), [2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR2)]. These theories focus on human cognitive processes, such as working memory, schema creation, and cognitive overload, advancing CLT and related theories like Richard E. Mayer’s cognitive theory of multimedia learning (CTML). For over three decades, cognitive load theory (CLT) has used models of cognitive architecture to generate and test instructional design hypotheses by examining how working memory limits can be reduced through domain-specific schemas, impacting comprehension, schema construction, automation, and problem-solving[[3](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR3)]. However, as educational technologies increasingly integrate Artificial Intelligence (AI), it is crucial to expand these frameworks to consider AI's unique processing capabilities and limitations [[4](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR4)].

A critical review of Twabu (2023)’s PhD study "A post-COVID-19 Pandemic Multimedia Framework for Teaching and Learning in Open Distance eLearning in South Africa", highlights the need to enhance Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) and CTML by integrating AI, proposing a framework that uses AI’s adaptive capabilities to personalise learning, improve content delivery, and support diverse learner needs. This approach underscores the transformative potential of aligning AI with established educational theories to create more dynamic learning environments.

2 Background: critique of “A Post-COVID-19 pandemic multimedia framework for teaching and learning in open distance eLearning in South Africa” study
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Twabu (2023)’s PhD study utilises the cognitive theory of multimedia learning, particularly Mayer's multimedia design principles and CTML but missed the opportunity to integrate AI into its proposed framework. This critique, based on conceptual analysis, identifies a gap in addressing how AI can enhance learner engagement, cognitive monitoring, and multimedia adaptation. The absence of AI considerations in managing cognitive load or automating schema development limits the scalability and personalization of the framework. Unlike Twabu’s [[5](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR5)] focus on confidentiality and multimedia design, this framework introduces practical AI-driven strategies to manage cognitive load and enable schema development dynamically. Updating curriculum, tuition, and ODeL policies is essential to ethically integrate AI and multimedia technologies ensuring adaptive, personalized learning while maintaining equity, privacy, and transparency in educational systems.

The current frameworks, including Twabu's [[5](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR5)], must address AI's potential to monitor and adapt to individual learners' cognitive loads, facilitate schema creation through adaptive learning pathways, or augment human learning through AI-human collaboration. Artificial intelligence (AI), now accessible through low-cost smart devices with capabilities for edge computing, cloud-based services, and extensive data access, enhances education by modifying pedagogical approaches and content, presenting both challenges and opportunities [[6](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR6)] and may replace some professions, transform others, and create new roles, thus reforming and facilitating educational operations.

3 Objective: proposal for an expanded framework
-----------------------------------------------

Integrating AI into educational frameworks aligns with the growing recognition of multimedia and AI as critical components in ODeL [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR2)]. AI's ability to recognise complex patterns, such as identifying a dog in various colours and pixels, mirrors how learners process and understand multimedia components [[7](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR7)]. This similarity underscores the potential for AI and multimedia to complement each other in educational settings, enhancing content delivery and learner engagement [[8](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR8)]. This study proposes an expanded framework (Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#Fig1)*: AI and Multimedia Integration Expanded Framework*) incorporating AI insights into CLT and CTML to address these limitations. This proposal comprises three key components: AI-enhanced cognitive load management, AI-mediated schema creation, and human-AI collaborative learning.

Firstly, ***AI-Enhanced Cognitive Load Management*** provides an opportunity for AI tools to monitor learner behaviors and adjust instructional materials in real time to minimize cognitive overload [[9](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR9)]. In an online mathematics module, an AI tutor may detect that a student is repeatedly failing word problems involving ratios. The system can then respond by simplifying the problem, adding a visual diagram, and breaking it into smaller steps to ease processing load. This is an example of how AI can analyse real-time data on learner performance and adjust instructional materials to reduce cognitive overload by simplifying input data and processing requirements for AI systems, which enables us to optimise performance and ensure that AI complements human cognitive processes [[10](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR10)].

Secondly, *AI-mediated schema creation* explores how AI can facilitate schema creation in humans through interactive multimedia and adaptive learning pathways, enhancing knowledge retention and transfer ***AI-Mediated Schema Creation*** allows AI to facilitate schema development through adaptive multimedia pathways that align with learner preferences [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR2)]. In a language learning platform, the AI identifies that a learner has mastered basic sentence structure but struggles with verb conjugations. It then curates an adaptive learning path, sequencing grammar lessons and interactive exercises to strengthen the learner’s schema for verb use validating the AI-powered models and their potential to improve learning outcomes [[11](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR11)]. This study involves developing *AI knowledge representation* schemes that mimic human schema creation, enabling learning and problem-solving.

Thirdly, ***Human-AI collaborative learning*** examines the potential of AI to augment human learning through collaborative systems that leverage AI's processing capabilities and human intuition, promoting more effective learning outcomes [[12](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR12)]. In a virtual science course, AI auto-grades weekly quizzes and generates individualised feedback reports for each student, with AI acting as a learning companion, offering support and feedback that complements human educators [[13](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR13)]. The instructor uses this data to identify learners in need of additional support and hosts targeted discussion sessions to address misconceptions. *Future research* should focus on empirically testing this framework and exploring its implications for policy and practice in higher education.

4 Literature on CTML
--------------------

The cognitive theory of multimedia learning (CTML), popularised by Richard E. Mayer, argues that integrating words and pictures enhances learning by leveraging how the human brain processes multimedia inputs [[14](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR14)]. The multimedia principle posits that learning is more effective when textual and visual information are combined rather than presented separately [[15](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR15)]. This principle underpins multimedia instructional design, which aims to optimise learning using graphical and textual elements to build mental representations [[16](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR16)]. CTML is grounded in cognitive theories like Baddeley’s model of working memory, Paivio’s dual coding theory, and Sweller’s cognitive load theory, all contributing to the information-processing model of cognition [[17](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR17)]. This approach suggests that multimedia presentations are more effective for learning because they engage verbal and visual channels, leading to better understanding and retention of information.

Similarly, Generative AI, which produces new content such as text, images, video, or audio based on user prompts, leverages extensive data training to recognise patterns and create outputs that resemble this training data. This technology is used widely in creating written copy, code, digital art, and designs across various industries, including entertainment, marketing, consumer goods, and manufacturing [[18](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR18)]. CTML and Generative AI focus on integrating and processing multimedia inputs to enhance user experience and output quality. CTML optimises learning by effectively combining verbal and visual information [[19](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR19)]. Generative AI seeks to create realistic and contextually appropriate multimedia content by analysing and replicating patterns from vast datasets [[20](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR20)]. In essence, both paradigms utilise the alignment of multiple media types to achieve their respective goals, improving learning outcomes in the case of CTML and generating innovative content in the case of Generative AI.

Integrating AI with multimedia principles, as outlined by the Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML), can significantly enhance teaching and learning capabilities by enabling more dynamic and personalised academic project enablement [[21](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR21)]. AI's advanced ability to process and adapt multimedia content opens new avenues for creating compelling, engaging, and individualised learning experiences [[22](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR22)]. This integration has revolutionised education, transitioning from essential computer technologies to intelligent systems such as web-based platforms and humanoid robots [[6](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR6)], which enhance instructional efficiency and personalise learning through adaptive content and automated administrative tasks [[22](#ref-CR22),[23](#ref-CR23),[24](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR24)].

AI's integration into educational tools aligns well with the principles of CTML, which advocates for using words and pictures to optimise learning by engaging both verbal and visual channels in the brain [[14](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR14)]. AI can dynamically adjust multimedia content to meet individual learning needs, thus creating adaptive, personalised learning environments that mirror CTML's emphasis on the effective combination of multimedia elements [[25](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR25)]. This alignment enhances academic projects by ensuring more efficient content delivery, improved engagement, and personalised instructional support [[26](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR26)]. The potential of AI to transform education is vast, as it can analyse individual learning patterns and preferences to adapt multimedia content, creating personalised learning experiences that cater to each student's unique needs and enhance overall educational outcomes [[27](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR27)].

5 Theoretical framework: applying cognitive load theory (CLT) and Mayer's cognitive theory of multimedia learning (CTML theories to AI)

Multimedia and AI have the potential to revolutionise Open Distance eLearning (ODeL) by providing more interactive, adaptive, and personalised learning experiences. AI can recognise patterns and make decisions that enhance multimedia learning components [[28](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR28)]. For instance, Generative AI can recognise a dog in different colors and pixels, demonstrating how AI can process and understand visual information similarly to multimedia components [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR2)]. This ability to handle complex visual data makes AI a powerful tool for enhancing multimedia learning in ODeL environments, offering new ways to engage and support learners [[29](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR29)]. A Survey on Generative AI and LLM for Video Generation, Understanding, and Streaming examining how generative AI and large language models (LLMs) are revolutionising video technology was conducted by Zhou et al. [[30](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR30)]. This survey revealed how LLMs show promise in video scene understanding by learning from vast data and generating natural language descriptions [[30](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR30)]. However, LLMs need help with challenges like large, labelled datasets and fine-grained details, requiring further research for improvement [[30](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR30)]. The paper concludes that while Generative AI and LLMs hold significant potential for advancing video-related tasks, their development must be balanced with caution due to challenges like data scarcity, high computational costs, and concerns over misinformation and security.

Another paper which explores how modern technology, including hardware, applications, and online services, has revolutionised instructional methods, highlighting the transformative role of artificial intelligence in multimedia education. Several approaches must be considered to effectively integrate Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) and Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML) with AI. Choosing between broadcast (one-way) and communicative (two-way) media depends on the intended balance of lecturer control and interactivity. Additionally, the choice depends on the appropriate multimedia teaching technologies, considering synchronous or asynchronous formats, while rich media, which combine elements like video and audio, offer more engaging learning experiences but require careful management to avoid cognitive overload [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR2)]. These approaches ensure that aligning human cognitive processes and AI capabilities enhances learning outcomes and optimises educational technology. The proposed approach includes simplifying AI inputs, optimising AI processing, and developing knowledge representation schemes that mimic human schema creation to create more effective and efficient learning environments [[31](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR31)]. AI can recognize patterns and make decisions that enhance multimedia learning components[[30](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR30)]. Generative AI’s ability to recognize visual data across variations mirrors how learners process multimedia [[14](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR14)]. These capabilities position AI as a valuable ally in enhancing multimedia-based education. Several approaches must be considered to effectively integrate CLT and CTML with AI:

### 5.1 Simplify AI inputs

Simplifying AI inputs reduces cognitive overload for humans and AI systems. In human learning, cognitive overload occurs when the working memory is overwhelmed by excessive information [[1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR1)]. Similarly, Twabu, [[5](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR5)] mentions that Germane's cognitive load allows the students to devote their cognitive mental resources to the learning process and contributes to developing a student's knowledge base [[32](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR32)]. Similarly, AI systems can experience inefficiencies if fed overly complex or voluminous data, leading to suboptimal performance [[33](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR33)]. Simplifying input data for AI involves preprocessing steps such as filtering, feature selection, and dimensionality reduction [[34](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR34)]. These steps help to ensure that AI systems receive only the most relevant information, which improves processing efficiency and reduces the computational burden [[35](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR35)]. By aligning this approach with human cognitive load management, educational tools can be designed to present information in a streamlined and accessible manner, enhancing learning outcomes [[9](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR9)] and optimize AI Processing AI systems should be designed to process information efficiently, thus discussed next.

### 5.2 Optimise AI processing

Optimising AI processing is another critical approach for integrating AI with CLT and CTML. Just as instructional design aims to minimise unnecessary cognitive load for learners [[36](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR36)], AI systems should be designed to process information efficiently [[37](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR37)]. This involves using advanced algorithms and computational techniques to reduce redundant computations and enhance processing speed [[38](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR38)]. For instance, parallel processing, caching, and optimised data structures can significantly improve AI performance [[39](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR39)]. By reducing the "cognitive load" on AI systems, these optimisations enable more effective real-time analysis and adaptation to learners' needs [[40](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR40)]. Consequently, AI-powered educational tools can deliver efficient and responsive personalised learning experiences that align with the principles of CTML [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR2)] which is critical for Knowledge Representation Schemas which is discussed next.

### 5.3 Knowledge representation

Developing AI knowledge representation schemes that mimic human schema creation is essential for enhancing learning and problem-solving [[41](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR41)]. Schemas are cognitive structures that help individuals organise and interpret information, facilitating efficient learning and recall [[1](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR1)]. In AI, knowledge representation involves structuring information to enable machines to understand and reason. Techniques such as ontologies, semantic networks, and knowledge graphs can be employed to create AI representations that parallel human schema creation. These representations allow AI systems to integrate new information, draw inferences, and support decision-making processes. By aligning AI knowledge representation with human cognitive structures, educational tools can provide more intuitive learning experiences, promoting deeper understanding and retention [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR2)].

By simplifying AI inputs, optimising AI processing, and developing knowledge representation schemes that mimic human schema creation, we can create more adequate and efficient learning environments [[42](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR42)]. These approaches ensure that AI systems complement human cognitive processes, providing personalised and adaptive learning experiences that align with established theories of cognitive load and multimedia learning. Future research should focus on empirically validating these integration strategies and exploring their practical implications in diverse educational contexts. 6 Psychological perspectives on AI in learning
----------------------------------------------

Integrating AI into learning is complex, with different psychological perspectives offering insights into how AI can support or hinder human cognition, making it essential to design AI systems which complement human learning [[43](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR43)]. Therefore, this article briefly reflects on:

1. (a)
   
   How Cognitive psychology focuses on understanding how people perceive, think, remember, and learn.
2. (b)
   
   Educational psychology, examining how people learn in educational settings, the effectiveness of educational interventions, and the psychology of teaching.
3. (c)
   
   Human–Computer Interaction (HCI) psychology studies, establishing how humans interact with computers and AI systems, emphasising user experience, interface design, and collaborative learning.
4. (d)
   
   How people learn from one another through observation, imitation, and modelling through social learning theory posits that [[44](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR44)].
5. (e)
   
   Neuropsychology, exploring he relationship between brain function and behaviour, including cognitive processes such as learning and memory.

From a Cognitive Psychology perspective, AI can significantly influence human cognitive processes such as attention, memory, and problem-solving. AI systems can support these processes by providing tailored instructional materials and real-time feedback, helping to manage cognitive load and improve learning efficiency [[9](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR9)]. However, there are concerns that over-reliance on AI might hinder the development of critical thinking and problem-solving skills if learners become too dependent on technology [[45](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR45)]. An Educational psychology perspective examines how people learn in educational settings, the effectiveness of educational interventions, and the psychology of teaching [[43](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR43)]. AI's potential to enhance learning outcomes is a key focus within this field. AI technologies such as personalised learning systems, adaptive assessments, and intelligent tutoring systems can create customised educational experiences that address individual learner needs [[2](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR2)]. These systems can identify knowledge gaps and adjust the difficulty level of tasks, accordingly, thereby optimising learning efficiency and effectiveness. Human–Computer Interaction (HCI) Psychology studies how humans interact with computers and AI systems, emphasising user experience, interface design, and collaborative learning.

AI integration in education demands a deep understanding of user engagement with technology, where well-designed interfaces foster intuitive interactions and boost user satisfaction [[46](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR46)]. Collaborative AI systems, where humans and AI work together, can leverage the strengths of both parties, fostering a rich learning environment [[12](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR12)]. Social learning theory posits that people learn from one another through observation, imitation, and modelling [[44](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR44)]. AI can facilitate social learning by creating virtual learning environments and enabling AI-mediated communication [[47](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR47)]. AI-powered discussion forums and collaborative tools can enhance peer interaction and knowledge sharing, fostering community and collective learning [[48](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR48)]. These tools can also support social skills development, especially in online learning contexts [[49](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR49)]. Neuropsychology explores the relationship between brain function and behaviour, including cognitive processes such as learning and memory [[50](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR50)]. Integrating AI into learning involves understanding the neural basis of human-AI interaction. Cognitive neuroscience and neuroplasticity studies can inform how AI influences brain development and learning processes [[51](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR51)]. AI tools that adapt to a learner's cognitive state can help optimise brain activity patterns, enhancing learning and retention.

### 6.1 Benefits and concerns

Psychologists acknowledge several benefits of integrating AI into learning, such as personalised learning experiences and increased accessibility to educational resources [[52](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR52)]. AI systems can provide tailored support to learners, making education more inclusive. However, there are also concerns, including the risk of cognitive overload, where the complexity of AI systems may overwhelm learners, and the potential decrease in human interaction, which is essential for social and emotional development [[53](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR53)]. Algorithmic bias poses a significant risk in education, as AI systems trained on non-representative data can reinforce inequalities by delivering inaccurate feedback or biased content to marginalised learners, thereby perpetuating systemic disparities [[26](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR26)]. To mitigate these concerns and maximise the benefits, psychologists emphasise the importance of human-centred design. AI systems should be developed to complement and enhance human cognitive processes rather than replace them[[54](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR54)]. Continuous empirical research is necessary to understand the full impact of AI on learning and cognition. Furthermore, ethical considerations must be at the forefront of AI development and implementation, ensuring that AI is used responsibly, transparently, and with minimal bias [[26](/article/10.1007/s44217-025-00592-6#ref-CR26)].

7 Recommendations for integrating AI into cognitive load theory (CLT) and Mayer's cognitive theory of multimedia learning (CTML)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

To integrate AI and multimedia technologies into education, institutions should develop AI-enhanced educational tools, such as adaptive learning platforms that adjust to individual cognitive loads and interactive multimedia content that engages both verbal and visual channels.

1. (a)
   
   AI should be used for schema creation and knowledge representation by designing personalised learning pathways and employing AI-driven knowledge representation techniques like knowledge graphs.
2. (b)
   
   Enhancing AI and human collaboration can be achieved by developing AI learning companions and AI-powered collaborative platforms that facilitate peer interaction.
3. (c)
   
   Optimising AI processing involves implementing advanced techniques and preprocessing educational data to simplify AI inputs.
4. (d)
   
   Ethical and practical concerns should be addressed by establishing data privacy and intellectual property rights guidelines.
5. (e)
   
   Empirical validation and continuous improvement can be ensured through empirical studies and iterative development based on feedback.
6. (f)
   
   Awareness and training programs should be promoted for educators and students, familiarising them with AI tools and their applications in teaching and learning.

By implementing these recommendations, educational institutions can harness the power of AI to enhance cognitive load management and multimedia learning, creating more dynamic, personalised, and applicable learning environments. This approach will not only improve educational outcomes but also prepare students and educators for the evolving landscape of AI-driven education.

### 7.1 Ethical considerations

AI use in education raises essential questions of fairness, privacy, and transparency. Algorithmic bias can reinforce inequalities, while data-driven personalization must be balanced with consent and data protection. Therefore, institutions should revise curriculum, tuition, and ODeL policies to include ethical AI standards. Responsible integration requires transparency, empirical evaluation, and learner agency.

8 Conclusion
------------

The integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) with Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) and Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML) offers significant potential to enhance educational experiences. By incorporating AI's advanced capabilities, we can address the limitations of traditional theories and create more dynamic, personalised, and effective learning environments. CLT emphasises the importance of managing cognitive load to optimise learning, while CTML focuses on effectively combining verbal and visual information to enhance comprehension. Both theories provide a solid foundation for understanding how AI can improve educational practices. AI’s ability to process and adapt multimedia content aligns with CTML’s principles, enabling more personalised and engaging learning experiences. Additionally, AI's role in managing cognitive load and facilitating schema creation complements the objectives of CLT, offering new methods to support individual learners' needs.

The proposed expanded framework, which includes AI-enhanced cognitive load management, AI-mediated schema creation, and human-AI collaborative learning, represents a comprehensive approach to integrating AI into educational settings. This framework aims to address the current limitations of CLT and CTML by leveraging AI's capabilities to optimise content delivery and personalise learning pathways. Despite the promising potential, integrating AI into educational frameworks must be cautiously approached. Issues such as data privacy, ethical use of AI, and potential biases must be carefully managed. It is crucial to update educational policies and practices to reflect the inclusion of AI, ensuring that these technologies enhance rather than detract from the learning experience.

Future research should focus on empirically validating the proposed framework and exploring its practical implications across diverse educational contexts. By aligning AI technologies with established cognitive theories, we can develop more effective and personalised educational tools that improve learning outcomes and support human cognitive processes. In conclusion, the synergy between AI and multimedia learning principles holds the promise of revolutionising education. By harnessing AI's capabilities to manage cognitive load, facilitate schema creation, and support collaborative learning, we can create enriched educational environments that cater to individual needs and promote deeper understanding. This integration represents a significant step forward in advancing educational theory and practice in the age of AI.
