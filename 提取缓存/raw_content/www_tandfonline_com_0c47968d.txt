## Introduction
The pervasiveness of computer science (CS) in education, careers, and civic life underscores the need for a more equitable and inclusive approach to CS education. Despite its importance, access to computer science education remains highly unequal, influenced by factors such as income, race, language background, and gender. Evidence shows that students who are female, Black, Latine and designated as English language learners have fewer opportunities to engage in CS education compared to their peers (Bahar et al., [Citation2022](#); Bruno & Lewis, [Citation2021](#)). This disparity not only limits these groups’ potential to contribute to and benefit from a technologically driven society but also perpetuates broader socioeconomic inequalities.
Addressing these inequities requires a deep understanding of both the systemic barriers to access and the data-driven effectiveness of various interventions aimed at broadening participation in CS education. Prior research has identified several strategies to foster more inclusive participation in computer science, such as culturally responsive computing instruction, inclusive pedagogies for students with exceptionalities, and promoting social justice in computing education (Kapor Center, [Citation2021](#); Madkins et al., [Citation2020](#)). However, while these approaches provide valuable frameworks, there is a pressing need for empirical studies that critically examine their implementation and outcomes. The current literature lacks sufficient data on the efficacy of these interventions, the contexts in which they are most successful, and the factors that drive or hinder their adoption. By focusing on empirical research, this special issue aims to highlight the conditions under which equitable access to computer science can be achieved, thus providing a foundation for more effective and inclusive CS education policies and practices.
In curating this special issue, the Guest-Editors employed a thorough and rigorous review process to ensure the highest quality and alignment with the theme of the special issue. Following an initial call for proposals, submissions were carefully evaluated for their relevance to the special issue’s focus, as well as their potential to contribute to a diverse range of topics. Authors of accepted proposals were invited to submit full manuscripts, which were then subjected to a double-blind peer review process. The manuscripts underwent multiple rounds of revisions based on the reviewers’ comments and the guest editors’ evaluations, ensuring that each contribution met the highest standards of academic rigor before final acceptance. We now turn to a thematic description of the articles published in this special issue.
## CS education and marginalized student groups
Without focused policies to broaden participation at the student level, computer science education risks continuing to exclude marginalized groups. Ottenbreit-Leftwich et al. ([Citation2025](#)) find that integrating computational thinking into early literacy education helps sustain interest and improve foundational skills for all students, especially girls, by providing contextual support and scaffolding. Similarly, Ryoo et al. ([Citation2025](#)) examine how student agency is fostered in CS programs for women and non-binary students, emphasizing the use of CS for social good and challenging societal inequities. Their findings suggest that encouraging discussions on underrepresentation, equity, and social responsibility, alongside opportunities for creativity and community belonging, are key to empowering underrepresented students to engage with technology as educators, advocates, and creators. Pereiro et al. ([Citation2025](#)) build on this by examining gender dynamics in Uruguay’s national computational thinking program. The study reveals that girls outperformed boys in computational thinking tasks, yet teacher biases favored boys in programming. Addressing these stereotypes against girls is crucial in fostering an inclusive and equitable learning environment for girls in computing. Similarly, Master et al.’s ([Citation2025](#)) experimental studies found that the stereotype against girls and underrepresentation in computer science reduce girls’ motivation to enroll in CS courses. Further, the results show that directly addressing these stereotypes by providing counter-stereotypical information can promote girls’ sense of belonging and enrollment in CS. Studies such as these suggest that targeted efforts are essential to make computer science education more inclusive and equitable for underrepresented students.
## CS education and teacher professional learning
Understanding computer science at the teacher level is vital for ensuring that educators are equipped to deliver inclusive, effective, and socially responsible instruction. For example, Jayathirtha et al. ([Citation2025](#)) analyze the co-design process of redesigning the Exploring Computer Science (ECS) program, demonstrating how involving experienced teachers in curriculum development fosters teacher agency, empowerment, and enhances the integration of social justice into computing education. Their findings underscore the value of centering teacher expertise in curriculum design and provide insights for future efforts to equitably include teachers in the creation of critical computing materials. Hopkins and Weddle ([Citation2025](#)) reveal that Multilingual Learners face barriers to accessing CS due to both structural challenges, such as English Language Development course requirements, and the lack of teacher preparation to engage these students effectively in CS content. The study underscores the importance of professional development to train teachers in teaching CS to Multilingual Learners by leveraging their cultural, linguistic, and experiential assets. Yadav et al.’s ([Citation2025](#)) multi-case study emphasizes this discussion on the importance of professional development on culturally responsive pedagogies. The study examines the impact of a co-teaching model, pairing novice CS teachers with experienced instructors, on teachers’ development of pedagogical content knowledge and self-efficacy during their first year of teaching AP Computer Science Principles. The findings emphasize the importance of long-term teacher development and culturally responsive pedagogies in addressing disparities in student outcomes, particularly among marginalized groups.
Lopez et al. ([Citation2025](#)) contribute to this discussion by demonstrating how PD focused on supporting English Learners (ELs) can enhance teachers’ abilities to teach ELs in AP Computer Science Principles. The study shows that the PD improved teachers’ awareness of their students’ linguistic needs and improved the ability to incorporate language supports, leading to higher engagement and outcomes for ELs. White et al. ([Citation2025](#)) evaluate the impact of professional development (PD) on educators’ ability to implement culturally-sustaining pedagogy in K-12 CS education, aiming to enhance teacher confidence and student inclusivity. Preliminary survey results show significant growth in educators’ understanding of CS and their confidence in teaching it, highlighting the importance of ongoing support and training in culturally responsive practices to address racial equity issues in CS education. Villavicencio et al. ([Citation2025](#)) examine two PD models used to train non-CS teachers to implement computer science education district-wide. The study compares a concept-based model, where teachers learn CS concepts and develop their own units, with a curriculum-based model, which provides teachers with pre-designed CS lessons and training on how to deliver the existing set of CS lessons. The findings show that the curriculum-based model resulted in more practical applications of CS in the classroom and higher teacher confidence. The study underscores the importance of equipping non-CS teachers with practical resources to ensure effective CS implementation.
## CS education and district initiatives
An often understudied area, research on district level CS implementation is crucial for understanding how systemic efforts can shape equitable and inclusive computing education pathways. For example, Coenraad et al. ([Citation2025](#)) explore how six school districts used a CT engagement inventory to set inclusive goals for K-12 computing pathways, revealing that while student access is often the starting point, the tool helped districts shift focus toward improving student engagement in computing education. The findings highlight the need for district-wide efforts to create equitable, cumulative CT pathways and demonstrate the utility of structured tools in guiding educational leaders to address participation gaps and enhance inclusive computing opportunities. Bowdon et al. ([Citation2025](#)) extend this perspective by examining how culturally sustaining and revitalizing pedagogies can be embedded into district-level computer science initiatives. Their work with students and teachers on the Wind River Reservation shows how collaboration between educators, community members, and professional development providers can foster culturally responsive computing curricula that center Indigenous languages and traditions. The findings emphasize that involving district and community members in curriculum design and implementation leverages CS as a tool for revitalizing Indigenous languages and histories, paving the way for inclusive K-12 computing pathways. Expanding on the theme of culturally responsive computing, Iwatani et al. ([Citation2025](#)) examine the distinctions and connections between computational thinking (CT) and Appalachian problem-solving within culturally responsive computing education, highlighting the risks of conflating the two concepts. The findings emphasize the importance of distinguishing CT from culturally rooted problem-solving to preserve traditional practices while teaching modern computing skills, particularly through research-practice partnerships that empower communities to appreciate both approaches.
## CS and community engagement
Finally, studies at the community level are essential for understanding how family and local engagement can address racial and socioeconomic disparities in computer science education. To this end, Nazario et al. ([Citation2025](#)) examine the role of parent and community engagement in addressing the racial and socioeconomic disparities in access to CS education, particularly for students who are Black, Latine, Native American, and low-income in California. Through mixed methods, the research highlights the importance of leveraging families’ cultural capital to advocate for equitable CS opportunities and challenges traditional views of parental involvement, emphasizing the need for a justice-oriented approach to CS education. Robillard et al. ([Citation2025](#)) further illustrate the importance of involving district educators in co-designing culturally responsive curricula. Their study shows how the co-design team identified a potential cultural issue during lesson rehearsals–a Scratch sprite that could be harmful to Navajo students due to its associations. This underscores the importance of involving both district and community members in the design process to ensure that curriculum elements are culturally relevant and inclusive.
## Conclusion
Looking ahead, the rise of generative artificial intelligence will undoubtedly amplify the role of technology in society, while presenting both new opportunities and challenges for promoting equity in education. As we enter an era where AI is increasingly integrated into computing education, the insights from this special issue—focused on the roles of students, educators, school systems, and communities in fostering equitable access to CS—will be crucial. These lessons can guide us in addressing the emerging complexities of AI-based education and ensure that efforts to broaden participation in CS continue to prioritize inclusivity for all.
