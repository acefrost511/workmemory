# Summary of UNESCO AI and the Future of Education
Authors: Teddy Kim; [info@ciddl.org](mailto:info@ciddl.org)
[Artificial Intelligence (AI)](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLWuYED1WVJIPHJLk84wWQbzeZcWLt5rwU) is poised to transform education at every level ([CIDDL, 2024](https://ciddl.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Artificial-Intelligence-The-Impact-of-AI-on-Education-for-All-Learners.pdf)). However, this advancement raises a deeper question: \*\*“Is AI strengthening education, or is it pulling it away from its core purpose?”\*\*
[UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/en/artificial-intelligence?hub=32618)’s 2025 report, [\*AI and the Future of Education: Disruptions, Dilemmas and Directions\*](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-and-future-education-disruptions-dilemmas-and-directions?hub=32618), addresses this global pivot point head-on. Bringing together scholarly voices, the report examines how AI challenges long-standing assumptions about teaching, learning, and the role of the human teacher. While some chapters point to unprecedented opportunities, others urge caution about the structural, pedagogical, and ethical risks that accompany rapid adoption.I intentionally highlight three chapters—3, 4, and 5—that speak most directly to classroom practice, instructional design, and the evolving work of educators.
## Report Overview — A broad look at AI’s educational transformation
Across eight major themes, the UNESCO report argues that AI should not be treated simply as a tool of automation but as a catalyst that forces education systems to reconsider their goals, values, and responsibilities.
Key throughlines in the report include:
\* AI is becoming an unavoidable presence in education.
\* Its effects are not universal; they vary widely across contexts, learners, and educational traditions.
\* Technical performance is insufficient—human judgment, responsible implementation, transparent use, and a renewed focus on learning goals must shape how AI is integrated.
Chapters 3, 4, and 5 offer a grounded, practical lens for teachers, researchers, and educational leaders. The following section provides a balanced summary of their key arguments.
## Chapter 3 — Debating the Power and Perils of AI
Chapter 3 presents a wide-ranging debate about AI’s potential to enhance education and its equally essential limitations. On one side, several authors highlight how AI could expand access to learning resources, support differentiated instruction, [reduce workload](https://ciddl.org/discussing-how-ai-can-support-special-education-teacher-workload-with-your-pre-service-teachers/), and introduce new instructional possibilities. These perspectives present AI as a powerful accelerator for improving efficiency and expanding educational opportunity.
In contrast, others caution that current AI systems—especially [large language models (LLM)](https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/large-language-models)—do not truly “[understand](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.00059)” meaning and may unintentionally undermine educational quality. Concerns include the erosion of teacher autonomy, [overreliance](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/wp-content/uploads/2022/06/Aether-Overreliance-on-AI-Review-Final-6.21.22.pdf) on opaque algorithms, reinforcement of existing [structural biases](https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/ai-bias), and the growing influence of technology companies in shaping educational priorities.
Taken together, Chapter 3 emphasizes that the core question is not whether AI should enter education but how it should be used, who benefits from it, and what values guide its integration.
## Chapter 4 — AI Pedagogies, Assessment, and Emerging Education Futures
Chapter 4 examines how AI is transforming instructional design and assessment practices, offering both caution and optimism.
The more critical voices argue that heavily automated or algorithmic approaches to learning risk oversimplifying complex educational processes. Personalized or adaptive systems may narrow learning pathways or reduce [student agency](https://ciddl.org/episode-1-rethinking-agency-in-the-age-of-ai-gaining-an-initial-understanding/). In [assessment](https://ciddl.org/rethinking-assessment-in-the-age-of-generative-ai/), the rise of generative AI exposes weaknesses in traditional assignments and raises new questions about [academic integrity](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0001299823000363?casa\_token=oNmZMI-xPBwAAAAA:oOgGnh1f\_EmrUc1tNoxE3RqMzrHuDBfKx5IZVrgKFX9d4lQc9fOyfRaNCic\_XUSVi9W1UBdscA), reliability, and fairness.
At the same time, other contributors to this chapter point out that AI can meaningfully support educators when used intentionally. AI has the potential to strengthen formative assessment, provide high-quality feedback at scale, visualize learning patterns, and free teachers from repetitive tasks, allowing them to focus more on relationships and instructional decision-making. Some authors describe emerging models, such as [cyber-social learning](https://newlearningonline.com/e-learning), in which AI acts not as a replacement for instruction but as a partner that enriches interaction among students and teachers.
Overall, Chapter 4 argues that AI’s impact depends less on the technology itself and more on how educators design learning environments and maintain educational goals at the center of any technological integration.
## Chapter 5 — Revaluing and Recentering the Human Teachers
Chapter 5 focuses directly on the role of teachers and the irreplaceable human element of education. Several authors emphasize that teaching is fundamentally relational—built on interpretation, judgment, emotional understanding, and the ability to guide learners as developing individuals. These dimensions cannot be automated or delegated to AI systems. In this view, the teacher remains the central figure who creates meaning, establishes trust, and nurtures learners’ growth.
Yet the chapter also acknowledges that AI can strengthen the teaching profession when aligned with human-centered priorities. AI may reduce administrative burdens, help monitor learning progress, or assist in designing supportive environments. Importantly, educators should actively participate in shaping AI systems through co-design, ensuring tools reflect real classroom needs and uphold educational values.
Chapter 5 ultimately positions AI not as a substitute for teachers but as a catalyst that highlights the enduring importance of human expertise, care, and professional judgment.
## Education in the Age of AI is Defined by our Choices, not the Technology
The UNESCO report delivers a clear message: \*\*AI does not determine the future of education—educators do.\*\*
AI presents genuine opportunities to enhance teaching and learning, but it also introduces systemic risks that must be confronted with transparency and critical awareness. The central issue is not technological capability, but the choices educators, researchers, school leaders, and policymakers make about how AI is designed, governed, and used.
In this sense, the future of education is not a story about machines. It is a story about the values we choose to protect, the learning environments we intentionally create, and the continued leadership of human teachers who guide students in an increasingly complex world.
