## Abstract
As AI technologies permeate daily life, adolescents' distinctive cognitive profiles make their decision-making highly sensitive to AI explanation features. The study aimed to examine the underlying mechanisms by which AI's explanatory features and time frame impact adolescents' decision-making. We created an online platform where adolescents interacted with an explainable AI. A preliminary survey identified 10 mathematics-related factors. Experiment 1 involved 158 students ( \_M\_ age = 13.7) and used a 3 (explanation type: prediction, causal, counterfactual) × 2 (perceived control: high, low) × 2 (perceived reliability: reliable, unreliable) mixed design. Experiment 2 recruited 225 students ( \_M\_ age = 13.7) and employed a 3 (explanation type) × 2 (time frame: short-term, long-term) mixed design. Decision-making and expectation (expected impact of each factor on math achievement) were the outcomes in both experiments. In Experiment 1, perceived unreliable counterfactual explanations for low-control factors produced the lowest expectation and decision-making probability, whereas predictions and causal explanations did not differ. For high-control factors, perceived reliable counterfactual explanations similarly reduced decision-making probability, although expectation remained constant across explanations. In Experiment 2, predictions and causal explanations led to higher decision-making probability for short-term events than long-term ones, while counterfactuals reversed this pattern. While counterfactual explanations help restore trust and motivate change in distant, uncertain contexts, they can trigger reactance and reduce action when events feel controllable or imminent. Although adolescents cognitively understand causality and time frames, they still struggle to effectively regulate their decisions. AI model explanations should therefore account for the developmental characteristics of adolescents and recognize the dual effects inherent in counterfactual explanations.
## Introduction
As generative AI models become increasingly integrated into mainstream technologies, their usage has expanded across all age groups. For example, recent surveys indicate that the percentage of U.S. teens aged 13–17 using ChatGPT for homework rose from 13 % in 2023 to 26 % in 2024 (Sidoti et al., 2025). However, this widespread adoption also means that users are more frequently exposed to the fabrications and biases of these systems. Generative models often produce confident yet potentially misleading responses, which can be especially persuasive in uncertain contexts (Kidd & Birhane, 2023; van den Bos & Hertwig, 2017). Adolescents, who are typically more curious about and trusting of AI, and whose knowledge structures are still developing, are increasingly turning to AI for guidance on open-ended issues, such as how to improve mathematics achievement, where human advice may be less available or more ambiguous. Surveys show that 54 % of teens find it acceptable to use AI to explore new topics, and 33 % use AI for serious discussions instead of real people (Robb & Mann, 2025). Nevertheless, the accuracy of AI-generated advice is far from guaranteed, making adolescents particularly vulnerable to belief distortion (Kuhn & Laird, 2011; Lee et al., 2025, pp. 1–20). Notably, the impact of AI on adolescents' trust and decision-making is shaped not only by the model's algorithms and data quality but also by the psychological nature and suitability of the explanations provided, which are crucial for their experience with AI and their personal development.
Explainable AI (XAI), designed to enhance transparency and user comprehension, plays a critical role in shaping beliefs and decisions. In fields like mental health and language learning, clear explanations have been shown to improve user understanding, trust, and engagement, helping users better assess both the capabilities and limitations of AI systems (Mahfouz & AbdelMohsen, 2025; Renftle et al., 2024; Ribeiro-Flucht et al., 2024, pp. 199–209; Türkmen, 2025). While human-computer interaction research has promoted transparency in XAI, it has not sufficiently addressed how explanation types interact with users’ deeper psychological processes. Human-centered XAI (HCXAI) argues that explanations should align with cognitive and emotional needs, not just offer technical transparency (Ehsan et al., 2023; Sarkar et al., 2023).
Indeed, our responses to AI explanations are shaped not only by explanation type but also by broader psychological factors, particularly perceived control and time frame. For example, explanations that enhance a sense of control can help users convert foresight into concrete action, consistent with autonomy-oriented psychological theories (Adenuga & Dodge, 2023). Socioemotional Selectivity Theory further suggests that perceptions of future time influence motivation and behavior, implying that temporal framing within AI explanations could meaningfully shape user responses (Bardach et al., 2021). Despite its potential significance, there is a lack of systematic research exploring the interactions among explanation types, perceived control, and temporal contexts in AI-driven decision-making.
Moreover, although many adolescents already use AI in their studies and daily lives, research on how their cognitive characteristics influence decision-making with AI remains limited (Schaper et al., 2023). Adolescents’ decision-making often relies on fast, intuitive thinking, making them more open to AI explanations and more inclined to engage with simplified models (Bardach et al., 2021). Nevertheless, limited inhibitory control and a preference for immediate feedback can hinder critical thinking and heighten susceptibility to biased beliefs, which are often reinforced through peer influence (Albert et al., 2013; Clemens & Rust, 1979; Thompson & Griffiths, 2021). These unique cognitive and behavioral patterns underscore the need for targeted research on how explanatory features of AI shape adolescent decision-making.
Still, current research on this topic remains inconclusive (Scharowski et al., 2023). Some studies indicate that certain explanations enhance adolescents' trust and improve decision quality, while others suggest explanations may increase cognitive load or lead to misplaced trust. Explanations employing counterfactual reasoning that highlight how alternative actions could yield different outcomes can boost adolescents' perceived control, self-efficacy, and persistence, thus motivating behavioral change, yet such effects might not consistently generalize across different contexts (Bauer et al., 2023; Markman et al., 2008). Given this mixed evidence, the core problem addressed in this study is to elucidate how AI explanation type, perceived control, and time frame interact to shape adolescents' decision-making and expectations in AI-assisted contexts. Clarifying these mechanisms can reveal why certain AI explanations enhance users’ satisfaction and willingness to act in some contexts yet fail or even backfire in others, ultimately guiding the design of psychologically informed, effective AI systems and deepening our understanding of adolescent cognition.
