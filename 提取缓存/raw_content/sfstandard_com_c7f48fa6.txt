Close share menuBy [Michelle Peng](https://sfstandard.com/author/michelle-peng/)Published Feb. 26, 2026 • 6:00am

Higher education is broken, says Sal Khan, founder of the free digital learning platform Khan Academy, and he aims to provide students with an alternative.

Khan [said Tuesday (opens in new tab)](https://www.charterworks.com/beyond-reskilling-what-companies-can-do-about-ai-displacement/) that he is working on a way to offer a new type of credential that would effectively serve as an alternative to an undergraduate or graduate degree from a top university.

“If someone’s able to achieve this, you would put them in the same category as someone who graduated summa cum laude from Harvard or Stanford,” he said at the [Leading with AI event co-hosted by The Standard and Charter in San Francisco.  (opens in new tab)](https://www.charterworks.com/events/ai-summit/2026/sf/)

ADVERTISEMENT

“Just imagine something that, as opposed to $400,000, costs $5,000,” said Khan, noting that the program would be open to anyone with the ability to complete it.

Through the program, he said, students would develop the skills they need, and Khan Academy would partner with employers to build recruiting pipelines to help graduates land roles at McKinsey, Google, Goldman Sachs, and other major companies.

A Khan Academy spokesperson declined to provide details on curriculum or when the program might launch. “In the next few months you’ll hear much more,” Khan said.

It’s also unclear what credential the program might offer, and what the mix of virtual, artificial intelligence, asynchronous, and in-person instruction might be. Khan separately highlighted the importance of teachers, even as Khan Academy offers AI-powered learning tools.

An increasing number of high school students are [opting for trade schools over traditional four-year degrees (opens in new tab)](https://5d0226c2-89ed-424e-8f35-9e50b6cb55cd.usrfiles.com/ugd/5d0226_0efab59ac44d44af86b99445a17981e2.pdf) as AI threatens to reshape white-collar work, and the [return on investment for MBA degrees (opens in new tab)](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-16/mbas-cost-more-and-are-less-profitable-as-roi-falls) is declining as tuition continues to climb.

Many employers, however, are going in the other direction.

While some have expanded recruiting to students at a wider range of institutions and are de-emphasizing specific degree requirements, the tight labor market and attacks on diversity, equity, and inclusion policies have other companies [training their sights (opens in new tab)](https://www.wsj.com/lifestyle/careers/elite-colleges-are-back-at-the-top-of-the-list-for-company-recruiters-ad9526ac?st=kPT6K5&reflink=desktopwebshare_permalink&utm_source=DO+NOT+EMAIL+-+Full+list&utm_campaign=4378e77993-EMAIL_CAMPAIGN_2026_01_08_04_51&utm_medium=email&utm_term=0_-4378e77993-1377660731) on a select number of elite schools.

Some 26% of employers said they were recruiting exclusively from a list of core schools, up from 17% in 2022, according to a 2025 [survey (opens in new tab)](https://verisinsights.com/resources/blogs/the-key-trends-defining-early-career-recruiting-in-2026/) from Veris Insights. The Wall Street Journal reported that GE Appliances, for example, has pared down the number of schools where it hosts recruiting events, and McKinsey has removed a statement from its career page that said, “We hire people, not degrees.”

That’s “one of the reasons why a really smart kid with a 1550 on their SAT might decide to go to Harvard instead of the full-ride scholarship at their state university,” said Khan. “McKinsey recruits at Harvard, and they don’t recruit at my state university.”

In other words, economic opportunity is still tied to a small number of elite universities that remain out of reach for most. Harvard and Stanford, for example, admitted fewer than 4% of applicants for the class of 2029, at or near historic lows.

Making matters worse, Khan said Tuesday, many students graduate from these schools saddled with thousands of dollars in debt and lacking the skills to thrive in the workplace.

“I’ve always aspired to think of Khan Academy ... as a global learning institution,” Khan said recently on the “Decoder” podcast. He argued that the problem with most elite institutions—the Harvards and Stanfords of the world—is that they can’t — or won’t — scale quality education to more students.

What Khan is aspiring to build is a global institution that would open its doors to any student with potential. “If you’re capable, we have as much capacity as you need,” he said Tuesday.
