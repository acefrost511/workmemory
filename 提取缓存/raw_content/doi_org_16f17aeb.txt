## Abstract
Although affective states play a crucial role in education, they are often difficult to communicate and observe in online learning environments. This challenge has led to growing research on systems that can automatically detect affective states. This systematic literature review used PRISMA to analyze 96 studies on affective computing in online higher education, published between 2019 and 2024. The findings show that the most frequently studied affective states include learning-centered states, such as engagement, confusion, frustration, sentiment, as well as basic emotions, such as happiness, anger, sadness, surprise, and fear.
Terminology often overlaps, and basic emotions are commonly used as proxies for learning-centered states. The most used modality is facial expression, with the dominant detection approach being deep learning, particularly convolutional neural networks. Most studies rely on self-collected datasets that, due to privacy concerns, are not publicly shared, limiting reproducibility and generalizability. FER2013, collected in a generic context, and DAiSEE, collected in an online educational setting, are the most used public datasets. A key limitation is that most systems are not evaluated in real classrooms, revealing a gap between technological development, and educational application. Ethical considerations are often overlooked, with privacy, when addressed, being the main focus. Finally, the review’s findings highlight the need for stronger integration between education and technology through interdisciplinary collaboration and real-world validation.

## 1\. Introduction
Emotions are fundamental to human experience, and play a critical role in learning. Emotion is defined as a complex process that involves neurophysiological responses, motor-expressiveness, and subjective experiences due to a contextual stimulus, which can be accompanied by organic changes [(Scherer, 2005)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0560). Over the years, various theoretical models have been proposed to conceptualize emotions. Discrete models, such as those of [Ekman (1992)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0165) [1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fn0005) and [Plutchik (1980)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0490), [2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fn0010) propose a set of core, universally recognized emotions. In contrast, dimensional models ( [Mehrabian, 1980](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0405); [Russell, 1980](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0535)) represent emotions within a continuous space, typically defined by the axes of valence, arousal, and dominance. Cognitive models take a different perspective, suggesting that emotions result from the interpretation and appraisal of triggering events [(Reisenzein, 2020)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0515). Facial expressions are among the most prominent channels of emotional expression. The scientific exploration of facial expressions dates back to Charles Darwin’s seminal work in 1872, which provided evidence for the universality of certain facial expressions across cultures, and helped explain how emotions can be identified visually [(Darwin, 1993)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0140). In recent years, advances in technology have given rise to the field of Facial Expression Recognition (FER), a subfield of affective computing that leverages computer vision algorithms to detect facial expressions
A broader concept than emotions is affective states. Affective states also include moods and less intense feelings that often lack a specific contextual trigger, and tend to last longer [(Clore et al., 2014)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0115). The study of these states is at the center of the interdisciplinary field of Affective Computing, which lies at the intersection of Computer Science, Psychology, and Cognitive Science [(Daily et al., 2017)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0135). First introduced by [Picard (2000)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0475), Affective Computing focuses on enabling computers to recognize, interpret, and simulate human emotions.
Affective states are closely connected to the learning process. Learning involves both acquiring and applying knowledge, and experiencing and managing emotions throughout. Bloom’s taxonomy classifies learning into cognitive, affective, and psychomotor domains [(Bloom et al., 1956)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0080), emphasizing that emotions, attitudes, and feelings are essential to educational experiences. Pekrun’s research on academic emotions [(Pekrun, 2014)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0470) has significantly advanced understanding of how emotions influence learning. His work introduces the following categories of learning-centered emotions: achievement, epistemic, topic, and social emotions. Achievement emotions relate to success and failure, such as enjoyment, hope, pride, anxiety, and shame. Epistemic emotions are evoked by cognitive problems, such as surprise, curiosity, confusion, frustration, and delight. Topic emotions relate to topics presented in the classroom, such as empathy, anxiety, and disgust when dealing with medical issues. Finally, social emotions, such as love, compassion, and envy, relate to the relationships between teachers and peers in the classroom environment. Pekrun also categorized emotions as positive or negative, and further, as activating or deactivating based on their impact on motivation, attention, and learning strategies. For example, activating positive emotions like enjoyment can enhance learning, while deactivating positive emotions may hinder it. Interestingly, even emotions that are traditionally perceived as negative in a general context, such as confusion or frustration, can promote deeper learning when appropriately regulated [(Richey et al., 2019)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0525) in the context of education. Theories such as Amsel’s frustration theory [(Amsel, 1990)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0050) support this view, framing frustration as a response to obstacles in goal attainment, potentially leading to greater effort and engagement.
In recent years, the widespread availability of digital devices and improved internet infrastructure has led to a surge in online learning. Defined by Dhawan as \_‘a learning experience conducted in synchronous or asynchronous online environments using internet-connected devices’\_ [(Dhawan, 2020)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0155), online learning is known by many terms, and lacks a universally accepted definition [(Singh and Thurman, 2019)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0595). In this paper, we use the term \_’online learning’\_ to refer to educational activities conducted entirely over the internet. While this mode of learning offers benefits like flexibility and accessibility, it also introduces challenges, including reduced face-to-face interaction, and insufficient real-time feedback [(Mukhtar et al., 2020)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0425). These limitations have been linked to decreased student engagement in online classrooms [(Gillett-Swan, 2017)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0220). As a result, increasing attention is being directed toward how emerging technologies, particularly artificial intelligence (AI), can be leveraged to detect and respond to students’ emotional and cognitive states in real time [(Hwang et al., 2020)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0275).
Several systematic reviews have examined the use of technology and affective computing in educational contexts. For example, [Yadegaridehkordi et al. (2019)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0675) reviewed research on affective computing in education from 2010 to 2017, finding that text-based analysis was the most common modality, and the Achievement Emotions Questionnaire (AEQ) was the most frequently used tool for measuring emotions. Most studies aimed to design emotion recognition methods or systems, with frequent focus on emotions like boredom, anger, anxiety, enjoyment, and frustration. More recent work by [Lek and Teo (2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0325) reviewed facial expression recognition (FER) in academic settings from 2018 to 2022. Their findings indicated that FER-2013 [(Goodfellow et al., 2013)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0225) was the most widely used dataset in general FER research, while DAiSEE [(Gupta et al., 2016)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0245) was predominant in educational contexts, and Support Vector Machines (SVMs) were the most common traditional classifiers, whereas Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) dominated deep learning approaches. Notably, they showed that most systems operated \_post hoc\_ rather than in real-time. [Fang et al. (2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0190) further reviewed FER applications in educational research from 2012 to 2023, highlighting the absence of a unified framework to deploy FER in education [(Fang et al., 2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0190). [Yu et al. (2024)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0705) offered a broader review of what they refer to as Affective Emotion Recognition (AER) from 2010 to 2024, revealing that while system development was often prioritized, practical application in learning environments was frequently overlooked. Their findings also noted that feedback from AER systems was primarily intended for teachers or the systems themselves, with limited integration into the learning process. Moreover, most studies targeted asynchronous online learning and focused heavily on online higher education.
This review of the literature shows that no comprehensive study has examined the period before, during, and after the COVID-19 pandemic, a time that marked the rapid increase of online learning. In particular, no review has systematically addressed all stages of affective computing in online higher education, namely, identifying the target affective states, determining the scientific methods to measure them, exploring how they can be communicated to stakeholders, and ensuring that these processes comply with ethical guidelines. Addressing this gap motivates our research questions and provides a basis for future studies and practical work in affective computing in online higher education. Therefore, building on previous reviews, this study aims to systematically review affective computing in online higher education over the past five years, up to the year this paper was written, the period 2019–2024. This review adopts a broad perspective by considering a range of modalities used for emotion recognition. In addition, it explores how emotion detection findings are communicated to key stakeholders, and whether this information is meaningfully integrated into the learning process. Lastly, the review also examines the ethical considerations associated with affective computing in educational contexts, analyzing whether the reviewed studies acknowledge ethical considerations and propose mitigation strategies to prevent misuse or unintended consequences. These strategies are essential components of responsible research in affective computing and are gaining increasing attention [(Iren et al., 2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0280), due to the sensitive nature of the emotional data involved, and the potential for ethical risks in its application.
To guide this literature review, the following research questions have been formulated to explore the different aspects of affective computing in online higher education:
- \*\*RQ1.\*\* \_Which affective states are most commonly studied in online higher education?\_
- \*\*RQ2.\*\* \_How are the affective states studied in online higher education measured?\_
- \*\*RQ3.\*\* \_How are the measured affective states conveyed to the target stakeholders in online higher education?\_
- \*\*RQ4.\*\* \_What ethical considerations are addressed when studying affective states in online higher education?\_
The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: [Section 2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#s0010) presents the methodology, detailing the procedures used to identify and select the studies included in the review, while [Section 3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#s0055) presents the results in relation to the research questions. [Section 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#s0160) discusses the implications of the findings within the context of the research questions, then [Section 5](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#s0185) discusses the limitations of the current studies and offers suggestions for future research. Finally, [Section 6](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#s0190) concludes the paper.
## 2\. Methodology
The systematic literature review was conducted following the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) approach [(Page et al., 2021)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0450), a peer-accepted methodology to ensure the quality, transparency, and applicability of our review. The details about the procedure that led to the final set of studies in the analysis are discussed in the following sections.
### 2.1. Identification
#### 2.1.1. Query
The search query aimed to find studies that use affective computing in online higher education. For that purpose, a search query using Boolean operators was conducted. Specifically, the search query consisted of two strings with statements combined with an statement. [Table 1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#tbl0005) displays the detailed search query. The terms regarding education were searched in pairs to avoid retrieving studies outside our scope. For example, words like \_“learning”\_ when searched alone can yield results with papers containing the term \_“machine learning”\_. Moreover, the term \_“e-learning”\_ was not included, as it usually refers to learning with the use of any electronic/multimedia device, and not necessarily from a distance, therefore its inclusion would have retrieved papers from a wider scope than the present study. Similarly, the term \_“sentiment analysis”\_ was not included in the search query, as it usually refers to categorizing opinions on a particular topic from textual information. The search query was run in the Scopus database. The search was performed in February 2025 to ensure that all the papers from 2024 would be included. The search resulted in a total of 630 papers.

#### 2.1.2. Bibliographic inclusion criteria
The search was limited to articles published in English within the preceding five years, i.e., 2019–2024. This timeframe was chosen to ensure the inclusion of recent literature while preserving the study’s relevance. We included papers whose document type was classified as book chapters or conference papers. While our focus was strictly on research papers rather than textbooks, some conference proceedings were categorized as “Books,” with their individual papers then labeled as “Book Chapters”. To avoid inadvertently excluding relevant studies, we included the document type “Book Chapter” in our inclusion selection. We included only the papers that were in the final stage of publication. Finally, regarding the source type, we strictly included papers only if they were from journals, conference proceedings, or book series. [Fig. 1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0005)(A) shows the above-mentioned inclusion criteria. The bibliographic inclusion criteria were applied to the records identified with the research query and resulted in a set of 450 papers sought for retrieval.

### 2.2. Screening
#### 2.2.1. Exclusion criteria
\*\*\_Content Exclusion Criteria\_\*\* A set of exclusion criteria was also applied during the screening process, assessing the title, abstract, and full text of the papers. Since these criteria addressed nuanced aspects not fully captured by the research query, they were applied manually to ensure a more accurate selection of relevant studies. This section describes the exclusion criteria regarding the content of the papers.
- •
\_‘effects on emotions’\_. This category refers to papers that do not focus on detecting emotions to enhance education but rather examine how various aspects of education and/or technology influence students’ emotions. For example, studies that explore the impact of storytelling strategies on students’ emotional responses.
- •
\_‘effects on education’\_. This category refers to studies that examine how different emotions influence education, particularly in terms of student performance and learning outcomes, and do not focus on detecting emotions to enhance the online classroom experience.
- •
\_‘not purely online classroom’\_. This category refers to papers that examine educational settings other than fully online environments, such as blended and hybrid learning. They are excluded because our study focuses exclusively on fully online classrooms. This distinction is important, as the educational setting fundamentally influences the choice and use of technological tools. By narrowing our scope to fully online classrooms, we ensure a focused analysis of learning environments that operate entirely in a digital space.
- •
\_‘underage’\_. The scope of this study was strictly narrowed to studies that target adult learners. We focused only on higher education, as adults and children differ in cognitive modalities, emotion regulation strategies, and ethical considerations.
- •
\_‘literature review’\_. Literature review papers were excluded from the analysis, which focused only on studies that presented original research.
- •
\_‘applications in health’\_. This category refers to papers that explore the intersection of health and education. For example, studies that examine the impact of online learning on burnout or the effects of educational technology on individuals with autism fall into this category.
- •
\_‘sentiment analysis towards a subject’\_. Sentiment analysis is the process of analyzing text to identify its emotional tone along a negative-neutral-positive spectrum. The terms \_sentiment analysis\_ and \_emotion recognition\_ are often used interchangeably in the literature. As a result, although \_sentiment analysis\_ was not explicitly included in our search query, relevant studies may still appear in the extracted papers dataset due to the inclusion of the term \_‘emotion recognition’\_. However, sentiment analysis was not included in the search query, as it can sometimes be irrelevant to our research scope. To ensure alignment with our study’s scope, in the screening process, we include sentiment analysis research only when it directly pertains to the course itself. Studies that focus on broader contexts, such as students’ sentiment towards forced remote education during the COVID-19 pandemic, are excluded.
- •
\_‘not education’\_. This category refers to studies that focus on something other than education, e.g., the development of a detection algorithm, and they only mention education as a potential field of study.
- •
\_‘teachers’ attitudes/evaluation’\_. This category refers to papers that explore teachers’ perspectives on various aspects of education, such as the integration of technology in teaching. Additionally, it refers to studies that investigate the use of technology to assess and evaluate teacher performance in educational settings.
- •
\_‘no emotion/affect’\_. This category refers to papers that do not involve any form of emotion recognition, but instead focus on other types of measurements in education. For example, studies assessing user experience in the interface design of an educational dashboard fall into this category.
- •
\_‘not a final research paper’\_. This category refers to papers that are not a research paper, or not the final version of one. Other than full research papers, other types of papers, such as workshop papers, were considered for inclusion only if they presented completed experiments with conclusive findings. As a result, work-in-progress studies, doctoral consortium papers, and similar preliminary submissions were excluded. Moreover, opinion papers were also excluded.
- •
\_‘no contribution to our scope’\_. Only studies that aligned with the scope of this research were included, meaning they had to contribute meaningfully to at least two of the research questions. This criterion ensured that the selected studies provided substantial insights relevant to the objectives of our systematic review.
- •
\_‘no evaluation of any type’\_. This category refers to papers that do not include any type of evaluation or validation of their proposed method. These papers were excluded.
One paper could fall into more than one of the aforementioned categories. However, since meeting a single exclusion criterion was sufficient to remove a paper from the final set for the analysis, only the first exclusion reason that was identified is reported in this paper.
\*\*\_Quality Exclusion Criteria\_\*\* To ensure the inclusion of high-quality studies, we applied an additional filter based on the quality of the publication venue. Specifically, for journal articles, we included only those published in journals ranked as Q1, Q2, or Q3 according to the Scimago Journal & Country Rank [3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fn0015) [(Guerrero-Bote and Moya-Anegón, 2012)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0235). Regarding conference proceedings papers, we included conferences that were ranked A, B, or C. For the ranking, we used three resources, namely, the Core Ranking List, [4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fn0020) and the portal Conference Rank [5](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fn0025) that uses as sources ERA (2010), Qualis (2012), and MSAR (2014). Conferences that had a ranking in those lists were included. These criteria ensured the inclusion of high-quality and credible literature.

#### 3.2.2. (RQ2) How are the affective states studied in online higher education measured?
##### Modality
\*\*(IN8)\*\* The affective states are expressed in different modalities. There are obtrusive modalities like physiological measures, such as Electroencephalography (EEG), heart rate through Electrocardiogram (ECG), Galvanic Skin Response (GSR), and Electrodermal Activity (EDA), as well as unobtrusive modalities, such as facial expression, gaze, speech, and keystroke. The larger distinction is between non-physiological and physiological data. The latter gives more reliable results, because the former is affected by age, culture, and sex [(Akhand et al., 2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0025). However, they are challenging to collect, since they require special equipment. Facial expressions are soft biometrics that are significant in emotion detection [(Revina and Emmanuel, 2021)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0520). Facial expressions of the speaker contribute 55 % to the communication, while the vocal part, e.g., vocal intonation, and the verbal part, i.e., spoken words, contribute only about (38 and 7) %, respectively [(Mehrabian, 2017)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0410).
In the included papers, some only report using one modality (unimodal) while some combine several (multimodal). The identified different modalities are: Facial Expressions, Text, Analytics/Self-Report, Physiological, Speech, Eye/Gaze, and Gestures/Head Movement. Facial expression approaches include using the face as input in its raw form or through proxies of expressions, such as action units or smiling. Text refers to studies where the input is textual information produced by students in or about the class. Analytics/Self-report modality includes information from the learning management system, such as activity data, e.g., clicks, and keystrokes, as well as self-reports from students regarding their emotional states and learning status. Physiological is the modality that uses data measurements collected from the body that reflect biological processes, e.g., EEG, and GSR. Speech refers to verbal communication. Eye/Gaze is the modality that uses information extracted from the eyes, e.g., through the use of eye trackers. Lastly, Gestures/Head Movements refer to non-verbal communication with hands or head movements, e.g., nodding.
[Fig. 8](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0040) shows the modalities of the included papers, which reveal that the majority of the studies use Facial Expressions (45 papers) and multimodal approaches (31 papers). Additionally, in the unimodal cases, 11 studies used text data, 5 used analytics or self-reports, 3 used physiological data, and 1 study used speech data. In the multimodal cases, [Fig. 8](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0040)(b) shows the frequencies of the different modalities, and [Fig. 8](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0040)(c) visualizes the frequencies of their co-occurrences. We observe that facial expressions and eye/gaze are the most common modalities in multimodal approaches, and likewise, the most frequent combination is facial expressions and eye/gaze, with the latter not being used in any unimodal cases.

##### Model/Approach
\*\*(IN9)\*\* In this section, we outline the methods employed for affective computing as reported in the included studies, i.e., the models for detecting the target affective states. Affective computing typically involves a multi-step process that extends beyond affect detection itself, including preprocessing, feature extraction, and other intermediate stages. This section provides an overview of the models and techniques used in the reviewed literature, highlighting the various approaches adopted at each stage of the pipeline. [Fig. 9](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0045) visualizes the overview. It is common for a paper to include more than one approach, for example, a deep learning model for feature extraction and a machine learning classifier for affect classification. Therefore, we classified the approaches into six categories, namely, \_Machine Learning\_: models that ‘learn’ from data with the use of statistical models ( [Fig. 9](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0045) a), \_Deep Learning\_: a subfield of ML, models that ‘learn’ from data with the use of neural networks with many layers ( [Fig. 9](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0045) b), \_Off-the-Shelf\_: libraries or toolboxes that are available, but still need configuration ( [Fig. 9](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0045) c), \_Algorithms\_ ( [Fig. 9](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0045) d): algorithms and mathematical operations that do not include ‘learning’, \_Products\_: products ready to use that do not need any programming from the user ( [Fig. 9](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0045) e), and \_Self-reports/Test\_: standardized and validated questionnaires or tests ( [Fig. 9](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0045) f). [Fig. 9](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0045) denotes the number of studies that used each. It is important to highlight that the subcategories shown in the restructured graphs do not add up. For example, in [Fig. 9](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0045)(b), ‘CNN, n=17’ indicates that 17 studies reported using convolutional neural networks without specifying the exact architecture, while ‘ResNet, n=12’ refers to 12 studies that explicitly mentioned using ResNet, which is a type of CNN. Similarly, in [Fig. 9](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0045)(c), ‘OpenCV, n=4’ denotes studies that mentioned using OpenCV without further detail, whereas ‘Haar cascade, n=6’ refers to studies that specifically reported using the Haar cascade function, which is included in the OpenCV library. Moreover, the categorization aims for visualization and improved readability, and is not a strict classification. The categories are not mutually exclusive, and overlaps do occur. For example, the Wild Facial Spatiotemporal Network (WFSTN) is a combination of MTCNN, DenseNet, and LSTM with self-attention. It is classified under ‘CNN’ in the graph because its core architecture relies on convolutional operations, but one could also argue for its inclusion under ‘LSTM’. Such ambiguity is inevitable, as advanced architectures often integrate components from multiple approaches. Lastly, there were three papers in which this information was not clear, and the methods they used were described vaguely, e.g., \_‘we used Python for text analysis’\_.

##### Datasets
\*\*(IN10)\*\* Affective computing approaches involving ‘learning’ require annotated datasets for training and testing. In this section, we report datasets that were used in the reviewed studies. Specifically, we report on datasets that the authors either used to train their models within their study or reported as the pre-trained models used in their research in their manuscripts. In this manuscript, we report the datasets that were identified in more than one paper. Datasets are classified into two categories, ‘relevant’ and ‘general,’ regarding their relevance to the field of education. [Fig. 10](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fig0050) visualizes the frequencies of the datasets, as well as their relevance. The identified datasets are the following:
- •
Facial Expression Recognition 2013 Dataset (FER2013) [(Goodfellow et al., 2013)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0225):
FER2013 aims for emotion recognition from facial information. This is a large-scale collection of 48 48 pixel grayscale facial images labeled with seven emotions: Angry, Disgust, Fear, Happy, Sad, Surprise, and Neutral. The dataset includes 28,709 examples in the training set, 3589 examples in the public test set, and 3589 examples in the private test set. All images were automatically collected and labeled using the Google Image Search API.
- •
Dataset for Affective States in E-Environments (DAiSEE) [(Gupta et al., 2016)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0245):
DAiSEE aims for learning-centered affect detection using webcam input in online courses. This is a multi-label video classification dataset consisting of 9068 video snippets collected from 112 users, designed to recognize boredom, confusion, engagement, and frustration in real-world conditions. Each affective state is annotated with four intensity levels: very low, low, high, and very high. These labels are crowd-sourcedand validated against a gold standard annotation created by expert psychologists.
- •
Extended Cohn-Kanade dataset (CK+) [(Lucey et al., 2010)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0370):
CK+ aims for emotion and facial expression recognition from facial information. This includes 593 video sequences from 123 subjects aged 18 to 50 years, with variations in gender and heritage. Each sequence captures the transition from a neutral facial expression to a peak expression, recorded at 30 frames per second. Among these, 327 sequences are labeled with one of seven emotion categories: anger, contempt, disgust, fear, happiness, sadness, and surprise. In addition, each sequence is fully annotated using the Facial Action Coding System (FACS), covering 30 action units. The FACS annotations were provided by one or two certified coders, with inter-observer agreement ensuring annotation consistency.
- •
Real-world Affective Faces Database (RAF-DB) [(Li et al., 2017)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0330):
RAF-DB aims at emotion recognition from facial information. This consists of 29,672 facial images collected from the internet and manually labeled through crowd-sourcing by 40 independent taggers. Each image is annotated with either basic or compound expressions, offering a wide range of emotional categories. The dataset includes diverse faces of different ages, genders, and ethnicities. It also features variations in head poses, lighting conditions, and occlusions such as glasses, facial hair, or self-occlusion.
- •
Online Learning Spontaneous Facial Expression Database (OL-SFED) [(Bian et al., 2019)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0075):
OL-SFED aims for learning-centered affective state detection using webcam input in online courses. This consists of 1274 video clips, each lasting 1 to 8 s, collected from 82 participants (29 male and 53 female). The facial expressions in these clips are spontaneous and recorded while participants watched stimulus videos from online learning platforms with durations ranging 10 to 60 min. The dataset focuses on five learning-related emotions: enjoyment, confusion, fatigue, distraction, and neutral. Recordings were made under varying lighting conditions, including both day and night settings. The labeling process combines self-reports from participants, who selected and annotated clips that best represented their affective states and external annotations from four independent coders.
- •
Eighth Emotion Recognition in the Wild Challenge (EmotiW2020) [(Dhall et al., 2020)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0150):
EmotiW2020 was created as part of a benchmarking effort for the grand challenge at the 22nd ACM International Conference on Multimodal Interaction in 2020. It focuses on automatic human behavior analysis, with one of the key tasks being engagement prediction in the wild, consisting of videos in which students were watching five-minute-long MOOC videos on new language learning in diverse environments. Recordings were conducted across different subjects, locations, and times of day to capture natural variations in behavior and setting. The videos are approximately five minutes long, and are annotated with engagement intensity levels on a scale from 0 to 3, corresponding to disengaged, barely engaged, engaged, and highly engaged. The EngageWild database includes a total of 264 videos, divided into 148 for training, 48 for validation, and 67 for testing.
- •
WIDER [(Yang et al., 2016)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0685):
The dataset aims to support face detection. This contains 32,203 color images with a total of 393,703 labeled faces. Each face is annotated with bounding boxes to indicate its location in the image. The dataset includes a wide variety of face scales, poses, and occlusion conditions, providing diverse scenarios for training and evaluating detection models.
- •
AffectNet [(Mollahosseini et al., 2017)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0420):
AffectNet aims at emotion recognition from facial information. This is a large-scale facial expression dataset containing over 1 million images collected from the internet using 1250 emotion-related keywords in six different languages. Approximately half of these images were manually annotated for the following facial expressions: neutral, happy, angry, sad, fear, surprise, disgust, and contempt as well as the intensity of valence and arousal.
- •
300 W-LP synthetic dataset [(Zhu et al., 2016)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0720):
300 W-LP aims at head pose detection. This contains 122,450 images with labeled head poses and facial landmarks. The images were synthetically generated to cover a wide range of head poses, including large angles above 45 degrees. This ensures that models trained on the dataset are robust to challenges such as self-occlusion, which commonly occurs in extreme head poses.
- •
Stanford MOOC posts dataset [(Agrawal and Paepcke, 2022)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0015):
This dataset aims at sentiment detection from textual data. The dataset consists of 29,000 anonymized discussion board posts from students enrolled in eleven public Stanford University SPOL courses.
- •
The Japanese Female Facial Expression database (JAFFE) [(Lyons et al., 1998)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0375)
JAFFE aims at emotion recognition from facial information. It contains 213 images of facial expressions from 10 Japanese female subjects, collected in a laboratory setting. Each subject demonstrated seven expressions, namely happiness, sadness, disgust, fear, surprise, anger, and neutral. The images are annotated with average semantic ratings provided by 60 annotators, and each subject has 3 to 4 images, with a relatively balanced distribution across expression categories.

##### Feedback design
This section presents how the detected information is conveyed to the stakeholders, teachers, and students. Specifically, we outline different strategies based on four characteristics: i) the temporal aspect, i.e., whether the information is provided in real-time or after the fact, ii) the intervention aspect, i.e., whether the information is coupled with an intervention such as a notification, iii) the anonymity aspect, i.e., whether the information is presented in aggregated form for the whole classroom, or per individual student, and iv) the evaluation aspect, i.e., whether the proposed strategy has been evaluated through any type of assessment, such as usability testing, interviews, or focus groups. Regarding the temporal aspect, 22 studies focused on ‘real-time’, four studies on ‘real-time’ and ‘after-the-fact’, and two studies on ’after-the-fact’. Regarding the aspect of notification, 18 do not discuss the intervention. From the five studies that target the student, two studies mention that they alert the students when they are detected as disengaged and inattentive; two studies mention that they send a pop-up message to the student informing them regarding their state; and one study provides students with adaptive feedback to keep them motivated. From the studies that target the teacher, two studies mention that they send a notification to the teacher that is configurable, i.e., they can choose in which incident to get notified, and two mention that they give a warning message to the teacher if learning emotions or levels of disengagement exceed a threshold. On the aspect of anonymity, in 13 studies there is no anonymity, since the information is given per student, six studies claim that anonymity is preserved because the information is facing the student, nine studies provide the information to the teacher in an aggregated form, and two of those nine also follow a privacy-preserving way of storing the data, and in one study, the aspect of anonymity is not discussed. Lastly, regarding the aspect of evaluation, 18 studies do not mention anything regarding evaluation, either because the system was not ready, or because it was not in their scope; one study used an already existing tool; one study used the User Acceptance Test for evaluation; four studies designed the system of giving feedback using insights from experts; and six studies evaluated the usability, two of which used the standardized System Usability Scale questionnaire, and the rest used their own crafted surveys.

## 4\. Discussion
This section discusses the implications of the findings in relation to each research question, highlighting both commonalities and gaps identified across the reviewed literature.
### 4.1. Commonly studied affective states in online higher education
The first research question of this systematic literature review was, \_Which affective states are most commonly studied in online higher education?\_. To answer this research question, the target affective states and their definitions were extracted from the reviewed papers. A total of 50 different affective states were detected as the target. This is a high number that leads to confusion in terminology and reflects the absence of a clear goal in the field. Among them, 26 affective states are studied in one or two papers, which also introduces a limitation, making it difficult to generalize and use existing research to build upon.
The most frequent target affective states are LCAS, basic emotions, and sentiment analysis. Specifically, the target LCAS are: achievement-related emotions, such as boredom and frustration; epistemic emotions like confusion; and cognitive states, such as engagement. This aligns with the work of [Pekrun (2014)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0470) and the arguments of [D’Mello (2013)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0130), as well as insights from experts [(Shingjergji et al., 2024)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0585). In addition, sentiment is often studied, i.e., whether the affective state is positive or negative, indicating that some studies consider emotion polarity alone sufficient for guiding teacher interventions. Lastly, the six basic emotions (happiness, sadness, anger, fear, disgust, and surprise) remain among the most frequently targeted affective states in affective computing research, even though they are not particularly relevant or informative within educational contexts.
A common issue in the reviewed literature is the overlap and inconsistency in the definition of affective states. Terms such as ‘fear’ and ‘scared’, or ‘stress’ and ‘anxiety’, are often used as different states, without clear distinctions. This conceptual ambiguity creates confusion and highlights the lack of consensus regarding terminology in the field. Moreover, many target affective states in the analyzed papers do not qualify as emotions from a psychological standpoint. For example, attention is better understood as a cognitive process tied to engagement, rather than an affective state [(Yadav et al., 2024)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0670). Similarly, constructs like engagement, distraction, and flow are more accurately described as learning-centered affective or cognitive states, while states like sleepiness and yawning are regarded as physiological states and responses. This distinction underscores the need for research that addresses affect specifically within learning contexts, rather than relying on generic emotion models developed for broader applications. In many studies, affective states are inferred through indirect mappings that lack empirical support. A common example is the use of basic emotions to estimate cognitive states like engagement, through non-validated formulas, e.g., defining engagement as a weighted sum of basic emotions. This practice reflects a growing interest in learning-related states, but also reveals a gap in available datasets and validated frameworks, leading to questionable inference methods.
Terminological inconsistency is also evident in how emotion recognition is described. Terms like \_‘Emotion Recognition’\_, \_‘Facial Emotion Recognition (FER)’\_, \_‘Facial Expression Recognition’\_, and \_‘Facial Expression Emotion Recognition’\_ are often used interchangeably, despite referring to different concepts. For example, facial expression recognition refers to observable muscle movements, while emotion is an internal, context-dependent affective state that may be expressed through multiple modalities, which include facial cues, vocal tone, gestures, and eye movement. This lack of conceptual clarity contributes to confusion and limits the interpretability and reproducibility of findings.
All in all, the reviewed literature shows no clear consensus on how to define and use key terms such as facial expression and emotion. It is common to find statements like \_“we detect facial expressions such as happiness”\_, which conflates observable behavior with internal states. This further underscores the need for greater conceptual precision in the field.
### 4.2. Methods of measuring affective states in online higher education
The second research question of this review was, \_How are the affective states studied in online higher education measured?\_ To address this research question, the modality, detection model, and dataset used were extracted from the reviewed papers. Facial expressions emerged as the most frequently used modality for emotion recognition in the studies reviewed. This marks a shift from earlier findings, such as those in [Yadegaridehkordi et al. (2019)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0675), where text-based input was the dominant modality. The growing use of facial data may be attributed to the increasing presence of camera-equipped devices in online education environments, enabling easier collection of visual cues.
Consistent with the focus on facial expression analysis, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) were the most commonly used technique. This aligns with findings from previous systematic reviews and reflects the dominance of image-based emotion recognition models in the field. Deep learning approaches remain the dominant method in affective computing research. However, recent regulatory developments, such as the European Union’s AI Act, [21](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#fn0105) have raised significant concerns. Under the current proposals, the use of AI for emotion recognition in educational settings is classified as a high-risk practice.
The most used dataset across the reviewed studies was FER2013, which, while widely adopted, is not designed for educational contexts. DAiSEE, a dataset more relevant to learning environments, appeared more frequently in studies specifically focused on education, but its usage remains limited. This also highlights the gap between the fields of Computer Science and Education Sciences, and calls for more focus on developing context-specific datasets for training models that aim at affective computing in online education. A large number of studies relied on self-collected datasets, often developed on a small scale and with limited participant diversity. These datasets are generally not publicly available due to privacy concerns, which prevents reproducibility and makes it challenging for new studies to build upon existing work, without collecting new data from scratch.
### 4.3. Communication of measured affective states to the target stakeholder in online higher education
The third research question was, \_How are the measured affective states conveyed to the target stakeholder in online higher education?\_ To address that research question, the way the information was conveyed to the target stakeholder was extracted from the reviewed papers. The results revealed that the majority of the reviewed studies do not include any research, discussion, or even a basic description of how the detected emotional information is conveyed to relevant stakeholders, i.e., students and teachers. This omission reflects a broader disconnect between the fields of Computer Science and Education Sciences, with the former dominating, as shown by the disciplinary backgrounds of the authors.
Among the studies that discuss the communication of the detected information, the majority focus on conveying the findings in real-time, intending to enable teachers to make timely, informed interventions during the learning process. Regarding the target stakeholders, the primary recipient of emotional data in most cases is the teacher. There is little attention paid to whether, when, or how this information might be shared with students or integrated into automated feedback systems. Stakeholder perspectives are largely absent from system design processes. There is a clear need for more participatory approaches, where system requirements are shaped by the actual needs, preferences, and expectations of end users.
Anonymity and data privacy are rarely addressed in the reviewed literature, raising concerns about ethical data handling and transparency, particularly when dealing with sensitive emotional information. Lastly, very few studies evaluate these systems in real online lecture settings to assess their practical impact on learning. This limitation further illustrates the gap between AI research and educational practice. In addition, recent regulatory developments, such as the EU AI Act, now place strict limitations on the deployment of emotion recognition systems in real educational environments, making such evaluations even more difficult to carry out.
### 4.4. Ethical considerations in affective computing in online higher education
The fourth research question of this review was, \_What ethical considerations are addressed when studying affective states in online higher education?\_ To address this research question, the presence of ethical considerations, the mitigation strategies, as well as the presence of ethical statements for conducting the study were extracted from the reviewed papers. The results revealed that in the majority of the reviewed studies, ethical considerations are either briefly mentioned or not addressed at all. This absence raises concerns about the extent to which the ethical implications of affective computing are being actively considered and reported. Several studies omit any explicit mention of ethical procedures or safeguards. However, this omission may be partially attributed to the nature of conference proceedings, which often have strict word limits. The lack of discussion does not necessarily indicate that ethical standards were not followed during the research process, but rather that these aspects may not have been prioritized in the reporting. Among the studies that do address ethics, the concept is often interpreted as data privacy. While privacy is undoubtedly a crucial concern in educational contexts [(Pardo and Siemens, 2014)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0460), it is not the only aspect of ethics in education. Treating privacy and ethics as synonyms overlooks other crucial dimensions, such as the risk of AI being used for harm, bias [(Wiese et al., 2025)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0650), data transparency, fairness, and accuracy [(Agarwal et al., 2024)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0020). Recent work also notes that some researchers tend to treat privacy and ethics as equivalent concepts, potentially limiting the depth of ethical reflection in system design and evaluation [(Corza-Vargas et al., 2024)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0120).
The oversight of ethical considerations is a crucial issue that must be addressed in future research and practice. The European Union (EU) AI Act [(European Union, 2024)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0170), a regulation on the use of AI in the EU, prohibits the use of AI systems for emotion recognition in educational institutions. This calls for researchers in or outside of the EU to develop systems that comply with the regulations.
## 5\. Limitations and future work
Despite the valuable insights this review provides, it is not without limitations, which also point to promising directions for future work. This review focused on studies indexed in the Scopus database, which includes all the publication venues we intended to cover. While we are confident that Scopus captured the relevant literature for our scope, it is still possible that additional relevant studies exist in other databases, and future reviews could consider a broader search to ensure full coverage. Furthermore, detailed analysis of model architectures and their performance metrics was beyond the scope of this study, which aimed to identify broader trends rather than evaluate technical implementations. Future work could undertake a more in-depth review of the computational methods used for affective computing in educational contexts, comparing architectures, training strategies, and evaluation criteria. Additionally, it would be valuable to investigate the impact of recent regulatory developments, such as the European Union’s AI Act, on the research and deployment landscape of emotion recognition systems in education through thematic analyses [(Iren et al., 2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0280) or systematic literature reviews. Finally, given that much of the reviewed literature spans the COVID-19 period, a future review could focus specifically on post-COVID studies to assess whether the shift to online learning has had a lasting impact on the use and perception of affective computing in higher education.
In light of the growing relevance of online learning, the increasing availability and performance of AI-based affective computing systems, forthcoming regulatory constraints, and the heightened awareness of ethical issues, future research in this field will need to evolve in several key directions, as indicated by the findings of this review.
First, regarding the target of the studies, our results show that there is a need to advance a more unified terminology, as the inconsistent use of concepts continues to limit comparability and synthesis across studies. These results build upon previous reviews that also identified the lack of an overall framework [(Fang et al., 2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0190). Establishing shared definitions and conceptual frameworks will require stronger collaboration between researchers developing affective computing methods and experts in education and emotion. Such interdisciplinary dialogue can ensure that technical constructs align with pedagogical realities and psychological theory. In addition, future studies should devote greater attention to the detection and interpretation of learning-centered affective states. Despite their central relevance for educational outcomes [(Shingjergji et al., 2024)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25001390?via%3Dihub#bib0585), these states remain understudied compared to basic emotions as this review showed. Addressing this gap would refine the scope of affective computing in online higher education and enhance its practical value for teaching and learning.
Second, regarding the development and application of affective computing systems, the results of this review underscore the need for stronger connections between the technical and educational dimensions of the field. System design should not be approached as a purely technical challenge but as an educationally grounded process in which stakeholders, such as teachers and students, are actively involved. Their insights and experiences are crucial for ensuring that the proposed systems are relevant, usable, and responsive to the realities of teaching and learning, something that is commonly missing from existing studies, as this review has shown. Future research should prioritize the creation of resources that are tailored to educational contexts, including datasets on learning settings and systems designed to be seamlessly integrated into learning management platforms, and benefit the teachers and students. Such efforts will require sustained collaboration between researchers in technology and education, fostering solutions that are technically robust, while also being pedagogically meaningful.
Finally, ethical considerations must be treated as essential to the system design and deployment. This review revealed the overall lack of ethical considerations in the previous studies, potentially putting ethical principles at risk as well as hindering the integration of the proposed systems in real classrooms. Ethical principles and policies are inherently dynamic and context-dependent. Therefore, research in affective computing should remain responsive to their evolution and ensure continuous alignment with current guidelines and societal expectations. Ethics should not be an external check applied once systems are developed, but a principle embedded throughout the design process. Building on the point of stakeholder involvement, this also implies that policymakers and experts in ethics should be directly engaged in system development. Their participation can help anticipate potential risks, safeguard the rights and well-being of learners, and promote responsible innovation in online higher education.
## 6\. Conclusion
This review analyzed 96 studies published between 2019 and 2024 on affective computing in online higher education. The most commonly studied affective states included both basic emotions and learning-centered affective states (LCASs) that are particularly relevant to education. These included achievement-related emotions, such as boredom and frustration; epistemic emotions, like confusion; and cognitive states, such as engagement and sentiment. The findings of this review highlight the lack of consensus in the terminology used to describe affective states. Terms are often used inconsistently or interchangeably, contributing to conceptual ambiguity in the field.
Facial expressions emerged as the most frequently used modality for emotion recognition, with deep learning approaches, especially Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), being the dominant detection approach. Despite technical advancements, there remains a significant gap in how emotion detection findings are communicated to stakeholders. The vast majority of systems have not been tested in real classroom settings. This highlights a broader disconnect between technical development and practical application in education. Finally, ethical considerations are often overlooked in the reviewed literature. This is concerning, given the sensitive nature of emotional data and the increasing relevance of regulatory frameworks, such as the European Union’s AI Act, which will make ethical transparency and compliance essential for the integration of affective computing into educational contexts. Advancing affective computing in online higher education will require sustained collaboration among technical experts, educational stakeholders, and ethical policymakers to ensure that future developments are methodologically rigorous, pedagogically meaningful, and ethically sound.

- ### [Emotional artificial intelligence in higher education: A systematic review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1096751626000072)
2026, Internet and Higher Education
Show abstract
Emotions are central to learning in higher education, influencing motivation, engagement, and achievement. As digital and hybrid instruction expand, however, students' affective cues often become obscured, limiting timely pedagogical support. Emotional Artificial Intelligence ( \_E\_-AI) has emerged as a potential solution, capable of detecting and responding to emotional states through multimodal sensing and machine learning. Yet current research frequently conflates recognition accuracy with educational validity, treats facial expressions as universal indicators, underreports cultural bias, and advances in disciplinary silos. This study conducts a configurative systematic review of \_E\_-AI in higher education (2010-2025) across six major databases and grey literature, guided by PRISMA. Findings reveal three historical research waves and four dominant lenses: technical, pedagogical, theoretical, and ethical. While pedagogical applications dominate, theory integration and ethical safeguards lag behind. We propose a maturity framework positioning \_E\_-AI as a socio-technical system that must be theory-anchored, pedagogy-first, bias-aware, and ethically governed.
