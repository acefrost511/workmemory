## Abstract
Teacher competencies encompass the dimensions of knowledge and attitudes, yet few studies have analysed teachers' latent profiles in relation to the interaction between these two dimensions. [Latent profile analysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/psychology/latent-profile-analysis), which examines the interplay between variables to distinguish subgroups of individuals, has recently emerged in studies of computational thinking (CT) as a method for identifying student profiles. However, the latent profiles of CT teachers have not yet been analysed. We therefore analysed the latent profiles of a sample of 493 teachers who had participated in a [teacher development](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/social-sciences/teacher-professional-development) programme in CT, according to their knowledge and perceived usefulness of CT. Three profiles were identified: (1) highly knowledgeable with high perceived usefulness, (2) knowledgeable with low perceived usefulness, and (3) less knowledgeable with low perceived usefulness. Qualitative data further triangulated these findings. We also examined the learning achievement of 12,105 students taught by these teachers. Students taught by highly knowledgeable teachers with a high perceived sense of usefulness demonstrated significantly better achievement than their peers. Our findings highlight the importance of nurturing both knowledge of and perceived usefulness in CT. They also provide valuable insights for similar teacher development programmes, emphasizing the importance of peer mentoring support for less knowledgeable teachers. Additionally, we underscore the value of having teachers create artefacts using CT for instructional purposes, allowing them to gain firsthand experience of its usefulness. As few studies have explored teachers' latent profiles based on the dimensions of knowledge and attitudes, the contribution of this study extends beyond the domain of CT.
## Introduction
Teachers are key to the success of various educational initiatives (Datnow, 2020; Kong et al., 2023). According to the framework proposed by Blömeke and Delaney (2012), teacher competencies have two fundamental dimensions, one focusing on knowledge and the other on affect, beliefs and attitudes. However, little research has been conducted on teachers' latent profiles between these two dimensions (Holzberger et al., 2021). A latent profile analysis (LPA) takes a person-centred approach, which, in contrast to a variable-centred approach such as a regression analysis, can take into consideration the interplay between variables in identifying subgroups of individuals (Woo et al., 2024). For example, while a variable-centred approach can be used to examine the relationship between teachers' content knowledge and students' achievement (e.g., Kong & Lai, 2023), it cannot tell us whether there are subgroups of teachers based on the configuration of their content knowledge and their perceptions of the usefulness of a domain and how that configuration relates to students’ learning outcomes.
To teach computational thinking (CT), an ability that has been identified as essential for the next generations (Wing, 2006), requires capacity building among teachers (Kong et al., 2020). While studies have examined the effectiveness of teacher development programmes in enhancing teachers' understanding of and attitudes towards CT (for reviews, see Mason & Rich, 2019; Ni et al., 2023), none have analysed the latent profiles of teachers who have completed such programmes and how these profiles relate to the learning outcomes of students. Despite the popularity of LPA in recent years (Woo et al., 2024) and its application in education studies in various domains (e.g., Dignath, 2021; Holmes & Schumacker, 2020; Huang et al., 2024), LPA of CT teachers remains unexplored, although researchers have begun to use this approach to understand the latent profiles of CT students (e.g., Guggemos et al., 2022; Uslu, 2023). A possible reason is that LPA requires a large sample size (Spurk et al., 2020). In this study, with the participation of around 500 primary school teachers in a teacher development programme in CT in Hong Kong, we analysed teachers' latent profiles based on their domain knowledge and perceived usefulness of CT. We then examined how these teacher profiles related to students’ learning achievement.
