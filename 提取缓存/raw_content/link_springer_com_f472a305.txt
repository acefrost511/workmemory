Abstract
--------

This study investigates the impact of artificial intelligence (AI) designed study materials on student learning experience. The objective is to measure engagement, explore the potential for acquiring digital skills, and assessing possibilities of using AI-enabled study materials to enhance student engagement across different personality groups. Thus, it seeks to present evidence to support arguments regarding innovative applications of digital pedagogy to improve student engagement. Design/methodology/approach: Tripartite framework grounded in contemporary literature evaluates student engagement and satisfaction and investigates the moderating role of learning style and dogmatism in the process. Survey data is collected from undergraduate business students at an Australian university who utilized AI designed study materials for learning. Data analysis employed structural equation modelling using SmartPLS. Findings: AI-designed interactive study contents engaged students at the cognitive, affective, and behavioural levels. Students’ different engagement level positively influenced their satisfaction; however, contrary to the previous research, results show that learning style and dogmatism do not affect engagement or satisfaction. This indicates that academics should acquire expertise and required training to develop innovative materials capable of mitigating the negative impact of personality traits on the learning experience. Originality: The study brings novelty by incorporating instructor-created AI-designed content and adding psychological and personality moderators to measure engagement, thus enriching the explanatory power of the Tripartite model. This study also enriches the technology acceptance model (TAM) theory, suggesting a mitigated effect of personality differences on accepting AI-designed innovative content. The study is a fresh call for educators to enhance digital skills through training.

1 Introduction
--------------

Digital pedagogy plays a crucial role in modern higher education by leveraging the affordances of digital tools to develop innovative instructional content. This aims to enhance student engagement and support diverse learning needs that cater to the evolving demands of contemporary learners (Zhu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR69)). Emergence of generative artificial intelligence (AI) tools presents promising opportunities for educators to develop learning contents to implement digital and multimedia learning theories (Hossain, Amin & Hossain, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR31); Almusaed et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR4)). AI contents are created through tools like ChatGPT, Di-Di, Leonardo AI etc., and presents immense potential to enrich study materials with audio-visual effects capable of enhancing student engagement level (Riaz & Mushtaq, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR50)). However, successful integration of this technology is still facing challenges due to educators’ time constraints (Alenezi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR3)), and knowledge (Tan, Voogt &Tan, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR60)). Scepticism is also slowing the adoption of these tools (Alsobeh & Woodward, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR5)) due to a lack of clear evidence about their potential benefits. While the effect of digital pedagogy is quite well-known, learning content developed through multiple AI tools by instructors and its impact on engagement and satisfaction is relatively unexplored. This article investigates how student engagement with AI-generated multimedia teaching resources in an undergraduate marketing subject impacts student satisfaction. This will further assess the need for essential skill development to design content introducing new learning modalities across a diverse student population for positive student engagement.

### 1.1 Theoretical Framework

The current paper stresses the importance of learning and teaching theoretical underpinnings in developing educational content. Discussion in the subsequent section encompasses digital pedagogy, its implementation through various tools, and impact on student engagement.

### 1.2 Digital Pedagogy

Digital pedagogy involves the thoughtful and strategic integration of digital technologies into educational practices, aligning with educational goals to enhance the overall teaching and learning experience (Tan, Voogt &Tan, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR60)). It focuses on utilising digital tools to foster student engagement (Nkomo et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR42)). Digital pedagogy allows for the integration of various online and in-class multimedia resources; however, creation of such teaching resources remains as a challenge. Many lack access to the necessary technology and tools required to produce high-quality materials (Ray & Sikdar, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR49)), while others struggle with digital literacy due to insufficient training and expertise in mastering these technologies (Rapanta et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR48)). The process of designing and developing digital resources can be prohibitively time-consuming. This limits their ability to invest the necessary effort into learning how to create these digital resources and integrate them in a pedagogically sound and relevant manner (Ray & Sikdar, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR49)). The need for effective digital pedagogy has become more pronounced in the post-pandemic educational landscape, as educators strive to adapt to students’ expectations for new learning modalities and ensure that educational practices remain relevant and effective (Rapanta et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR48)). This creates an avenue for educators to utilize AI tools adding a new dimension to the digital pedagogy.

### 1.3 AI Tools and Student Engagement

The growing body of literature explores the various applications of AI in teaching and learning, ranging from personalised learning experiences (Riaz & Mushtaq, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR50)), intelligent tutoring systems (Farrokhnia et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR21)), automated grading and assessment (Zawacki-Richter et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR67)), chatbots to answer students’ questions (Almusaed et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR4)), learning analytics, administrative tasks (Hashem et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR29)), and content creation (Riaz & Mushtaq, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR50)). With the increasing capabilities of generative AI, a diverse range of powerful tools has emerged that educators are integrating into their teaching practices. Tools like ChatGPT, Gemini, ElevenLabs, Dall-E, and Midjourney have enabled educators to develop educational content through illustrations and visual aids (Hossain, Amin & Hossain, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR31)).

Student engagement refers to students’ active participation and investment in their learning process, characterised by three interrelated dimensions: cognitive, emotional (or affective) and behavioural engagement. This engagement significantly impacts the achievement of learning outcomes, as engaged students interact more with learning content. The cognitive dimension refers to the psychological investment students make in their learning activities, including the extent to which they are intellectually involved in their learning. This is exhibited when students can see the value of what they are learning and demonstrate a desire to master and learn the content. Higher levels of cognitive engagement are associated with better academic performance and deeper learning. The emotional, or affective, dimension relates to the emotional reactions students have towards their learning experience, including feelings of interest, enjoyment, boredom, or anxiety, and belonging in the learning setting. Positive emotional responses are vital for fostering a supportive learning environment in which students feel emotionally connected to their learning and are more likely to participate. The behavioural dimension encompasses the observable actions of students during the learning process, including participation in learning activities, attendance, and completion of assignments. This reflects the effort and persistence students demonstrate in their learning. Positive behaviours, such as active participation, are essential for creating a positive learning environment and are positively linked to higher academic achievement (Nkomo et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR42)) (Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#Fig1)).

At this early stage in generative AI development, however, there are mixed opinions regarding its implementation in educational settings; that is, having students directly engage with generative AI tools presents potential challenges and pedagogical risks. Students’ reliance on generative AI can impair the development of academic skills, bypassing the cognitive processes crucial for critical thinking development. This superficial engagement with course material can lead to reduced comprehension and retention (Alsobeh & Woodward, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR5)). On the other hand, according to TAM theory, perceived utility and enhanced interactivity of novel study materials could facilitate acceptance (Davis, [1989](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR16)) and active participation with it (Dixson, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR19)). However, it is not clear whether students will embrace AI-designed materials, given its usefulness, ease of use, and positive learning features have not fully explored and understood (Dahri et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR15)). For these, among other reasons, many educators and institutions are currently hesitant to use generative AI outputs in educational settings in an unsupervised manner.

However, little is known about how student engagement with AI-generated pedagogical content impacts overall student satisfaction with their learning experiences. The authors of this paper assert that AI tools can be used by educators to assist them in creating multimedia content for use in a digital pedagogical context to enhance student engagement. Digital pedagogical content can significantly enhance student engagement through interactive and personalised learning experiences that cater to diverse learning preferences (Zhu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR69)), leading to better motivation and academic performance ( Alenezi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR3)). To substantiate this claim, AI-designed study materials were introduced in a recent undergraduate marketing subject first, through creating a video script by ChatGPT (<https://www.chatgpt.com>). The educator then used D-ID (<https://www.d-id.com>) to generate a video of a virtual human presenting a topic, curated in Canva (<https://www.canva.com>) and embedded it in the learning management system. The LMS platform also included an interactive H5P (<https://h5p.org>) study material with visual elements, animation, and text descriptions created by ChatGPT, InVideo AI (<https://www.invideo.io>), Canva, and Microsoft Bing’s Image Creator (<https://www.bing.com/images/create>). Noting that educators often lack the time and expertise to create high quality content, and a gap exists in understanding how students engage with educator-created multimedia content (Ray & Sikdar, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR49)), the objective was to explore whether there is any opportunity for utilising these newly designed study materials in higher education.

### 1.4 Conceptual Framework and Hypothesis Development

This study uses the three-dimensional framework of student engagement proposed by Furlong et al. ([2003](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR26)) the seminal paper Fredricks et al. ([2004](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR25)). This tripartite framework is widely used in research, as it captures the multifaceted nature of student engagement (Wong & Liem, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR65)), reflecting the various ways students can be involved in their learning. The framework has been supported by extensive empirical research, including studies that validate the measurement instruments used to assess each dimension of engagement.

Research has shown that higher levels of engagement in these three dimensions positively correlate with academic performance, retention rates, and overall student satisfaction (Nkomo et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR42)). The three-dimensional framework of student engagement is employed in this research to assess the effect on student engagement of the incorporation of AI-generated multimedia content in an undergraduate Marketing subject.

Emotional, or affective, engagement refers to the emotional responses students experience in relation to their learning environment – this includes both positive and negative feelings such as interest, enjoyment, anxiety, or boredom (Wong & Liem, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR65)). Emotional engagement is crucial as it positively influences students’ motivation and persistence towards completing learning activities; when students feel emotionally connected to their learning, they are more willing to actively participate and invest effort towards their learning. Research shows that positive emotional engagement leads to enhanced academic performance, increased motivation and a sense of belonging among students, whereas negative emotional engagement (i.e., experience of negative emotions) can hinder academic performance and lead to disengagement (Nkomo et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR42)). Therefore, we hypothesise:

### Hypothesis (1):

Positive affective/emotional engagement with AI-generated content will enhance students’ overall satisfaction with the subject.

Behavioural engagement refers to the notion of participation; it includes students’ positive conduct (Fredricks et al., [2004](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR25)) concentration, persistence, attention and overall involvement in learning activities (Wong & Liem, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR65)), which are critical for fostering a positive learning atmosphere. Behavioural engagement is a crucial component that incorporates students’ participation in learning activities, for example, actively participating in classroom discussions or engaging in interactive tasks (Nkomo et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR42)). Research shows that when students are actively participating in their learning, they are more likely to develop a deeper understanding of the subject matter which leads to improved academic performance and retention of knowledge (Riaz & Mushtaq, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR50)).

The authors hypothesize that AI designed activity due to its interactive features may contribute to enhanced behavioural engagement and elevated satisfaction level.

### Hypothesis (2):

Behavioural engagement with AI-generated content will influence overall student satisfaction in the subject.

Cognitive engagement refers to the psychological investment that students make in their learning activities, and it involves self-regulation, the use of strategies for learning (Wong & Liem, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR65)), and the psychological investment made towards learning activities. This is exhibited when students see the value in what they’re learning and demonstrate a desire to learn and achieve mastery of the subject matter. Research shows that cognitive engagement is associated with self-regulated learning and the willingness to tackle intellectual challenges, making it a critical component that needs to be measured in any teaching and learning initiative (Nkomo et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR42)). This indicates that by fostering cognitive engagement, educators can promote deeper learning and overall interest and involvement in the subject material, ultimately leading to improved academic performance. We hypothesise that the interactivity of AI-designed materials featuring a critical reasoning opportunity may enhance engagement and satisfaction.

### Hypothesis (3):

Cognitive engagement with AI-generated content will improve overall satisfaction in the subject.

Although three elements of student engagement are interrelated and collectively contribute to overall student engagement, they are distinct and should be treated separately. Behavioural engagement may not correspond with cognitive or emotional engagement, and vice versa: separate treatment as a first-order construct (Wang et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR61)) may reveal nuances of different engagement levels on the satisfaction process (Fredricks et al., [2004](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR25)). The proposed conceptual model is illustrated in Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#Fig2). This comprises the three-dimensional framework of student engagement, in which behavioural, cognitive and emotional engagement lead to overall student satisfaction with their learning experience.

### 1.5 Learning Style and Dogmatism as Moderators

Individual traits can play a role in how students engage with learning content. This study also conceptualised the moderating effect of learning style and dogmatism between engagement and student satisfaction.

Learning style is a student’s preferred way of processing information. According to Kolb’s ([1984](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR34)) experiential learning theory, learning occurs through concrete experience, reflective observation, abstract conceptualisation and active experimentation. A learning style may influence how effectively a student engages with content. With an in-depth reading style, learners engage in detailed, comprehensive reading to deeply understand concepts, contexts, and reasoning. This learning style is aligned with Kolb’s learning style “assimilator”. On the other hand, convergers, who focus on scanning for summaries, headings, and key takeaways, are efficient for application-oriented tasks (Kolb, [1984](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR34)). Study also found that learning style influences satisfaction (Baherimoghadam et. al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR7)). So, it can be hypothesised that when students’ learning styles are different, they may prefer different learning content, which may influence their overall satisfaction. We hypothesise:

### H1a:

Learning style moderates the relationship between student affective engagement and student satisfaction.

### H2a:

Learning style moderates the relationship between student behavioural engagement and student satisfaction.

### H3a:

Learning style moderates the relationship between student cognitive engagement and student satisfaction.

Dogmatism refers to a specific form of intellectual closed-mindedness, characterised by rigid thinking, resistance to new information, and reluctance to consider alternative perspectives (Ferrando et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR23)). This form of cognitive inflexibility directly affects a learner’s ability to meaningfully engage with unfamiliar or complex content (Battaly, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR9)), resulting in reduced satisfaction with the learning experience. TAM literature also explains that a dogmatic individual may resist change (Ehrlich & Lee, [1969](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR20)) due to their own beliefs (Silva, [2007](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR56)) and a rigid, authoritarian mindset (Rokeach, [1960](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR53)). Close-minded individuals prefer a certain mode of communication and assess innovation compatibility with the existing belief to accept a change (Ehrlich & Lee, [1969](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR20)). Research supports that the positive effect of engagement on satisfaction is weaker for students with high dogmatism (Polczynski & Shirland, [1981](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR47)). Hence, personal traits as an auxiliary variable may improve and deepen our understanding of engagement as a core behaviour (Silva, [2007](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR56)). The study thus adds dogmatism (high vs low) to enhance the explanatory power of the conceptual framework.

Therefore, it can be hypothesised that:

### H1b:

Dogmatism moderates the relationship between student affective engagement and student satisfaction.

### H2b:

Dogmatism moderates the relationship between student behavioural engagement and student satisfaction.

### H3b:

Dogmatism moderates the relationship between student cognitive engagement and student satisfaction.

2 Research Methods
------------------

### 2.1 Data Collection & Sample

The study aimed to investigate the engagement of students with study materials in an Australian university. Data thus represents opinions about interactivity resulting from rich audio-visual effects, animation, instant feedback, and gaming properties of activities, and does not include perceptions about traditional static and information-heavy LMS-based materials. The sample included a total of 253 students who enrolled in this subject in Spring 2024 and Autumn 2025 semesters across various campuses. To avoid any potential bias, library staff notified students and collected data through an online survey, without offering any incentive.

### 2.2 Sample Characteristics

Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#Tab1) shows the demographic characteristics of the sample, suggesting a higher female participation rate (59%) compared to males (41%). Around 56.5% of students fall within the 0–20 years age category, while the rest is distributed throughout the other four categories, as shown in the table. The majority of students in the sample, totalling 56.5%, are in their first year of study. The remaining 50 students (19.8%) are in the second year, 47 students (18.6%) are in the third year, and 13 students (5.1%) are in the final year. Participation of domestic students (71.9%) in the survey exceeds that of international students (28.1%), which is in line with the actual ratio of this demographic within the total university population. Approximately 65% of students are employed part-time, 9.5% are employed full-time, and 19.8% are not employed. The survey included questions about students’ learning style, and Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#Tab1) shows that 47.4% engage in an in-depth study of concepts, while 52.6% concentrate on main topics. A query aimed at assessing the degree to which students display features of dogmatism reveals that 41% of students identify as highly dogmatic, whilst 31% exhibit low levels of dogmatic traits.

### 2.3 Constructs and Measurements

The variables in the current study were operationalised according to the study and measurements established by Lam et al. ([2014](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR35)). The study used previously validated items to measure engagement and satisfaction. However, items were adapted to reflect experience gained from the use of AI-generated content. A comprehensive list of items for each variable and sources is included in Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#Tab2). All items in the questionnaire were found to be reliable and valid. Twenty (20) questionnaires contained incomplete responses, resulting in 253 completed surveys used for final data analysis. Skewness and kurtosis ranged from − 0.107 to + 0 .116 and − 0.132 to 1.82, respectively, remaining acceptable for analysis using SmartPLS (Hair et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR28)). No missing values were found in the completed 253 surveys.

3 Results and Findings
----------------------

### 3.1 Measurement Model

Smart PLS Version 4.1.1.2 was used to evaluate to fit of both the measurement model and the structural model. Items for all constructs included reflective indicators, and assessment of the measurement model shows outer loadings for each construct are all  > 0.70 and statistically significant (Table [2](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#Tab2)). Table [3](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#Tab3) demonstrates construct and convergent validity. Average variance extracted (AVE) values are all  > 0.50, establishing convergent validity. Composite reliability and Cronbach’s alpha are both > 0.828 and are within the acceptable limit. Heterotrait-Monotrait (HTMT) ratio and Fornell-Larcker criterion were used to assess discriminant validity. HTMT values in Table [3](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#Tab3) are  < 0.9 (Table [3](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#Tab3)). Cross-loading analysis indicates that items for the same construct only loaded highly with other items of the same construct, and no significant overlap is evidenced between items of different constructs (Hair et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR28)). Table [4](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#Tab4) shows square root of AVEs for each construct is higher than its correlation with any other constructs satisfying the Fornell-Larcker criterion (Fornell & Larcker, [1981](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR24); Hair et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR28)). Standardised root mean square (SRMR) 0.047, and normed fit index (NFI) 0.908 shows adequate model fit (Hair et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR28)).

No multicollinearity issues were identified in the inner model, as Table [5](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#Tab5) shows all Variance Inflation factor (VIF) values are less than the threshold of 3.30 (Hair et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR28)). Common latent factor analysis shows that the Chi-square Minimum (CMIN) difference between the latent and the main model is less than 3.84 (1degree of freedom; p = 0.05), indicating no presence of common method bias (Richardson et al., [2009](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR51)). Harman’s one-factor test did not account for the majority of the variance (Lederer et al., [2000](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR36)).

### 3.2 Structural Model: Hypothesis Testing

Findings from structural path analysis are presented in Table [6](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#Tab6). AI-designed tutorial materials in this context engage students emotionally, and that, in turn, influences their satisfaction level. The relationship is supported as shown in Table [6](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#Tab6) with (β = 0.303; t-statistic = 4.652). Similarly, it is also evidenced that the newly designed teaching materials influence students to actively complete the task and repeat the task over time to ensure more efficient learning. The hypothesised relationship between behavioural engagement and satisfaction level is thus supported (β = 0.293; t-statistic = 3.299). In addition, findings reveal that students are also engaged with critical thinking exercises and take an active interest in completing tutorial activities, which in turn influence their satisfaction. Hence, cognitive engagement significantly influenced satisfaction level (β = 0.356; t-statistic = 4.328). Findings are consistency with previous studies (Baidoo-Anu & Ansah, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR8); Liang et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR38); Masruddin et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR40)), however, shows differences in the strength of the engagement levels The effect of cognitive engagement is the highest (β = 0.356), followed by affective engagement (β = 0.303), and behavioural engagement with the weakest effect (β = 0.293). In addition, results also deviated significantly from studies where either behavioural (Sun et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR59)), cognitive (Lewis et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR37)), or affective engagement (Guo et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR27)) played a dominant role, or no influence of engagement was evidenced (Pelletier et al., [2013](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR17)). Explanations for these differences will be discussed in the preceding section.

4 Discussion and Conclusion
---------------------------

Findings of this study align well with the theoretical underpinnings of Tripartite theory and have been demonstrated through similar studies such as Wang et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR63)). Study materials in this context may be perceived as enjoyable, engaging and interactive, hence fostering interactivity through active participation (Mun & Hwang, [2003](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR41)). Students thus perceived interactivity as incentives and actively engaged with these learning materials (Jovanovic & Matejevec, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR32)). According to the TAM theory (Al-Adwan et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR1)), it is reasonable to conclude that novelty, usefulness, ease of use and enjoyable learning experience actively influenced student participation.

Furthermore, activities incorporated real-life scenarios (Sugden et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR58)), an opportunity to apply prior knowledge (Schindler et. al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR54)) and critical thinking exercises (Winkler et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR64)), which may have helped students learn through a new context. Students perceived AI-designed study materials as useful (Wang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR63)), felt curious and became enthusiastic (Obaid, Alnaqbi & Omar, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR43)), and engaged in deep thinking (Chittaro & Ranon, [2007](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR12)) through a social learning process (Okazaki & Renda dos Santos, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR44)). Thus, the critical thinking opportunities (Siemens & Long, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR55)) and positive emotion (Derry, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR17)) led students to exert considerable effort, maintain consistency, and concentrate on interactive activities with elevated engagement and participation level. However, the findings present a sharp contrast with studies where positive cognitive and emotional engagement accompanied by negative behavioural engagement (Sun et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR59)); learners may not exhibit repeat behaviour due to time pressure or boring and non-rewarding tasks (Guo et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR27)). Interesting features of activities in this context may have mitigated such effects and contributed to persistent behaviour through cognitive stimulation and emotional appeal. Equal engagement at all three levels also contradicts findings where cognitive (Lewis et al., [2011](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR37)) and behavioural engagement (Xia et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR66)) played a dominant role, or engagement showed no role in influencing student satisfaction (Pelletier et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR45)). If context is a possible explanation for these discrepancies (Pelletier et al., 2023; Guo et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR27)), this study sets an ideal example of an AI-driven study environment to enhance students’ engagement levels. The environment grasps learners’ attention through deep thinking and positive feelings, resulting in a technology-mediated, enjoyable learning experience.

The findings add further insights suggesting that audio-visual elements aid in understanding relevant concepts and enhance engagement level through a positive learning experience (Di Paolo et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR18)). Literature indicates that interactivity in study materials fosters persistence, results in concentrated attention (Kokoç, IIgaz, & Altun, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR33)) and repeated attempts until a solution is found for any challenge encountered. According to Lukosch and Schümmer ([2006](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR39)), interactive materials may enhance confidence and help students prepare for exams, which may form the very basis of findings from this study. Therefore, it is reasonable to conclude that students positively perceived AI-designed interactive study materials, which in turn impacted the level of involvement and satisfaction.

However, the moderating effect of learning style and dogmatic personality on the engagement and satisfaction level is not evidenced in the current study. While evidence suggests that engagement level with study materials may depend on reading style, such as in-depth vs. key points, this style may only reflect the choice of preferred behaviour unique to an individual and does not necessarily lead to engagement deficiencies (Phillips & Phillips, [2007](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR46)). The findings in this case are consistent with those of Wang and Liu ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR62)). Similarly, while a dogmatic attitude may negatively affect interest in innovative teaching materials (Brown & Prudente, [2012](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR10)), relevance, playfulness, and information-rich features in this context, through positive interest, may have enhanced engagement. The study demonstrated equal engagement level across different personality groups despite differences in individual beliefs, perceptions, relevance, and interest levels (Connell & Wellborn, [1991](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR14)). Thus, these study materials incorporating both auditory and visual learning elements promoted equitable learning, removing predispositional barriers of diverse learner groups. This fulfils the core expectations of universal learning design, promoting multiple means of engagement, representations and expressions (Slattery, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR57)) and achieves inclusivity without disadvantaging any subgroup.

### 4.1 Practical Implications

From a practical perspective, the study underscores the importance of integrating multiple AI tools to design study materials that incorporate audio-visual content, feedback mechanisms, and simulated case studies to enhance student engagement. It presents evidence explaining how AI can assist in crafting customised elements by generating content based on specific parameters, thereby saving educators time and enhancing the relevance of the materials. Providing training to use AI-powered skills may help academics transform the way educators develop and deliver course materials, using tools that can significantly enhance student engagement. By leveraging these technologies, academics can create dynamic, interactive learning experiences that cater to diverse learning styles and preferences. In addition, academics can learn why interactive elements can be incorporated using tools like H5P to encourage students to engage directly with the material, thereby improving retention and comprehension. Acquiring these skills not only enhances the quality of their teaching materials but also addresses engagement challenges, particularly in educational contexts that have yet to fully integrate technology into their teaching practices. Embracing these innovations empowers educators to create more inclusive and effective learning environments, ultimately leading to improved student outcomes.

### 4.2 Theoretical Contributions

This study enriches Tripartite learning theory, expanding the scope of digital pedagogy through innovative technology and showing evidence to reduce the gap between different engagement levels. Heightened attention and active participation among diverse learner groups add a new insight explaining that task characteristics, in this context, innovative materials may reduce engagement differences between three levels (Anghel, Wry & Davier, [2025](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR6)). Consistent engagement regardless of personality and learning characteristics also highlights the achievement of technology to promote an inclusive learning environment through Tripartite learning experience, contrasting with previous findings showing differences in engagement due to personality factors (Zhang et. al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR68)). Within this new context of technology and psychological moderators, findings also add robustness to the TAM theory showing evidence for academics to improve digital literacy (Ray & Sikdar, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR49)) to address perceived difficulties (Fathema, Shannon & Ross, [2015](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR22)) and strengthening the argument that innovative materials may reduce student’s perceptual differences and enhance perceived usefulness (Cheung et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09952-5#ref-CR11)) regardless of individual differences.

### 4.3 Limitations and Future Research

Novelty-seeking behaviour and learning style could vary due to personality traits associated with different types of students, hence requiring diversified data from various universities for future study. Also, as the current model does not show any impact of moderating variables, this could be due to the absence of other more relevant personality traits. It could be interesting to relate learning style and receptiveness with the Big Five theory and introduce them as moderators to capture further insights about engagement and satisfaction. Learning style may also vary with intellectual capacity; hence, criteria such as GPA could be applied to collect data accurately representing different groups, thereby establishing strong support for current findings. It would also be interesting to evaluate attitude towards these contents from a universal design principle perspective and investigate if engagement, as a second-order construct, plays any mediating role between content design and satisfaction.
