## Abstract
With the increasing application of generative AI tools, such as ChatGPT, many schools have developed school-based policies to regulate teachers' and students' use of these technologies. However, empirical evidence on the effectiveness of such policies remains limited. This study examines the relationship between school-based ChatGPT policies and students' computer and information literacy (CIL), and whether these policies moderate CIL disparities across students' gender, socioeconomic status (SES), and immigration background. The data come from International Computer and Information Literacy Study (ICILS) 2023, where school-based ChatGPT policies are distinguished for teachers and for students, each with three categories: unrestricted use, restricted use, and not allowed. Employing cross-level interaction models with random slopes, three findings emerge: (1) female, higher-SES, and non-immigrant students exhibit higher levels of CIL after controlling for student- and school-level covariates, including school-based ChatGPT policies; (2) policies permitting students' unrestricted use are negatively associated with CIL, whereas other policies for students and teachers show no significant direct relationship with CIL; (3) moderation is observed. Policies allowing teachers' use, whether unrestricted or restricted, narrow SES-related CIL gaps, while those permitting students’ unrestricted use exacerbate digital gender divide. These results highlight the need for well-designed and institution-supported AI use policies to promote equitable digital competence development in schools and to prevent the widening of existing digital divides.
## Introduction
The emergence of generative AI tools such as ChatGPT, renowned for their capabilities in natural language processing, adaptive responses, and real-time interaction, has drawn considerable attention for their potential in education and is reshaping school practices (Castonguay et al., 2023; Chiu, 2024; Copur-Gencturk et al., 2024; Zhang & Tur, 2024). Meanwhile, their widespread adoption has raised concerns regarding over-reliance, misinformation, and violations of academic integrity (Farrokhnia et al., 2024; Kasneci et al., 2023; Naamati-Schneider & Alt, 2024). In response, scholars underscore the importance of school-based AI policies to promote responsible and ethical use and offers conceptual frameworks and general recommendations from a school perspective (Chan, 2023; Chiu, 2024; Farrokhnia et al., 2024; Hadi Mogavi et al., 2024; Zhang & Tur, 2024). Many schools, based on their own culture and climate, are formulating school-based AI-related policies to support teachers and students in navigating the opportunities and challenges brought by this rapidly evolving technology. Yet empirical evidence on the associations between the emerging policies and student learning and development remains scarce, particularly large-scale quantitative studies. This gap constrains our understanding of the effectiveness of such policies and limits the design of evidence-based strategies for integrating generative AI into education.
Among the various student outcomes of interest, digital competence has sparked growing interest in the AI era, as it is now widely recognized as essential for both academic success and modern citizenship (Muammar et al., 2025; Pagani et al., 2016; Sezer, 2020; Zou et al., 2021). One important component of digital competence is computer and information literacy (CIL), defined as “an individual's ability to use computers to investigate, create, and communicate in order to participate effectively at home, at school, in the workplace, and in society” (Fraillon & Duckworth, 2025, p. 26). This literacy is regarded as foundational for effective participation in the digital age (Fraillon et al., 2014; Schulz et al., 2020) and as a key element of 21st-century skills (Binkley et al., 2012). However, persistent digital divides in CIL have been found. Prior studies consistently document significant disparities in CIL across student background characteristics such as gender (Gebhardt et al., 2019; Gnambs, 2021; Punter et al., 2017), socioeconomic status (SES) (Aydin, 2022; Heldt et al., 2020), and immigration background (Fraillon, 2024; Heldt et al., 2020). These inequities highlight the need for sustained attention and systematic approaches, such as school-based policies, to reduce gaps in digital skills and ensure more equitable opportunities for all students.
To address these gaps, the present study draws on data from the International Computer and Information Literacy Study (ICILS) 2023 to examine the relationship between school-based ChatGPT policies and student CIL performance. Employing cross-level interaction models with random slopes, the study investigates not only the direct association between ChatGPT policies and CIL but also the moderating role of such policies in shaping the relationship between student background characteristics (gender, SES, immigration status) and CIL. By examining school-based ChatGPT policies as a representative example of generative AI tools, this research provides early empirical insights into the significance of school-based AI policies in fostering student digital skills and promoting digital equity, contributing to ongoing discussions on the responsible and effective integration of generative AI into school systems.
