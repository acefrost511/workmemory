## Definition
This entry examines the growing role of artificial intelligence (AI) in special education. The authors discuss applications of AI in the field, including its uses for personalized learning, adaptive technologies, teacher support, and AI’s potential to address issues related to student accessibility and engagement. The entry draws on recent syntheses of literature, highlighting studies that reveal AI’s capacity to improve educational outcomes for students with disabilities, mitigate teacher workload, and foster inclusion. Despite these promising developments, the authors address ethical considerations, potential biases, and privacy concerns surrounding the use of AI, as well as the need for high-quality research that validates AI’s effectiveness in special education. The authors conclude that while AI can offer substantial support, it should be integrated thoughtfully, guided by empirical research, and accompanied by skilled professional oversight to ensure that it truly benefits students with disabilities.

## 1. Introduction
The purpose of special education is to provide specially designed instruction to individuals with disabilities. This involves ensuring that young children, school-aged children, and young adults have access to the supports and services that meet their unique needs and prepares them for further education, employment, and/or independent living [[1](#B1-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. Special education and related services are designed to provide students with equitable access to instruction, often through differentiating and personalizing learning opportunities and curricula through early intervention, instructional and behavioral accommodations, or modifications based on a student’s strengths and needs and coordinated resources which prepare a young person with a disability for a successful transition from school to postsecondary life. For many students with disabilities, the use of instructional or assistive technologies can be advantageous for mitigating the impacts of a disability and for providing this equitable access.
Artificial intelligence (AI), a branch of computer science concerned with building technology capable of complex tasks that closely resemble human thought, has tremendous potential to enhance learning for students with disabilities and alleviate the teacher workload crisis in special education. Cognitive behaviors commonly cited in definitions of AI include natural language processing, reasoning, problem solving, realistic communication, and adapting to new circumstances by identifying and extrapolating patterns [[2](#B2-encyclopedia-05-00011)] (p. 2). Generative AI, a subset of AI, takes this potential even further by creating original material such as multimedia assets, computer code, and sophisticated writer or verbal responses [[3](#B3-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. While early, data-centric applications of AI in education, such as intelligent tutoring systems and learning analytics, demonstrated the promise of personalized instruction, the rapid evolution of Generative AI represents an unprecedented opportunity to transform teaching and learning, particularly in special education [[4](#B4-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. AI can address persistent challenges in special education including automating routine tasks, strengthening individualized education programs (IEPs), providing adaptive interventions for diverse learner needs, and increasing teacher efficiencies. However, the field has yet to capitalize on these advancements.
Technologies traditionally designed to support students with disabilities have been referred to as assistive technologies (ATs). The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act [[5](#B5-encyclopedia-05-00011)] defines an AT device as “any item, piece of equipment, or product system, whether acquired commercially or off the shelf, modified, or customized, that is used to increase, maintain, or improve the functional capabilities of a child with a disability”. Over time, the use of technologies to support students with disabilities has evolved given the advances in today’s mainstream tools and platforms, including the digital accessibility features embedded in common devices such as tablets, laptops, and smartphones [[6](#B6-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. This evolution allows practitioners to meet many of the needs of students with disabilities through universal tools, which reduces the stigma associated with using specialized equipment and offers increased portability for use in natural settings [[7](#B7-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. The emergence of AI now marks a major shift in this evolution. Traditional AT focuses on addressing specific functional needs (e.g., mobility, communication, or sensory support), while AI-based systems expand these capabilities to personalize as well as enhance learning for students through predictive assistance and real-time adaptation. More recently, generative AI has further enhanced this potential by facilitating the creation of dynamic content, natural language processing, and tailored support for diverse learning needs. The transition from AI to generative AI signifies a critical advancement in the field, offering new ways to empower students with disabilities and support their teachers.
## 2. Applications of Artificial Intelligence in Special Education
Across the field of education at large, AI has rapidly become a prominent area of discussion. The research relevant to AI in special education can best be summarized through several recent literature reviews. An examination of the literature reveals that many of these studies address the applications of AI within special education. For example, Chen et al. [[8](#B8-encyclopedia-05-00011)] conducted a large-scale review of all AI studies published over a 20-year period in education (between 2000 and 2019) and found that approximately 14% of all published literature was related to the use of AI in special education, namely tutoring and individualized learning in K-12 special education. In special education, there were two recently published literature reviews which described the themes in this scholarship. Hopcan et al. [[9](#B9-encyclopedia-05-00011)] conducted a systematic review on the use of AI in special education between 2008 and 2020, examining 29 studies. Most focused on the integration of AI with students who had autism, followed by students with learning disabilities. According to the results of Hopcan et al.’s [[9](#B9-encyclopedia-05-00011)] review, most researchers focused on the use of AI to improve the individual skill development of students. Nearly half of the AI studies addressed the cognitive and affective domains of students with disabilities, while the remainder focused on AI’s capacity to improve students’ cognitive skills [[9](#B9-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. Similarly, Rice and Dunn [[10](#B10-encyclopedia-05-00011)] conducted a review, examining studies addressing the use of AI in special education from 2009 to 2022. Rice and Dunn [[10](#B10-encyclopedia-05-00011)] analyzed the study characteristics of 18 publications, examining how researchers positioned people with disabilities and their families. They found that the intention for using AI with this population ranged from supporting various domains of student development, improved efficiency for educational decision makers, increasing student and family engagement in interventions, and instrumentation.
There have been a growing number of positive research findings indicating the role that AI plays in advancing the education of students with disabilities. Rakap [[11](#B11-encyclopedia-05-00011)] broadly categorized the advantageous use of AI systems for students with disabilities into three areas of research and implementation: (a) enhancements for lesson planning and curriculum support; (b) individualization and differentiation in instruction, and (c) the provision of just-in-time professional development for educators. Recent literature provides several illustrative examples, highlighting the diverse ways in which AI contributes to the educational experiences of students with disabilities. For instance, Rakap and Balikci [[12](#B12-encyclopedia-05-00011)] used an experimental design to provide empirical evidence that the use of ChatGPT supports the development of high-quality IEP goals for preschool children with autism. ChatGPT was found to significantly improve the quality of IEP goals when compared to the goals written by teachers who did not use the technology. In another use, the integration of web-based training programs have been shown to increase the working memory capacity of students with autism spectrum and related developmental disorders [[13](#B13-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. Additionally, in recent years, the use of dynamic user interfaces with similar technologies have shown early results that provide a promising strategy to increase accessibility for individuals who are visually impaired [[14](#B14-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. AI-powered interventions have also been shown to improve the phonics skills among students with dyslexia [[15](#B15-encyclopedia-05-00011)], and more accurately diagnose students with reading disabilities such as dyslexia [[16](#B16-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. Furthermore, an AI-driven matching database was shown to be effective in helping reduce the unemployment rate of adults with disabilities [[17](#B17-encyclopedia-05-00011)].
AI has also been suggested as an intervention to reduce the workload of special education teachers. Goldman et al. [[18](#B18-encyclopedia-05-00011)] asserted that the nationwide shortage of special education teachers, shown in research to be tied to increasing workloads, might be mitigated by offering increased efficiencies through the thoughtful integration of AI. For example, special education teachers may be taught to utilize AI to develop IEP snapshots; to develop IEP summaries and other related resources that support parents and guardians; to create progress monitoring tools; or to modify curricula, such as adjusting the reading level of a text or offering additional passages so that students with disabilities can improve their reading fluency and comprehension [[18](#B18-encyclopedia-05-00011),[19](#B19-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. While the exploration of AI’s potential to support IEP development is still in its nascent stages, this area shows promise based on an initial study conducted by Rakap and Balikci [[12](#B12-encyclopedia-05-00011)] in the use of ChatGPT to improve the quality of IEP goals. Researchers and special education experts emphasize that, since generative AI algorithms still require further development, it is critical that a skilled professional be taught and have ample practice discerning the accuracy of AI-generated IEP content [[20](#B20-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. Since federal regulations in the United States currently state that only human members of an IEP Team can ensure that an IEP is designed to substantively match a student’s needs, highly skilled professionals are needed to make these ultimate determinations [[20](#B20-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. With that premise in mind, many experts in the field, including those included in journals published by the Council for Exceptional Children, are beginning to outline effective practitioner-oriented practices for using AI in IEP goal development, aligned with current statutes and widely accepted frameworks for IEP goal writing and development [[21](#B21-encyclopedia-05-00011)].
## 3. Benefits and Affordances
### 3.1. Problems of Practice in Special Education
The authors of this publication and others within the JHU Center for Technology in Education routinely work with state education agencies and local school districts to address problems of practice which arise in the field of special education. The most pressing of these problems are aligned with current published research in the field, including but not limited to increases in special education teacher workload, meeting both the technical and substantive compliance requirements of the IDEA, and decreasing the persistent achievement gap between students with disabilities and their general education peers. AI technologies offer potential for mitigating many of the risks posed by these problems, provided that leaders in the field develop strategic mechanisms for using these tools to their fullest potential while balancing their enthusiasm of AI’s benefits with the potential challenges.
### 3.2. Increased Accessibility
AI-driven technologies are breaking down access barriers that students with disabilities often face in education. Almufareh et al. [[22](#B22-encyclopedia-05-00011)] describe how artificial intelligence-driven linguistic technologies play a pivotal role in supporting students with communication disabilities. These innovations encompass tools like speech recognition software, text-to-speech systems, and language translation platforms. Speech recognition technologies enhance students’ ability to share thoughts and ideas while also supporting them to feel more connected and included in social settings. These authors highlight that AI can create tools such as 3D models, simulations, and virtual environments, enabling students with physical disabilities to actively engage in hands-on learning experiences that might otherwise be inaccessible.
However, the effectiveness of these AI-driven assistive technologies significantly depends on matching the right tool to the individual needs of each student, setting the appropriate parameters for technology use, and providing a whole-user approach [[23](#B23-encyclopedia-05-00011),[24](#B24-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. This holistic approach ensures that a technology used by a student is aligned with the student’s unique abilities, preferences, and learning context, maximizing its impact while fostering independence, confidence, and a more inclusive educational experience.
### 3.3. Enhanced Individualization
Personalized learning through AI represents a transformative approach to education, tailoring instructional experiences to meet the unique needs and preferences of each student [[9](#B9-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. At the core of this approach are adaptive learning systems, which are AI-driven platforms that dynamically adjust to students’ learning strategies, the sequence and complexity of tasks, the timing of feedback, and an individual’s preferences to optimize their educational outcomes [[25](#B25-encyclopedia-05-00011),[26](#B26-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. These systems provide a foundation for creating flexible and responsive educational experiences, which is especially valuable in diverse classroom environments. For instance, Waterfield et al. [[27](#B27-encyclopedia-05-00011)] highlight multiple AI platforms that are publicly available and equipped with features specifically designed to differentiate materials for students with disabilities, further emphasizing the potential of AI to address varied learning needs.
Building on this foundation, the integration of AI with universal design for learning (UDL) principles takes personalized learning a step further by fostering inclusion and autonomy. By combining the innovative potential of AI with UDL’s focus on diverse methods of representation, engagement, and expression, educators can reduce obstacles, improve accessibility, and create more inclusive environments that support the learning of all students [[28](#B28-encyclopedia-05-00011),[29](#B29-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. This synergy affords that AI not only addresses individual learning preferences but also aligns with the broader educational goals of equity and inclusion.
AI-driven intelligent tutoring systems (ITSs) significantly enhance personalized learning by leveraging technologies such as machine learning, natural language processing, and adaptive algorithms to deliver tailored, and adaptive instruction. These systems address challenges like diverse learning needs, limited resources, and the lack of individualized support often found in traditional educational settings [[3](#B3-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. ITSs are particularly advantageous for students with disabilities, as they provide consistent, data-driven feedback, and customized interventions that help close learning gaps and promote sustained skill development. By offering scalable and adaptable solutions, ITSs demonstrate the transformative potential of AI in meeting individualized instructional needs and addressing the unique challenges faced by students with disabilities.
### 3.4. Augmentation for Teachers
Goldman et al. [[18](#B18-encyclopedia-05-00011)] identified ways that AI supports both student-facing and teacher-facing tasks. For teacher-focused tasks, such as creating IEP snapshots to simplify complex IEPs, generative AI tools like ChatGPT can save time by generating templates or summarizing redacted content. When using any AI tool for these types of tasks, it is necessary for users to ensure compliance with privacy policies and statutes, particularly when the functions include personally identifiable data. Similarly, AI can be used to streamline the creation of parent resources, including resource lists, home strategies, and multilingual translations, reducing the workload and supporting diverse families [[18](#B18-encyclopedia-05-00011)].
For student-facing tasks, special education teachers often adapt complex texts, such as grade-level novels, to meet the decoding and comprehension needs of students with reading disabilities, and they are required to monitor students’ IEP goals through tailored assessments and data collection. These time-intensive processes can be significantly streamlined using generative AI, which can produce summaries, revised content at specific reading levels, and customized word lists, fluency passages, and comprehension questions. In all cases, it is necessary for a skilled professional to thoroughly review the generated materials to ensure accuracy and appropriateness.
Overall, AI systems can offer transformative advantages in special education by streamlining lesson planning, enabling individualized instruction, and providing on-demand professional development, empowering teachers by saving time and enhancing their instructional practices while addressing the diverse needs of students with disabilities [[11](#B11-encyclopedia-05-00011)].
## 4. Potential Challenges
While the substantial array of perceived benefits has led to increased interest in AI among special educators, widespread adoption has been tempered by a number of well-known risks and uncertainties, including ethical implications, the potential for algorithmic bias, and issues of privacy and data security.
### 4.1. Ethical Considerations
Ethical issues surrounding AI in special education primarily involve challenges related to accuracy, accountability, transparency, and consent, particularly as the output is used to inform critical decisions about students. An inaccurate or biased AI algorithm could unfairly influence the educational trajectory of a student. Furthermore, if the AI system produced incorrect or harmful recommendations, it would be unclear who is responsible—the developers, the educators using the system, or educational leaders who implemented the system.
Researchers highlight the importance of human oversight in AI, and that “teachers remain at the helm of major instructional decisions” [[30](#B30-encyclopedia-05-00011)] (p. 17). Transparency is a fundamental ethical consideration. Parents, guardians, and students must be informed when AI is used in decision-making processes to promote trust and communication around decisions made with AI assistance. Additionally, it is important to obtain explicit permission before leveraging AI-driven technologies, particularly in cases where students or parents and guardians have limited knowledge about AI and how it could be used to influence educational decisions [[31](#B31-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. Without clear consent, stakeholders might not fully understand how their children’s data are being collected, analyzed, or shared, leading to issues of safety and trust in the educational system.
### 4.2. Potential for Bias
The large language models that drive AI’s text generation functions are trained with large, public datasets that contain biases, including stereotypes and demographic under-representations [[32](#B32-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. Such biases can be present in both the data used to train the AI and the algorithmic model itself, disproportionately affecting certain groups and resulting in misleading or inaccurate outputs. Strategies to mitigate the impact of such biases include training to ensure that educators are aware of the issue of AI bias and training on AI prompt generation to help teachers refine and improve the quality of their input queries and commands [[21](#B21-encyclopedia-05-00011)].
### 4.3. Privacy and Data Security
Utilizing AI in special education often involves leveraging sensitive student data, including disability type, assessment results, student behaviors, and disability information. As with other technologies and systems that involve such data, security and privacy concerns are paramount. Student data entered into an AI system should be anonymized, with names and other personally identifiable information removed, while ensuring that students and parents or guardians are aware of how the data are being used to inform decisions about a student’s learning [[27](#B27-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. Establishing clear policies about the use of student data with AI can help ensure trust with stakeholders that any AI queries and recommendations align with the specific needs and goals of each student [[11](#B11-encyclopedia-05-00011)].
## 5. Future Directions
AI is currently being used in the field of special education to support the needs of students with disabilities and the professionals who serve as providers of their specially designed instruction. However, as the technology advances, educators and researchers alike must proceed with caution to ethically and judiciously evaluate how and to what extent AI can be leveraged to support learners with disabilities as well as their families and teachers. Like other technologies, AI is one possible tool that can be used to support a student with a range of diverse learning needs. However, generative AI provides tremendous potential for us to empower people with disabilities, offering effective forms of accessibility and even potentially acting as a “cognitive prosthesis” [[31](#B31-encyclopedia-05-00011)] (p. 406) beyond the borders of what we may have previously thought possible.
Given the possibilities inherent in AI, publications related to its use in special education emphasize that it is not a suitable replacement for a well-trained practitioner [[33](#B33-encyclopedia-05-00011)], nor should it be advertised as a solution designed to ‘fix’ students. When AI is used to remove the perceived burden of disability, this construct is inherently based on ableist assumptions, which operates from a deficits-based perspective. Rather, AI can be best leveraged when it is coupled with the expertise of a knowledgeable professional, particularly one who knows the unique strengths and challenges of their student with a disability. For this reason, it is critical that educators be trained to design effective AI queries; to evaluate responses through hands-on professional development with AI platforms; and to assess the efficacy and relevance of the content that has been generated.
For students with disabilities and the educators who serve them, it is critical that researchers, educators, and policy makers attend to the ways AI is changing how we teach and how students learn. Historically, the introduction of new tools, such as calculators, did not eliminate the need for students with disabilities to develop foundational skills [[31](#B31-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. Rather, calculators provided a pragmatic accommodation that enabled more students to easily access academic content and real-life applications. This allowed educators to concentrate on additional skills that required targeted instruction. In this way, AI is similar. It is not as though we want students to stop writing or teachers to stop lesson planning, but we have an opportunity to examine how AI can now augment these processes to improve the outcomes of more students with disabilities.
In educational research, it is critical that scholars conduct and publish interdisciplinary studies related to the development and use of AI technologies within populations of individuals with disabilities, including both school-age students and adults. This includes, as a start, partnerships between educators and computer scientists who focus on the development and refinement of large language models [[33](#B33-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. Finally, many initial or preliminary studies involving AI with individuals with disabilities must be replicated. Policymakers, researchers, and practitioners have historically argued in favor of increased quality and rigor in special education research and replication, or the expansion of pivotal studies as an effective approach to achieve this aim. However, over time, replication studies have continued to be rare in special education journals [[34](#B34-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. At its core, replication confirms that the results from any individual study are reproducible and not due merely to chance—a central tenet of trustworthy scientific results [[34](#B34-encyclopedia-05-00011),[35](#B35-encyclopedia-05-00011),[36](#B36-encyclopedia-05-00011)]. In special education, this allows us to confidently apply findings to practice and to identify evidence-based practices that benefit students with disabilities. AI is developing quickly, and the technology is proliferating rapidly; therefore, practitioners and policymakers need scientific evidence to know when and how to adopt it for use with students who have disabilities.
