Abstract
--------

How K-12 education will handle generative artificial intelligence (GAI) is dependent upon educators’ preparedness and support structures. This study explores educational professionals’ perceptions regarding GAI and their support needs. 452 educational professionals, representing all years levels and educational roles in K-12, participated in a professional development (PD) intervention focused on GAI in education. Drawing on this cross-sectional design we adopted a mixed-methods approach. Data from polls, surveys, and collegial reflections were analysed using descriptive statistics, thematic analysis and Transformative Learning Theory. One of the key findings revealed a notable gap between positive attitudes toward GAI and an actual confidence in using it: although only 31% had used AI chatbots, 88% were inclined to adopt them. These results signal simultaneous enthusiasm and hesitation, and emphasise the need for comprehensive PD and organisational support that extends beyond technical skills to advocate for responsible, sustainable and ethical GAI use. This study offers insights for policymakers aiming to achieve effective, whole-school GAI integration, highlighting how design and innovation perspectives can guide the process.

1 Introduction
--------------

Generative artificial intelligence (GAI) is challenging traditional pedagogical approaches, knowledge structures, power dynamics, teacher roles, and classroom interactions in K-12 education (Smith & Lee, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR56); Weng et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR65)). While not all educational technologies with algorithms are AI driven (Dignum, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR19)), GAI extends to AI-driven chatbots such as ChatGPT, Claude, Bing, Co-Pilot, and Gemini (see Labadze et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR38) for an overview), which may influence administration, planning, creation, learning, assessment, and other school-related practices. Compared to more conventional educational technologies (Chen et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR15); Ghafouri et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR25); Henkel et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR28); Luckin, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR40)), GAI poses new challenges for teachers and students alike. Research indicates that K-12 educators and learners often hold positive attitudes towards AI, citing benefits such as immediate, individualised support (Impact Research, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR34)), reduced cognitive load (Herm & Janiesch, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR29)), detailed feedback (Darvishi et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR18)), and adaptation to individual learning preferences (Robert et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR52); Manik, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR41)). Researchers have also demonstrated that intelligent AI-driven systems can minimise administrative burdens, allowing educators to focus more on direct student interaction and thereby enhance job satisfaction (Alam, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR2); Ghafouri et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR25)). Despite these promising developments, evidence remains disparate. Bond et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR7)) highlight, in a higher education context, that decision-making agency can shift from the teacher to AI-driven applications, particularly when educators lack strong technological awareness. This resonates with calls for human–machine co-existence, where interacting with AI is considered a skill in its own right (Dignum, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR19)) and where AI-generated data may also complicate decision processes (Gunneflo & Noll, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR27)). Moreover, AI’s reach extends beyond the classroom to influence professional and private spheres, potentially affecting individuals’ well-being and future outlook (Can et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR11); Dignum, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR19)). Simply learning to manoeuvre a GAI tool is less demanding than critically understanding its ethical, social, and privacy implications (Dignum, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR19); Strümke, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR57)). At the same time, widespread access to GAI has sparked debates about student cheating (Mehdaoui, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR43)) and fears that AI might replace teachers (Manik, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR41)). Some findings indicate that GAI use can negatively affect student learning outcomes (Rezende Junior & López-Simó, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR51); Shiau & Chau, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR55); Wangdi & Rigdel, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR64)), whereas others suggest it can boost learners’ critical thinking, problem-solving, and creativity (Weng et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR65); Salas-Pilco, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR53)). Salas-Pilco ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR53)) further recommends contextualised teacher training and calls for additional research on AI across a range of educational settings. Teacher perceptions of AI integration range from enthusiasm to apprehension (Rezende Junior & López-Simó, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR51); Wangdi & Rigdel, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR64)), and teachers are increasingly aware of data privacy and algorithmic bias issues (Shiau & Chau, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR55)).

Although the benefits of GAI are indisputable and educational professionals are the key stakeholders in determining its integration (Casal-Otero et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR13)), schools often struggle to keep pace with the rapidly evolving AI applications (Bawack et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR5); Kharbanda, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR37)). Moreover, teachers wishing to implement GAI frequently lack the necessary support (Tan & Subramonyam, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR60)). Although recent inquiries have started to explore teachers’ perspectives on GAI in K-12 (Bateman, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR4); ElSayary, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR21); Ahmed, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR1)), a literature reviews (Li et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR39)) noted that most studies are small scale. Fullan ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR24)) proposes that a “whole school” approach is needed to drive any change. We ran a professional development (PD) initiative that included multiple roles throughout the K-12 setting, and could therefore explore perceptions and experiences of educational professionals in a “whole school” approach; meaning that all employed educational professionals involved in educational practice concerning GAI integration was included.In line with Fullan ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR24)) we believe that such approach can be very insightful as educational professionals together influence the attitudes and culture, and thus decision making and practices, in schools. We raise the following research questions:

1. 1.
   
   How do educational professionals perceive challenges and opportunities regarding the integration of generative AI (GAI) in K–12 education?
2. 2.
   
   What are the support needs of educational professionals to responsibly integrate GAI into their practices?

We draw on Transformative Learning Theory (TLT) (Mezirow, [1991](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR44), [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR45)) to analyse the data, as this theory can be used to explain shifts in foundational perspectives through reflection, knowledge acquisition, and re-examination of professional roles in the educational environment.

2 Background
------------

### 2.1 Generative AI and K-12 education

The release of OpenAI’s GPT-3 in November 2022 made AI accessable to the wider public (Brown, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR9); Floridi & Chiriatti, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR23)). The newly released AI-tool had an amazing ability to mimic human-like text and later the generative AI tools could be used to generate both text, tables, images, media and communicate through speaking in natural language (Ramesh et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR50); OpenAI, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR49)). Since then, GAI has experienced rapid advancements, profoundly influencing society in general, and some would argue, education in particular (Zhai et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR68); Dwivedi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR20)). These advancements have sparked critical discussions about AI bias and wider societal impact (Bender et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR6); Dignum, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR19); Selwyn, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR54); Strümke, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR57)). In particular, concerns about the ethical use of AI in education have been raised, emphasising the need for *responsible* integration (Holmes et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR31)). In the context of K-12 education, GAI offers potential benefits such as personalised learning experiences, automated content creation, and enhanced student engagement (Henkel et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR28); Zhai et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR68)). Henkel et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR28)) demonstrated that large language models could effectively mark short answer questions in K-12 education, indicating potential for AI-assisted grading. Similarly, AI tools have been utilised to facilitate formative assessments, providing immediate feedback to students (Jeon & Lee, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR35)). In addition to this, teachers have reported that AI tools like ChatGPT can streamline lesson planning and introduce innovative teaching materials, thereby enhancing educational practices and reducing workload (e.g., Ifenthaler & Schumacher, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR33); Gibson & Ifenthaler, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR26)). Moreover, AI has been shown to be effective in personalising learning and engaging students through interactive experiences (Ahmed, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR1); Hojeij et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR30)) and research has started exploring a new form of intelligence, so called hybrid intelligence (e.g., Järvelä & Nguyen, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR36)). We are recurringly seeing educators expressing apprehension about student misuse, including plagiarism and over-reliance on AI-generated content (Ahmed, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR1); Bateman, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR4)) and the impact on students’ critical thinking skills (Manik, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR41); Mintz et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR46); Rezende Junior & López-Simó, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR51)). Emerging warnings are however also raised concering a potential negative influence on human interaction, and development of students’ social and emotional skills (ElSayary, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR21); Uğraş & Uğraş, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR63)).

These perspective signal the need for more knowledge to guide decision making on *if*, *when* and *how* GAI could or should be used in education. A *responsible* integration of GAI into K-12 education however, relies on educators’ preparedness to not only effectively, but ethically, utilise these technologies (Williamson & Eynon, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR66); Selwyn, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR54)). The lack of training and resources (Mehdaoui, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR43)), clear guidelines, and policies for AI integration in schools adds to these challenges (Holmes et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR32)). Studies have called for comprehensive PD programmes to enhance teachers’ technological proficiency and confidence in using AI tools effectively (Crompton et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR17); Tan & Subramonyam, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR60)). Even though research have begun to explore teachers’ initial perceptions and experiences with AI integration (e.g., Bateman, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR4); Tan & Subramonyam, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR60)) a significant gap remains in understanding how educational professionals in K-12 education perceive and respond to GAI integration (Zhai et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR68)) and what their support needs are. Addressing this gap is essential for developing appropriate PD programmes and support structures that facilitate transformative learning processes.

### 2.2 Transformative learning theory

TLT offers a framework for understanding how adults change their frames of reference through critical reflection and experiential learning (Mezirow, [1991](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR44), [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR45)). TLT has been found to be suitable to analyse transformative phases within the educational sector (McComish & Parsons, [2013](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR42)). The theory outlines ten phases of transformative learning: self-examination, critical assessment of assumptions, recognition of shared experiences, exploration of new roles, planning, acquiring knowledge and skills, provisional trying, building competence, and reintegration (Mezirow, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR45)). We will here try to situate these phases in an educational setting: first the concept of a “disorienting dilemma” which is characterised by challenging existing beliefs and prompts individuals to reassess their thoughts and actions (Mezirow, [1991](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR44)). Critical reflection is essential for transforming one’s frame of reference, involving not only evaluating one’s thoughts but also examining the underlying premises that support them (Mezirow, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR45)). Here, Mezirow distinguishes between instrumental learning (task-oriented problem-solving) and communicative learning (understanding values, beliefs, and feelings through dialogue) (Mezirow, [1991](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR44)). Increased hands-on experience with the GAI tools (instrumental learning) like ChatGPT can lead educational professionals to re-evaluate and consider new pedagogical approaches (Cao & Dede, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR12); Ifenthaler & Schumacher, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR33)) and increase their AI literacy, understand AI tools’ capabilities and limitations, and building confidence in their use, (Tedre et al. [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR62)). But collaborative learning environments and sharing experiences (communicative learning) is needed to foster best practices when it comes to AI integration (Nolan & Molla, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR47)).

3 Method
--------

To explore the current perceptions of educational professionals regarding GAI integration in K-12 education (Research Question 1) and to identify their support needs for responsible integration (Research Question 2), we adopted a cross-sectional design and a mixed-methods approach comprising quantitative and qualitative data collection during a PD seminar-workshop. Purposive sampling strategy (Bryman, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR10)) was utilised to recruit participants who were actively engaged as educational professionals within the K-12 public school system in a Swedish municipality. Leveraging an established partnership between the university and a Swedish city’s Department of Education, we ran two seminars and workshops that introduced AI and then GAI as a pedagogical tool and gathered participants’ reflections and experiences on GAI use in education. Approximately 600 educational professionals were invited to participate; 452 attended, yielding an overall response rate of 75.3%. Participants represented all levels of compulsory education: early years (K-3) (n = 70), middle years (4–6) (n = 76), lower secondary (7–9) (n = 95), upper secondary (10–12) (n = 72), and other school personnel (n = 11). The ‘Other’ category included other educational professionals involved in pedagogical and support roles (see Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Tab1)).

Data were collected using three methods: polls, surveys, and written reflections, chosen to capture both quantitative and qualitative insights. To gain a deeper understanding of educational professionals’ perceptions, we asked them questions related to their experiences, beliefs, and attitudes towards GAI (Figs. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig1), [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig2), [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig3) and [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig4)), as well as their confidence, intentions, and perceived support needs (Fig. [5](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig5)–10). Poll questions were deliberately paired to contrast respondents’ theoretical attitudes or beliefs against their practical experiences and actual behaviours to make potential tensions visible. For example, questions examining educators’ general openness to GAI (‘accept’ vs. ‘develop’) were paired with items addressing practical implementation, thus highlighting any gaps or contradictions between attitudes and actions. Similarly, poll items related to perceived benefits of GAI for students were matched with questions regarding educators’ confidence in integrating GAI ethically and effectively, thus illuminating possible discrepancies between positive expectations and current abilities. An example poll question includes: “Have you used a chatbot in class?” (see Appendix 1). The poll questions were administered sequentially during the PD seminar-workshop to maintain engagement and ensure thoughtful responses. Participation was voluntary, resulting in response rates ranging from 86.1–93.6%, with 389 to 423 responses to individual poll questions, and 324 responses to the survey containing demographic data (see Table [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Tab1)). Quantitative data from polls and surveys were analysed using descriptive statistics to identify trends and contrasts in participants’ responses and to inform deeper qualitative analysis (Bryman, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR10)). For qualitative analysis, participants wrote collaborative reflections using breakout rooms and shared Google Drive documents, enabling collective meaning-making and co-reflection. Examples of the reflection prompts were: *(1) ‘What does the development of AI mean for schools*, *today and in the long term?’* (school-level reflection), and *(2) ‘What specific actions can I take already today?’* (individual-level reflection). These shared documents had the following headings: Common for K-12, specific reflections for school years K-3, 4–6, 7–9 and 10–12 respectively. The ID for each citation is kept in the result section. The qualitative data, comprising open-ended survey responses and written reflections, were analysed using Thematic Analysis following Braun and Clarke ([2012](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR8)). Researchers familiarised themselves with the data through repeated reading, initially coding the data informed by TLT (Mezirow, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR45)). It was noted that participants frequently used the term “AI” interchangeably when specifically referring to ChatGPT or similar GAI tools. To preserve participants’ authentic voices, these references were left unchanged. Participant statements (n = 63) were coded and subsequently mapped onto the theoretical phases outlined by TLT. These initial categories were then iteratively reviewed and refined through a back-and-forth process, ultimately resulting in fewer consolidated themes. For instance, the phases “Disorienting Dilemma” and “Self-Examination” were combined because participants’ reflections frequently intertwined uncertainty, initial struggles, and introspection. Thus, consolidating these categories offered a more coherent representation of participants’ lived experiences. This iterative process led to the identification of six refined themes, each closely aligned with Mezirow’s transformative learning phases yet more accurately reflecting participants’ collective narratives: (1) Disorienting dilemma and self-examination, (2) Critical assessment and recognition of shared experiences, (3) Exploration of new roles and planning, (4) Acquiring knowledge and skills, (5) Provisional trying and building confidence, and (6) Reintegration and new perspectives. This study adheres to ethical guidelines (Swedish Research Council, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR59)) and informed consent was secured from all participants, who were assured of anonymity, data confidentiality, and their right to withdraw without consequence. No participants requested withdrawal from the study.

4 Results
---------

The results are structured into three main parts: poll and survey responses and Thematic Analysis of participants’ written reflections and group discussions. A minority of participants, 126 out of 406 (31%), reported having used GAI (such as ChatGPT) in teaching, indicating that the technology is not yet widely integrated into the school environment (Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig1)).

### 4.1 Poll responses

In their reflections, participants cited specific barriers to adoption, including a lack of training, and feeling unsafe: “We haven’t yet used AI in our teaching because”: “I don’t feel safe using it” (K-3)… “we don’t know how to” (Years 4–6). Thus, the low adoption rate may be better understood as the result of specific organisational and pedagogical barriers rather than merely general reluctance or negative attitudes. Figure [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig2) reveals respondents’ attitudes towards GAI in education. The vast majority of participants (354 out of 389; 91%) indicated a positive inclination towards GAI, either expressing willingness to ‘accept’ (198; 50.9%) or a desire to actively ‘develop’ (156; 40.1%) its integration into their educational practice. In contrast, a small minority of respondents explicitly chose to ‘ban’ (15; 3.9%) or ‘ignore’ (20; 5.1%) GAI altogether. These figures highlight a nuanced distinction within the generally positive attitudes: while many educators are prepared to accept GAI, a significant proportion recognise the necessity of further professional development before fully integrating these tools into daily practice. Participants’ written reflections underscore this distinction, emphasising their cautious optimism.

Figure [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig3) shows that while a majority of participants believe that chatbots generate interest among students (259 out of 391 participants, 66.2%), most (53.1%, 213 out of 401) think that their students lack knowledge of GAI (with 32.4% being unsure). One citation illustrates this tension: “We use AI as a tool, for example as homework help. However, more than before, the students need to learn to be critical and understand that it is a computer that is answering them. And *how* it answers is likely dependent on what it thinks you want to know.” (Years 7–9). But, the opinions regarding whether the use of AI tools contributes to becoming a better teacher were more evenly distributed with 111 out of 399 participants believing so, 117 not believing it, and 171 reported feeling unsure, corresponded to 27.8%, 29.3%, and 42.9% respectively. This uncertainty likely reflects the early stage of GAI integration in education and the lack of widespread, concrete evidence of its impact on teaching quality.

Figure [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig4) shows the distribution of viewing AI as a threat or possibility, where 207 participants (48.9%) view AI as an opportunity, compared to 41 (9.7%) who view it as a threat, while 175 (41.4%) are unsure. Interestingly, only 3.9% of educational professionals would ban AI (see Fig. [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig3)), suggesting that even among those who view AI as a threat, there’s a recognition of its potential value or inevitability in education, which is also reflected by an upper secondary school teacher: “We must carefully educate the students to think independently; a challenge that is old, yet still highly relevant.” (Years 10–12). The high percentage of uncertain responses might indicate a need for more information and clarity about AI’s role in education, “It’s difficult to perceive it as an opportunity or threat when you must know how to use it in the ‘right’ way, and ensure students understand AI as a tool, not a ‘shortcut’.” (Years 4–6).

In sum, the results from Figs. [1](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig1), [2](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig2), [3](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig3) and [4](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#Fig4) show an educational community that is cautiously optimistic about AI and particularly GAI integration. The generally positive attitude towards AI in general, coupled with low current adoption of GAI and high levels of uncertainty, suggests a significant opportunity for PD and support. The participants seem to recognise GAI’s potential to engage students and enhance teaching, but they may lack the knowledge, confidence, or resources to effectively integrate it into their practice.

### 4.3 Thematic analysis

Analysing the written reflections, we categories the responses into six themes, based on key stages of transformative learning: (1) Disorienting dilemma and self-examination, (2) Critical assessment and recognition of shared experiences, (3) Exploration of new roles and planning, (4) Acquiring knowledge and skills, (5) Provisional trying and building confidence, and (6) Reintegration and new perspectives.

#### 4.3.1 Theme 1: disorienting dilemma and self-examination

This theme includes expressions of uncertainty, initial challenges, and self-reflection as teachers begin to integrate AI into their educational practices.

> *“We haven’t yet used AI in our K-3 teaching. I don’t feel confident in it yet and need more knowledge to implement it”* (Years K-3). Similar sentiments were echoed across different grade levels: “*We feel uncertain and are just at the starting point when it comes to AI. It’s difficult to know which direction it will take*” (Years 10–12).

While many participants expressed uncertainty and a lack of confidence when faced with AI integration, the type of uncertainty among early years dealt mostly with gaining rudimentary competence to being exploring. Uncertainties in higher grades related more to future development concerning AI and education.

#### 4.3.2 Theme 2: critical assessment and recognition of shared experiences

This theme covers critical thinking about AI use and recognising common challenges among colleagues, fostering a shared understanding of the integration process. The participants engaged in critical reflection on the challenges and potential of AI in education. Many recognised the shared nature of these experiences. A common concern was the need for critical thinking skills among students.

> “*It’s important to teach students to be critical of sources and that you as an educator feel that you can master it [GAI] [there is a] possibility to use AI when planning lessons.* Another teacher, said “*It’s more difficult to use AI in the lower grades because students tend to ‘overuse’ the tool and don’t always understand that information from AI isn’t always true.*” (Years K-3).

Teachers display a cautisous approach, which reflect a need for discussing shared experiences which can help educational professionals to address common issues collectively.

#### 4.3.3 Theme 3: exploration of new roles and planning

Theme 3 encompasses ideas for AI integration and planning for implementation, reflecting the proactive steps taken by teachers despite initial uncertainties. Despite uncertainties, many participants began exploring potential uses of AI in their teaching. Ideas ranged from lesson planning to subject-specific applications.

> “*Tips for lesson planning*, *finding facts*, *offering feedback to students*, *analysing students’ difficulties by analysing their texts*, *translating texts to students’ mother tongues*” (Years 4–6), and “…*using AI for programming in mathematics – algorithms*” (Years 10–12).

Theme 3 shows that the participants are recognising the challenges and are actively seeking ways to integrate GAI into the teaching practices, demonstrating a willingness to innovate and adapt.

#### 4.3.4 Theme 4: acquiring knowledge and skills

Theme 4 includes comments about learning needs and PD, highlighting the importance of continuous learning and skill acquisition.

> Respondents widely recognised the need for further training and skill development. Many expressed a desire for PD opportunities. One group stated, “*We need a lot of professional development to be able to use it properly”* (Years K-12). Another group suggested a strategy for teaching and learning: “*Take help from colleagues who are already at the forefront. Dare to try something*, e.g., *a specific task*, *and continue from there*” (Years K-12).

Teachers express that acquiring knowledge and skills is central to gaining competence in using GAI in their classrooms, and that collegial support and courage can serve as a good starting point.

#### 4.3.5 Theme 5: provisional trying and Building confidence

Theme 5 covers experiences of testing AI in teaching and building competence, illustrating the iterative process of trial and improvement. Some participants reported initial attempts at integrating AI into their teaching practices and others try to encourage exploration.

> “*I have tested using it to create images in connection with story writing - creating images for stories*” (Years 4–6). “*I have used it to create study guides and when teaching second languge learners*, *I have used it for translation”* (Years 4–6*).* “*Inspiration for lesson plans*, *etc. However*, *you need to be able to review its content and have an idea*” (Years 7–9).

Results show that teachers do use GAI for in-class and other teaching related practices. Among teachers working with younger students, GAI is described primarily as a tool for creative image generation, often in connection with storytelling or aesthetic tasks. In contrast, teachers working with years 4–6 and upwards report using GAI more explicitly as a support for learning – for example to illustrate subject content, explain abstract concepts, or scaffold writing tasks.

#### 4.3.6 Theme 6: reintegration and new perspectives

This theme includes broader reflections on AI’s impact on education and society, indicating a shift in perspectives and considerations for future educational practices. In Mezirow’s phases, this phase reflects the highest maturity. In this data, statements that reflected this phase were less prominent. A few participants began to consider the broader implications of AI in education and society. This included reflections on how AI might change teaching roles and educational priorities.

> “*We must talk about AI. Many students use AI*, *about 50% of my Year 7 had used AI at some point when I asked them*” (Years 7–9). But questions mainly circulated round the more narrower hindrances to continue teaching and testing: “*How do we deal with cheating?*” (Years 10–12), indicating a need to reconsider traditional educational practices in light of AI advancements.

The analysis reflects participants at different stages of the transformative learning process when it comes to AI integration in their educational practices. The “Exploration of New Roles and Planning” theme suggests that many teachers are actively seeking ways to incorporate AI into their teaching, demonstrating a willingness to innovate and adapt. However, the responses under the themes “Acquiring Knowledge and Skills” and “Reintegration and New Perspectives” indicate areas where teachers may need more support and PD. The differences between educational professionals in lower and higher grades in the group reflections further highlight the varying levels of uncertainty, confidence, and integration of new perspectives among teachers.

5 Discussion
------------

### 5.1 GAI and perceived challenges, opportunities and support needs in K–12 education

Our findings shows that the perceptions and support needs of educational professionals centre on a cautious yet positive interest in GAI. Addressing research questions a significant majority (88%) expressed willingness to adopt GAI tools, yet only 31% had utilised them in teaching. Initial attempts by some participants to incorporate GAI into their teaching, such as using chatbots to assist in lesson planning or employing GAI to generate creative writing prompts, reflect the provisional experimentation and confidence-building stages, contributing to incremental growth in expertise (Bateman, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR4); Chang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR14); Nolan & Molla, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR48)). Our findings illustrate distinct pedagogical, technical, and ethical support needs among educators. Pedagogically, our respondents emphasised practical guidance and exemplars; technically, there was a demand for foundational skills and ethically, the necessity of explicit training in call for community-wide ethical frameworks. Yet, participants’ expressed undertaking initial attempts to incorporate GAI. These were mapped against: ‘Provisional Trying and Building Confidence’ phase of TLT (Mezirow, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR45)), demonstrating early stages of transformative learning. Throughout the PD event, participants expressed a desire for hands-on training with AI tools, opportunities for peer collaboration, and ongoing technical support, aligning with the ‘Acquiring Knowledge and Skills’ phase, highlighting specific support needs (Research Question 2). Although there was a positive attitude towards GAI, the adoption rates were low. Educators may intellectually accept GAI’s potential, yet remain hesitant to translate this acceptance into practice due concerns about data privacy and student dependency. We interpreted this as a gap between interest and practical implementation, with factors contributing to this gap include a lack of confidence, insufficient training, and uncertainty about how to integrate GAI effectively. The study also revealed notable differences in AI integration across educational stages. Early years educators exhibited greater hesitation, aligning with concerns about technology use in early childhood settings (World Health Organization, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR67)). This hesitancy places them in the initial ‘Disorienting Dilemma and Self-Examination’ phase of TLT. In contrast, educators of older students are progressing towards ‘Exploration of New Roles and Planning’, actively seeking ways to integrate GAI, indicating a readiness to advance through transformative learning stages and thinking more about “the uncertain future of AI”. The variation in readiness highlights the need for tailored PD initiatives that cater to the distinct needs and challenges faced by educators at different educational levels. While our data primarily identify overarching support needs across K-12 contexts, we observed a general pattern suggesting early-year educators (K-3) mainly need foundational skills and familiarity with GAI, whereas educators in higher grades (7–12) require advanced training focused on ethical considerations, critical assessment, and integration strategies. Tailored PD are critical because they can take current recommendations into account, as for example, recommendations by The World Health Organization’s ([2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR67)) on screen exposure, which differs for children in different age groups. Interestingly, the participants emphasised the need to learn how to foster critical thinking skills among students to be able to use GAI responsibly. While previous research identified educators’ ethical scepticism (e.g.,Hojeij et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR30); Holmes et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR31)). the nature of ‘Critical Assessment and Recognition of Shared Experiences’, in our study mostly related to problems with assessment or cheating, rather than development of pedagogical practices. Therefore, and in addition to considering recommendations, PD should be tailored to the use-cases and needs of the teachers in the differs year groups when using GAI in class for teaching and learning as well as for teaching practices outside of the classroom. The PD programmes should include ethical dimensions of AI use (cf. Banihasham et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR3)), ensuring educators are fully equipped to engage critically with AI systems in and outside of the classroom. In line with our findings, (a clear minority of 3,9% said they would “ban AI”) we call for a broader approach to PD intiatives, than to merely succeed with GAI adoption and in class integration. Educational professionals need to model agency, displaying, for example, *how* to use AI responsibly, *how* to make decisions with tools, *what* transparency means, *what* data to ask for, and *what* questions to raise, and so forth. Yet, participants rarely articulated explicit strategies for developing such agency.

AI’s integration in society is becoming more extensive than just the generative capabilities of creating stories or images (Dignum, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR19); Strümke, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR57)). Given the ubiquitous nature of AI in daily life, educational professionals must explicitly assume the responsibility of fostering AI literacy from compulsory education and onward. The critical need for ongoing PD and robust support structures resonates with existing literature on technology integration, emphasising that such support is vital for facilitating transformative learning and sustainable practice change (Crompton et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR17); Fischer et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR22); Tan & Subramonyam, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR60); Williamson & Eynon, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR66)). Challenges like insufficient training and resources (Holmes et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR32); Selwyn, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR54)) or lack of support (Söderström et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR58)) underscore the importance of institutional investment in PD. Prioritising AI literacy and competency among educational professionals can enhance the responsible integration of GAI tools, fostering transformative learning experiences for both teachers and students (Chiu et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR16); Tedre et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR61)). In order for transformative learning to mean ‘a whole school approach’ (Fullan, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR24)), professionals in all pedagogic roles must progress to the later stages of Mezirow’s theory, including provisional trying, where they actively experiment with GAI in controlled ways, and reintegration, where they confidently incorporate GAI into their professional practices with new perspectives. Our findings suggest that educational professionals working in the early years remain in the earlier stages of transformative learning, requiring substantial support to advance through these phases. Tailored PD initiatives, particularly those that include communicative learning - dialogue with colleagues and ongoing reflection - will be critical for helping these educators build the confidence and competencies needed for GAI integration (Mezirow, [2000](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR45)). Educational professionals, especially in the early years, need opportunities to reflect on and critically assess their assumptions about technology, engage in dialogues with colleagues, and develop new strategies that align with both pedagogical goals and ethical standards. This transformative process, supported by targeted training, will enable educational professionals to build confidence and develop their professional practice. That the respondents expressed enthusiasm for GAI does not automatically equate to transformative practices; instead, it points toward the necessity of comprehensive, ethically grounded PD initiatives with pragmatic guidelines, adoptable frameworks to guide the professional learning communities.

### 5.2 Recommendations

* Develop comprehensive PD programs focused on GAI integration and broader impact on AI in society tailored to the specific needs educators at different levels.
* Create peer support networks where educators can share experiences, resources, and best practices.
* Establish clear guidelines and ethical frameworks for GAI use in classrooms, addressing concerns such as data privacy and algorithmic bias.
* Ensure access to necessary technological resources and support within schools.

### 5.3 Limitations and future research

This study has several limitations. First, the reliance on self-reported data may introduce bias, as participants might overestimate their positive attitudes or underreport challenges. Second, the sample is limited to educational professionals in one Swedish municipality, which may affect the generalisability of the findings to other regions or countries. Third, the cross-sectional design captures perceptions at a single point in time, not accounting for changes over time. Future studies would benefit from observational and interview-based research to gain richer, nuanced insights into the specific professional development needs of educators at different K-12 levels, in diverse educational settings as well as longitudinal studies.

6 Conclusion
------------

Most educational professionals, in this study, perceive the integration of GAI as both a significant opportunity and a challenge, characterised by simultaneous enthusiasm and hesitation, primarily due to limited confidence and experience. Although the respondents were interested in learning how they could use GAI in their professional role and increase their understanding of the more direct hindrances or benefits, the actual implementation was constrained by gaps in knowledge, insufficient training, and uncertainty regarding responsible use and ethical implications (cf. Dignum, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR19)). To responsibly integrate GAI into their practice, educators therefore require comprehensive professional development, clear guidelines, and ethical frameworks tailored specifically to their varying support needs at different K–12 levels. Decision makers and AI researchers must prioritise comprehensive strategies beyond immediate classroom integration of GAI (e.g., Gunneflo & Noll, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13762-8#ref-CR27)). We call on decision makers to take a broader perspective when integrating GAI into schools, adjusted both to different year levels and encompassing broader competencies.
