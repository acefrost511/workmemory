## Abstract
Writing requires both metacognitive support for cognitive monitoring to ensure clear and logical organization of ideas and motivation to sustain this process. These factors complicate writing instruction. In K–12 education, writing instruction often does not provide authentic learning experiences, resulting in low student motivation. Augmented reality (AR), which enhances real-world contexts with interactive virtual elements, has been shown to increase motivation, yet it may also introduce extraneous processing that does not effectively support metacognitive development during writing. By contrast, generative learning strategies (e.g., summarizing, imagining, and self-testing) are well recognized for fostering metacognition by prompting learners to actively organize and integrate knowledge to construct coherent mental representations, but they are often perceived as cognitively effortful and thus struggle to sustain student motivation. To overcome these limitations, this study integrates AR with generative learning strategies to propose a generative-learning-based AR (GLAR) approach. This approach aims to combine the motivational benefits of AR with the metacognitive benefits of generative learning strategies to support students' writing. Additionally, the mediating roles of motivation and metacognition in the effects of pedagogical approach on students' writing performance were examined. The effectiveness of the GLAR approach and the validity of the mediation model were evaluated in a quasi-experimental study involving 117 pupils in China. The students were randomly assigned to one of three groups: the experimental group, which received the GLAR intervention; control group 1, which received a generative learning (GL) intervention without AR; and control group 2, which received an AR-based learning (AL) intervention without GL. The following results were obtained. (1) The GLAR group outperformed the AL group in writing accuracy, organization, and metacognition. (2) The GLAR group outperformed the GL group in writing organization, creativity, motivation, and metacognition. (3) Compared with the AL approach, the GLAR approach improved writing accuracy, organization, and overall writing performance through metacognition as a mediator. (4) Compared with the GL approach, the GLAR approach improved writing creativity and overall writing performance through motivation as a mediator and improved writing organization through the sequential mediation of motivation and metacognition. The present results demonstrated both the effectiveness of the proposed GLAR approach in improving students’ writing performance and the mediating role of motivation in its effect on metacognition.
## Introduction
Writing is a powerful skill that aids learning, reading comprehension, self-awareness, and communication (Graham & Harris, 2019). However, writing can be challenging due to its complex, fluid nature, meaning that ongoing problem-solving and progress monitoring are required (Chen et al., 2022). The complexity of writing tasks often leads students to have negative emotions and develop inefficient writing habits, creating motivational challenges (Bruning & Horn, 2000). Teachers have also reported that students’ writing motivation declines over an academic year, often due to a lack of authentic learning opportunities (Boscolo & Hidi, 2007).
Augmented reality (AR) can increase learners' motivation by providing authentic experiences directly related to writing topics (Li et al., 2023). As an immersive technology, AR enriches real-world environments by overlaying virtual elements such as text, images, or videos, thereby creating interactive learning experiences (Azuma, 1997). The use of AR in teaching can increase learners’ active engagement in writing (Koç et al., 2022). Although Koç et al. (2022) suggested that AR-induced motivation can considerably improve writing performance, additional evidence is needed to fully understand how motivation mediates the effects of AR on writing performance.
In addition to motivation, metacognition is essential to the writing process; it enables learners to effectively plan, monitor, and evaluate their work (Lee & Mak, 2018). With metacognition, students can progress beyond simply describing their knowledge and purposefully transform it into a written work while considering the work's audience, genre, and rhetorical stance to achieve their writing objectives (Bruning & Horn, 2000). Without metacognitive support, students may struggle to meaningfully apply their knowledge (Lee & Mak, 2018). Although AR is a promising educational tool, its ability to foster metacognition is limited. Tugtekin and Odabasi (2022) reported that AR promotes metacognition under only specific learning environment conditions. If used ineffectively, AR can result in extraneous cognitive processing, causing students to become more focused on the technology itself than their writing task. This shift can create an imbalance between metacognitive and motivational factors, preventing students from achieving meaningful learning outcomes (Chen et al., 2020, Chen, Li, Huang, et al., 2024; Mayer et al., 2023). Because successful writing requires a delicate balance of motivational and metacognitive factors (Igo, 2008; Nückles et al., 2020), AR must be paired with effective learning strategies if it is to sustain students' motivation, promote metacognitive engagement to achieve instructional objectives, and guide self-monitoring of learning progress.
Within this context, generative learning (GL) has exhibited promise as a strategy for improving AR-enhanced writing education (Chen, Li, Huang, et al., 2024). Rooted in constructivist theory, GL fosters active learning engagement by encouraging students to reorganize and integrate course materials with their prior knowledge (Fiorella & Mayer, 2016). For instance, a generative summarizing strategy can enable students to monitor their understanding, which in turn improves their performance and retention, conferring metacognitive benefits (Thiede & Anderson, 2003). Similarly, GL activities, such as engaging in debates, may deepen students' learning more than is achieved by nongenerative activities, such as reading course materials or watching recorded lectures (Zhou et al., 2024). GL can also reduce extraneous cognitive processing, honing students' focus on understanding the learning material. Efficient generative processing directs students’ focus to core cognitive processes and thus fosters a balance between motivational conditions and metacognition (Mayer et al., 2023; Petersen et al., 2023).
In this paper, we integrate AR with generative learning strategies to propose a generative-learning-based AR (GLAR) approach, which was designed to improve both learning motivation and metacognition in relation to writing tasks. We experimentally evaluated the effect of the GLAR approach on students' writing performance and determined the mediating roles of the students’ motivation and metacognition.
