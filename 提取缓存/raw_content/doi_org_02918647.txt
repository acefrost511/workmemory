Abstract
--------

This systematic review aims to provide a detailed and comprehensive overview of the use of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) in K-12 education. Using the PRISMA method, this research examined articles published between 2016 and 2024, selecting 197 relevant studies from 5 databases and 2 journals. The methodology included the use of the Mentefacto Map to identify keywords and define inclusion and exclusion criteria, ensuring a systematic and replicable approach. The findings of the review indicate that GAI offers significant opportunities to personalize learning experiences, motivate students, improve assessment methods, and introduce innovative and immersive teaching practices. The use of ChatGPT in educational environments is a clear example of this. There are several critical issues that need attention, such as the need for continuous teacher training on ICT and the development of ministerial guidelines that also address ethical and privacy concerns. There is a notable lack of concrete studies and specific experiments, not only focused on STEM disciplines but also open to the arts/humanities, as well as practical examples of GAI use in daily teaching practices, which should guide educators in selecting the most suitable educational technologies for their teaching objectives. An original contribution of this review lies in identifying specific gaps in the existing literature, along with the proposal of research perspectives and insights that have so far been underexplored.

1 Introduction
--------------

Educational Technologies (ET) have long supported teaching through innovation and access to resource. Despite these advantages, teacher training on ICT has posed a constant challenge, due to the need to continuously adapt to evolving tools and methodologies. From the rise of personal computers in the mid-1990s to Augmented, Virtual and Mixed Reality, technologies have continuously transformed and challenged teaching. Artificial Intelligence (AI) is set to disrupt current teaching more than any other technology, especially following the introduction of Machine Learning (ML), Deep Learning (DL) and Natural Language Processing (NLP). With the launch of ChatGPT, the first user-friendly Large Language Model (LLM), AI has transformed human–machine interaction, marking a significant, irreversible shift in education. The evolution of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) promises to transform many sectors, including education, but it raises complex ethical and knowledge-related issues for students and teachers, as well as challenges regarding its correct applicability in the teaching–learning process. This aspect becomes even more relevant when considering a particularly sensitive age level, such as K-12 students, who have more specific and individualized needs. Although empirical evidence attests to the effectiveness of emerging Educational Technologies in teaching practice (Crompton, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR44); Dawson, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR47); Zafari, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR176)), current literature lacks comprehensive guidance on access to resources, content creation and methodologies combining pedagogy with advanced educational tools (Kasneci et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR82); Zhang & Aslan, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR178)). Many studies also point to the lack of ministerial guidelines or frameworks for training on AI, with AI or by AI (Casal-Otero et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR25); Sanusi et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR132); Wang et al., [2024a](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR159), [2024b](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR160); Xia & Chiu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR165)). This research investigates the current use of GAI in the K-12 Education, based on limited recent literature. A period from 2016 to the present was chosen because that year marked the beginning of growing academic interest in the use of AI in education.

The *PICOC framework* (Population—Intervention—Comparison—Outcome—Context), a widely used framework in systematic reviews in education, health sciences, public policy and other fields to structure research questions and ensure rigorous analysis of evidence, was used in this way:

* *Population* (*P*) the target population comprises K-12 primary and secondary school students;
* *Intervention* (*I*): the intervention involves the integration of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) with complementary technological tools, such as learning apps, interactive educational platforms, and personalized learning software. This intervention also includes the application of innovative teaching methodologies that can be supported or enhanced by GAI. For exemple, GAI supports Flipped Classroom models by generating adaptive learning materials, Adaptive Learning through tailored content and real-time feedback, and Project-Based Learning with AI-assisted research and multimedia creation. In Gamification, AI develops interactive challenges, while in Collaborative Learning, virtual assistants facilitate discussions and task management.
* *Comparison* (*C*): the comparison could be made with more traditional teaching methodologies that do not incorporate GAI or with the isolated use of technologies without a coherent integration strategy. It might also involve comparing different degrees of technological integration or different types of GAI tools;
* *Outcome* (*O*) includes a GAI-based pedagogical framework that improves student understanding, engagement, collaboration, creativity, and inclusion, and can be adapted to diverse educational needs;
* *Context C* the context for this research is the K-12 school environment, which may vary from public to private schools, from urban to rural areas, and may include classroom, distance or hybrid learning modes.

Through the lens of the PICOC model, the following sub-questions have been formulated:

*Q1* How have AI and Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) been used to support enhance, and innovate teaching and learning?

*Q2* What opportunities and challenges have been identified regarding the use of AIEd in the K-12 educational context, particularly in terms of individualized and digitally innovative teaching?

*Q3* What specific pedagogical and technical challenges emerge for teachers in effectively integrating GAI into teaching practice, and what characteristics should define their initial and ongoing training to facilitate this integration?

*Q4* What are the current gaps in the scientific literature regarding the use of GAI in K-12 Education, and how can future research address these gaps?

This work systematically investigates the literature to provide answers to GAI's challenges and opportunities in K-12 education. The objective is to map the current state of research, carefully examining studies conducted to date, and outline future development directions in this area. To achieve this, a rigorous and replicable methodology was used, including selection of relevant databases, strategic keywords for bibliographic research, criteria for inclusion/exclusion of studies and critical analysis of collected data, with suggestions for potential future research paths.

The structure of this study is outlined as follows:

1. a.
   
   As the research is centered on the study of the evolution of Generative Artificial Intelligence, it is necessary to introduce the topic that forms the background of the study, seeking to outline its contours.
2. b.
   
   A general overview of the existing literature will then be presented, with the intent of outlining the main orientations of current research. The various perspectives through which Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) have been studied by researchers in the field of Education will be considered.
3. c.
   
   Subsequently, any gaps identified from the studies examined will be highlighted, focusing on aspects that require further exploration and future research.
4. d.
   
   The methodology adopted for the selection and analysis of the studies included in the systematic review will then be described.
5. e.
   
   This section will include the inclusion and exclusion criteria, data sources, and tools used to ensure a rigorous and replicable approach.
6. f.
   
   Finally, the discussion will revisit the most significant insights from the analyzed research, highlighting the main findings and implications for educational practice.
7. g.
   
   The conclusions will summarize the gaps identified in the reviewed literature and provide suggestions for future research trajectories, aiming to guide scholars toward new directions that could contribute to the improved integration of AI and GAI in K-12 Education.

2 What is GAI (Generative Artificial Intelligence)
--------------------------------------------------

Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) is one of the most innovative branches of AI. It employs Machine Learning (ML) and Deep Learning (DL) algorithms to generate content traditionally considered exclusive domain of human creativity, such as text, audio, video, images or computer code. GAI operates using AI models trained on large datasets, generating new content by replicating the most probable characteristics derived from the training data. This process begins with prompts or descriptions formulated in natural language by users (either humans or software), from which the generative AI software produces various types of content.

Content generation combinations include:

* *Text-to-Text* generates text from other text (e.g. creating new types of text as summaries, paraphrases, translations, or completions from provided text).
* *Text-to-Image* generates images from text descriptions (e.g. concept art for new products, story settings or characters for stories or games).
* *Text-to-Speech* converts text into synthetic speech (e.g. voice assistants like Siri or Alexa, reading books and articles in audio format, or generating narration for videos, presentations or multimedia content).
* *Text-to-Video* creates videos from text (e.g. animated videos, visual presentations or stories).
* *Text-to-Music* composes music based on text descriptions (e.g. create soundtracks for films, games and videos, jingles and theme songs).
* *Speech-to-Text* transcribes spoken speech into text (e.g. generation transcripts from meetings, conferences or interviews).
* *Speech-to-Speech* translates speech or modifies vocal styles in real-time (e.g. real-time translation from one language to another or speech synthesis with changes in tone, timbre or accent).
* *Speech-to-Video* creates videos based on vocal input (e.g. generating video presentations with vocal narration).
* *Image-to-Image* manipulates existing images (e.g. enhancing, restoring old or damaged images, applying different artistic styles).
* *Image-to-Text* generates textual descriptions from images (e.g. detailed description of image content, captioning and tagging).
* *Image-to-Speech* converts images into spoken descriptions (e.g. audio narration of image content for visually impaired individuals, automatic storytelling, audio stories from a sequence of images).
* *Video-to-Text* transcribes and describes video content into text (e.g. providing textual descriptions of scenes and actions in videos).
* *Video-to-Speech* creates vocal narrations or translations based on video content (e.g., voice-overs for scenes or dialogue, translations of spoken content into another language).
* *Music-to-Text* transcribes music into notation or generates detailed description of musical content (e.g. creating musical scores or automatically describing genres, rhythms and instruments used in a track).
* *Music-to-Speech* creates spoken narration or commentary based on music tracks (e.g., audio guides explaining musical compositions, narration describing musical content).

*LLMs*, an acronym for Large Language Models, are a class of AI models specifically designed to understand and generate natural language text. These models are trained on massive datasets, often containing billions of words from various sources such as books, articles, websites and conversations, enabling them to learn a wide range of linguistic and contextual information. Many LLMs utilize architectures based on deep neural networks, such as transformers, which are very effective in processing natural language. One prominent example is the *GPT* (Generative Pre-trained Transformer) model by OpenAI. GAI not only expands creative and practical possibilities but also raises significant ethical and privacy issues. For example, its ability to generate fake images and videos (commonly referred to as *deepfakes*) poses significant challenges regarding the authenticity of information and the protection of privacy.

3 Overview of Existing Literature
---------------------------------

The primary goal of schools has always been to enhance students’ educational success and improve the quality of teaching. Over the years, new digital technologies have provided significant support in terms of access to educational resources, individualized teaching, and the facilitated and motivating processing of content. In the last thirty years, Education has undergone a significant transformation due to the use of Information and Communication Technologies (ICT). The introduction of personal computers in classrooms in the 1990s marked the beginning of this revolution, enabling access to new forms of learning and the use of educational software. With the advent of Internet, there has been a democratization of access to information, facilitating knowledge exchange and remote collaboration.

At the turn of the new millennium, ICT began to profoundly influence teaching methodologies, with the spread of Learning Management Systems (LMS), which allowed the management and distribution of online teaching materials, and the implementation of blended learning strategies. Additionally, the widespread use of Interactive Whiteboards (IWBs) made lessons more engaging and interactive. In the past decade, the rapid development of mobile devices and educational applications has made learning ubiquitous, allowing students to access educational resources anytime and anywhere. The adoption of Augmented Reality (AR) and Virtual Reality (VR) has introduced further interactive and immersive dimensions into the educational experience. Today, like many other sectors, the Education sector has been strongly impacted by the advent of Artificial Intelligence (AI), which has shown opportunities to extend and improve learning (Chen et al., [2020a](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR33), [2020b](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR35); Crompton & Song, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR45); Crompton et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR43)). The term AIEd (Artificial Intelligence in Education) is starting to be discussed as the “engine behind much of intelligent educational technology” (Chen et al., [2020a](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR33), [2020b](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR35); Crompton, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR44)), designed to be a “powerful tool for opening what is sometimes called the black box of learning, giving us a more detailed understanding of how learning actually happens” (Luckin & Holmes, [2016](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR97)). According to Luckin et al. ([2016](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR97)), the algorithms and models that comprise AI in Education form the basis of an essentially human activity. AI in Education offers the possibility of more personalized, flexible, inclusive and engaging learning.

Zhang and Aslan ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR178)) summarize some of the most widely applied AIEd technologies and their proven or potential benefits for Education (Fig. [1](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#Fig1)): for students, AIEd can facilitate various interactions, increase student engagement, generate adaptive learning materials, offer metacognitive suggestions, provide enriched learning environments, and improve learning outcomes. For educators and administrators, AIEd can provide predictive models, identify gifted or at-risk students, monitor learning progress, create personalized teaching materials, assessments and feedback, and instantly analyze data at scale for assessment or administrative purposes. AI-enhanced learning environments can improve LMS for both teachers and students through expert systems, generate visual feedback, and enrich the learning experience with visualization and immersive technologies.

Proven and potential educational benefits of AI technologies / *Source* Zhang & Aslan, ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR178))

For teachers, AI can facilitate effective formative and summative assessment of students’ complex knowledge (Chen et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR34)), in both asynchronous and synchronous online Education contexts, promoting effective student learning and performance (Black & Wiliam, [1998](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR14)) and combating school dropout (Papamitsiou & Economides, [2014](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR117)).

The concept of an *adaptive learning system* is frequently cited recently, referring to the aim of facilitating individual student learning by combining various elements of learning systems, such as user interfaces, content and learning paths, according to the status of each student (Xie et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR167)). In other words, personalized/adaptive learning has a significant number of potential applications on the intelligent devices with the rapid development of AI, VR, Cloud computing and wearable computing.

Other research has shown that the typical intelligent agents of AI assist teachers in designing the learning and teaching process by leaving more repetitive tasks to systems and allowing them more time to focus on other aspects such as individualized strategies, data analysis, and creativity (Chan & Zary, [2019](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR29); Hwang et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR71); Zawacki-Richter et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR177)). Ouyang and Jiao ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR116)) attempted to synthesize the use of Artificial Intelligence in Education through three paradigms:

* *AI-Directed Learning* the student is the direct recipient of the AI system, which provides opportunities for individualized learning and immediate feedback.
* *AI-Supported Learning* cooperative learning where there should be interaction between the student and the AI system, facilitated through the student’s engagement with information and technology.
* *AI-Enhanced Learning* the use of AI in Education is considered a complex system involving students, teachers, stakeholders and AI techniques, serving as a tool to enrich human intelligence.

The latest frontier in the development of ICT for teaching is the GAI, the practical implementation of AI evolution as envisioned by Huang et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR70)). GAI can simulate human listening (automatic translation, speech recognition), speaking (speech synthesis, human–computer dialogue), observing (artificial vision, image recognition, text recognition), thinking (theorem proving), learning (machine learning, intelligent adaptive learning) and acting (robotics).

4 Gaps Identified During Review
-------------------------------

Research highlights gaps in professional development of teachers and differentiation of AI usage across educational levels (Chai et al., [2020a](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR26), [2020b](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR27); Lee et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR93)). Van Mechelen ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR152)) notes that despite the growing importance of emerging technologies, they still receive minimal attention compared to broader digitalization efforts. Although the relevance of AI in K-12 Education is increasing, there is still a lack of a comprehensive systematic review examining its potential (Chen et al., [2020a](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR33), [2020b](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR35)). Crompton ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR44)) analyzed the use of AI in Education, highlighting key functions such as student monitoring, group management, automated assessment, and data-driven decision-making. Despite the importance of these themes, the effective implementation of AI in K-12 schools has not yet realized its full potential, emphasizing the need for further empirical research for more effective integration (Hrastinski et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR68); Zawacki-Richter et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR177)). Beyond usage and application, the quality of Education is further complicated by the dynamics of teaching primary and lower secondary school children, whose needs go beyond mere fact recall. With a different set of goals and objectives and further disparity in learning methods and approaches, AI for teaching primary and secondary schools must be more adaptive and sensitive to the needs of younger students.

Students in primary and lower secondary education have distinct educational needs compared to older learners, as their learning is deeply influenced by sensory experiences, direct interaction, and curiosity-driven engagement. At this stage, teachers play a crucial role not only as facilitators of knowledge but as active mediators who guide cognitive, emotional, and social growth through dialogue, hands-on learning and personalized instruction. The integration of GAI in this context goes beyond functioning as a simple support tool: it introduces dynamic and interactive elements that shape educational relationships, tailor learning pathways and accommodate different cognitive styles. GAI facilitates real-time content adaptation, provides immediate feedback, encourages teamwork in problem-solving, thereby reinforcing the teacher’s role in skill acquisition and student development. This transformation also influences teachers’ perceptions of GAI, which is increasingly regarded not merely as an instructional aid but as an active agent for educational processes. As a result, this raises fundamental questions about how AI technologies may redefine pedagogical practices and effectively respond to the evolving needs of an ever-changing student population.

Positive attitudes toward technology will not only increase its use but also improve classroom outcomes and online learning environments (Herman, [2002](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR66)). Other research indicates a growing trend towards using AI beyond the field of computer science, suggesting the need for an interdisciplinary approach in Education. However, most studies remain focused on STEM fields, highlighting the importance of exploring AI possibilities in other subjects such as art, geography and history to ensure a more comprehensive educational application (Crompton, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR44); Zawacki-Richter et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR177)).

The interest in machine learning and its social impacts has demonstrated the effectiveness of AI Education units (Marques et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR101)), but the diversity of students and available resources makes designing AI curricula for K-12 Education particularly complex, requiring greater personalization and increased support for teachers in mediating curriculum policies (Chiu, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR36); Priestley, [2011](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR122); Simmons & MacLean, [2018](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR138)). Su et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR145)) also raises the need to adapt AI content appropriately for age. These elements highlight the urgent need to develop teaching strategies that improve the effectiveness with which teachers apply emerging technologies, bridging the gap between technical skills and practical application in Education (Flogie & Aberšek, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR52)). However, the level of digital competence is very low among teachers, who believe they have some knowledge of ICT but do not know how to use them properly (Alferez-Pastor, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR5)). Despite the growing body of research, the literature reveals a lack of clear frameworks on how AI should be taught (Ng et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR115); Su et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR145); Van Brummelen et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR20)). Providing guidance to teachers on how to use AI to create efficient learning environments is crucial (Chiu, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR38); Miao et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR104)), as the technological literacy among students (Wong & Looi, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR163)). Teachers play a key role in technological transformation, and their attitude towards educational technologies is a determining factor in responding to educational innovation and technological advances today.

The importance of the TPACK (Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge) model, introduced by Mishra and Koehler ([2007](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR105), [2008](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR86)), emerges as a fundamental element for bridging the gap between AI theory and its practical application, emphasizing the integration of technological, pedagogical and content knowledge. Therefore, teacher training must be renewed to include these aspects, going beyond simply teaching technical skills and encouraging critical reflection on how technology affects the learning process and teaching practices. The challenges are even more evident with the development of GAI after 2022, year in which ChatGPT was launched sparking wide adoption and intense debate about its impact in several areas, including education.

Lee ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR93)) notes a lack of clarity on how AI, particularly GAI, can be effectively implemented in Education. Wang and Cheng ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR161)) identified three main areas of AI research: learning from AI, about AI and with AI. Chiu et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR37)) expanded on these themes, analyzing AI use in learning, teaching, assessment and administrative management, emphasizing how AI can improve task allocation and enhance teachers' teaching skills.

The adoption of GAI in the educational field underscores the need to train students who not only use, but also actively build and critique knowledge, capable of identifying ethical issues and engaging in critical reflections on the social implications surrounding AI (Schleiss & Laupichler et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR135)). Lee ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR94)) proposes the Know-Build-Critique (KBC) model to guide students in the conscious use of GAI, emphasizing the importance of understanding the capabilities and limitations of technology, integrating new information critically and responsibly, and ethically evaluating generated information. Van Brummelen et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR20)) state that assessment tools for AI literacy are lacking. This paradigm shift requires updating educational infrastructures to support innovative learning in an AI-mediated environment. Chiu ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR37)) highlights the growing importance of new skills such as AI and digital and media literacy, which should become central educational outcomes. Hsu ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR69)) adds that although GAI offers endless possibilities, it is essential to plan and learn how to best use these technologies to personalize Education, assist teachersand support collaborative learning. However, concerns exist regarding privacy, information accuracy, and students’ critical ability to use GAI. Jauhiainen ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR76)) recommends caution in using GAI, suggesting the importance of balancing to maximize benefits while minimizing potential risks. In terms of practical impact, Cope et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR41)) and Trust et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR149)) note that GAI, as in the case of ChatGPT, can play multiple educational roles, from providing automated feedback to differentiating teaching. Educators must learn to adapt to these emerging technologies by treating them as evolutions of existing teaching tools and integrating them effectively into educational strategies (Halaweh, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR63); Hwang & Chen, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR72)). Lee ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR93)) expresses concern about the lack of clarity on how GAI can be adopted and effectively used in Education. Furthermore, Siemens et al. ([2022](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR137)) suggest that the future of learning should focus on collaboration between humans and artificial agents, promoting learning modes that enhance educational experiences in meaningful and relevant ways.

Future research developments should specifically focus on research into AIEd*,* considering the crucial distinctions in AI implementation at various educational levels, as the AIEd research is predominantly limited to higher Education contexts, leaving the K-12 sector relatively unexplored (Popenici & Kerr, [2017](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR121); Zawacki-Richter et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR177)). It is imperative that research not only encourages the development of new technologies but also addresses the pedagogical, ethical, and practical challenges associated with their real-world implementation. Artificial Intelligence can refer to a variety of cutting-edge technologies that could have disparate educational implications for primary and secondary schools. The absence of a unified concept of AI generates ambiguity and hinders the effective integration of AIEd in schools (Wang, [2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR161)). While higher Education requires distinctly more advanced curricula and pedagogical approaches, K-12 environments require tailored methodologies to respond to the diverse cognitive levels of primary and secondary school students (Chen et al., [2020a](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR33), [2020b](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR35); Crompton et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR43)). This will require close collaboration between researchers, educators and policymakers to ensure the ethical and effective use of AI, thus deeply enriching students’ educational experiences. This implies active involvement of teachers in developing teaching activities and learning tools, supporting the adoption of participatory and student-centered design approaches in school programs (Chiu, [2020](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR38); Crompton, [2022](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR44)).

5 Toward a Selection and Analysis of Specific Studies
-----------------------------------------------------

After providing a comprehensive overview of the existing literature on the use of AI and GAI in educational processes, a transition to a more in-depth analysis of selected studies has become necessary. This shift is crucial for consolidating the arguments and critical issues identified, thereby providing a solid foundation for the final conclusions of the research. The subsequent step will involve outlining the methodology employed for the selection of studies, followed by a critical analysis of the results presented in these studies.

6 Method
--------

The systematic literature review in this study followed a rigorous method to answer the research questions posed in the introduction, ensuring a systematic and replicable approach. The goal of this approach was to provide a comprehensive and balanced overview of the published studies on Artificial Intelligence in Learning, specifically focusing on the new frontiers concerning Generative Artificial Intelligence in the Teaching and Learning process. A systematic review aggregates research findings to provide a comprehensive overview of trends and patterns in the literature. It assesses the consistency and variability of studies by identifying influencing factors such as methodologies, geographic contexts and prior research. By adhering to a strict protocol, it ensures a transparent, reproducible evaluation of scientific evidence, minimizing bias in the interpretation of results. Systematic reviews identify insufficient research areas and suggest future study directions.

The PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) Statement was used as the review model, a widely recommended and utilized method (Moher et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR108)). Following the PRISMA method helps ensure the transparency, completeness and reliability of systematic reviews, minimizing bias risks by applying a standardized scientific methodology at every stage of the review process.

This systematic review was conducted between July 2023 and May 2024.

The Mentefacto Map is a tool created by Conceptual Pedagogy to represent concepts. It is an ideogram that represents and conceptualizes a complex idea (Fig. [2](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#Fig2)). In the context of a systematic review, using a Mentefacto Map helps identify keywords and accurately define inclusion and exclusion criteria.

The research was conducted following the steps outlined by the PRISMA method, beginning with the initial exploration of three specialized databases, namely Scopus (27% of articles), ERIC—Educational Resources Information Centre (13%), and WOS—Web of Science (29%). The sample identified at the end of the selection as relevant to my research questions consists of 197 articles, of which 130 are relevant or highly relevant and 67 are semi-relevant.

Additionally, two searches were conducted in specific journal databases, considering that they might have indexed more recent articles on such an innovative topic as GAI in Education compared to traditional articles.

I selected two journals, the British Educational Research Journal (BERJ) (103 articles, or 15%) and Springer Journals (107 articles, or 16%).

The list of articles meeting the research criteria was refined according to the following criteria:

* *Publication years* 2016–2024 (2022–2024 for journal articles).
* *Document type* articles, book chapters, reviews, conference proceedings.
* *Languages* English, Italian, Spanish.
* *Thematic areas* relevant to my research keywords, varying according to the databases queried (e.g., Social Sciences, Computer Sciences, AI, Educational Technology, Computers and Education, etc.).

In the initial phase of the PRISMA method, called "Identification", the results of the preliminary searches were recorded.

The exclusion criteria (defined as A–B) referenced in the PRISMA flowchart in Fig. [4](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#Fig4) were different educational levels (A) and different field of study (B). Based on these, the Scopus database identified 182 valid articles, of which 19 were excluded based on criterion (A) and 25 based on criterion (B), reducing the number to 138 articles that proceeded to the next screening phase. The ERIC database identified 91 valid articles, of which 26 were excluded based on criterion (A) and 25 based on criterion (B), leaving 40 for the next screening phase. The WOS database identified 193 valid articles, of which 33 were excluded based on criterion (A) and 116 based on criterion (B); the articles proceeding to the next screening phase totaled 44. Regarding journals, BERJ identified 103 articles, of which 19 were excluded for criterion (A) and 62 for criterion (B), reducing to 22 for the subsequent screening phase. The Springer database allowed the selection of 107 articles, of which 10 were excluded for criterion (A) and 34 for criterion (B), advancing 63 articles to the next screening phase. Therefore, the total number of initially selected articles was 676, and the number admitted to the subsequent screening phase was 307. Duplicates (-33), or the same article present in multiple databases, were removed to reach a total of 274 articles. In the screening phase, these articles were analyzed based on the relevance of the abstracts and subjected to further exclusion criteria (A–B–C–D–E–F) such as:

* Focus on ethics (A = 27)
* Future developments of ongoing research (B = 6)
* Purely technical approach (C = 17)
* Research methods and models not focused on the research (D = 19)
* Too much gaming-oriented (E = 3)
* Specifically oriented towards a particular subject (F = 5)

7 Analysis of Results
---------------------

All studies agreed that AI reveals great potential for use in educational systems to enhance their functionality. Several studies focused their research on the system known as AIEd. The benefits and challenges of AI for Education, as identified in the selected studies, were primarily evaluated from two perspectives: for teachers and for students. For students, the advantages include personalized learning, rapid feedback, intelligent tutoring, adaptive learning, and facilitation in completing assignments. The role of the teacher in applying AI in Education, initial and ongoing training, the necessity for a new pedagogical approach aligned with new tools, and the need for a new literacy to be included in a revised curriculum were also discussed. 8 Discussion
------------

This systematic review aimed to address four fundamental questions concerning the integration of AI and GAI in K-12 Education:

* *Q1* How has AI, particularly GAI, been used to support, enhance, and innovate teaching and learning?
* *Q2* What opportunities and challenges have been identified regarding the use of AIEd in the K-12 educational context, particularly in terms of individualized and digitally innovative teaching?
* *Q3* What specific pedagogical and technical challenges do teachers face when effectively integrating GAI into teaching practices, and what characteristics should define their initial and ongoing training to facilitate this integration?
* *Q4* What are the current gaps in the scientific literature regarding the use of GAI in K-12 Education, and how can future research address these gaps?

In the discussion, we will examine the key findings related to each of these questions, highlighting the main evidence emerging from the literature. We will also evaluate the practical implications of these findings for educators and educational policymakers, emphasizing the areas where further research is needed.

*Second Answer*: while AI offers significant opportunities to personalize learning and introduce innovations in K-12 Education, there are also notable challenges to overcome. The lack of resources, the need to develop structured frameworks, and issues of ethics and data quality are critical aspects that require attention. There is a need to develop additional strategies to ensure inclusivity and educational effectiveness. Only through ongoing research and well-structured implementation can AI fully realize its potential in the K-12 educational context.

*Q3*: The integration of AI and GAI in K-12 Education represents a significant challenge, but also a unique opportunity to improve AI literacy among students and teachers.

* *Pedagogical challenges:* one of the main pedagogical challenges involves the development and evaluation of specific pedagogical tools for teaching AI. Karalekas et al., ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR81)) emphasize that these tools must be designed to improve AI literacy through collaborative and game-based approaches, making learning more engaging and understandable for students. Yim and Su ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR173)) highlight the importance of preparing teachers to adapt AI technologies to students' different learning styles, promoting active and engaging learning.
* *Technical challenges:* a significant technical challenge is the need for continuous evaluation of the AI methodologies used. It is essential that teachers are trained in collaborative and experimental approaches, including a constant evaluation of methodologies to ensure that teaching practices remain up-to-date and relevant for students (Xia & Chiu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR165)). Additionally, pedagogical tools must be effective for different age groups and learning levels. Teachers must be prepared to use assessment techniques and curriculum adaptation to respond to students' varying needs inclusively (Yang et al. [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR169)). Another technical challenge is adapting AI technologies to students' different learning styles. Teachers must be ready to manage the complexity of personalizing AI technologies to ensure that each student can benefit maximally from these innovations (Yim & Su, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR173)). Finally, the rapid evolution of AI technologies requires that educational tools be constantly updated. Teachers must be trained to monitor and implement the latest available AI methodologies and tools, ensuring that educational resources remain relevant and effective (Xia & Chiu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR165)).
* *Characteristics of initial and ongoing training:* to facilitate this integration, teacher training must have certain fundamental characteristics. It is essential to develop game-based educational resources that make learning AI more engaging and understandable for students (Karalekas et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR81)). Teachers must be trained in collaborative and experimental approaches, with continuous evaluation of AI methodologies to ensure their effectiveness and relevance (Xia & Chiu, [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR165); Yang et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR169)). Training must include elements of transparency and understanding of AI technologies to reduce teachers' concerns and increase their confidence (Nazaretsky et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR113)). Promoting individual innovativeness is crucial; it is important to encourage teachers to experiment with new technologies creatively and responsibly (Uzumcu & Acilmis, [2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR150)). Hays et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR65)) emphasize the need to provide ongoing support and professional development for the effective use of advanced AI tools such as ChatGPT, including practical examples and ethical guidelines. Finally, according to Addo ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR2)), Antonenko and Abramowitz ([2023](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR9)) and Sanusi et al. ([2024](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR131)), teacher training should be designed to overcome misconceptions about AI, improving motivation and developing advanced digital skills.

*Third Answer*: the integration of GAI into teaching practice requires addressing pedagogical challenges, such as developing effective teaching tools, continuous evaluation of methodologies used, and adapting technologies to different learning styles, as well as technical challenges, such as the need to continuously update educational tools and ensure they are effective for different age groups. Teacher training, both initial and ongoing, must be collaborative and continuous, including innovative, inclusive, and transparent approaches to ensure the effectiveness and constant updating of educational practices, also improving AI literacy among students.

9 Conclusions
-------------

The systematic review has highlighted that GAI has significant potential to revolutionize K-12 Education. It is still an under-explored area, research is still in full swing investigating AI. GAI can personalize learning experiences, improve assessment methods and introduce innovative teaching practices. The adoption of tools like ChatGPT has shown remarkable potential in transforming educational environments, making learning more engaging and tailored to individual students' needs. However, the integration of GAI presents several challenges. One of the main challenges concerns teacher trainings, which must be continuous and targeted, covering technical, pedagogical and ethical aspects. Concerns about student privacy and the accuracy of information generated by AI require specific attention. Additionally, the lack of Ministerial guidelines makes it difficult for educators to adopt these technologies in a conscious and systematic way. Comparison with previous studies, such as those by Crompton et al. ([2020](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR43)) and Zhang & Aslan, ([2021](/article/10.1007/s10758-025-09853-7#ref-CR178)), confirms the benefits of AI for Education, although some studies do not show significant improvements in student outcomes. This variability underscores the need for further research to clarify the effectiveness of AI and GAI in different educational contexts.

The review identified significant gaps in the existing literature, particularly regarding teacher training and the practical implementation of GAI in K-12 schools. A lack of a shared theoretical framework and detailed guidelines for AI implementation was noted. Therefore, it is essential to develop effective methodological strategies and curricular models that can support inclusive and sustainable use of AI.

Future research should assess the long-term impact of AI on K-12 education based on longitudinal empirical research, and should focus on creating comprehensive frameworks that include technological, pedagogical and ethical aspects.

Moreover, it is important to explore the effectiveness of AI technologies in various disciplinary contexts, extending the analysis beyond STEM fields to include humanities and social sciences. Considering these findings, it is recommended to develop continuous training programs for teachers, focusing on the integration of AI and GAI technologies. Establishing ethical guidelines to ensure responsible use of these technologies is also crucial. Future research should explore the long-term impacts of GAI on Education, developing collaborative and interdisciplinary approaches that involve teachers, students, and technology developers.

Should also be considered that, although the integration of GAI into education has shown great potential for the entire educational ecosystem, its impact should be critically examined rather than viewed in a purely optimistic or deterministic way. Technological progress does not automatically lead to educational improvement, rather, it evolves through a complex interaction of innovation, institutional frameworks, teaching methods, and socioeconomic contexts. Looking at the progressive introduction of ICT in education, it is evident that this process did not simply enhance existing practices but led to a redefinition of teaching approaches, sometimes replacing even the most established and effective ones, as well as reshaping the role of educators. If adopted uncritically, GAI risks replicating these patterns, emphasizing efficiency and productivity at the expense of developing deeper cognitive and epistemological skills. Examining educational contexts where GAI may need to be reconsidered—or even excluded—moves away from the focus of this study. However, future research should explore this issue, considering education as a space for the construction of critical knowledge and cultural awareness. The analysis should cover key aspects such as critical and metacognitive learning, the development of creativity and divergent thinking, educational and experiential interactions, and, finally, the acquisition of fundamental competencies, especially in primary and lower secondary education, where the gradual consolidation of knowledge is essential.

An innovative aspect of this review is the identification of specific gaps in the literature, providing perspectives and research insights that have been little explored so far.

1. 1.
   
   *Lack of concrete examples of AI in teaching:* practical examples for constructing teaching units are needed, whereas the current literature mainly focuses on integration strategies.
2. 2.
   
   *Teacher training:* the necessity for resources and development programs for teachers is discussed, but practical and daily training based on the constant application of AI and GAI according to provided guidelines could facilitate the integration of these tools into curricular teaching.
3. 3.
   
   *Skills in ML and balance between disciplines:* the current literature focuses more on STEM disciplines, neglecting the humanities and creative subjects with which many experiments could be conducted.
4. 4.
   
   *Lack of studies and experiments in Europe:* current studies have not paid particular attention to this element.
5. 5.
   
   *Role of teachers and pedagogical frameworks:* the need to define the role of the teacher and create specific pedagogical frameworks based on specific ICT is mentioned generally in studies, and it is suggested to further explore the use of models like TPACK or AI4K12.
6. 6.
   
   *Inclusivity and support for disabilities:* studies that have explored the use of AI for special Education were oriented towards specific disabilities, while concrete indications are needed on how AI can support learning in class for students with disabilities.
7. 7.
   
   *Connection with pedagogical theories and innovative methodologies:* the current literature mentions the need to link AI with pedagogical approaches, but this review calls for new experiments with specific methodologies and strategies.
8. 8.
   
   *Applications of emerging new technologies:* in parallel with the integration of emerging technologies such as wearable devices, robot control and mobile communication technologies, new research directions for their educational application would be needed.
